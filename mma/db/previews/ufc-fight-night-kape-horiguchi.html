Overview

UFC Fight Night 279 is a flyweight title-eliminator wrapped around a reasonably entertaining undercard. The main event is a genuine rematch worth watching: Manel Kape, who has quietly become one of the most dangerous flyweights on the planet, gets another crack at Kyoji Horiguchi, the Rizin and Bellator legend who submitted him back in 2017. Both men have rebuilt themselves considerably since that first meeting, but Kape's knockout of Brandon Royval last December made the argument louder than it has ever been. He is a genuine title contender with one-punch power at 125 pounds, and this is his moment.

Below the main event, there is a mix of compelling and puzzling matchups. Murtazali Magomedov makes his UFC debut as a 10-0 finisher with nukes in both hands and a submission arsenal to back it up, taking on the streaky Melsik Baghdasaryan in a fight that figures to end early. Navajo Stirling, the 9-0 City Kickboxing product riding a wave of hype, gets his first main card slot against Ion Cutelaba, a man who has been ending hype trains in this division for the better part of a decade. And Beatriz Mesquita continues her campaign to make women's bantamweight violent again, going for her sixth submission win in seven fights against Melissa Mullins.

The prelims have the feel of a UFC roster audit. Kevin Borjas, Christian Rodriguez, and Gaston Bolanos are all walking into the APEX knowing another loss makes the roster cut conversation real. Meanwhile, legitimate prospects like Andre Lima and Allan Nascimento are using this card to remind the brass that they still belong, after long stretches on the sideline that have done nothing to cool their momentum.


Main Card Picks

Manel Kape vs. Kyoji Horiguchi

This is a rematch nine years in the making. When these two first met at Rizin in December 2017, Kyoji Horiguchi submitted Manel Kape via arm-triangle choke. Since then, both men have overhauled their careers entirely. Horiguchi went on to collect Rizin and Bellator flyweight championships and is now on a 5-fight win streak since returning to the UFC, including a dominant three-round beating of Amir Albazi in February 2026. Kape, meanwhile, has become one of the most dangerous strikers at 125 pounds, capping his recent run with a vicious KO of Brandon Royval last December that announced him as a genuine title contender.

The tactical puzzle for Horiguchi is the same one it was in 2017, just harder: Kape is a counter puncher who works behind subtle stance switches, carries one-punch knockout power, and has shown the patience to avoid danger before unloading combinations. Horiguchi bounces between ranges and controls distance brilliantly, but he enters this fight with a five-inch reach disadvantage and no real knockout power of his own. His path to victory runs through the mat. The winner walks directly into a flyweight title conversation.

Pick: Manel Kape

Method: KO/TKO

Kape looked incredible taking out Royval. He went out there and put a top-ranked contender's lights out cleanly, and he has the finishing ability to do the same here. Horiguchi has looked good in his UFC comeback, but the matchup quality needs scrutiny: Tagir Ulanbekov is not a title contender, and Amir Albazi has been proved a fraud repeatedly, still hovering near the top of the division largely thanks to a gift decision over Kai Kara France. Horiguchi is the more well-rounded fighter with better grappling, but Kape has done well against grapplers in the UFC, and the days of Horiguchi just dragging someone to the mat and controlling the round are behind him against a fighter of this caliber. Kape keeps his poise, finds his moment, and closes it out on the feet, the same way he did against Royval. It is going to be a long night for the aging Japanese man.

Ion Cutelaba vs. Navajo Stirling

Navajo Stirling is 9-0 and riding the City Kickboxing wave onto his first main card appearance. He called out Ion Cutelaba specifically after his TKO of Bruno Lopes at UFC Seattle, targeting the veteran as the right fight to break into the rankings. It is a confident move for a 9-0 prospect. The question is whether he is ready for what Cutelaba actually brings to a cage fight.

Cutelaba is a light heavyweight journeyman who has been in this division for nearly a decade, and he has the kind of resume that puts hype machines on notice: he has shared the cage with Ankalaev, Johnny Walker, Ryan Spann, and Khalil Rountree. He just pulled off an upset submission win over highly-touted prospect Oumar Sy in March 2026. The Hulk is inconsistent, but he is big, strong, explosive in the first round, and has nothing to lose. Stirling has looked polished and composed in his UFC run, but he has been slow and clunky at times, and has not yet faced anyone who can match Cutelaba's physical volatility. The APEX small cage favors the aggressor, and Cutelaba is always the aggressor.

Pick: Ion Cutelaba

Method: KO/TKO

Stirling is going to get fraud-checked here. In his UFC appearances, he has not looked like an athlete who belongs in the top tier of the division. He is slow, clunky, and his wins have not come against anyone who forces you to take notice. Cutelaba, whatever his inconsistencies, is a legitimate light heavyweight who has gone in with serious fighters and survived. He is also a man who can end a fight in an instant when his power lands. The bet here is that Stirling walks into something early, and Cutelaba ends the Kiwi hype train before it gets any further down the track.

Hyder Amil vs. Christian Rodriguez

Both Hyder Amil and Christian Rodriguez are on two-fight losing streaks, and both are fighting for their UFC lives. Amil, the 36-year-old Filipino-American featherweight, was finished by Jose Delgado and then outpointed by Jamall Emmers in back-to-back outings. He comes in as a +170 underdog and has spoken publicly about this fight being a redemption moment and a first step toward a UFC homecoming in the Philippines. Rodriguez, posted as a -200 favorite despite his own skid, dropped two straight decisions and needs to show he can control a fight and put together clean rounds again.

The key issue with Rodriguez is that his best performances in the UFC came when he was fighting bantamweights after missing weight. Since actually competing at featherweight against natural 145-pounders, he has looked slower and gassed earlier, losing his physical edge against opponents who can match him. Amil is not a top contender, but he has a legitimate win over William Gomis, and he has finishing power. This fight has the energy of two desperate men swinging.

Pick: Hyder Amil

Method: Decision

Rodriguez's UFC success was built on a size advantage he no longer has at featherweight. Against bantamweights, he could bully his way to wins. Against legitimate 145-pounders, he has looked slower, gassed quicker, and has been unable to impose himself. Amil is not going to be bullied, and without that physical edge, Rodriguez is exposed as a fighter who does not have the tools to outpoint you over three rounds. Amil grinds this out and takes the decision, and Rodriguez's roster spot becomes a very real conversation after this one.

Melsik Baghdasaryan vs. Murtazali Magomedov

Murtazali Magomedov is 10-0, and every single one of those wins has come by finish. Not one judge's scorecard has ever been needed. He is a Contender Series product making his official UFC debut as a former Octagon League featherweight champion, with a finishing arsenal that works in both directions: knockouts, triangle chokes, rear-naked chokes, arm-triangles. He is a -315 favorite, and that number is not an accident.

Standing across from him is Melsik Baghdasaryan, who announced himself to UFC audiences with a Performance of the Night knockout of Collin Anglin in his debut. He is a sharp stand-up specialist with genuine finishing power, but his UFC run since that debut tells a familiar story: when opponents take him down and hold him there, as Joshua Culibao did, he loses. When opponents can match his striking, as Jean Silva proved, he gets stopped. His path to winning this fight is standing and trading. The problem is that Magomedov does not need to be one-dimensional to beat him.

Pick: Murtazali Magomedov

Method: KO/TKO

Whenever Baghdasaryan faces anyone genuinely dangerous, he ends up as a highlight reel. It seems destined to happen again here. Magomedov is an undefeated prospect with knockout power in both hands and the submission game to pivot if the striking exchange does not go his way. It is difficult to build a convincing argument for a world in which Baghdasaryan survives to a decision, let alone wins one. Magomedov gets the finish, likely in the first or second round, and announces himself to the UFC roster in exactly the manner the Contender Series intended.

Andre Fili vs. Vinicius Oliveira

Vinicius Oliveira was originally booked against Giga Chikadze, a significantly tougher assignment. When Chikadze withdrew, Andre Fili stepped in on short notice, which tells you everything you need to know about where Fili sits in the featherweight pecking order. He is 13-12 in the UFC, a genuine gatekeeper who has beaten respectable names and lost to anyone with a clear step up in talent. He is coming off a split decision loss to Jose Delgado and is exactly the kind of short-notice opponent the UFC hands to a fighter they want to give a soft landing in a new weight class.

Oliveira is making his featherweight debut after being submitted by Mario Bautista at bantamweight in February. The move up makes sense: the weight cut was draining him, and with more natural size he should be stronger on the feet and have more gas in the tank. He has solid UFC credentials at 135, with wins over Ricky Simon, Said Nurmagomedov, and Kyler Phillips. The real question is whether his power translates at featherweight. Against Fili, it probably does not need to be spectacular to get the job done.

Pick: Vinicius Oliveira

Method: KO/TKO

Oliveira is coming into this matchup with something to prove after the Bautista submission loss, and the move to featherweight should suit him. He will not be drained by the cut, he will carry more natural power, and he will have the skill advantage against a featherweight journeyman in Fili who is essentially a placeholder opponent at this point. Fili tends to reinvent himself as a gatekeeper, winning the fights that do not matter and losing the ones that do. This is the latter category for Oliveira. Expect him to make quick work of this one and set himself up for a legitimate contender in his next featherweight outing.

Prelims Picks

Andre Lima vs. Kevin Borjas

Andre Lima is 11-0 and has been out of action since March 2025, which he has made no secret of being frustrated about. He is a patient, technically developed flyweight with a solid leg kick game, and he already beat Mitch Raposo in a split decision back in June 2024, which is relevant context given that Raposo and Borjas are competing in a similar tier of the flyweight division. Lima is a clean favorite here and returns to the Octagon looking to stay active and push his way up the rankings.

Kevin Borjas is 1-4 in his last five, on a two-fight skid that includes both a stoppage and a decision loss. He has power, eight of his ten wins are by KO, but he has been consistently beaten by opponents who can match him physically or outlast him beyond the first round. His cardio after round one is a recurring problem: he tends to gas and get finished. He is fighting for roster survival here, and the odds are not in his favor. Worth noting: Borjas missed weight going into this matchup, which suggests his preparation has been anything but ideal.

Pick: Andre Lima

Method: Submission

Borjas missed weight, and even if you set that aside, his cardio is the real issue. He consistently looks strong early and then falls apart as the fight goes on, getting finished in the second or third round when his gas tank runs dry. Lima is legitimately good and has been deliberately staying inactive rather than taking fights when he is not right, which suggests he comes in sharp. Borjas may take the first round, but Lima will have his way once the gas goes. He gets the takedown, works to the back, and locks up the submission. This will almost certainly be Borjas's last night on the UFC roster.

Beatriz Mesquita vs. Melissa Mullins

Beatriz Mesquita is a Brazilian BJJ legend making her third UFC appearance and first as a ranked fighter. Her record in MMA reads like a submission highlight tape: five rear-naked chokes, one arm-triangle, a TKO, and a DQ victory. She has been completely dominant in the Octagon, showing total control on the mat in every outing. The real test this camp is whether she can handle the size and physical presence of an opponent who is three inches taller with a four-inch reach advantage.

Melissa Mullins is 5'7" with a 68" reach against Mesquita's 5'4", and her best path to winning this fight is keeping it standing and using every inch of that size to make life difficult from the outside. She is coming off a unanimous decision loss to Darya Zheleznyakova in June 2025, her second loss in four fights, and has also been TKO'd by Nora Cornolle. The physical tools are there, but so is the evidence that she struggles when opponents make her uncomfortable. Mesquita specializes in making people uncomfortable.

Pick: Bia Mesquita

Method: Submission

Mesquita is an absolute force in the women's bantamweight division who has brought genuine violence back to the weight class. She is a grappling phenom who finds full mount with frightening ease and inflicts nasty ground and pound before locking in the finish. Mullins will try to keep it standing and use her reach, but Mesquita is patient and relentless. Once she gets Mullins to the mat and into mount, the ground and pound will come, Mullins will roll to her stomach to escape, and Mesquita will take the back and sink in the rear-naked choke. Submission, round two.

Allan Nascimento vs. Mitch Raposo

Allan Nascimento is on a 4-fight win streak and has been vocal about feeling overlooked in the flyweight rankings despite finishing people and earning performance bonuses. His anaconda choke of Cody Durden last November was the kind of finish that should move the needle. This fight was originally booked for UFC Ottawa but Raposo fell ill and the card got reshuffled, dropping it here. Nascimento is the clear betting favorite and has a five-inch reach advantage to work with.

Mitch Raposo is 1-2 in his last three and enters this matchup as a significant step down in competition for Nascimento. The Lima connection is damning context: Raposo lost to Andre Lima in June 2024, and Lima is a fighter Nascimento has already beaten. Raposo is not a particularly strong grappler, does not carry elite knockout power at the UFC level, and has struggled to define what his actual skill set is at 125 pounds. He bounced back from his skid with a decision over Azat Maksum, but that is a floor, not a ceiling.

Pick: Allan Nascimento

Method: Submission

Mitch Raposo is not UFC caliber, and Nascimento is. That is the fight in one sentence. Nascimento is a large flyweight who will use his reach to control range, pick his shots from distance, and force Raposo to charge forward to do anything meaningful. At some point in the second or third round, Raposo will shoot or fall, and Nascimento will sink in the choke. He has done it repeatedly and there is no reason to think Raposo presents anything different from what he has already handled.

Gaston Bolanos vs. Michael Aswell

This is a prelim fight between two featherweights who are firmly on the UFC bubble, and both of them know it. Gaston Bolanos has been alternating wins and losses with no real momentum, was submitted by Quang Le in May 2025, and is 2-2 in his UFC run. Michael Aswell is 2-3 in his last five with results scattered across stoppages and decisions, coming off a unanimous decision loss to Luke Riley in March 2026. Neither fighter has established themselves as someone the promotion is excited to develop. One of them needs a clean, decisive performance tonight to stay relevant.

Both fighters trend toward decisions, which suggests this goes the distance unless one of them catches the other early. The fighter who executes a cleaner, more consistent game plan over three rounds wins this one.

Pick: Michael Aswell

Method: Decision

Aswell does not have an impressive record, but he is young and improving. His loss to Luke Riley earlier this year is actually more encouraging than anything Bolanos has done recently: the UFC tried to serve Aswell a soft landing against a domestic prospect and he pushed Riley the full distance, making it genuinely competitive. That showing is more impressive than anything on Bolanos's recent ledger. Aswell wins the exchanges, crafts his way through three rounds, and takes a clear 30-27 decision.

Leon Shahbazyan vs. Levan Chokheli

This feels like a matchup assembled from the UFC's free-agent pool to fill out a card that lost a fight or two elsewhere. Both men are making their UFC debuts, both were signed as free agents, and neither has a UFC track record to evaluate. What we do have is their histories elsewhere.

Leon Shahbazyan rebuilt his career from the ground up after struggling at middleweight, and has done it by becoming a submission machine at welterweight. He is riding a 4-fight first-round submission streak, with guillotines as his weapon of choice. Eleven of his twelve wins are by submission. He is a pure grappler making his debut in the Octagon at 170 pounds.

Levan Chokheli is a heavy-handed Georgian striker with 11 knockouts in 14 wins and a Bellator run that went 4-3. He was knocked out by Lorenz Larkin in his most notable Bellator appearance and bounced back with a TKO win over Alexandre Goncalves. He is the striker in a textbook striker-vs-grappler debut matchup, and he has shown in the past that he can be troubled by grapplers. His path to winning is keeping it standing and landing his power before Shahbazyan gets into the clinch.

Pick: Leon Shahbazyan

Method: Submission

Chokheli has struggled with grapplers throughout his career, and was knocked out in his last Bellator fight by a man who is not a UFC-caliber striker, which raises questions about whether he is ready for this level at all. Shahbazyan, meanwhile, has been submitting everyone he touches and has the game plan clarity of a man who knows exactly what he is doing. He will get the clinch, find the guillotine or the back, and get the tap. Another first-round submission to add to the streak.

Early Prelims Picks

Karol Rosa vs. Luana Santos

This is a ranked women's bantamweight fight buried in the early prelims, which is a shame because both fighters have genuine division stakes riding on it. Karol Rosa sits at #8 and has been a division stalwart for eight years, a fighter who consistently beats the mid-tier and loses to the elite. She is coming off a unanimous decision win over Nora Cornolle in August 2025 but was clearly beaten by Ailin Perez in the fight before that, a grappling-dominant performance that exposed her on the mat.

Luana Santos holds the #14 ranking and is on a 2-fight win streak, with five UFC wins in six starts. She submitted Tainara Lisboa in May 2025 and followed with a decision over Melissa Croden in December. She is younger, ascending, and the kind of grappling-heavy fighter that Rosa has shown she struggles against. The winner makes a clear statement in the 135-pound rankings; a win for Santos could jump her several spots toward the top ten.

Pick: Luana Santos

Method: Decision

Rosa's loss to Ailin Perez is the telling data point here. Perez controlled Rosa with grappling for the entirety of that fight, and Santos operates in a similar manner. Santos will get Rosa to the canvas early, control her with top position, and grind through three rounds of grappling-heavy work that the judges will reward. Rosa tends to win the fights where she can set the pace on the feet; Santos is exactly the kind of opponent who will not let her do that. Santos cruises to a 30-27 grappling-heavy decision.

Shane Collins vs. Otari Tanzilovi

With a new season of Contender Series right around the corner, this is the kind of matchup that raises a reasonable question about why it is here rather than there. Shane Collins is 7-0 and has been on a finishing tear in the APEX, with back-to-back KOs in his last two outings. He has the look of a prospect the UFC is deliberately fast-tracking, and he has delivered each time the cage door has closed.

Otari Tanzilovi is a DWCS alum who went 1-1 in his first two UFC fights, losing a split decision to Josias Musasa and bouncing back with a decision over Guillermo Torres. Musasa has since gone on a two-fight losing streak in the UFC, which puts the quality of that loss in context. Tanzilovi has finishing ability, but his UFC appearances have trended toward decisions, which is not the ideal profile when facing a man who has been knocking people out in successive fights.

Pick: Shane Collins

Method: Decision

Collins is the better fighter and Tanzilovi is not UFC caliber. The fight should be entertaining given both men have finishing ability, but the expectation is that Collins outworks him over three rounds and takes the nod. Tanzilovi lost on the Contender Series to a fighter who has since struggled badly in the UFC, which is not a resume that inspires confidence. Collins gets the 29-28 decision and keeps his unbeaten record intact for a more meaningful matchup next time out.
