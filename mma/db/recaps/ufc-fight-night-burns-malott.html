Summary

UFC Winnipeg delivered exactly what the preview promised: a fine local fight show with real consequences in exactly zero of its matchups. The fights were watchable — some were even good — but the name value was not there, the stakes were not there, and most Apex cards held in a half-empty Vegas performance center carry more weight than what the promotion trucked up to Canada for this one. A 6.7 is generous, and it only gets there on the backs of a couple of genuinely entertaining prelim scraps.


The main event gave us what we needed from it: Mike Malott walked down a shot, declining Gilbert Burns, dropped him twice in the third, and finished with ground and pound. Burns retired afterward. Good. It needed to happen, and credit to him for recognizing it. But let's be honest — Malott being fed a fighter who can no longer compete at the top-fifteen level doesn't exactly tell us where Malott fits in the welterweight picture, and the answer is probably somewhere outside the top ten. He's a guy who got the right opponent at the right time.


The judging on this card was a circus. JJ Aldrich won a unanimous decision in a fight where her opponent was the more active fighter for fifteen minutes straight. Melissa Croden took home a decision that the Russian dominated for the better part of two rounds. And the Castañeda vs. Vologdin fight ended in a draw after a point deduction that may have saved Castañeda's roster spot. The judges at UFC Winnipeg were apparently doing something else.


The broader frustration is harder to shake. The UFC is putting on cards like this with increasing frequency — low stakes, recycled names, regional filler dressed up as competitive matchmaking — and then Dana White stands at the podium and tells everyone they don't know what they're talking about. We're getting to a point where it genuinely feels like the organization would rather coast on its brand than invest in actually building something worth watching. It's hard to get to the top. It's harder to stay there.


Final Score: 6.7/10


Main Card

Mike Malott vs. Gilbert Burns

Result: KO/TKO, Right Hook to Ground Punches at 2:08 Round 3 of 5, 12:08 Total

Gilbert Burns came into Winnipeg with five losses in his last six fights and a game that hasn't evolved in years — going forward, loading up on the big right hand, shooting for takedowns he can't finish. None of it worked. Mike Malott sprawled on every single takedown attempt in the first round and tagged Burns clean all night with straight rights and lefts, the crowd getting louder with every exchange.

The finish came in the third. A big uppercut in the second had already hurt Burns badly, and he spent much of the back half of that round covering up and retreating. By round three Malott was hunting him, walked him down, sat him down with a combination, watched Burns stumble back to his feet, then put him down again and finished with ground and pound. Burns retired afterward.

Good. Burns was a legitimate welterweight title contender once, one of the best grapplers in the division, and he deserves respect for that career. But watching him get used as a stepping stone — unable to implement his grappling, getting pieced up on the feet by guys who aren't known as strikers — was not entertainment. As for Malott: he got the right opponent at the right time. Burns hasn't been a top-fifteen fighter for a while now, and Malott winning here doesn't tell us much about where he actually sits in the welterweight division. The answer is probably outside the top ten. He'll find out soon enough.

Charles Jourdain vs. Kyler Phillips

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Kyler Phillips is not an entertaining fighter. That's the takeaway from this one. He controlled large stretches of the fight with his wrestling — taking Charles Jourdain down repeatedly, grinding on top, smothering — but never tried to do anything with the position. No ground and pound, no submission attempts, just dead weight on top. The crowd could tell.

Jourdain, for his part, was a highlight reel without a plan. Flying knees, arm bars from the bottom, creative scrambles — the UFC loved every second of it, even when none of it was actually working. He had his moments on the feet in the later rounds and landed some flashy shots as Phillips started to tire, but it was style over substance for most of the night. The judges gave it to Jourdain unanimously.

Phillips is now on a three-fight losing streak and almost certainly done in the UFC. His non fan-friendly approach to fighting — clinging to opponents, refusing to take risks, running out the clock — is the kind of style that gets you cut even when you win. He didn't win. Jourdain moves to 2-0 at bantamweight, but the question of how good he actually is remains unanswered.

Jai Herbert vs. Mandel Nallo

Result: KO/TKO, Punches at 2:05 Round 1 of 3

What a fight. Both of these guys can barely take a punch, and together they created something genuinely chaotic. Jai Herbert went down twice inside the opening minute — once from a kick, once from a right hand — and looked like a man who had no business surviving the round. Then Mandel Nallo ate a right hand that sent his eyes to the back of his head, somehow scrambled back to his feet, and kept coming. Herbert, to his credit, kept his composure, continued to piece Nallo up, and eventually got the referee stoppage at 2:05 of the first.

The right call was confirmed here: Herbert wins and gets the credit. But let's be honest about what we watched. If Nallo had landed those opening shots on anyone with a functional chin, the fight would have been over in thirty seconds. Herbert got dropped twice and survived on sheer stubbornness. That's admirable, but it's also a preview of problems the next time he faces a real finisher.

Both of these guys have serious issues. Nallo's five-fight finishing streak looked a lot more impressive before we watched him collapse from a counter right hand in his UFC debut. Herbert's UFC losses have always come against quality opponents — and he deserves credit for that resume — but his chin is not a long-term asset. Entertaining fight. Don't read too much into either result.

Jasmine Jasudavicius vs. Karine Silva

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Some real boring shit. Jasmine Jasudavicius controlled Karine Silva on the ground for most of the fight — taking her down, passing to top position, smothering — but never once tried to do anything with it. No meaningful ground and pound. No submission attempts. Just laying on top of Silva for fifteen minutes like she was trying to run out a clock in a sport that doesn't have one. The crowd was not pleased.

Silva wasn't much better. She was most dangerous in round one, throwing up an arm triangle from the bottom and landing ground and pound from her back, but even that was more about disrupting than finishing. By round two she was playing off the back foot, attempting guillotines to avoid takedowns rather than to actually submit anyone, and by the third she was just being smothered under Jasudavicius's weight as elbows trickled down from on top.

Jasudavicius gets the unanimous decision win and technically bounces back from the Fiorot knockout. But this performance did nothing to advance her title case — if anything, it raised questions about whether she has the finishing instinct to compete at the top of the division. Silva, meanwhile, continues to be proof that the hype around her a couple of years ago was ill-advised. She just isn't that good, her cardio fades, and the UFC needs to stop investing in the narrative. Both women need to work on their striking. Silva needs to work even more on her grappling.

Gauge Young vs. Thiago Moisés

Result: Decision, Split at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Thiago Moisés came out aggressive, immediately clinching and grinding Gauge Young against the cage in round one. He got Young's back at one point, worked for a face crank, and controlled much of the first frame. Then the gas ran out. By round two Moisés was visibly fading, and Young began to take over — walking him down, landing cleaner shots, knocking Moisés down and landing ground and pound before letting him back up. By the third it wasn't close: Young was the one throwing, Moisés was the one shooting desperate takedowns and pulling guard just to survive.

Young won a split decision, which is honestly a gift given how the last two rounds played out. The split exists because there's a legitimate argument Moisés took the first round — and if you score it that way, Young needed the third to bail him out. He got it, but not before spending part of it showboating in a fight he may well have been losing on two scorecards. If you're potentially down two rounds heading into the third, you go for the finish. You don't point at your opponent and smile for the crowd.

Young is a more entertaining fighter than Moisés and the better long-term prospect at 25 years old, but he needs to fix his grappling — Moisés is nowhere near the best wrestler in the lightweight division, and he still gave Young real problems for a full round. There are much stronger grapplers ahead of him. Moisés, at 9-8 in the UFC, is running out of time.

Prelims

Márcio Barbosa vs. Dennis Buzukja

Result: KO/TKO, Counter Left Hook at 1:20 Round 1 of 3

One and done. Márcio Barbosa landed a massive left hook flush on the chin of Dennis Buzukja and sent him to the shadow realm at 1:20 of the first round. Absolutely nasty. There was nothing to dissect, no fight to speak of — Barbosa connected, Buzukja went out, it was over before it started.

Barbosa now has five consecutive first-round finishes and looks like exactly the kind of finishing machine the UFC needs more of on cards like this one. He's a legitimate prospect at featherweight and this debut couldn't have gone better. Buzukja, who was 1-4 in his last five UFC fights heading in, is almost certainly done.

Robert Valentin vs. Julien Leblanc

Result: Submission, Rear Naked Choke at 2:22 Round 1 of 3

Total domination from Robert Valentin. Both fighters opened with body kicks, Valentin landed some right hands near the fence, took Julien Leblanc down to the canvas and mounted him. Leblanc scrambled, gave up his back trying to get up, and Valentin locked in the body triangle, landed ground and pound, transitioned to a face crank, and got the tap at 2:22 of the first round.

Tough look for Leblanc, who was signed directly off the Canadian regional circuit without going through DWCS or TUF, and looked exactly like what he was: a fighter who wasn't ready for this level. Valentin, on the other hand, desperately needed this win — he was 0-3 in the UFC and almost certainly getting cut if he didn't perform. He performed. A first-round submission finish in emphatic fashion probably buys him another fight, and maybe another after that if he can string something together.

Gokhan Saricam vs. Tanner Boser

Result: KO/TKO, Straight Right to Ground Punches at 4:43 Round 2 of 3, 9:43 Total

This was a legitimate heavyweight scrap. From the opening bell, Gokhan Saricam and Tanner Boser were swinging with bad intentions, and the crowd was loud for every exchange. Saricam landed on Boser's chin early in the first and had him stumbling, then clinched against the fence to end the round rather than go for a finish — a curious choice given how hurt Boser looked. Both fighters had cardio for days and nukes in their fists, and the first round ended with both men still standing and the arena buzzing.

The finish came in the second. Saricam landed a straight right that put Boser down, Boser briefly turned the tables with some hammerfists from on top in a chaotic scramble, but Saricam recovered, got back to the feet, landed the right hand again, and finished with ground and pound at 4:43 to seal it.

Solid debut from Saricam, who showed the power and toughness to back up his pre-fight billing. Boser, who was previously cut from the UFC and came back on the strength of a Winnipeg crowd, gave a good account of himself — both men were in it until the end. Not a bad fight at all for two debutants. Saricam is a heavyweight to watch.

Melissa Croden vs. Darya Zheleznyakova

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Darya Zheleznyakova controlled the first two rounds. She moved well around the octagon, circled away from Melissa Croden's forward pressure, and did a much better job working at range while Croden plodded forward flat-footed and struggled to cut the cage off. Croden's face was taking damage early, while Zheleznyakova stayed composed and crisp. For two rounds, it wasn't particularly close.

Then the third happened. Zheleznyakova slowed noticeably, Croden started integrating grappling, got her to the cage, and went for a submission — only for Zheleznyakova to grab the fence twice to prevent it, heinously. Whatever momentum she'd built over the first two rounds evaporated in the third as Croden wore her down with pressure and ground and pound to take the frame convincingly.

The judges gave it to Croden unanimously, which is a stretch. Two rounds to one for the Russian is the honest read on this fight, and Croden's path to victory required Zheleznyakova to fade and commit two blatant fence grabs. The result stands, but both women have significant work to do — Zheleznyakova on her grappling, and Croden on her footwork. For the first two rounds she looked like she had no business being in the UFC.

JJ Aldrich vs. Jamey-Lyn Horth

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Call Kash Patel, because something went very wrong at the judges' table. Jamey-Lyn Horth was the more active, more aggressive fighter for the entire fifteen minutes. She went forward, threw the teep, put together combinations, and consistently had answers when JJ Aldrich engaged. Aldrich, meanwhile, did essentially nothing. She stood across the octagon, moved around, and threw when Horth came to her — which wasn't a strategy so much as an absence of one.

The judges scored it unanimously for Aldrich, with every single one of them apparently awarding her the first and third rounds. Those were the more competitive rounds, sure, but the idea that Aldrich did enough to win them — let alone both of them — strains credibility. Horth was the cleaner, busier fighter throughout, and watching the scorecards come in was genuinely baffling.

A really poor start to the picks on the night, and an outcome that's hard to make sense of. Horth deserved better. Aldrich survives to fight another day on the strength of judges who apparently reward breathing in the octagon.

John Castañeda vs. Mark Vologdin

Result: Ends in a Draw, Majority at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

The crowd booed this one before it even got going, which was a fair preview of what followed. Mark Vologdin came in methodical and conservative — not the aggressive scrapper his Contender Series reputation suggested — while John Castañeda spent the first round getting clinched against the cage, reversed, and slowly bloodied up on the feet. Neither man was throwing with any conviction. Vologdin tried a rolling kick late in the first. The crowd was not impressed.

Round two was more of the same until a point deduction changed everything. Castañeda landed a low blow mid-combination — referee Herb Dean took a point away — and it looked for all the world like Vologdin had been fading before the stoppage bailed him out. Castañeda landed a spinning back elbow against the fence in the same round, Vologdin absorbed it and kept moving. The third saw Castañeda come alive: walking Vologdin down, piecing him up, clinching against the fence, denying a desperation takedown attempt. He even did the Max Holloway point-down with 45 seconds left and the two went wild to close it out.

It ended in a majority draw — a result that only happened because of the point deduction. On merit, Castañeda won that fight, and the draw may have inadvertently saved his UFC career. Vologdin is simply too green right now. He has no dominant skill set, doesn't put it together in the octagon, and if he comes back and fights like that again, he's a quick three-and-out.

John Yannis vs. Jamie Siraj

Result: KO/TKO, Elbows From Top Position at 2:43 Round 1 of 3

The card opened with an early TKO finish, but not the one anyone expected. John Yannis stopped Jamie Siraj with elbows from top position at 2:43 of the first round — a result that, in hindsight, should have surprised no one given Yannis's three consecutive knockouts heading into his UFC debut, but did surprise everyone who watched Siraj's submission-heavy career and assumed the grappling would be the story.

It wasn't. Yannis got on top, landed the elbows, and that was that. Siraj, who overcame serious health setbacks to earn this UFC shot and made it a point of personal significance, got stopped in his debut. That stings.

The real takeaway here is the one the notes pointed to before the event: who exactly thought it was a good idea to sign a Canadian on effectively short notice just to put a Canadian on the card? Siraj wasn't ready, the result showed it, and the UFC's geography-driven booking strategy claimed another victim. Yannis bounces back from his debut submission loss with a finish of his own and earns another shot.
