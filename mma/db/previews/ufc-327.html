Overview

UFC 327 lands in Miami with a card built around a vacant light heavyweight title and a handful of fights that have been years in the making. Alex Pereira's decision to vacate the 205-pound belt in pursuit of the interim heavyweight title has opened the door for two contenders who have earned their shot the hard way — and the result is a main event that stands on its own merits, not as a legacy defense.

The biggest storyline outside the title fight is Paulo Costa's light heavyweight debut. "Borrachinha" has spent his career at middleweight but has always had the frame of a natural 205-pounder, and now he finally tests that theory against Azamat Murzakanov, the most terrifying knockout artist at light heavyweight who isn't fighting for the belt. Whether this is a career renaissance or a mismatch that exposes Costa's limitations at a new weight class is the fight within the fight.

Cub Swanson is fighting for the last time. The 42-year-old UFC Hall of Famer has announced this is his retirement bout, and the promotion has given him a Miami main card send-off against Nate Landwehr — a fight that has "absolute bloodbath" written all over it. Meanwhile, the Patricio Pitbull vs. Aaron Pico fight is one of those matchups that MMA fans have wanted for years, finally happening in the UFC, with the added stakes of the loser almost certainly receiving their walking papers.

The prelims are quietly loaded. Mateusz Gamrot and Esteban Ribovics are running it back. Tatiana Suarez is one win from a title shot at strawweight. Kevin Holland's career trajectory is taking him somewhere, but it is increasingly unclear where. And Vicente Luque has made the somewhat desperate jump up to middleweight, arriving on a two-fight losing skid to face Kelvin Gastelum, who stepped in on short notice.


Main Card Picks

Jiří Procházka vs. Carlos Ulberg

The UFC Light Heavyweight Championship is vacant for the first time in years, and two finishers are competing to fill it. Jiří Procházka enters at 32-5-1 and on a two-fight win streak after finishing both Jamahal Hill and Khalil Rountree Jr. — but the 0-2 record against Alex Pereira looms large over his candidacy. He has since rebuilt his title case methodically, but those two losses revealed a ceiling: against technical, precise kickboxers, Procházka's chaos-driven style can be neutralized. Carlos Ulberg is the counter-argument. The New Zealand native is 13-1 and riding nine consecutive wins, including the third-fastest KO in UFC light heavyweight history — a 12-second demolition of Alonzo Menifield — and a first-round stoppage of Dominick Reyes in his last fight. He's never fought beyond three rounds at this level, and this will be his first UFC title fight.

Pick: Carlos Ulberg

Method: Decision

Procházka's career has essentially been defined by two modes: absolute tornado of chaos that somehow ends in a finish, or slow-building brawl where he's losing on the cards until something breaks. Against Rakić, Rountree, and Hill, he was down on the scorecards before pulling out the finish late. The only opponents who have truly beaten him were Pereira — twice — and the common denominator is technical, disciplined kickboxing that doesn't let Procházka's wildness dictate the tempo.

Ulberg is cut from that same cloth, and there's a real argument he might be faster. He doesn't carry the same one-punch power as Pereira, which means the knockout option is less likely — but it also means Ulberg will fight more patiently, picking apart Procházka from range rather than loading up for the kill shot. The real test will be on the ground: once Ulberg starts chewing up Procházka's legs, Jiri will inevitably look for takedowns. If Ulberg has any functional defensive wrestling, this fight goes the distance — and over five rounds of precise kickboxing, the more technical fighter wins.

Azamat Murzakanov vs. Paulo Costa

Azamat Murzakanov is 16-0 and the most dangerous finisher at 205 lbs outside the title picture. He has five KO/TKO wins in six UFC appearances, including back-to-back first-round stoppages of Brendson Ribeiro and Aleksandar Rakić in 2025, and he enters ranked #6 at light heavyweight. Paulo Costa is making his light heavyweight debut, moving up from middleweight at 15-4. "Borrachinha" spent years depleting himself with brutal weight cuts to 185 and has always carried the frame of a natural 205-pounder. His most recent fight was a unanimous decision win over Roman Kopylov in July 2025, and before that, back-to-back decision losses to Whittaker and Strickland. This is uncharted territory for Costa — he has never competed professionally at light heavyweight.

Pick: Paulo Costa

Method: Decision

Murzakanov's win over Rakić was the biggest of his career, but it's worth calibrating how much weight that deserves. Rakić has been in a four-fight tailspin and hasn't looked like a legitimate top-five contender since 2021. Murzakanov has yet to be tested by anyone close to Costa's level, and the honest question about him remains unanswered: is he a complete fighter, or just a very dangerous power puncher?

Costa is a well-rounded fighter with a big frame who should be a natural light heavyweight — and coming up to 205 means he arrives without the damage and depletion of an extreme weight cut. He will be faster on his feet at this weight, and should show the lighter-man skills that get buried when he's fighting drained at 185. The pick is Costa by volume — hammering Murzakanov over three rounds and outworking him to a decision.

Curtis Blaydes vs. Josh Hokit

Curtis Blaydes is 19-5 and one of the best wrestlers at heavyweight — a former top-five contender who was KO'd by Tom Aspinall in one minute at UFC 304 and has been working his way back. He rebounded with a split decision over Rizvan Kuniev in June 2025 and brings an elite grappling base that no one at Hokit's level has ever had to deal with. Josh Hokit is 8-0 with eight finishes and has generated serious buzz as a prospect, but his resume is effectively non-existent in terms of name recognition. His most recent win came when Denzel Freeman retired in round one. Hokit has been impossible to miss in fight week — wielding a lightsaber on media day, creating an assortment of Mexican characters for content, and hitting on Laura Sanko at the weigh-ins — but entertainment value and octagon readiness are two different things.

Pick: Curtis Blaydes

Method: KO/TKO

Hokit's finishing ability is real. The grappling and ground-and-pound are legitimate tools. But he has not been tested by anyone remotely close to Blaydes' caliber, and Blaydes has fought the who's who of the heavyweight division — his only losses have come to elite fighters with elite hands. Hokit doesn't have either.

Blaydes recently shut down Jailton Almeida — a fellow elite grappler — and should be more than capable of neutralizing whatever Hokit brings on the mat. The more interesting question is what happens on the feet. Blaydes has been stopped before, but by killers. Hokit's striking ability remains a genuine unknown at this level. This is a step too far too fast for Hokit, and Blaydes should make that clear inside the distance.

Dominick Reyes vs. Johnny Walker

Dominick Reyes is 15-5 and had put together three straight first-round finishes over Dustin Jacoby, Anthony Smith, and Nikita Krylov before running straight into Carlos Ulberg, who stopped him in round one last September. The former Jon Jones title challenger still has serious power, but he's now been finished in the first round twice in his last four fights. Johnny Walker is 22-9 and back in the win column after a second-round TKO of Mingyang Zhang in August 2025, rebounding from back-to-back stoppages by Oezdemir and Ankalaev. Walker has always been a chaotic, explosive finisher on his best nights — the problem is those nights have been intermittent throughout his UFC career. Both men have a common opponent in Anthony Smith, whom they both beat.

Pick: Dominick Reyes

Method: KO/TKO

There is no way this fight goes the distance. With two of the chinnier knockout artists at light heavyweight going head-to-head, it's more likely the fight doesn't survive the first round than it goes to the scorecards.

Walker's style is essentially controlled mayhem — he wants to wreak havoc, but there's no discernible system behind it. Reyes, for all his recent struggles, actually has boxing. There's real craft in what he does on the feet, and his recent KO of Nikita Krylov is more impressive than anything on Walker's resume. Reyes finds the chin first.

Cub Swanson vs. Nate Landwehr

Cub Swanson is 42 years old, 30-14, and fighting for the last time. A UFC Hall of Famer (Fight Wing) and featherweight legend, he's been involved in some of the most entertaining fights in the division's history. He's coming off a third-round KO of Billy Quarantillo in December 2024 and gets his retirement fight on a Miami main card. Nate Landwehr is 18-7 and on a two-fight losing streak — stopped by Doo Ho Choi and then Morgan Charrière in 2025 — and desperately needs a win to keep his job. At 37 years old and 5-5 in the UFC, he is one of the most reliably entertaining fighters in the promotion, but the roster math is not in his favor. A win over a Hall of Famer on a numbered card would be the biggest result of his career.

Pick: Nate Landwehr

Method: Decision

Two aging veterans going to war is always compelling, but Swanson is 42 years old and coming back from an extended layoff. Landwehr is a legitimate slugger who is going to walk him down and scrap from the first bell, and it's hard to see how that ends well for someone who has been away as long as Swanson has.

There's no shame in the pick — Landwehr is fighting for his livelihood and has every reason to push the pace all night. Swanson deserves a great send-off, but Landwehr is going to make it a brutal one.

Prelims Picks

Patricio Pitbull vs. Aaron Pico

Patricio Pitbull is 37-8 and a former two-division Bellator champion who has yet to crack the upper echelon since joining the UFC. He's 2-2 in his last four, with UFC wins over Jeremy Kennedy and Dan Ige sandwiched around losses to Chihiro Suzuki and Yair Rodriguez. Aaron Pico is 13-5 — once the most hyped prospect in MMA during his Bellator days, a career that never fully lived up to the billing. He was stopped cold by Lerone Murphy's spinning back elbow in August 2025, snapping a three-fight winning streak. This fight was discussed for years when both men were in Bellator, and it's finally happening — with the added context that the loser is almost certainly getting cut from the UFC roster.

Pick: Aaron Pico

Method: Decision

Pitbull is aging and has not impressed in the UFC. Nothing he's done in the promotion suggests he's the kind of fighter who goes out and knocks someone out anymore. He grinds, he grapples, he finds ways to win decisions — but against a younger, hungry Pico, the margins may not be there.

Pico got brutally knocked out by Murphy, but he actually looked fine until the spinning elbow landed. He's also had a similar stoppage loss earlier in his career, which means he knows what rock bottom looks like — and he's not going back. In a fight where both men desperately need the win, Pico's youth and still-unrealized ceiling give him the edge. He takes this decision.

Kevin Holland vs. Randy Brown

Kevin Holland is 28-15 and on a two-fight decision losing skid — dropped back-to-back unanimous decisions to Daniel Rodriguez and Mike Malott in 2025. He's 5-6 in his last 11 fights. Holland is always entertaining and brings legitimate submission grappling, but he has developed a habit of either getting a finish handed to him or fading out over the course of a fight. Randy Brown is 20-7 and coming off a second-round stoppage loss to Gabriel Bonfim in his first-ever UFC main event slot. "Rude Boy" is long, physical, and dangerous at range, but has consistently struggled when the level of competition steps up. This is the first "gimmick" fight Brown has taken — every previous opponent has been someone he viewed as a legitimate step toward the top.

Pick: Randy Brown

Method: Decision

Holland's career trajectory has been a slow-rolling landslide. It's genuinely hard to believe that the same fighter who submitted Anthony Hernandez and knocked out Joaquin Buckley has now lost five of his last eleven. He comes out swinging haymakers and either gets the finish early or runs out of gas and loses a decision. Brown has seen that movie before.

Brown is more strategic, more disciplined, and actively trying to climb the rankings — he doesn't take fights against guys like Holland for fun, he takes them because he thinks he can win. Holland would never take a fight against a Gabriel Bonfim. That gap in competitive ambition matters. Brown outworks him over three rounds.

Mateusz Gamrot vs. Esteban Ribovics

Mateusz Gamrot is 25-4 and one of the better wrestlers in a stacked lightweight division. He was submitted by Charles Oliveira in round two at UFC 326 in October 2025 — a loss that stings, but one that's hard to hold against him given Oliveira's pedigree. Before that, he'd beaten dos Anjos and Fiziev and was firmly in the lightweight title conversation. Esteban Ribovics is 15-2 and has quietly put together a solid UFC résumé — four wins in his last five, with the only blemish a split decision loss to Nasrat Haqparast. His most notable win remains a 37-second head kick KO of Terrance McKinney in 2024. This is reportedly a rematch, and Ribovics will be looking to prove the first result wasn't a fluke.

Pick: Mateusz Gamrot

Method: Decision

Ribovics is a capable fighter, but his résumé has a ceiling problem. He went to the wire with Daniel Zellhuber — one of the bigger fraud checks the UFC has produced in recent years — and that's a tough data point to get past when evaluating how he'll handle an elite wrestler like Gamrot.

Gamrot is the more complete fighter and has a clear path to victory: take Ribovics down early and often, control on the mat, and outpoint him over three rounds. Ribovics hasn't shown the defensive wrestling to stop that, and Gamrot isn't going to give him enough space on the feet to make it interesting. Decision, and it probably isn't that close.

Tatiana Suarez vs. Lupita Godinez

Tatiana Suarez is 11-1 and ranked #2 at strawweight. She suffered the first loss of her career to Weili Zhang at UFC 312 in February 2025, but bounced back with a decision over Amanda Lemos and is now one win away from a likely title shot against champion Mackenzie Dern. Suarez is primarily a dominant wrestler and guillotine specialist — when she gets her hands on opponents, fights tend to end badly for them. Lupita Godinez is 14-5 and ranked #6 at strawweight, riding a two-fight winning streak that includes the biggest win of her career — a unanimous decision over former UFC champion Jéssica Andrade in August 2025. She has publicly stated that a win here should earn her a title shot or eliminator. Her one notable blemish is a loss to Mackenzie Dern — the current champion — which raises an interesting question about how Godinez would fare against Suarez's offensive grappling.

Pick: Tatiana Suarez

Method: Decision

Suarez has been running through the strawweight division for years. The only fighter who has beaten her is Weili Zhang — a former champion with world-class technical striking. Godinez lost to Mackenzie Dern, who is a jiu-jitsu specialist, which suggests her grappling defense is a vulnerability. That is exactly the wrong weakness to have against Suarez.

Godinez's wins over Andrade and Polastri are real, and she's peaking. But Suarez at her best is an elite grappler who can drag anyone into deep water within the first minute, and the gap in wrestling credentials between these two is significant. Expect Suarez to grind on her for 15 minutes and win a comfortable decision.

MarQuel Mederos vs. Chris Padilla

MarQuel Mederos is 11-1 and on a nine-fight winning streak, earning his UFC deal via the Contender Series with a knee KO of Issa Isakov. He's gone 2-0 in the UFC with wins over Austin Hubbard and Mark Choinski — both decisions. Chris Padilla is 17-6 and has been one of the more quietly impressive fighters on the UFC roster, going 5-0 in the promotion with finishes via multiple methods. His most recent win was a ground-and-pound stoppage of Ismael Bonfim in November 2025. He stepped in late to replace the injured Nazim Sadykhov, turning this into a prospect-vs-prospect clash — though it's worth noting Padilla missed weight today, which raises questions about his camp preparation.

Pick: Chris Padilla

Method: Decision

Mederos is unbeaten since joining the UFC, but his wins have come over unremarkable competition and several of them have been closer than they should have been. His résumé doesn't inspire a lot of confidence heading into a fight with Padilla, who has already proven himself against better names.

Padilla missing weight is a legitimate concern — it suggests either a rough cut or camp issues — but the skill gap still favors him. He has better boxing, more finishing diversity, and has been tested against stiffer competition. If he's close to full strength, he pieces Mederos up on the feet and cruises to a decision, or finds a finish somewhere in the middle rounds.

Kelvin Gastelum vs. Vicente Luque

Kelvin Gastelum is 20-10 and stepped in on short notice to replace the injured Kyle Daukaus. He is 2-2 in his last four — wins over Daniel Rodriguez and Dustin Stoltzfus, losses to Sean Brady and Joe Pyfer — and has held his own against legitimate middleweight competition far more recently than his opponent. His interim title fight against Israel Adesanya was inducted into the UFC Hall of Fame's Fight Wing in 2025. Vicente Luque is 23-12-1 and has made the jump up to middleweight for his debut at 185 after spending his career at welterweight. He arrives on a two-fight losing streak — submitted by Kevin Holland and then methodically beaten up by Joel Alvarez, who absolutely smashed Luque in a fight the referee was slow to stop. Luque's anaconda choke remains one of the more dangerous weapons in the sport, but the overall trajectory of his career has been grim.

Pick: Kelvin Gastelum

Method: KO/TKO

Luque simply shouldn't be in the UFC anymore. He has aged poorly in the sport, has taken significant damage in recent fights, and the move up to middleweight reeks of desperation rather than a calculated career pivot. The Alvarez fight should have been stopped earlier than it was — the fact that it went to the scorecards says more about the referee than Luque's durability.

Gastelum is not a world-beater in 2026, but he has recently shared the cage with Sean Brady and Joe Pyfer and been competitive. That's a higher bar than anything Luque has cleared lately. Gastelum clips him in the second round and finishes with ground and pound.

Charles Radtke vs. Francisco Prado

Charles Radtke stepped in to replace Christopher Alvidrez and enters on a one-fight win streak — a rear naked choke finish of Daniel Frunza in November 2025. He is 2-3 in the UFC with a mixed record against varied competition, but has the momentum advantage heading into this one. Francisco Prado is 12-4 and on a three-fight losing skid — consecutive decision losses to Nikolay Veretennikov, Jake Matthews, and Daniel Zellhuber. The Argentine has also moved up from lightweight to welterweight, adding an additional adjustment variable. He was originally paired against a UFC newcomer in Alvidrez; the switch to Radtke is not a favorable one.

Pick: Charles Radtke

Method: Decision

Prado has lost four of his five UFC fights and is dropping decisions to mid-level competition. His three-fight losing skid includes a loss to Zellhuber — a name that does not carry weight in any serious scouting report. He also shouldn't be at welterweight. The whole picture is bad.

Radtke is not an elite welterweight either, but he has more momentum, more recent success, and a cleaner path through this fight. He's the more skilled fighter on paper and should outpoint Prado over three rounds without much drama.
