Overview

UFC Fight Night: Allen vs. Costa lands at the APEX on a Saturday, and on paper it reads like a mid-tier card with a genuinely interesting main event propped up by a bunch of roster-spot fights and debut matchups. This is not a must-watch card, but the main event alone justifies tuning in, and there are a couple of prelim fights worth keeping an eye on.

The headliner puts Arnold Allen against Melquizael Costa in a five-round featherweight bout that is essentially a referendum on whether Costa is a legitimate top-10 threat or just a finishing machine running through faded competition. Allen has gone 1-3 in his last four, losing to Max Holloway, Movsar Evloev, and Jean Silva, but those are three of the best fighters on the planet and all three went the distance. Costa, meanwhile, has six straight wins with highlight-reel finishes including a spinning back kick KO over Dan Ige in February. He has not been tested in a five-round championship fight, and tonight is when we find out what he actually is.

The co-main is Doo Ho Choi vs. Daniel Santos, which is one of those fights where the narrative writes itself: a 35-year-old legend on a two-fight win streak getting run over by a younger, hungrier fighter on a mission. Santos has made it his brand to target Korean featherweights and he comes in as the favorite for good reason.

The rest of the card is loaded with guys fighting for their jobs. Andre Petroski vs. Cody Brundage at middleweight is a pure survival fight: Brundage has lost four in a row and is almost certainly done if he drops this one. Daniel Barez and Luis Gurule are in the same position at flyweight. Ketlen Vieira vs. Jacqueline Cavalcanti is the most meaningful prelim fight with title implications on the line for Cavalcanti, who enters as the favorite on a five-fight UFC win streak.

Two lightweight newcomers are making their UFC debuts against each other: Tommy Gantt (11-0, trained by Daniel Cormier) takes on Artur Minev (7-0) who steps in on three days' notice. That is a genuinely fun matchup on paper. Christian Edwards also debuts on short notice against the established Modestas Bukauskas, which is less fun.


Main Card Picks

Arnold Allen vs. Melquizael Costa

Arnold Allen (20-4) is at a crossroads. The #7 ranked Englishman has gone 1-3 in his last four fights with losses to Max Holloway, Movsar Evloev, and Jean Silva, but context matters here. All three losses went the distance, and there is a reasonable argument he was robbed against Evloev. These are three of the best featherweights on the planet. Allen is 32 and technically sound: patient, disciplined, and difficult to finish.

Melquizael Costa (26-7) is the #12 ranked Brazilian riding a six-fight win streak into his first main event. His spinning back kick KO over Dan Ige earned a Performance of the Night bonus in February and he has been finishing fighters left and right. He posts 4.38 significant strikes per minute at 59.40% accuracy, both figures outpacing Allen, and he has vowed a knockout win. The question no one has answered yet: what does Costa look like when the fight goes into the championship rounds? He has never been tested over five rounds in the UFC.

Pick: Arnold Allen

Method: Decision

Costa is on a serious streak but has been finishing low-ranked fighters who are past their primes. Allen has gone the distance with the best in the division and knows how to survive early danger. Costa's only path is a nasty early shot, but Allen will be prepared for it. As the fight moves into the championship rounds, Costa's unproven gas tank and inexperience at this level becomes the deciding factor: Allen takes over and wins a masterclass decision.

Doo Ho Choi vs. Daniel Santos

Doo Ho Choi (16-4-1) is 35 years old and a UFC Hall of Fame Fight Wing inductee for his legendary war with Cub Swanson at UFC 206. He's on a two-fight win streak with finishes of Bill Algeo and Nate Landwehr, but he has not exactly been active, and the competition level of those wins does not prepare you for what Santos brings.

Daniel Santos (13-2) is 31 years old, in the prime of his career, and has built a specific reputation: he hunts Korean featherweights. Back-to-back wins over Jeong Yeong Lee and Joo Sang Yoo — the latter stopped in 21 seconds of round two — put him on a four-fight win streak and into this co-main slot. He enters as the -175 favorite and has declared he came to dominate this division.

Pick: Daniel Santos

Method: KO/TKO

Choi simply has not been active enough and is now 35 years old trying to fight at featherweight. Santos is a decade younger and actively targeting Korean fighters for a reason. People forget that being Southeast Asian is one of the worst stylistic bases for MMA, and Choi is not the exception. Santos is going to be too fast, too hungry, and too fresh: this ends by knockout, likely in the second round.

Malcolm Wellmaker vs. Juan Díaz

Malcolm Wellmaker (10-1) had rattled off four straight wins, including back-to-back first-round KOs of Cameron Saaiman and Kris Moutinho, before suffering his first career loss — a unanimous decision to Ethyn Ewing at UFC 322 in November. The loss stings but the context matters: Ewing is a savage who was massively underestimated in that short-notice matchup. Wellmaker is a proven UFC-level finisher with elite one-punch knockout power at 135.

Juan Díaz (15-1-1) makes his UFC debut off the back of the most spectacular finish in Contender Series history, landing the first-ever spinning back elbow KO to stop Won Il Kwon in October. The 27-year-old Peruvian has a flair for the highlight reel, but he is walking into a meaningful step up in competition and experience. There is simply not enough tape on him at this level to know how he handles adversity.

Pick: Malcolm Wellmaker

Method: KO/TKO

The Ewing loss may have been the best thing that ever happened to Wellmaker. It was a jump in competition he was not fully prepared for, but a fighter of his talent learns from that. He comes back here against someone without a single UFC fight to his name and with every motivation to make a statement. Wellmaker's natural power and experience advantage carry him to a finish.

Modestas Bukauskas vs. Christian Edwards

Modestas Bukauskas (19-7) had put together four straight wins and a 6-1 run since returning to the UFC in 2023 before running into Nikita Krylov at UFC 324 in January, where he was stopped by TKO in round three. He stands 6'3" with a 76-inch reach and has fought genuine light heavyweight contenders. He is the clear establishment fighter in this matchup and is looking to get back on track toward the top 15.

Christian Edwards (8-4) makes his UFC debut on three days' notice after Rodolfo Bellato withdrew. The 27-year-old Oklahoman stands 6'5" with a 78.5-inch reach and has finished six of his eight wins, including four in the first round. He has gotten hurt by some questionable competition in Bellator. Three days' notice for a UFC debut against a veteran is an enormous ask.

Pick: Modestas Bukauskas

Method: KO/TKO

Another "what the hell is this matchup" fight. Bukauskas is coming off a TKO loss to Krylov but has competed against some of the best in the LHW division and held his own. Edwards is a raw debutant who has been finished by scrubs in Bellator and is stepping in on essentially no preparation. Bukauskas gets this done.

Timmy Cuamba vs. Benardo Sopaj

Timmy Cuamba (10-3) is on a two-fight win streak that includes a second-round TKO of Roberto Romero and a comeback unanimous decision over Chang Ho Lee: a fight where he survived a brutal first round before taking over with a spinning elbow and relentless pressure. He has shown both finishing ability and mental toughness. His debut loss to Bolaji Oki by decision is concerning, though: that is not a name you should be losing to on the scorecards.

Benardo Sopaj (12-3) bounced back from a brutal flying knee KO loss to Vinicius Oliveira with a unanimous decision win over Ricky Turcios at UFC 311 in January 2025. He has one-punch KO power: he ended Julien Lopez in 20 seconds. But the Oliveira loss raised real questions about his chin. He is the less-proven fighter with the more dramatic resume.

Pick: Benardo Sopaj

Method: Decision

This is a genuine crapshoot. Neither fighter has done anything that stands out as particularly impressive. Cuamba's wins are against unexceptional opposition and that Oki loss is hard to shake. Sopaj, despite being more green in the UFC, is younger and has fought Vinicius Oliveira: a level Cuamba has not approached. Getting knocked out by Oliveira is humbling but educational, and Sopaj has improved through the experience. He squeaks out a decision.

Prelims Picks

Nikolay Veretennikov vs. Khaos Williams

Nikolay Veretennikov (14-7) is 2-3 inside the UFC and 36 years old. He picked up a standing TKO over Niko Price in February 2026, which is nice on paper but Niko Price is a name that nearly everyone on the UFC roster can crack. He averages just 2.69 significant strikes per minute, which tells you everything about his output: dangerous in spurts, invisible the rest of the time.

Khaos Williams (15-5) has a 6-4 UFC record and elite knockout power, with multiple sub-90-second UFC finishes to his name. He has fought and beaten real fighters: Randy Brown, Abdul Razak Alhassan, Michel Pereira. He comes in on a two-fight skid with losses to Gabriel Bonfim and Andreas Gustafsson, and has not competed since June 2025. He has been hesitant and clinch-prone of late, which is a problem.

Pick: Khaos Williams

Method: KO/TKO

What has Veretennikov actually done in the UFC? One TKO over Niko Price. Williams has faced legitimately better competition across his entire UFC career and brings significantly more dangerous hands. If Williams comes out and actually throws, Veretennikov has no answer. Williams clobbers him in the first round.

Tuco Tokkos vs. Ivan Erslan

Tuco Tokkos (11-5) bounced back from two straight losses with a clinical arm triangle submission over Junior Tafa in July 2025, dominating with nearly eight minutes of control time before finishing in round two. Tafa has actually looked decent since moving to light heavyweight, so that win means something. Tokkos is 1-2 inside the UFC overall but showed he has the tools to finish fights at this level.

Ivan Erslan (14-6) has gone 0-3 inside the UFC including a first-round rear naked choke loss to Jimmy Crute in September 2025. He has 10 career KO wins and raw power, but he has not been able to get anything going at the UFC level. Three losses in a row, with a submission finish on his record: his ground-game vulnerability is well-documented.

Pick: Tuco Tokkos

Method: Decision

Erslan has had a brutal run in the promotion and has not shown he belongs at this level. Tokkos is the better technical grappler and his arm triangle win over Tafa proved he can grind and finish. He outclasses Erslan over three rounds.

Tommy Gantt vs. Artur Minev

Tommy Gantt (11-0) is a DWCS Class of 2025 graduate making his UFC debut with 10 finishes from 11 fights. He trains under Daniel Cormier at The Academy Fight Club in Gilroy, California, which is about as good a coaching pedigree as you can have. He was originally scheduled against Trey Ogden before Ogden withdrew injured, and Gantt learned he would be facing Minev with only days to adjust.

Artur Minev (7-0) is a 22-year-old Ukrainian based in Las Vegas making his own UFC debut on three days' notice. He has finished all but one of his seven fights, five of them inside the first round. Despite the extremely short notice, he has been active and battle-tested on the regional scene throughout 2024 and 2025.

Pick: Tommy Gantt

Method: Submission

Two undefeated prospects making their UFC debuts against each other is a fun concept. Gantt's wrestling foundation from Cormier's camp is going to be the decisive factor here. He gets Minev down early and works for the submission. Even if the finish does not come immediately, he controls Minev for three rounds. Minev's power is real but Gantt is too well-rounded to get caught.

Ketlen Vieira vs. Jacqueline Cavalcanti

Ketlen Vieira (15-5) is a 34-year-old veteran who has tested herself against the best in women's bantamweight, taking on Raquel Pennington, Kayla Harrison, and Norma Dumont. She came up short in those matchups but kept them close, and there is a reasonable argument she beat Dumont in her most recent fight in November 2025. She enters as the +145 underdog despite her experience advantage, posting 2.76 significant strikes per minute to Cavalcanti's 5.65.

Jacqueline Cavalcanti (10-1) is on an eight-fight win streak and is 5-0 inside the UFC. At just 28 years old, she capped 2025 with a unanimous decision over Mayra Bueno Silva and enters as the -170 favorite with title contention on the line. Her volume is striking: 5.65 significant strikes per minute at 46% accuracy is genuinely elite output at this weight class.

Pick: Ketlen Vieira

Method: Decision

Mayra Bueno Silva has had an embarrassing fall from grace, and a win over her at this point means almost nothing. That is Cavalcanti's signature win. Vieira, by contrast, has been in with Pennington, Harrison, and Dumont, going the distance with all three and arguably beating Dumont. Cavalcanti has simply not fought anyone of this caliber. Vieira is too big, too smothering, and too experienced. She outgrapples Cavalcanti for three rounds.

Andre Petroski vs. Cody Brundage

Andre Petroski (13-5) is a switch-stance wrestler out of Renzo Gracie Philly who controls fights by taking the back and grinding. He is 2-3 in his last five with back-to-back losses, including a brutal first-round KO against Cam Rowston in Perth in September 2025. His roster spot is in genuine jeopardy.

Cody Brundage (11-9-1) has lost four straight and is effectively fighting for his UFC life. The split decision loss to Donte Johnson at UFC 326 in March 2026 is his most recent outing, and it followed a back-mount TKO loss to Cam Rowston in January. His back-control defense has been a consistent weakness across his career. At 11-9-1, there is no real argument for keeping him around after another loss.

Pick: Andre Petroski

Method: Decision

This is a hate-watch for the ages. Brundage should not still be in the UFC at this point and has no good wins to show for his run in the promotion. Petroski is not exactly lighting the world on fire either, but he is the craftier fighter and has the grappling tools to control this fight. He out-crafties Brundage and gets the decision.

Alice Ardelean vs. Polyana Viana

Alice Ardelean (11-7) is on a two-fight win streak coming off back-to-back unanimous decisions in 2025 and enters as the -200 favorite. She is by far the higher-volume striker in this matchup, landing 7.35 significant strikes per minute at 45% accuracy. She has been on the UFC bubble for a while but consecutive wins have given her some stability.

Polyana Viana (13-8) has 21 professional fights on her resume and very little to show for it. She is on a three-fight losing streak with all three losses coming by submission in round two: rear naked choke, ground strikes from back mount, arm triangle. That is a pattern, not a coincidence. Despite her jiu-jitsu credentials and 12 career first-round finishes, she is being consistently controlled and finished in her UFC outings.

Pick: Alice Ardelean

Method: Decision

Viana has 21 fights and nothing convincing to show for them. Ardelean is on a hotter streak, hits harder on the feet, and has better grappling than Viana at this stage of both their careers. She incorporates her grappling into a well-rounded approach and outclasses Viana over three rounds.

Daniel Barez vs. Luis Gurule

Daniel Barez (17-7) is a 37-year-old Spaniard who is 1-2 in the UFC. His only promotional win came against Victor Altamirano at UFC Paris in 2024; he then dropped a third-round submission loss to Andre Lima in March 2025. He fights with sharp combination boxing that mixes head and body well, complemented by calf kicks. He enters at essentially even odds against a fighter on a three-fight losing streak.

Luis Gurule (10-3) is 0-3 inside the UFC and fighting to keep his job. Three straight losses by KO and decision suggest he simply has not been able to impose himself at this level. He works from a modified cross guard with clean counters and leg kicks, and is five years younger than Barez at 32. He enters as the -132 favorite despite the losing streak.

Pick: Luis Gurule

Method: Decision

Both of these guys probably should be cut, but someone has to win. The competition Gurule has faced in his three UFC losses: Ode Osbourne, Jesus Aguilar, Alden Coria: is hard going. Barez is 37 and running out of time. Gurule is younger, has been more active, and his development curve over his UFC career gives him the edge here. He squeaks out a decision.

Shauna Bannon vs. Nicolle Caliari

Shauna Bannon (7-2) is 2-2 inside the UFC with a Performance of the Night armbar finish over Puja Tomar in March 2025 before dropping a rear naked choke loss to Sam Hughes in September 2025. The 32-year-old Irish fighter posts 4.3 significant strikes per minute and holds a two-inch reach advantage. She has a win over Alice Ardelean — who is fighting on this same card — which is relevant context for evaluating her level.

Nicolle Caliari (8-4) is 0-2 inside the UFC and fighting to stay employed. A split decision loss in her debut was followed by a knee-to-the-liver TKO loss to Carli Judice in July 2025. She has strong submission credentials with multiple armbar and rear naked choke finishes on her career record, but her UFC performances have been underwhelming.

Pick: Shauna Bannon

Method: Decision

Caliari is not UFC caliber. Bannon has shown real promise with wins over Puja Tomar and Alice Ardelean and brings a clear striking advantage in volume and reach. She keeps this on the feet and wins the distance.
