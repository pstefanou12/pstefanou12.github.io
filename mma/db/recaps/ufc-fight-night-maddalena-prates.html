Summary

Perth delivered. UFC Fight Night Perth was one of those cards where the UFC's homecoming formula actually worked — not because of favorable matchmaking (though there was plenty of that), but because the main event delivered in a way nobody was fully prepared for.


Carlos Prates put on an absolute masterclass against Jack Della Maddalena, ending the fight by elbows from top position in round three after spending the better part of two-and-a-half rounds dismantling the hometown hero methodically and mercilessly. Flow-state Prates is one of the best fighters on the planet, full stop. The only blemish on the night was Mike Beltran, who let the stoppage drag on way too long while JDM was getting pieced up in the finish sequence. The path to a title shot against Islam Makhachev is clear, and Prates vs. Islam would be a genuine banger — the question is whether Islam will ever actually sit down at the welterweight table long enough to fight one of these guys.


The co-main had a brief but compelling twist. Beneil Dariush wobbled Quillan Salkilld early — a head jab had him bouncing off the canvas — before Salkilld regrouped, found his range, and finished the veteran with right hooks to ground punches in the first. Heartbreaking for Dariush, who at this point needs to seriously consider retirement. His chin is gone, his career is gone. Salkilld, on the other hand, is for real. The callout of Gamrot makes sense, and if Gamrot can't get a higher-profile fight, that's the matchup.


The best fight on the card happened on the main card undercard: Brando Peričić and Shamil Gaziev spent two rounds trading fire in a fight that deserved far more than a standard performance bonus. Those two earned ten bonuses worth of entertainment. Peričić knocked Gaziev out with a right hook at 3:44 of round two in what was, clinch-separation issues aside, a genuinely nasty heavyweight battle.


The low point was Louie Sutherland winning a decision over Tai Tuivasa in a fight that should never have been made. Sutherland lay on Tuivasa for fifteen minutes, getting warned by the ref repeatedly to work. Tuivasa is done — cut him from the sport. The UFC should have held him for a rematch with Derrick Lewis on the White House card, someone who would have stood and traded. Instead we got Sutherland calling out Tybura, which says everything you need to know about his ambitions.


The prelims were a mixed bag, as expected for this type of regional card. Marwan Rahiki is must-see TV — 9-0, 100% finish rate, and he dropped Ollie Schmid inside three minutes. Cam Rowston was solid against Bryczek. The Malkoun-Meerschaert situation was exactly what it looked like on paper. And the scorecard in Thicknesse-Morales defies basic comprehension — every judge gave Thicknesse round two, which is frankly insane.


Final Score: 7.3/10



Main Card

Carlos Prates vs. Jack Della Maddalena

Result: KO/TKO, Elbows From Top Position at 3:17 Round 3 of 5, 13:17 Total

Carlos Prates came into Perth as the challenger, but he fought like a man who had already decided how this was going to end. Through the first round, he circled the outside of the octagon with that languid, effortless looseness — tapping up Jack Della Maddalena with jabs, landing body kicks and knees up the middle, doing more than JDM without even seeming to try. JDM controlled the center and kept walking forward, but he wasn't throwing anything meaningful. The only real takeaway from round one was JDM shooting a takedown and actually getting it — Prates wasn't taking it seriously and got caught. A minor blip.


Round two is when Prates started entering flow state. He came out more aggressive, landed a body kick, slipped a JDM takedown attempt, and then started teeing off on the feet. JDM managed to get to Prates's back and try a rear naked choke, but Prates shook him off like it was an inconvenience. From there, it was just Prates walking JDM down and picking him apart. JDM was getting pieced up against the fence, absorbing shot after shot, and yet somehow staying on his feet. It was remarkable heart from JDM. It was not enough. A calf kick dropped JDM toward the end of the round, and he looked completely defeated.


By the third, it was pure predator-prey. Prates opened with a body kick, found his shots easily, and just kept hunting. A head kick dropped JDM. Prates let him back up. He knocked JDM down again. Prates let him back up. A calf kick sent JDM back to the canvas, and this time he didn't get up — Prates swarmed from top position, landing elbows until Mike Beltran finally stepped in, though not before letting the finish drag on about fifteen seconds too long. Nasty stoppage. Nasty performance.


Flow-state Prates is one of the best fighters on the planet, full stop. This was an absolute masterclass. The only reason the title shot conversation isn't completely settled is the loss to Ian Garry last year, which is valid — but Prates vs. Islam Makhachev would be a genuine banger, and the path is clear. On the feet, Prates beats anyone in the UFC. The jury is still out on whether his grappling is good enough to survive Islam, but that's a problem for another day. As for JDM — his future is murky. Depending on the next matchup, he could spiral. A rematch with someone like Mike Malott seems likely, and JDM wins that one going away, but right now he needs to rebuild.

Quillan Salkilld vs. Beneil Dariush

Result: KO/TKO, Right Hook to Ground Punches at 3:29 Round 1 of 3

Beneil Dariush looked like the veteran in control early — a head jab wobbled Quillan Salkilld within the first minute and sent him bouncing off the canvas. Salkilld grabbed a quick takedown to buy time, and Dariush defended it, the two of them grinding along the fence for a stretch. It looked like Dariush was getting the foothold he needed to turn this into a fight. Then Salkilld separated, found his range, and it all came apart for the veteran.


Once Salkilld started teeing off, Dariush had no answers. A desperation takedown attempt didn't get there, and Salkilld kept landing to the side of the head until the referee stepped in at 3:29 of the first. Heartbreaking for Dariush, who was on the brim of a title shot not long ago. His chin is gone, his career is gone. At this point he should seriously consider retirement and go back to competing in grappling or jiu jitsu — because the MMA version of Beneil Dariush is not what it once was.


Salkilld, on the other hand, is for real. He got hurt early, kept his composure, found his range, and finished. That's a real fighter's response to adversity. The callout of Gamrot is the right move — Gamrot may deserve someone higher up the rankings, but if he can't get that fight, this matchup makes complete sense as the next step.

Steve Erceg vs. Tim Elliott

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Tim Elliott came in doing Tim Elliott things from the opening bell — stomping the leg, keeping an odd distance, doing random stuff that was more chaotic than effective. He managed to clip Steve Erceg early in the first and had him bouncing off the canvas, and mixed in a couple of late takedowns to take the round. Erceg, for his part, was extremely technical and orthodox, picking his spots and refusing to get drawn into Elliott's weird rhythm.


Through the second, Elliott kept doing random things — some of which weren't even landing — while Erceg steadily started touching him up. Erceg took Elliott's back at one point, tried for a rear naked choke, and abandoned it. Elliott kept grinding out takedowns to stay competitive, but Erceg was clearly starting to impose his will.


By the third, it wasn't close. Erceg was in flow state — finding range, landing a head kick, and just systematically picking Elliott apart on the feet. Elliott's face was getting messed up, his takedown attempts weren't getting there, and he was essentially just jumping around the octagon in survival mode. Erceg won the last round convincingly and took the unanimous decision.


It's a solid win for Erceg, who is now on a two-fight win streak and needs a top-10 opponent next — something like Amir Albazi would make sense before anyone starts talking title eliminators again. There were some matchmaking and management questions after his loss to KKF led to a Brandon Moreno fight instead of a step down to rebuild; regardless, Erceg is back on track. As for Elliott — he's 39, and while he's not completely washed, the window for a serious run at the top five is basically closed.

Marwan Rahiki vs. Ollie Schmid

Result: KO/TKO, Left Hook to Ground Punches at 2:47 Round 1 of 3

Marwan Rahiki was the most aggressive man in the building from the moment the cage door closed. While Ollie Schmid — on short notice for his UFC debut — was cautious and timid, Rahiki was already moving dynamically, throwing spinning kicks and showcasing the kind of footwork you don't see at this level from a 9-0 prospect. It took him less than three minutes to end it. He dropped Schmid with a left hand and finished on the ground, barely breaking a sweat.


Rahiki is must-see TV. Nine and oh with a hundred percent finish rate — he is going to be a genuine problem in the featherweight division. Props to Schmid for taking the fight on short notice and making his UFC debut on short notice; give him another fight. But the story here is Rahiki. One more opponent outside the rankings, and then it's time to start feeding him real problems.

Brando Peričić vs. Shamil Gaziev

Result: KO/TKO, Right Hook at 3:44 Round 2 of 3, 8:44 Total

This was the best fight on the card, and it wasn't particularly close. Shamil Gaziev came in as the aggressor in the first, putting together big combinations and mixing in a clinch against the fence, but Brando Peričić was no victim. Peričić opened with a head kick, weathered the early pressure, and then found his right hand — a shot that jerked Gaziev's head back and sent the energy in the building somewhere else entirely. From there, both men were just swapping bombs across the octagon. Gaziev had Peričić hurt at points, clinching to recover. Peričić came roaring back. The ref was separating them from the clinch repeatedly, which slowed the rhythm, but every time they were back in the open it was a firefight.


Round two started with Peričić being more methodical, jabbing off the back foot, working his way in. He opened a cut on Peričić's forehead. Gaziev, to his credit, was a zombie — eating shots and returning fire every time. But Peričić was the one landing the cleaner, harder shots, and when he let a bomb go and followed up across the entire octagon, Gaziev was done. He went to the canvas and didn't beat the count.


Fights like this deserve ten bonuses, not one. Chimaev got a performance bonus for beating DDP — if you're giving those out for that level of performance, this one should have come with a $1 million check. If there aren't performances on a card that genuinely deserve bonuses, don't give them out; save the money for nights like this one. Peričić wants to fight in Serbia, and that should happen. As for Gaziev — he lost, but his stock doesn't drop. If he keeps showing up to scrap like that, people will keep tuning in.

Louie Sutherland vs. Tai Tuivasa

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Louie Sutherland took this fight on short notice, and that's about the most charitable thing you can say about his performance. For three rounds, he shot for takedowns, got Tai Tuivasa to the canvas, and then laid there. Literally. The referee warned him to work repeatedly in rounds two and three. In the third, Sutherland was doing so little that the ref eventually stood them up — at which point Sutherland immediately grabbed Tuivasa, hugged him back down to the ground, and resumed doing nothing. A point was taken from Tuivasa's corner at some point in the chaos. It was an exhausting fifteen minutes to watch.


The question isn't whether Sutherland deserved the win — he controlled the fight, even if he did nothing with that control. The question is why this fight was made at all. Tai Tuivasa is on a massive losing skid and is one fight away from being cut. The UFC's answer was to book him against a newly signed heavyweight who just goes out and lies on him for three rounds. It's lose-lose: Tai collects another loss, and nobody comes away liking Sutherland. The right call would have been holding Tuivasa for a rematch with Derrick Lewis on the White House card — someone who would have exclusively stood and traded. Instead we got Sutherland calling out Tybura after the fight, which tells you everything about the level he's comfortable operating at.


Tuivasa doesn't have it anymore. Nobody wants to watch this version of him. Cut him from the sport. RIP Tai.

Prelims

Cam Rowston vs. Robert Bryczek

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Robert Bryczek came out as the aggressor early, walking forward and putting Cam Rowston on the back foot with some solid left hands to the body. But Rowston used his reach effectively, started landing with jabs and 1-2s to keep Bryczek at distance, and eventually secured a single leg that put the fight on the canvas. From there, Rowston was in full mount landing ground and pound — and that pattern basically carried all three rounds. Rowston kept dragging the fight to the floor, grinding in top position, and consistently threatening a finish with nasty shots from on top.


Rowston looks solid — he's got a real ground game, decent enough striking on the feet, and he was always trying to go for the finish rather than just coast. Bryczek's boxing is actually pretty clean, but he couldn't maintain the range he needed to keep Rowston honest on the feet. After the fight, Rowston called out Marvin Vettori — and honestly, that's the matchup the UFC would probably run. Vettori isn't what he once was, but he gives Rowston some legitimacy and hype as an Aussie up-and-comer.

Junior Tafa vs. Kevin Christian

Result: KO/TKO, Elbows From Top Position at 2:42 Round 1 of 3

Whenever Junior Tafa is listed as the favorite, you know something is off — and yet, here we are. Kevin Christian had his moment early, cracking Tafa with a calf kick that visibly hurt him. But Tafa shook it off, started swinging for the bleachers, and eventually connected. He chased Christian down, dropped him, and sent him to the shadow realm with ground and pound at 2:42 of the first. Blunt and straightforward.


Tafa's calf was clearly bothering him after that one kick — he was lucky to get the finish before his leg became more of an issue. If you're getting finished by Junior Tafa, your UFC future is going to be short. Tafa called out Zhang Mingyang after, which is a reasonable callout given where both guys are on the roster.

Jacob Malkoun vs. Gerald Meerschaert

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

This fight was exactly what the matchup looked like on paper. Jacob Malkoun plodded forward for three rounds, and Gerald Meerschaert backed up around the outside of the octagon, looking like an old man trying to hang with younger, hungrier fighters — and failing. Malkoun got takedowns, landed elbows from top position, controlled the positions. Meerschaert was warned by Mike Beltran to work when he had control. His output when on top was essentially non-existent. There was a brief stretch in the third where Meerschaert landed some decent shots and even threatened the neck in the final thirty seconds, but it was nowhere near enough.


The real frustration here is Malkoun. He's now been gifted Torrez Finney and Gerald Meerschaert as back-to-back opponents, and he couldn't finish either of them. Wasn't even close to finishing either. He's been handed two lives and hasn't done anything with them. The UFC will keep him around for the Australian home crowd appeal, but the level of performance he's turning in does not justify a roster spot if that were the only consideration. As for GM3 — he's done. Cut him.

Colby Thicknesse vs. Vince Morales

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Vince Morales stalked forward for all three rounds, was the more active striker, and finished the fight on Colby Thicknesse's back landing ground and pound. In the first, he also survived a genuine onslaught — Thicknesse had a triangle and an armbar threatening, somehow survived both, and then Morales reversed and got on top. That survival alone was remarkable. In the third, Morales landed two right hands that wobbled Thicknesse and put him against the fence, then ended the round from Thicknesse's back.


And then every judge gave Thicknesse the second round. Morales clearly won that round. The scorecard is baffling. Thicknesse's standup isn't good, his overhangs aren't connecting, and landing a spinning elbow once doesn't win you a round. Both of these guys are limited fighters — Morales is probably the better one — but the judging in round two defied basic comprehension.

Wes Schultz vs. Ben Johnston

Result: Submission, Guillotine Choke at 1:50 Round 3 of 3, 11:50 Total

After round one, there was no reason to believe Wes Schultz had any chance in this fight. Ben Johnston stuffed Schultz's early takedown, got on top, worked into mount, landed ground and pound, and basically spent fifteen minutes establishing that he was the better grappler. Schultz's answer to adversity was to shoot for takedowns he wasn't getting, try guillotines he wasn't finishing, and hug Johnston every time things got uncomfortable. Round two was more of the same, with Johnston spending extended time in mount while Schultz tried to grab his way out of danger.


Then round three happened. Schultz came out swinging, got tied up in a scramble, went for a guillotine, and somehow Johnston got back to the feet. But Johnston was visibly exhausted at that point — his strikes were slow and labored, and he was clearly fading. Schultz sank in a guillotine choke halfway through the round and got the tap. Against all odds.


It's a good win for Schultz, but the concerns don't go away. He still looks like a fighter who either gets exactly the position he wants or just resigns himself to the bottom and tries to hug his way through. His striking is not going to cut it at the top of middleweight — someone like Pyfer would make this look embarrassing. Johnston looked excellent early but gassed so badly that his cardio became the deciding factor. Better conditioning and he's probably not ending up in that guillotine to close. Also: Schultz was genuinely awful on the mic post-fight.

Jonathan Micallef vs. Themba Gorimbo

Result: Decision, Split at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Themba Gorimbo looked more comfortable on the feet early, investing in calf kicks and landing a right hook that had Jonathan Micallef stumbling. But round one turned when Micallef got a takedown and started landing elbows from top position. Gorimbo scrambled, worked back to his feet, but Micallef had taken the round.


Round two went the other way. Micallef shot for a single leg and lost the position — Gorimbo ended up on top, controlled there for extended stretches, and at one point got to Micallef's back with a body triangle. Micallef was throwing desperation triangles from the bottom and couldn't get back to his feet cleanly. It was the round where you expected Micallef's grappling — supposedly his bread and butter — to bail him out, and it didn't. Getting dominated in the grappling round when grappling is your thing is a problem.


The third was quiet until Micallef got his second wind late and started stalking Gorimbo down around the octagon, with Gorimbo floundering and running. The split decision for Micallef came down to round one being decisive, with one judge apparently disagreeing. It was a close, scrappy fight. Gorimbo isn't bad — his striking is reasonable, his grappling is real — but he needs better cardio and fewer unforced errors to be consistent at this level.

Kody Steele vs. Dom Mar Fan

Result: Submission, Heel Hook at 3:56 Round 1 of 3

Kody Steele came in with a clear game plan and executed it cleanly. He got the takedown early, moved through the guard to half guard, smothered Dom Mar Fan, and started threatening the arm triangle. Mar Fan scrambled and reversed the position — but that reversal put Steele in a weird spot on the bottom where he immediately started hunting heel hooks. He set up a kneebar from his back, got the tap, and collected his first UFC win.


The strategy was much smarter than what Steele showed in his previous UFC outing against Rongzhu. Mar Fan simply wasn't ready for Steele's jiu jitsu — he should have disengaged and tried to get the fight back to the feet, but he followed Steele into the grappling and paid for it. This is once again RoadToUFC proving to be one of the worst possible training bases for actual MMA. Give Steele a fight in the United States — both of his UFC bouts have been in Australia, which is rough on his purse early in his career and limits the exposure he can build stateside. He didn't take any damage in this one, so a quick turnaround makes all the sense in the world.
