Summary

UFC Freedom 250 was a legitimately great event. Two title changes in one night, both stunning in their own way, on the most theatrical stage the UFC has ever used. The matchmaking had real holes in it, but the fights that mattered delivered.


The main event was the night's most stunning result. Justin Gaethje, a 37-year-old given essentially no shot by the betting markets, somehow outlasted the most dangerous finisher in the sport. Ilia Topuria came in reckless, got tagged badly early, had his orbital broken, and by the time his corner threw in the towel after Round 4 he couldn't see out of his eye. Gaethje ate everything Topuria threw and kept coming back for more. The new undisputed lightweight champion is Justin Gaethje. Still can't believe it.


The co-main was just as shocking. Ciryl Gane dismantled Alex Pereira in two rounds, knocking him down with a stiff right jab in Round 2 and finishing him with a barrage of follow-up shots. Pereira came in at a massive weight disadvantage, and the size gap was simply too much to overcome. There is a reason no one has ever been a three-division UFC champion. Gane is the new heavyweight champion.


Sean O'Malley walked through Aiemann Zahabi in two rounds, staying disciplined on the outside before landing a straight left that crumbled his opponent and walking off with the finish. Josh Hokit dominated Derrick Lewis across two rounds, dragging him to the ground in Round 1, controlling him completely, and grinding out a TKO finish in Round 2 when Lewis essentially quit against the cage. Mauricio Ruffy made Michael Chandler look completely cooked, running him around the octagon and finishing him with a spinning back kick in Round 1. On the prelims, Bo Nickal got the standup KO after a ground sequence in Round 1, and Diego Lopes looked shaky early before putting together the TKO finish over Steve Garcia in Round 2.


The gripe with the card was the matchmaking. Nickal, Hokit, and O'Malley were all given opponents well below their level at a moment when the stage called for something bigger. But the ambiance of the White House lawn, the walkouts, the energy in the air: it made everything feel like it mattered. This was a genuine moment. Other sports organizations should be watching and taking notes.


Final Score: 8.7/10



Prelims

Diego Lopes vs. Steve Garcia

Result: KO/TKO at Round 2

Steve Garcia came out the aggressor from the jump, landing an uppercut early and following it with a body kick. He was the sharper, more active fighter through the first half of Round 1, tagging Diego Lopes with a good straight left and even stumbling for a moment on a whiffed hook. Lopes ate an eye poke and kept fighting, but he looked genuinely uncomfortable for long stretches. Garcia was controlling the pace and it was not particularly close.


Then Round 2 happened. Both guys were still mostly whiffing and not fully engaging when Lopes finally sat down on a left hook that stumbled Garcia. Garcia tried to survive but Lopes smelled blood and kept the pressure on, eventually walking him down for the TKO finish.


The win was nice but it raised real questions about Garcia's chin. He wasn't taking sustained punishment before that left hook landed, he was actually winning the fight, and the second something clean touched him, he was done. Tough break for Garcia, who was on a serious win streak and deserved better, but he clearly can't take the heat when the right shot finds him. An Aaron Pico or Lerone Murphy matchup makes sense next for him. As for Lopes, the fight to make is Lopes vs. Yair Rodriguez: real bad blood, real stakes in the featherweight division.

Bo Nickal vs. Kyle Daukaus

Result: KO/TKO at Round 1

Bo Nickal got a takedown early in Round 1 and went to work from inside Kyle Daukaus's guard, landing elbows from on top and cutting him up. Daukaus tried to control with a body triangle from the bottom and kept Nickal tight on his chest, but referee Herzog wasn't satisfied with the work rate and stood them up.


That turned out to be a gift. Back on the feet, Nickal whiffed on a headkick, then immediately connected with a left hook that put Daukaus down. He followed up with a flurry of elbows and Herzog stopped it.


Nice finish from Nickal, though Herzog's standup may not have been necessary given the elbows Nickal was landing. The bigger story is what's next: it's time for a legitimate step up in competition. Bruno Ferreira or Gregory Rodrigues would be the right call. As for Daukaus, he curled up a little too easily when things got bad. It's hard to see a path for him beyond UFC journeyman territory at this point.

Main Card

Mauricio Ruffy vs. Michael Chandler

Result: KO/TKO at Round 1

Mauricio Ruffy controlled the center from the opening bell while Michael Chandler circled around the octagon with his hands conspicuously low. Ruffy was tagging him with overhand rights early, and Chandler was doing nothing but bouncing around trying not to get destroyed. He tried a superman punch off the cage that whiffed completely. He shot a desperate takedown late and Ruffy sprawled it without breaking a sweat.


Ruffy was just playing with his food. He landed a spinning back kick that buckled Chandler, tracked him against the cage, and finished it with another spinning back kick that put Chandler on the canvas. A couple of follow-up shots and it was over.


Ruffy is good, but there was no real stock increase here because Chandler gave him nothing to work with. This was complete garbage from Chandler: four losses in a row, 1-6 in his last seven, and a performance where his entire game plan appeared to be surviving. It has to be retirement. He can probably still compete in other promotions and do well, but he does not belong in the UFC anymore. As for Ruffy, his corner calling for a title shot is ludicrous. He needs to fight an actual contender first. If Charles Oliveira isn't fighting Arman later this year, that's the matchup to make.

Josh Hokit vs. Derrick Lewis

Result: KO/TKO at Round 2

Josh Hokit was the smaller man but the more mobile one, using kicks early to stay out of Derrick Lewis's range before eventually taking the fight to the ground. He got the takedown with relative ease, stood over Lewis landing ground and pound, passed the guard, and kept him pinned. Lewis tried to stand, Hokit dragged him back down with a single leg. By the end of Round 1, Lewis had a cut on his face and had visibly resigned himself to his fate. He looked like a man who had already quit.


Round 2, Lewis pushed forward knowing he needed a knockout, but he couldn't land anything clean. Hokit was tagging him on the feet, and when they clinched against the cage Hokit was just teeing off. Lewis had nothing left. The stoppage came with Hokit unloading on him against the cage.


Total domination from Hokit. Lewis looked defeated from the first round and quit on the White House lawn. It's time for retirement: his body simply cannot take the damage anymore. As for Hokit, a title fight feels premature. He still has plenty to work on, and at heavyweight the size disadvantage against someone like Pereira could get him seriously hurt. A matchup with Volkov or Waldo Cortes-Acosta for the number one contender spot makes more sense as the next step.

Sean O'Malley vs. Aiemann Zahabi

Result: KO/TKO at Round 2

Sean O'Malley controlled the center while Aiemann Zahabi spent Round 1 on the backfoot, circling away and looking visibly uncomfortable. O'Malley worked the body with his right hand and dug in body shots throughout, but he was not loading up on anything or really getting after it. Zahabi's only consistent output was calf kicks. It was a jab fest in Round 1 and nothing more.


Round 2 was more of the same until the finishing sequence. O'Malley stalked Zahabi down with a nice combination, and in a "you go, I go" kind of exchange, landed a straight left that stumbled Zahabi before following with a right hand that put him away. The walkoff KO.


The stoppage was questionable: Zahabi did not appear to be fully out when the referee jumped in, and it felt like the ref wanted to give O'Malley a highlight-reel finish and obliged accordingly. The broader problem is that O'Malley was not impressive here. He has never quite panned out as the finishing machine everyone projected when he was climbing the rankings. He doesn't really like to fight; he is good at what he does and leans on his distance management rather than taking risks. The result is performances like this one: technically fine, largely uneventful until the last thirty seconds. A title shot next seems premature. The right fight is O'Malley vs. the winner of Umar vs. David Martinez, though the timing may force another title shot anyway.

Ciryl Gane vs. Alex Pereira

Result: KO/TKO at Round 2

Alex Pereira came out throwing headkicks early while Ciryl Gane worked to establish outside foot position and find his range. Gane was picking his shots: inside low kicks, jabs, teeps to the body. By the midpoint of Round 1, Pereira's output had dropped significantly and a welt was forming under his left eye. Gane was landing cleaner and more consistently, and Pereira's best answer was a counter right hook to close the round.


Round 2 was brief. Gane knocked Pereira down with a stiff right jab, then swarmed with a barrage of follow-up shots and that was all she wrote. TKO, new heavyweight champion.


There were definitely some back-of-the-head shots in that finishing sequence, but the result was not in doubt. Pereira just did not look good at heavyweight. The size gap was too large to overcome: he came in at a significant weight disadvantage and it showed in every exchange. There is a reason no one has ever been a three-division UFC champion, and this fight illustrated exactly why. Gane vs. Aspinall II is next, and the case for Gane is real: his Muay Thai technique is simply too much for brawlers like Aspinall and Pereira. As for Pereira, he is 39, he got badly hurt, and the smart move is to take real time off and be extremely selective about what comes next. If the money is right for one more fight, fine. If not, it may be time to walk away.

Justin Gaethje vs. Ilia Topuria

Result: KO/TKO, Corner Stoppage at 5:00 Round 4 of 5

Ilia Topuria came out walking Justin Gaethje down from the first bell, not respecting anything Gaethje brought to the table. That turned out to be a mistake. Gaethje used his jab well early, and Topuria was getting tagged while going forward recklessly. Topuria put together some combinations near the fence that hurt Gaethje, and Gaethje ate them and kept swinging back. A right hand, an uppercut, another right: Gaethje was throwing with bad intentions and making Topuria feel every one. Topuria got cut on his eye. By the end of Round 1, both men were marked up and neither had taken a step back.


Round 2, Topuria came out aggressive again. Gaethje shot a takedown and Topuria shucked it off. Topuria pressed him against the fence and went to the body, but Gaethje just folded up and refused to go down cleanly. Eventually Topuria got Gaethje to the canvas and took full mount, going for a mounted triangle and then an armbar, but the submission attempts stalled out. Topuria was tired and took a moment to breathe from the top position. He had done damage but hadn't finished.


Round 3 was the turning point. Gaethje came out and got into the clinch early, started getting his jab going, and landed a big shot that sent Topuria down. Gaethje followed to the canvas and threatened a d'arc choke. Topuria got back to his feet but his face was completely wrecked. They traded uppercuts in the clinch, Gaethje landed a jump knee, and a headkick from Gaethje landed right at the bell. The momentum had completely shifted.


Round 4 was a war of attrition. Gaethje was still stinging Topuria with shots, got a body lock takedown and briefly had him down before they separated back to the center. Topuria landed a right hand against the cage that hurt Gaethje and sent him running, but Topuria himself was seriously hurt and running in the other direction moments later. Both men were barely holding on. Topuria got a trip and took full mount, landed elbows, went for the back, and fell off. He shot a double leg late and Gaethje sprawled. Gaethje landed a knee to the body at the end of the round and Topuria was clearly in bad shape. His corner threw in the towel before Round 5.


Gaethje is a dog. It is absolutely insane to think about what he just did: broke Topuria's orbital, had his own face turned inside out, and kept coming for four rounds against the best finisher in the sport. Add this to Ferguson, Pimblett, and the rest: Gaethje delivers in the biggest moments. He is the new undisputed lightweight champion at 37 years old and will get paid accordingly. Topuria needs serious time off. He got completely wrecked out there, couldn't see by the end, and the aura took a real hit. What was strange was how reckless he looked going forward, like the game plan was off from the start. At 155, the size difference matters, and Topuria never quite imposed himself the way he has at lower weights.
