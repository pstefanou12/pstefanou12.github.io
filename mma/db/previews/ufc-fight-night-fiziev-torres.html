Overview

The UFC heads to Baku, Azerbaijan for the first time since 2023, and the card is loaded with political subtext: half the fighters on this card have ties to the Caucasus or Central Asia and are riding a massive home crowd advantage. This is Rafael Fiziev's city, and the UFC knows it. What makes this card interesting is not just the Azerbaijani nationalism angle, but the genuine quality at the top and some fascinating cross-fight connections running throughout the middleweight bouts.

The main event deserves more attention than it has gotten. Manuel Torres is one of the most lethal first-round finishers in the sport right now, with 16 of his 17 career wins coming inside the opening frame. Fiziev is the hometown hero and the betting favorite, but he has lost four of his last five and his recent record against fighters with range has been concerning. Torres is not the kind of opponent you want when you are trying to rebuild confidence in front of your home crowd. The Bahamondes thread is worth noting: Torres lost to Bahamondes, who Fiziev then beat, so there is a circular logic propping up the Fiziev line that may not hold up once Torres starts throwing.

The co-main event between Sharabutdin Magomedov and Michel Pereira is a genuine treat on paper, two fighters who produce chaotic, highlight-reel action, though Pereira's cardio has always been the elephant in the room. When the gas tank is full, there is nobody more unpredictable. When it is empty, there is nobody more predictable. The card also features a fascinating subplot across the middleweight bouts: Abusupiyan Magomedov, fighting in the sixth slot, has already beaten both Pereira and Brunno Ferreira at separate points in his UFC career, two fighters who appear in the co-main and main card respectively.

Further down the card, AbdulRakhman Yakhyaev is the fighter to watch on the prelims. He is 9-0 with the fastest submission in modern UFC light heavyweight history (33 seconds) and has not broken a sweat in any of his UFC appearances. The Daniil Donchenko fight in the prelims is another quiet one to keep an eye on: he trains with Fiziev at Tiger Muay Thai and won his UFC debut over Alex Morono without much trouble. The early prelims are filler, but the main card and prelims have enough compelling matchups to carry the night.


Main Card Picks

Rafael Fiziev vs. Manuel Torres

Rafael Fiziev is 13-5 and the sentimental favorite heading into this main event, but the resume requires some honest context. Four of his last five losses look better than they read: two split and majority decisions to Justin Gaethje, an injury stoppage against Gamrot, and a second-round TKO loss to the ascending Mauricio Ruffy in January. The common thread in his recent setbacks is reach. Ruffy has long arms and used them to control distance in exactly the way Fiziev hates; the Azerbaijani is at his most dangerous when he can get inside and unload his Muay Thai combinations, but fighters who can keep him at the end of a jab have exposed that limitation repeatedly.

Manuel Torres is 17-3 and 5-1 in the UFC, with all five promotional wins coming by stoppage and every single one earning a Performance of the Night bonus. He has never seen the second round in a winning UFC appearance, with 16 of his 17 career wins coming inside the first frame. He stopped Grant Dawson and Drew Dober in his last two outings and is headlining a UFC main event for the first time. He is ranked #13 at lightweight and carries real finishing power in both hands. His one UFC loss came against Ignacio Bahamondes, the same fighter Fiziev defeated last June, which is the most compelling argument for Fiziev's chances. The stylistic matchup is a striker's dream: two elite finishers who have no interest in the judges' scorecards.

Pick: Manuel Torres

Method: KO/TKO

The reach advantage is the deciding factor here. Torres holds a meaningful reach edge over Fiziev, and Fiziev has consistently struggled against opponents who can keep him at range and punish him before he can get inside. Ruffy proved this blueprint works. Fiziev needs to lunge forward to close the distance and those lunges have been getting him hurt, because when his head is traveling in a straight line, he is vulnerable to a clean right hand. Torres is exactly the kind of fighter who will be standing on the outside waiting for that lunge, and when Fiziev commits, Torres will crack him early. The Bahamondes win is the most commonly cited reason to back Fiziev, but Bahamondes has since lost his next fight and was arguably a diminishing prospect when Fiziev beat him. The home crowd will be electric, but finishing power at range tends to cut through noise. Torres lands a big right hook in the first round, Fiziev goes down, and Torres seals it with ground and pound.

Sharabutdin Magomedov vs. Michel Pereira

Sharabutdin Magomedov is 16-1 with only a decision loss to Michael Page on his record and is one of the most electrifying middleweights in the promotion. His arsenal of spinning techniques and long-range combinations makes him unpredictable in a way that is genuinely dangerous rather than just flashy, and he proved he can grind through adversity by bouncing back from the Page loss with a clean unanimous decision over Marc-Andre Barriault. He is the slight betting favorite and the sharper technician in this pairing.

Michel Pereira is 32-14 and coming off three losses in his last four fights, with his only recent win a narrow split decision over Zachary Reese in February. He was knocked out by Kyle Daukaus in August 2025 and lost by decision to Anthony Hernandez before that. The broader middleweight subplot: he already lost a unanimous decision to Abusupiyan Magomedov, the other Magomedov on tonight's card, in April 2025. Pereira is still one of the most entertaining fighters on any card he appears on, capable of producing a moment at any time, but the tank has been the same problem his entire career.

Pick: Sharabutdin Magomedov

Method: Decision

Pereira's ceiling in any given fight is determined entirely by how long his gas tank lasts, and historically the answer is not very long. If he cannot get the finish inside the first round, he starts to fade, and opponents who can weather the early storm and stay composed tend to outwork him comfortably from the second round onward. Magomedov has shown he can go the distance, most recently against Michael Page, a technical, disciplined striker who is not easy to outwork. The fact that Shara Bullet kept that fight close and competitive over three rounds suggests he has the cardio and the striking fundamentals to survive Pereira's early burst. Expect Pereira to come out throwing everything in rounds one and two, but once that energy dissipates, Magomedov will take over and cruise to a comfortable decision on the scorecards.

Nazim Sadykhov vs. Matheus Camilo

Nazim Sadykhov is 11-2-1 and the latest Azerbaijani fighter on this card who will have the Baku crowd firmly in his corner. He was one of the more quietly impressive lightweight prospects in the UFC before running into Fares Ziam in December 2025 and getting stopped by elbows and punches. Prior to that loss, he had gone on a remarkable run that included wins over Terrance McKinney, Ismael Bonfim, and Nikolas Motta, all legitimate lightweight names. He enters as a heavy -260 favorite and carries eight KOs in his career with genuine knockout power.

Matheus Camilo is 10-3 and 25 years old, a developing fighter who has been steadily building his UFC record through decision wins. His most notable recent result is a unanimous decision over Viacheslav Borshchev in November 2025, which is a useful data point given that Sadykhov drew with Borshchev in 2023. Camilo decisioned the same man Sadykhov could not finish, which is the strongest argument for the underdog here. He does have submission ability, but has been predominantly a points fighter in the UFC.

Pick: Nazim Sadykhov

Method: KO/TKO

Sadykhov is the better fighter, full stop. His wins over Bonfim and McKinney represent a significantly higher tier of competition than anything Camilo has faced, and the Ziam loss looks like a bad night against a dangerous opponent rather than evidence of a systemic flaw. The Borshchev comparison cuts both ways: Sadykhov drew with Borshchev, Camilo decisioned him, but Sadykhov's draw happened in November 2023 while Camilo's win came two years later against a different version of that fighter. Sadykhov has the finishing power to end this in the second or third round, and with the home crowd pushing him from the opening bell, expect him to come out aggressive and apply sustained pressure until Camilo breaks.

Asu Almabayev vs. Charles Johnson

Asu Almabayev is 23-3 and quietly one of the best flyweights in the world right now. He is 6-1 in the UFC with his only loss coming against Manel Kape, the current title contender, and he has 10 submission wins in a division where submissions barely happen. His most recent finish was a flying guillotine choke over Alex Perez in November 2025, and he enters as a -260 favorite with good reason. He is a Kazakh fighter who has been methodically building one of the strongest 125-pound resumes in the promotion without generating much mainstream attention.

Charles Johnson is 19-8 and occupies a particular archetype in the flyweight division: the volatile, streaky puncher whose fight record looks like a sine wave. He was TKO'd by Alex Perez in January 2026, the same fighter Almabayev submitted ten months earlier, then rebounded with a split decision over Bruno Silva in March. He has eight KO wins and genuine one-punch power, and his physical dimensions are an unusual advantage at 125 pounds, standing 5'9" with a 70-inch reach against Almabayev's 5'4" and 65-inch frame. The size difference is real and it is the primary reason to consider the upset.

Pick: Asu Almabayev

Method: Decision

Johnson's best wins are almost all puncher's chances: he hangs around in a fight he probably should not be winning, stays loose, and then lands a shot that changes everything. At this point in his career, flyweights know this about him, and Almabayev is not the kind of fighter who walks into those situations carelessly. He will enter with a sound gameplan, avoid a wild fire fight, and use his superior foot speed to win the striking exchanges at range before folding in the grappling when needed as a tiebreaker. Johnson's size is a factor, but Almabayev's grappling is the equalizer that nullifies the physical advantages. Expect three controlled rounds from the Kazakh and a unanimous decision.

Ikram Aliskerov vs. Brunno Ferreira

Ikram Aliskerov is 17-2, ranked #14 at middleweight, and on a two-fight win streak after TKO losses to both Khamzat Chimaev in Brave FC and Robert Whittaker in the UFC. His wins over Andre Muniz and Jun Yong Park have rebuilt his momentum and re-established him as a legitimate mid-tier middleweight threat. He is a combat sambo specialist with a high work rate and dangerous hands, entering as a -265 favorite.

Brunno Ferreira is 15-3 and had been on one of the more impressive runs in the middleweight division before it came to an abrupt end. He strung together three consecutive victories including a unanimous decision over Marvin Vettori and submission wins over Armen Petrosyan and Jackson McVey before being knocked out by Gregory Rodrigues in March 2026. His triangle armbar and armbar combinations make him a legitimate submission threat on the ground, but the Rodrigues KO puts a question mark over how he handles clean punches to the chin.

Pick: Brunno Ferreira

Method: TKO

The chin question runs both ways in this fight. Aliskerov has twice been stopped cleanly: once by Chimaev and once by Whittaker in the very first exchange of their fight. Neither of those men is primarily known as a one-punch KO artist over high-level competition, which suggests the issue is structural rather than situational. Ferreira has genuine power and the kind of submission pressure that will force Aliskerov to defend on the ground, where his grappling is serviceable but not elite. Aliskerov is the definition of a well-rounded fighter without a dominant area, which is exactly the wrong profile to bring against a specialist who can threaten you both standing and on the mat. Ferreira gets the fight into a scramble, lands a clean shot in the process, and the Aliskerov chin does not hold up.

Abus Magomedov vs. Michal Oleksiejczuk

Abusupiyan Magomedov is 28-7-1 and one of the more experienced veterans on this card at 35 years old. He had strung together three consecutive UFC wins, including unanimous decisions over Michel Pereira and Brunno Ferreira (both of whom appear elsewhere on tonight's card), before being submitted by Joe Pyfer via face crank in October 2025. With 21 of his 28 career wins coming by finish, he is still a dangerous submission threat who can end a fight at any moment.

Michal Oleksiejczuk is 22-9 and riding a three-fight winning streak that includes TKO stoppages of Gerald Meerschaert and Sedriques Dumas plus a unanimous decision over Marc-Andre Barriault. He previously lost consecutive bouts to Kevin Holland by technical submission and to Sharabutdin Magomedov by decision in the summer of 2024, which frames the current win streak appropriately. The subplot: Oleksiejczuk lost to the co-main event fighter earlier in his recent skid, and now faces another Magomedov in a crossroads fight where neither man can afford to continue sliding.

Pick: Abus Magomedov

Method: Submission

Oleksiejczuk's current three-fight win streak deserves honest scrutiny. Meerschaert is a veteran who gets finished regularly and is best known for his own submission wins rather than his defensive acumen. Dumas and Barriault are solid mid-tier middleweights, but neither is a litmus test for where a fighter genuinely stands in the division. Magomedov, by contrast, has been competing against the upper tier consistently, beating Pereira and Ferreira before losing to Pyfer. The cardio has always been the concern with Magomedov, but that concern is irrelevant if this fight ends in the first round, which is exactly the plan. Magomedov will look to drag this to the mat early and close the show with a submission before the gas tank becomes an issue.

Prelims Picks

Farman Hasanov vs. Eric Nolan

Farman Hasanov is 4-0 and making his UFC debut in the most favorable possible circumstances: in front of his home crowd in Baku as one of several Azerbaijani fighters on a card that the UFC has explicitly built around local appeal. He earned two stoppages in 2025 and carries genuine finishing ability, but his opponent pool has been limited and this debut will be the first real litmus test of whether he belongs at this level.

Eric Nolan is 8-4 and arrived in the UFC through strong regional showings, with three consecutive KO wins before making his promotional debut and getting submitted by Baisangur Susurkaev in August 2025. He has real power and is not someone to dismiss, but his submission defense has already been exposed at UFC level, and he is walking into a hostile environment as a clear visitor.

Pick: Farman Hasanov

Method: Decision

There is not a lot of footage to go on for either of these fighters at UFC level. Nolan's only UFC appearance ended in a submission loss to Susurkaev, who is not the kind of fighter who should be finishing opponents as a matter of course. That loss does not inspire confidence. Hasanov is an unknown quantity at this level, but he is fighting on home soil with an arena behind him that will treat every exchange like a title fight moment. In an even matchup between two inexperienced UFC fighters, that crowd advantage is a real factor. Expect a competitive three rounds that goes to the cards and breaks in Hasanov's favor.

AbdulRakhman Yakhyaev vs. Julius Walker

AbdulRakhman Yakhyaev is 9-0 and has been one of the more impressive UFC debuts in recent light heavyweight memory. He submitted Raffael Cerqueira in 33 seconds on his promotional arrival, setting the record for the fastest submission in modern UFC light heavyweight history, then followed it with a TKO win over Alik Lorenz and a rear-naked choke of Brendson Ribeiro in April 2026. He is 9-0 with eight submissions and is featured in the UFC's Fighters on the Rise heading into this event. The jury is still out on the level of competition he has faced, but the manner of his finishes has been emphatic.

Julius Walker is 7-2 and a former Division II basketball player whose athleticism has translated reasonably well to MMA. He decisioned Raffael Cerqueira, the same man Yakhyaev submitted in 33 seconds, which is a useful comparison point. He was TKO'd by Dustin Jacoby in February 2026 and is on a one-fight losing skid. His UFC record of 1-2 is not an encouraging indicator, and that one win is over a veteran who is no longer on the roster.

Pick: AbdulRakhman Yakhyaev

Method: Submission

Walker's UFC track record does not inspire confidence. His lone promotional win came over a fighter whose most notable recent result is getting submitted in 33 seconds by tonight's opponent. Yakhyaev has been dominant across the board, finishing fights quickly and making it look effortless, and there is nothing in Walker's profile that suggests he has the grappling defense to survive what is coming. The TKO loss to Jacoby also raises questions about how he holds up under pressure. Yakhyaev gets this to the mat early and closes it out in the first round.

Nursulton Ruziboev vs. Andrey Pulyaev

Nursulton Ruziboev is 36-9-2 and one of the most experienced fighters on this entire card. Standing 6'5" with a 76-inch reach, he is an imposing physical presence at middleweight with 13 KOs and 20 submissions across his 36 wins. His UFC debut was a KO win over Brunno Ferreira, a fighter who appears elsewhere on tonight's main card, and he has since added wins over Sedriques Dumas and Eric McConico while absorbing a decision loss to Joaquin Buckley. He is on a two-fight win streak and brings legitimate finishing ability from both positions.

Andrey Pulyaev is 10-4 and stands 6'4" with a 78-inch reach, making this a matchup between two physically enormous middleweights. He has lost two of his last three, dropping decisions to Ateba Abega Gautier and Christian Leroy Duncan in between a TKO win over Nick Klein via body kick and punches. He has shown genuine striking ability but has been outworked in the longer fights.

Pick: Nursulton Ruziboev

Method: Decision

Pulyaev has solid kickboxing and can make a competitive fight on the feet, but Ruziboev is a more complete fighter with a far deeper resume. The win over Ferreira alone, a legitimate UFC middleweight who went on to beat Vettori, is better than anything Pulyaev has produced. Pulyaev's two decision losses suggest that when he cannot land the big shot early, he runs out of ideas, while Ruziboev has the grappling background to shift gears and grind. If the striking stays even or gets dicey, Ruziboev incorporates the wrestling and controls his way to a clear decision.

Kaan Ofli vs. Javier Reyes

Kaan Ofli is 13-4-1 and a TUF Season 32 finalist who has strung together back-to-back wins after a rough patch that included consecutive losses to Muhammadjon Naimov and a KO defeat against Mairon Santos. His most recent win, a majority decision over Zha Yi in January 2026, was a fight that did not look like a comfortable victory watching it. Before that he submitted Ricardo Ramos via rear-naked choke in October 2025. He is a well-rounded fighter with submission ability, but his ceiling remains uncertain and he has not convincingly beaten anyone at UFC level.

Javier Reyes is 23-5 and has been on an absolute tear since earning his UFC contract through the Contender Series. He is 3-0 in the promotion, with all three wins coming by stoppage, including a first-round TKO of the experienced Douglas Silva de Andrade in his most recent outing. He is the physically bigger fighter with a height and reach advantage, and at 23-5 overall he brings a finishing pedigree that Ofli cannot match right now.

Pick: Javier Reyes

Method: KO/TKO

That majority decision win over Zha Yi was not a convincing performance, and there is a reasonable argument Ofli did not actually win that fight. It was gifted on the scorecards and does not represent a genuine step forward in his UFC run. Reyes, by contrast, has been finishing everyone in front of him since arriving in the promotion and looks like a legitimate prospect with the physical tools and finishing instinct to go deep in the division. This is the kind of fight where a rising prospect exposes a gatekeeper, and the result should not be particularly close. Reyes stops this inside the distance and continues his unbeaten UFC run.

Daniil Donchenko vs. Theodor Berggren

Daniil Donchenko is 13-2 and on a six-fight winning streak, most recently winning his UFC debut by unanimous decision over Alex Morono in February 2026. He trains at Tiger Muay Thai alongside tonight's main event fighter Rafael Fiziev and is featured in the UFC's Fighters on the Rise ahead of this card. Winning a decision over Morono in 2026 is not an especially ringing endorsement given where Morono currently sits in the welterweight pecking order, but the manner of the win was assertive and he controlled the bout from start to finish.

Theodor Berggren is 8-3 and making his UFC debut after two consecutive TKO wins on the regional circuit over Abdulatip Samedov and Daniele Battaglia. Before that run he was submitted by Justin Burlinson and lost a decision to Leonardo Damiani, so the two-fight streak is real but built against modest opposition. He is a compact welterweight with genuine punching power, and his finishing instinct is his best path to victory.

Pick: Daniil Donchenko

Method: Decision

Berggren's best shot at winning this fight is landing something big early before Donchenko can settle into his rhythm, because once this fight reaches the second and third rounds, the UFC experience gap will start to show. Donchenko has already navigated the debut nerves and knows how to pace himself across three rounds, while Berggren is stepping into the promotion for the first time against a fighter who has already solved that problem. The power threat is real, but it tends to be less dangerous against opponents who have felt UFC-level pressure before. Donchenko wins a methodical decision.

Early Prelims Picks

Bekzat Almakhan vs. Jean Matsumoto

Bekzat Almakhan is 12-3 and has been tested against the absolute top of the bantamweight division, with decision losses to both Umar Nurmagomedov and Aleksandre Topuria bookending a KO win over Brad Katona. He has 10 KOs in his 12 wins and is a dangerous finisher when given the right opening, but has struggled to maintain consistent output against elite competition. Coming off the Topuria loss, he needs a clear-cut win to stay relevant in the division.

Jean Matsumoto is 17-2 and his UFC run tells an interesting story. He has been involved in four consecutive split decisions, alternating between wins and losses, and the pattern reveals a fighter who is skilled enough to compete with anyone on the prelims but tends to make small errors at key moments. He lost a split to Farid Basharat in February 2026 and previously lost a split to Rob Font before winning close ones over Miles Johns and Katona. Both fighters have faced comparable opposition within the UFC and produced similar results.

Pick: Bekzat Almakhan

Method: Decision

Matsumoto's issue is a recognizable one: he does the vast majority of things well, but has a habit of finishing combinations lazily and getting countered in the process, then abandoning promising sequences before they land. Those mental lapses are what tip close decisions against him, and this fight has all the hallmarks of another narrow one. The differentiator is Almakhan's resume at the top of the division. Having gone full rounds against Nurmagomedov and Topuria, two of the sharpest technical fighters in the bantamweight division, Almakhan brings a level of high-pressure experience that Matsumoto simply has not had. That experience edge matters in a close fight that comes down to who executes better in the championship rounds.

Tahir Abdullayev vs. Jefferson Nascimento

Tahir Abdullayev is 19-3 and the current UAE Warriors welterweight champion, making his UFC debut on home soil in Baku. He is on a four-fight winning streak with three consecutive submission finishes, including a shoulder choke and a north-south choke that suggest genuine creativity on the mat. His regional resume is solid, but his three losses indicate he is beatable at the top of the regional scene, and his presence on this card reads more like a Baku hometown slot than a merit-based signing.

Jefferson Nascimento is 13-0 and the LFA interim lightweight champion, making his own UFC debut under unusual circumstances: he is jumping a full division from lightweight to welterweight on less than a month's notice after knocking out Gian Maurente with a front kick to the body on May 29. Fighting a heavier, more physically prepared welterweight in your first UFC appearance on short notice is an enormous ask, regardless of what the regional record says.

Pick: Jefferson Nascimento

Method: Decision

This is filler on a card that has been stretched to thirteen fights, and neither name is one that would generate much excitement in a different context. The pick comes down to simple logic: Nascimento has never lost, Abdullayev has, and the primary argument for Abdullayev is that he is Azerbaijani and the UFC needed local fighters on this card. That is not a reason to back someone in a fight. The size disadvantage for Nascimento moving up a full division on short notice is a genuine concern, but his undefeated record suggests he has a high floor and can adapt. A close, forgettable three rounds that goes to the judges.
