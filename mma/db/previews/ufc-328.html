Overview

UFC 328 is one of the better cards the promotion has put together in 2026 — two title fights, a genuine grudge match headlining, and enough legitimate intrigue on the undercard to make it worth watching from start to finish. This is the kind of card you circle on the calendar.

The main event is the one everyone is talking about. Khamzat Chimaev makes his first middleweight title defense against Sean Strickland, and the build-up has had a different energy than most championship fights — there's actual disdain here, not the manufactured kind. Strickland's gun comment. Chimaev laughing it off. The underlying question of whether Chimaev has ever been truly tested — whether the Burns war, the Usman slog, the Whittaker face crank, the DDP decision add up to something — or whether he's been managed carefully enough that we don't really know what he is yet. The narrative case for Strickland is real.

Underneath that, Joshua Van puts his flyweight title on the line against Tatsuro Taira in a fight that carries a piece of history — the first UFC title fight between two Asian-born fighters. Van is listed as the underdog in his own title defense, which tells you everything you need to know about the respect Taira's grappling commands. He knocked out Brandon Moreno and stopped Hyun Sung Park in his last two outings. Van's 8.84 significant strikes per minute is the highest rate in UFC history, but this fight almost certainly doesn't stay standing long enough for that to matter.

Alexander Volkov and Waldo Cortes-Acosta is the de facto heavyweight title eliminator, with the winner in pole position for whatever comes next at 265. Cortes-Acosta had the most active and impressive 2025 of any UFC heavyweight — five fights, three finishes, including a KO of Derrick Lewis. Volkov is the rangier, more experienced fighter who uses that size methodically. Sean Brady and Joaquin Buckley round out the main card in a welterweight rebound fight that Jon Anik correctly identified as career-defining — the loser could realistically be out of the title picture for two years. Both men are coming off losses, both want to keep this in their comfort zone, and there is real heat between them.

The prelims aren't filler. Yaroslav Amosov continues his UFC ascent against Joel Alvarez in a welterweight matchup with real stakes. Baisangur Susurkaev gets his toughest test yet. And Grant Dawson and Mateusz Rębecki are ATT teammates forced to compete, which never gets old as a storyline.


Main Card Picks

Khamzat Chimaev vs. Sean Strickland

Khamzat Chimaev (15-0) makes his first middleweight title defense nine months after winning the belt by unanimous decision over Dricus Du Plessis. He's undefeated across 15 professional fights, with a finishing rate that includes d'arce chokes, face cranks, and decisions — but critics have questioned the quality of the opposition and the manner of some wins. The Burns fight was a war, but everything since has been somewhere between calculated and uninspiring. Chimaev himself has acknowledged that only a finish will silence the "boring" label.

Sean Strickland (30-7) is a former middleweight champion and two-time Du Plessis opponent who bounced back from a unanimous decision loss to DDP in February 2025 by TKO'ing Anthony Hernandez in round three in February 2026. His style is built on volume, forward pressure, and cage craft — he out-works opponents rather than out-athletes them. He's beaten Adesanya, Brady, and Imavov, and his wins over that core top-five group have aged well. The bad blood heading in is real — Strickland's comments about showing up to fight week armed drew a public laugh from Chimaev, but the underlying animosity is genuine.

The key stylistic question is whether Chimaev's pressure and grappling threat can break Strickland early, or whether Strickland can survive the first round and make Chimaev work for fifteen-plus minutes. No one in Chimaev's recent history has been a bigger, stronger, more durable fighter than Strickland. If this goes deep, the dynamic could shift significantly.

Pick: Sean Strickland

Method: KO/TKO

There hasn't been heat like this in the UFC in a while — genuine disdain, not the promotional kind. And the case for Strickland is more than just narrative. Look at what Chimaev has actually done since the Burns fight: he ran away from a post-coaching Kamaru Usman for three rounds, got a fluke finish on Whittaker, and laid on Du Plessis for fifteen minutes. The undefeated record is real, but the level of adversity he's faced is not. Strickland, on the other hand, has systematically taken out the middleweight top five — Imavov, Allen, Fluffy, Adesanya, Costa — all on short notice or under difficult conditions, and those wins have held up.

The blueprint for beating Chimaev exists: survive the first round, make him work, and break him in the championship rounds. Strickland is the biggest, strongest, most durable fighter Chimaev has faced. When this gets to rounds three, four, five, Chimaev is going to find out what it feels like to be in there with someone who simply won't fold. Strickland walks him down, breaks him, and gets a round four TKO.

Joshua Van vs. Tatsuro Taira

Joshua Van (16-2) won the flyweight title by injuring Alexandre Pantoja's arm on a takedown just 26 seconds into round one at UFC 323. He owns the highest significant strike rate per minute in UFC history at 8.84, and he's been on a six-fight win streak that includes a unanimous decision over Brandon Royval. He pulled out of this title defense at UFC 327 due to injury, pushing it here — and enters as a +162 underdog despite being the champion, which is a rare and telling dynamic.

Tatsuro Taira (18-1) has been building serious momentum heading into his first title shot. He knocked out Brandon Moreno from back mount in round two in December 2025, then stopped Hyun Sung Park with a face crank. His only loss was a split decision to Brandon Royval in October 2024 — the same Royval that Van beat on the way to the title. He's the -200 betting favorite as the challenger and brings suffocating submission grappling and ground-and-pound that are the polar opposite of Van's wide-open striking game. This is the first UFC title fight between two Asian-born fighters.

Van's striking volume is extraordinary on paper, but the question has always been what happens when this fight hits the mat — and against Taira, it will hit the mat. Van's grappling was exposed as a question mark when he won the belt the way he did against Pantoja; it's still unclear how elite his ground defense actually is. Taira isn't just going to find out — he's going to stress-test it.

Pick: Tatsuro Taira

Method: TKO

Van is a paper champion. He didn't really beat Pantoja — Pantoja got injured on a takedown, and we never found out how that fight actually plays out. Van's best win is over Brandon Royval, and Royval didn't seriously threaten to grapple him in that matchup. His only top-tier win was delivered under circumstances that left the real stylistic question unanswered.

Taira answers it. He has strong, submission-level grappling, a recent TKO of Brandon Moreno, and the kind of methodical pressure that will drag Van out of the striking range he needs. Van will get exposed on the ground, and Taira will finish him by TKO. The fact that Pantoja most likely won't get the opportunity to right that wrong is genuinely unfortunate.

Alexander Volkov vs. Waldo Cortes-Acosta

Alexander Volkov (39-11) is the most physically imposing fighter in the UFC's heavyweight division — 6'7" with a reach that creates problems for anyone trying to close distance. He rebounded from a split decision loss to Ciryl Gane in December 2024 with back-to-back wins over Jailton Almeida and Evgeny Goncharov, demonstrating he can still operate at elite level. He uses his length methodically, controls range with his jab, and doesn't make mistakes. He's a veteran who has been in this position before.

Waldo Cortes-Acosta (17-2) was the most active and impressive UFC heavyweight of 2025 — five fights, three straight finishes, including a first-round KO of Shamil Gaziev, a first-round TKO of Ante Delija, and a second-round ground-and-pound stoppage of Derrick Lewis in January 2026. His only stumble in that stretch was a decision loss to Sergei Pavlovich in August 2025, a three-round performance that was uninspiring at best. He's publicly angling for an interim title, and a win here makes that case impossible to ignore.

This is a de facto heavyweight title eliminator. Cortes-Acosta brings explosive finishing power and serious momentum; Volkov brings range, composure, and the physical advantage he's always used to grind opponents out. The central question is whether Cortes-Acosta can close the distance and land his power shots against a longer, rangier Volkov — or whether Volkov picks him apart from range over fifteen minutes.

Pick: Alexander Volkov

Method: Decision

Cortes-Acosta had a breakout 2025, but the question of championship mettle is still open. He's been finishing fighters who weren't operating at the absolute top of the division, and when he fought Pavlovich — a legitimate elite — he lost a three-round decision in a fight that was largely uneventful. That's the template Volkov will follow here. He'll use his jab, maintain range, and not give Cortes-Acosta the clean look he needs to land his bombs. Fifteen minutes of being out-ranged and picked apart, and Volkov walks away with the decision and the number-one contender slot.

Sean Brady vs. Joaquin Buckley

Sean Brady (18-2) has been one of the most quietly effective welterweights in the UFC over the past two years — unanimous decision over Gilbert Burns, guillotine choke of Leon Edwards in round four, grappling him at a 5-for-5 clip on takedowns. He's a Renzo Gracie Philly product who smothers opponents and submits them. His only stumble was a brutal first-round KO loss to Michael Morales in November 2025, which ended a three-fight win streak and raised chin questions. He needs this win to stay in the title picture — Jon Anik framed it plainly: the loser of this fight could be out of contention for two years.

Joaquin Buckley (21-7) is a knockout artist on a two-fight skid — a unanimous decision loss to Kamaru Usman in June 2025 followed by a tech fall loss to Patrick Downey in November. His run through Ruziboev, Thompson, and Covington felt like a title trajectory; the Usman loss deflated that entirely. He's been training with Usman since, a detail Brady has publicly dismissed. There was a heated face-to-face where Brady told Buckley "you didn't punk nobody." The bad blood is real, and Buckley needs to keep this fight standing to have any real chance.

This is a textbook grappler-vs-striker matchup with real stakes on both sides. Brady has to get this to the ground; Buckley has to land his power before Brady can neutralize him. Both men have finished quality opponents, but they finish in completely different ways — and whoever dictates where the fight takes place will almost certainly win.

Pick: Sean Brady

Method: Submission

Buckley's performance against Usman was bad — looked like he didn't belong in there, couldn't do anything to stay off his back, and got taken down for three straight rounds. And Usman is not a serious competitor right now: one fight in three years, and it's already been nearly a year since that. Brady is still in his prime, still hungry, and still has a major grappling advantage over a striker who has shown real vulnerability on the ground. Buckley has three rounds to catch Brady — and Brady is just going to maul him the same way he took out Leon Edwards last year. Submission, probably in the third.

King Green vs. Jeremy Stephens

King Green (34-17-1) is riding back-to-back wins — a split decision over Lance Gibson Jr. in December 2025 and a second-round TKO of Daniel Zellhuber in February 2026. He averages 6.4 significant strikes per minute, brings real pace and volume, and has been a consistent fraud-checker for fighters who don't belong at UFC level. After losses to Mauricio Ruffy and Paddy Pimblett in 2024-2025, he's back doing what he does best.

Jeremy Stephens (29-22) personally messaged Dana White on Instagram asking for this UFC return — which tells you something. He hasn't won a UFC fight since 2018, is tied for the most losses in company history, and has been splitting time between UFC and BKFC. He's coming off back-to-back losses: a decision to Mason Jones and a fifth-round KO at the hands of Mike Perry. He averages 3.1 significant strikes per minute. His 19 career KOs are real, but they belong to a different era.

Both men are 39. The momentum gap is not small — it's a chasm. Green is the more active, more contemporary fighter by a significant margin, and Stephens is here on reputation and nostalgia more than any current competitive standing.

Pick: King Green

Method: KO/TKO

Green is the fraud-check king, and Stephens is handing him the easiest fraud check of his career. There is no reason for Stephens to be in the UFC right now — he asked for it on Instagram, he got it, and he's going to get knocked out for it. Green stops him in round two.

Prelims Picks

Ateba Gautier vs. Ozzy Diaz

Ateba Gautier (10-1) is 21 years old, from Cameroon, and one of the most hyped middleweight prospects in the promotion. He's on a nine-fight win streak with a finish rate that includes multiple first-round stoppages — elbows at 1:41, a knee at 3:32, a punch at 1:10. The criticism that follows him is consistent: the UFC has been feeding him soft opposition rather than stepping him up, and it's hard to evaluate what he actually is as a fighter without tougher tests. This fight was originally booked for UFC 320 before Diaz withdrew with an injury.

Ozzy Diaz (10-3) has gone 1-1 in his UFC tenure — early TKO'd on debut by Zhang Mingyang, then bounced back with a unanimous decision win over Djorden Santos. He represents the first time Gautier will face someone with actual UFC wins and losses on their record, making this nominally a step up in competition. The narrative around this fight is less about Diaz as a genuine threat and more about whether Gautier can look dominant enough to silence the can-crusher criticism.

Pick: Ateba Gautier

Method: KO/TKO

Apparently a matchup against Andrey Pulyaev was still too big a jump in competition for Gautier, so the UFC has served him another lamb. Diaz is not a real test for a prospect of Gautier's hype level. Round one KO, and the can-crusher argument gets louder.

Joel Alvarez vs. Yaroslav Amosov

Joel Alvarez (23-3) moved up from lightweight and beat Vicente Luque by unanimous decision in October 2025, establishing himself as a genuine welterweight. He's on a four-fight win streak featuring multiple finishes — a flying knee TKO of Drakkar Klose, knees and ground-and-pound over Elves Brener, a d'arce choke of Marc Diakiese. He's fought and lost to Arman Tsarukyan — the kind of elite lightweight competition that gives context to his level. He's a tank with a finisher's instinct that travels across weight classes.

Yaroslav Amosov (29-1) is the former Bellator welterweight champion making his second UFC appearance. His debut saw him submit Neil Magny via anaconda choke in round one in December 2025; he submitted Curtis Millender the same way in his Bellator campaign. His only loss was a late TKO to Jason Jackson in November 2023. He's an elite grappler with steadily improving striking, and the UFC has fast-tracked him into increasingly credible opposition. This is his biggest test yet in the promotion.

The stylistic clash is genuine: Alvarez is a high-pressure chaos agent comfortable in scrambles and finishes; Amosov wants to control tempo, grind, and strangle. Both men finish fights at a high rate. Amosov's grappling pedigree and UFC momentum represent the biggest challenge Alvarez has faced at welterweight — and a win for Alvarez here would be a major scalp.

Pick: Joel Alvarez

Method: Decision

Alvarez is a tank who has been fighting elite competition at lightweight for years — Tsarukyan, Ismagulov — and has now successfully transitioned to welterweight, nearly decapitating Vicente Luque in Rio last fall. Amosov is a well-rounded grappler, but his win over Magny doesn't move the needle much. Alvarez has the experience advantage, the lightweight's conditioning base, and the finishing instinct to mix everything together and out-point Amosov over three rounds. Decision win for Alvarez.

Grant Dawson vs. Mateusz Rębecki

Grant Dawson (23-3-1) was TKO'd by Manuel Torres in the first round at UFC 323 in December 2025, ending a three-fight win streak that included wins over Diego Ferreira and Rafa García. He's a grinding grappler who controls fights on the canvas and wears opponents down — the Torres loss was the rare case where he got caught before he could impose his game. He's come in with a clear message: "I'm better than you thought." The notable subplot here is that Dawson and Rębecki are teammates at American Top Team in Miami, making this a fight between training partners.

Mateusz Rębecki (20-4) is on a two-fight losing skid — decisions to Chris Duncan and Ludovít Klein — but earned Fight of the Night bonuses in both, which underlines his entertainment value even in defeat. He's a chaos agent on the feet with real power, and he's the kind of fighter who makes every fight interesting regardless of outcome. He needs to stop the skid here or his UFC spot becomes genuinely uncertain.

Both men are grapplers who generate scrambles, and the ATT teammate angle adds an unusual personal dimension. A Fight of the Night bonus seems like a natural outcome of this stylistic pairing regardless of who wins.

Pick: Mateusz Rębecki

Method: KO/TKO

Dawson has a glass chin and his best wins come against fighters with limited ground games — the moment someone matches him physically and brings power on the feet, he's in trouble. Rębecki has the physicality and the nuclear power to make Dawson uncomfortable from the jump. Dawson will be intimidated by Rębecki's physical presence early, and Rębecki puts him down with one clean shot to the chin in the opening minutes.

Jim Miller vs. Jared Gordon

Jim Miller (38-19) is 42 years old and making his 47th UFC appearance — the most in company history — in his home state of New Jersey. He holds the record for both the most UFC wins (28) and most UFC losses (19). His last outing was a unanimous decision loss to Chase Hooper in April 2025; before that he submitted Damon Jackson for win number 28. These two were originally booked to fight at UFC on ESPN 46 in June 2023 before Gordon was medically pulled, so there's a layer of unfinished business. Every Miller fight now carries a legacy dimension.

Jared Gordon (21-8) went 1-1 in his last two — a first-round TKO of Thiago Moisés in May 2025, then stopped by Rafa García via elbows in round three in September 2025. He's a well-rounded lightweight who has fought quality competition: losses to Paddy Pimblett and Nasrat Haqparast, wins over Mark Madsen and Moisés. He's 21-8 and operating in the mid-tier of the division, where both men currently sit.

Neither man is a title contender at this stage. This is a competitive fight between two veterans in the middle of the lightweight pack, with Miller's record and home state narrative adding the emotional layer.

Pick: Jared Gordon

Method: Decision

Miller shouldn't be in the UFC anymore. The only times he wins at this point are when the UFC serves up a submission on a platter against an opponent who walks into his game. Gordon has fought significantly better competition recently and brings a more complete, well-rounded skillset. Miller will fade over three rounds, and Gordon has enough to grind out a decision.

Roman Kopylov vs. Marco Tulio

Roman Kopylov (14-5) is on a two-fight losing skid — unanimous decisions to Paulo Costa in July 2025 and Gregory Rodrigues in November 2025. He's a southpaw kickboxer with genuine KO power: he head-kicked Chris Curtis in the final second of round three earlier in 2025 to win that fight, and he's never been knocked out in his career. He needs a win here to avoid serious roster pressure.

Marco Tulio (14-2) earned his UFC spot off the 2024 Contender Series and went 2-0 in his first two UFC bouts — a right hand TKO of Tresean Gore and a right hook finish of Ihor Potieria — before being stopped by Christian Leroy Duncan via spinning backfist in November 2025. He's a Brazilian power puncher who finishes fights quickly, but the Duncan loss raised questions about how he handles more experienced, technically sound opposition.

Both men are coming off losses and need a win to avoid further roster pressure. Kopylov's southpaw angles and range against Tulio's straight-ahead power punching is an interesting style clash — either Kopylov maintains distance and picks him apart, or Tulio closes the gap and turns it into a brawl.

Pick: Roman Kopylov

Method: Decision

Tulio is not UFC caliber — he doesn't have the grit or the technical toolkit to compete consistently at this level. Kopylov has been in with Costa and Rodrigues, legitimate top-10 middleweights, and while he lost both, he survived and showed what he can do. Tulio is fighting at a completely different level of competition. Kopylov's boxing is cleaner, his southpaw angles create problems Tulio isn't equipped to handle, and if Tulio tries to force a grappling exchange, Kopylov has enough to survive. Decision for Kopylov.

Pat Sabatini vs. William Gomis

Pat Sabatini (21-5) is a Renzo Gracie Philly product on a three-fight win streak — unanimous decisions over Joe Solecki and Chepe Mariscal, plus a "fleeing the mat" stoppage of Richie Lewis. His only UFC losses came to Damon Jackson and Diego Lopes, both elite featherweights. He's an 8-2 veteran in the promotion who builds through grappling, smothers opponents, and looks for submissions at every opportunity.

William Gomis (15-3) is the French Sanda and European champion out of Paris — an explosive, speed-based striker who is 1-1 in his last two. His only UFC loss was a narrow split decision to Hyder Amil. He's coming off a unanimous decision win over Robert Ruchała in September 2025, and French outlets have framed this as a career-defining moment — a convincing win here would push him into the official featherweight rankings for the first time.

The stylistic matchup is clean: Gomis needs to keep this standing and use his speed and striking to stay out of Sabatini's grappling range; Sabatini needs to get this to the ground early and often and take away any standing comfort Gomis has built. Gomis has not been seriously tested in the grappling department in the UFC yet — this is that test.

Pick: Pat Sabatini

Method: Decision

Sabatini is a tough, smothering grappler who doesn't need a finish to win — he just needs to drag this to the mat and grind. Gomis hasn't been tested on the ground in the UFC, and this is where that gap gets exposed. Even if Gomis connects cleanly once or twice on the feet, Sabatini will eat it and keep pressing. Gomis needs an early finish to win this fight, and the odds of that happening against a durable, relentless grappler are slim. Sabatini grinds to a decision.

Baisangur Susurkaev vs. Djorden Santos

Baisangur Susurkaev (11-0) is the UFC's current unbeaten middleweight prospect — Chechen, 6'2", 79-inch reach, with TKO and RNC finishes in his last two UFC bouts at UFC 319 and UFC 322. He's finishing fights at different levels of competition and doing it convincingly. The market has him at roughly 74% implied probability. He and Chimaev are known to be close, and the UFC has placed him on the same card — a promotional signal about where they see his trajectory.

Djorden Santos (11-2) is coming off a unanimous decision win over Danny Barlow last October after losing to Ozzy Diaz at UFC 313. He averages 7.0 significant strikes per minute — notably more than Susurkaev's 4.6 — and brings the kind of volume and pressure that will test how the prospect handles sustained activity. He faces a significant size and reach disadvantage, but he's not here just to fill a spot.

This is the most credible test of Susurkaev's UFC career to date. Santos's output and pressure will reveal something about how the undefeated Chechen handles a fighter who comes to work rather than folding to his physical tools.

Pick: Baisangur Susurkaev

Method: KO/TKO

Susurkaev getting upset here would be entertaining, but it's not happening. The UFC is building him deliberately — placing him on Chimaev's card is a statement — and Santos, while active, faces a size and reach disadvantage that Susurkaev will exploit. He'll make some sloppy mistakes early as he figures Santos out, then land something clean in round two and get the TKO.

Clayton Carpenter vs. Jose Ochoa

Clayton Carpenter (8-2) trains at The MMA Lab in Phoenix and carries a genuinely impressive athletic background — USA Junior National Muay Thai champion, junior Golden Gloves boxing champion, and IBJJF world champion in both gi and no-gi grappling. On paper, he's a complete fighter. In practice, his UFC run has hit a wall: back-to-back losses to Tagir Ulanbekov (decision) and Jafel Filho (kimura, R1). He needs a win here to stay on the roster.

Jose Ochoa (8-2) trains out of Chute Boxe Diego Lima in São Paulo and had a mixed 2025 — a Performance of the Night KO of Cody Durden at 0:11 of round two in June, followed by a decision loss to Asu Almabayev in July. Both men enter at identical 8-2-0 records, which makes this as symmetrical a prelim booking as it gets.

The contrast is straightforward: Carpenter's American wrestling and striking blend against Ochoa's Chute Boxe Brazilian style. Both men are on the roster bubble — whoever gets the early momentum in this fight likely pulls away with it.

Pick: Jose Ochoa

Method: KO/TKO

Ochoa has at least been in there with credible UFC-level opposition — Almabayev, Durden — and has a Performance of the Night KO on his record. Carpenter's losses have been to opponents who exposed real gaps in his game, and his UFC career so far suggests he isn't quite at the level his credentials promised. Ochoa gets a TKO in round two and ends Carpenter's farewell party early.
