Summary

UFC Fight Night 279 was a 6.8, and that number probably feels generous the further you get from it. Coming off the spectacle of the White House card, this one landed with a thud. The kind of card where you finish watching and think: okay, why are we back to this?


The main event delivered the result the division needed: Manel Kape stopped Kyoji Horiguchi in the third round, and the winner-take-all title eliminator goes to the man who knocked out Brandon Royval six months ago. But the fight itself was messier than the narrative. Horiguchi took Kape down twice, controlled him on the mat for stretches, and had Kape in a genuinely bad spot heading into the third. The finish came after Kape landed a big right, Horiguchi went down, and Herb Dean stepped in. Was it early? Yes, Horiguchi was still in the fight. Kape's path to a title shot now depends heavily on who comes out of the next flyweight title fight: if Pantoja beats Van in the rematch, Kape has no chance in that matchup. His grappling deficiency is serious and was exposed tonight.


Below the main event, the card had its moments. Murtazali Magomedov announced himself to the UFC with a submission victory over Melsik Baghdasaryan via a twister at 1:17 of the first round, which is one of the rarest finishes in MMA history. Genuinely had never seen one executed like that. Vinicius Oliveira proved he belongs at featherweight by getting his teeth kicked in during the first round and then coming back to stop Andre Fili in the second. And Beatriz Mesquita got rocked, nearly finished, and then submitted Mullins with an armbar from the bottom on the same exchange. That is an elite grappler.


The prelims were a different story. Luana Santos got a decision over Karol Rosa in a fight so boring it demands a serious conversation about what the women's bantamweight division is actually for. This was Santos's second terrible performance in a row. Mitch Raposo somehow squeaked out a split decision over Allan Nascimento in a fight that frustrated everyone involved. Levan Chokheli knocked out Leon Shahbazyan in 23 seconds, which was spectacular in isolation but tells us nothing because Shahbazyan has no business being in the UFC. The card also lost real potential when Ethyn Ewing was pulled and moved to a numbered event. That fight could have given the prelims a pulse.


One lingering question coming out of the night: why is the UFC not pushing Bia Mesquita harder? She actually wants to finish people and is delivering in the Octagon, but her striking was exposed tonight. If she ever runs into someone with real takedown defense and elite hands, she is in serious trouble.


Final Score: 6.8/10



Main Card

Manel Kape vs. Kyoji Horiguchi

Result: TKO (Punches) at R3 2:42

This was not the clean statement performance the flyweight division wanted, but Manel Kape got the job done against Kyoji Horiguchi in three rounds, finishing the Japanese legend with ground and pound at 2:42 of the third.


The story of the first two rounds was Horiguchi's grappling. He took Kape down in round one, settled into full guard, and imposed himself. Round two was more of the same: a head kick stumbled Kape, Horiguchi got another takedown, and spent time in half guard landing ground and pound while Kape chest-held him and waited. By the end of round two, the situation was clear: if Kape got taken down again, it was over.


Round three is where Kape flipped the script. He landed a big combination, Horiguchi went down, and Herb Dean stepped in. Was the stoppage early? Yes. Horiguchi had been taking punishment but was still in the fight, and the finish felt rushed. Kape is now the frontrunner for a flyweight title shot, but his path to that title is heavily dependent on who comes out of the next championship fight. If Pantoja beats Van in the rematch, Kape has no answer for what Pantoja would do to him on the mat. The grappling deficiency was exposed tonight and it is a real problem. As for Horiguchi, a fight with Tatsuro Taira makes sense as a next step to keep him relevant at the top of the division.

Navajo Stirling vs. Ion Cutelaba

Result: TKO (Punches) at R2 3:23

The prediction here was Ion Cutelaba to fraud-check Navajo Stirling in round one, and the fight went roughly the opposite direction. Stirling won a TKO in round two and the scoreboard says the fraud check was wrong. The caveat: Cutelaba is not exactly a reliable litmus test.


The first round was a messy brawl. Cutelaba went for a guillotine early, jumped to the ground, abandoned it, and then tripped Stirling twice to get him down. Stirling held his own, stung Cutelaba on the feet, and played possum detector while Cutelaba tried to sell a fake hurt. Neither man was particularly clean, but Stirling was outworking the veteran.


By round two, Stirling had found his rhythm. He hurt Cutelaba with an elbow, the ground exchanges were competitive but Stirling was grinding him down, and by the end he was landing with authority and Cutelaba could not keep up. The TKO came at 3:23. The verdict: Stirling is still a big, slow, unathletic fighter who looks clunky and uncoordinated in the Octagon, but Cutelaba is also just not that good. The fraud-check narrative was wrong in outcome but the underlying concerns about Stirling's athleticism and opponent quality have not gone away. Give him slight bumps in competition and see what develops.

Christian Rodriguez vs. Hyder Amil

Result: Technical Submission (Guillotine Choke) at R1 3:43

Christian Rodriguez made a very loud statement in a very short amount of time. Amil was the pick here, the theory being that Rodriguez had been exposed at featherweight against natural 145-pounders. That theory is now looking foolish.


Rodriguez controlled the entire round on the feet. He was landing a straight right repeatedly, tagged Amil with a head kick that somehow did not put him away, and then locked up a guillotine and squeezed it tight. Technical submission at 3:43 of the first round. Amil's eye was swelling before the choke even went on.


So is Rodriguez actually good? The honest answer is: we do not know yet. That was a nasty finish and he looked sharp the whole fight, but Amil was chinny, timid, and frankly looked like a man whose time has passed. Making any grand proclamations about Rodriguez off this performance requires seeing him against actual competition. What is clear is that this performance earns him that chance. As for Amil: the chin is gone, the confidence looked shot, and it might be time to pack it up.

Murtazali Magomedov vs. Melsik Baghdasaryan

Result: Submission (Twister) at R1 1:17

Murtazali Magomedov submitted Melsik Baghdasaryan with a twister at 1:17 of the first round, which is one of the rarest finishes in MMA history. It was surreal to watch in real time. A twister. In a UFC debut. Against an opponent making his first appearance on a main card. Whatever the circumstances, you had to rewind it just to confirm it actually happened.


The fight itself was brief. Baghdasaryan came out swinging, Magomedov shot for the takedown, got to the back with a body triangle with stunning ease, then twisted his body and torqued the spine until the tap came. The whole sequence took barely over a minute. The submission was as nasty as it was uncommon.


The verdict on Baghdasaryan is simple: cut him. He cannot handle any pressure once the fight hits the mat, he got overwhelmed the moment Magomedov decided to take it there, and he has now been finished twice in his last three fights. Magomedov, on the other hand, needs a serious bump in competition. He is 11-0 with all finishes, his debut was spectacular, but Baghdasaryan was not a real measuring stick. Give him someone who can actually test whether that submission arsenal translates against UFC-caliber opposition.

Vinicius Oliveira vs. Andre Fili

Result: TKO (Punches and Elbows) at R2 4:56

Vinicius Oliveira is kind of a gangster. He got his teeth kicked in during the first round and then came back to stop Andre Fili with punches and elbows at 4:56 of the second. That is not the resume of a man who wilts under pressure.


Round one belonged to Fili. He was going forward, landing body kicks that clearly hurt Oliveira, and had him retreating to the cage in visible pain. The critical mistake came when Fili did not pounce. He had Oliveira hurt, badly, and let the moment pass. Oliveira, to his credit, stayed alive, traded in the pocket knowing he needed a finish, and survived to the bell.


Round two was a different fight. Oliveira found his calf kicks, started stinging Fili with right hands and body shots, and the momentum flipped completely. By the end of the round, Oliveira was teeing off and Fili was done. The finish came with ten seconds left. There was a poetic footnote: Fili was bleeding from the back of the head at the end, and Herb Dean was the referee. Classic.


The honest assessment of Oliveira is this: he won, and he looked better than he did against Bautista, but a featherweight journeyman nearly finished him in the first round. He has no grappling to speak of, and if he were to step in with Volkanovski or Movsar Evloev, he would get destroyed. He is exciting to watch, but championship aspirations are not realistic. Fili made the mistake of not finishing when he had the chance and paid for it.

Prelims

Kevin Borjas vs. Andre Lima

Result: Decision (Unanimous) at R3 5:00

This fight was supposed to be a comfortable win for Andre Lima, the undefeated flyweight prospect returning from a long layoff. Instead it ended in a unanimous decision loss, and the most talked-about moment was a low blow in the second round that may have changed the entire outcome.


Round one was competitive: Lima was going forward, Kevin Borjas was circling and landing combinations, and Borjas stung Lima to end the round. Then round two began, Lima landed a nasty low blow, and the action stopped. Borjas took the full five minutes of recovery time. Given that cardio has historically been Borjas's biggest problem, those five minutes were a gift. He came back, won the second round, and carried the momentum into the third.


The third was another competitive round that Lima could not close. He never got going enough to seal the victory, and Borjas walked away with a unanimous decision that was littered with controversy from start to finish. Did Borjas milk the groin shot? That is a reasonable question. The recovery time he was given was significant, and it is hard to know how the fight plays out without that stoppage.


Lima's undefeated record is gone, and the path forward is unclear after what looked like a winnable fight slipped away under controversial circumstances. Borjas gets the win, but this one is going to have an asterisk on it for a while.

Beatriz Mesquita vs. Melissa Mullins

Result: Submission (Armbar) at R1 3:16

Beatriz Mesquita got rocked, nearly finished, and then submitted Melissa Mullins with an armbar from the bottom in the same sequence. Round one. That is a great grappler.


The early part of the round was alarming for Mesquita fans. Mullins clipped her and hurt her badly up against the cage, then followed her to the ground. It looked like the striking question was about to be answered in the worst possible way. Instead, Mesquita kept her composure, got Mullins into her guard, put up an armbar, and got the tap. The whole comeback happened within a single exchange.


The takeaways cut both ways. Mesquita's guard work and submission instincts are elite, and staying calm in a moment of crisis like that is not something you can teach. But her striking was genuinely exposed tonight, and if she runs into someone with real takedown defense and elite hands at the top of the bantamweight division, she is going to be in trouble. Mullins, for her part, had the fight right there and made the catastrophic game-plan error of taking it to the ground. She had Mesquita hurt standing up. Going down was the one thing she absolutely could not do.

Mitch Raposo vs. Allan Nascimento

Result: Decision (Split) at R3 5:00

Mitch Raposo beat Allan Nascimento by split decision, and somehow that outcome is more frustrating than a clear loss would have been.


Raposo's style is a specific kind of aggravating to watch: go forward, throw combinations that do not actually hurt anyone, circle away, repeat. It is lame, boring point-fighting with no intention of finishing the fight. He did it for three rounds and the judges rewarded him. Round one, he was aggressive and made Nascimento stumble off the cage. He cut Nascimento above the eye with punches, controlled the pace, and took the round clearly.


Nascimento, meanwhile, spent the first two rounds doing the opposite of what he needed to do. He was letting Raposo get close and tag him without any real adversity, trying takedowns that Raposo was able to stuff against the fence, and wasting rounds on a strategy that was not working. Then in round three, knowing he needed a finish, he dropped Raposo with a right hand, swarmed him, landed elbows on the canvas, moved through to half guard and then full guard, and landed ground and pound. He needed to finish that fight and could not close it out. The judges gave it to Raposo.


The aftermath delivered its own absurdity: Raposo called out Brandon Moreno after the fight. That is some genuinely delusional stuff from a man whose style consists entirely of not getting finished. What the hell was Nascimento doing in the first two rounds? He showed in the third that he was the better fighter. The mystery is why he waited that long.

Gaston Bolanos vs. Michael Aswell

Result: Decision (Unanimous) at R3 5:00

Gaston Bolanos beat Michael Aswell by unanimous decision in a fight that did absolutely nothing for anyone who watched it.


Aswell came out of the gate aggressive, took the center, and landed early right hands that suggested this might be a quick night. Bolanos came back with his own combinations, landed a right that stumbled Aswell, and by the end of round one had hurt him against the cage. From there the fight settled into a grind. Bolanos used leg kicks consistently in round two, got a takedown, let Aswell back up, and continued to chip away. Aswell kept trying to get takedowns but Bolanos defended them. Round three was more trading, neither man pulling away, Aswell getting the fight to the ground late but doing nothing with it from inside Bolanos's guard.


The verdict: competitive fight, but it did not do anything. Neither fighter did anything spectacular, neither looked like someone the UFC brass is excited about, and Bolanos pulling off the upset as a serious underdog is genuinely crazy in isolation. Aswell gets one more fight in the promotion. If he loses that, it is probably over.

Levan Chokheli vs. Leon Shahbazyan

Result: TKO (Leg Kicks and Punches) at R1 0:23

Levan Chokheli walked out, threw a calf kick that stunned Leon Shahbazyan, walked forward, and put him down for the count. TKO at 0:23 of round one. It was over before most people had finished their first drink of the night.


There is almost nothing to analyze about the fight itself because it lasted less than half a minute. What there is to say about Shahbazyan is not kind: you cannot get that compromised from a single calf kick in the opening exchange of a UFC fight. Getting dropped and finished that quickly off a leg strike is not a one-fight anomaly, it is a structural problem. He needs to go back to the regional scene and figure out whether he actually belongs at this level.


As for Chokheli, the 23-second debut was spectacular and got everyone's attention, but it tells us almost nothing about how good he actually is. Shahbazyan was not a credible measuring stick. Give him a legitimate opponent before drawing any conclusions about where he fits in the welterweight division.

Early Prelims

Luana Santos vs. Karol Rosa

Result: Decision (Unanimous) at R3 5:00

Luana Santos beat Karol Rosa by unanimous decision in what was, without question, the worst fight on the card. Get rid of the women's bantamweight division.


Round one set the tone immediately. Santos went for a takedown early, they clinched against the fence for two minutes, Santos landed little stomps and knee taps, Rosa replied with taps to the head that accomplished nothing, and they finally separated. Rosa then stood in the center doing nothing. Santos circled. It was excruciating. Santos eventually got a trip takedown and spent the rest of the round in a headlock landing small shots from on top.


Rounds two and three followed the exact same script. Santos on the outside, Rosa in the center, Rosa lands a combination that moves nothing, Santos catches a kick and pulls her into guard or trips her to the ground. Santos lying on top with both hooks in during round three and not even threatening a submission. Just lying there.


This was Santos's second terrible performance in a row. The outcome was correct, but the manner in which it was delivered has to be part of the conversation about what the women's bantamweight division is actually offering at this point. Rosa remains stuck in a loop of beating fighters below her and losing to fighters above her. One more loss at this level and the ranking conversation gets very uncomfortable for her.

Shane Collins vs. Otari Tanzilovi

Result: Decision (Unanimous) at R3 5:00

Shane Collins beat Otari Tanzilovi by unanimous decision in three competitive rounds that showed both what Collins can do and where he still needs work.


Tanzilovi was the more active fighter early, landing calf kicks, a spinning back kick, a nice overhand right, and a knee in the clinch. He got Collins to the canvas in round one and was wearing on him against the fence. Collins was looking more timid than expected and his takedown defense was suspect. Tanzilovi got him down too easily, more than once.


The tide started turning in round two. Collins found his body shots, got the body lock, and started teeing off on Tanzilovi against the cage. Tanzilovi was shelling up and not countering. His cardio was visibly going, and by the middle of the round he was retreating and shooting desperation takedowns that Collins was starting to stuff. Collins nearly had a d'arc choke sunk in at the end of round two before the bell saved Tanzilovi. Round three was more of the same: Tanzilovi started with a decent combination, got walked down, covered up against the cage, and tried takedown after takedown that Collins denied. By the end, Tanzilovi was just backing up and running on empty.


The win is correct and Collins keeps his unbeaten record, but it is worth noting he was on ten days' notice for this fight. His takedown defense needs work, he got taken down way too easily out there. Give him a full training camp and a proper build-up for his next outing and see what the real Collins looks like.
