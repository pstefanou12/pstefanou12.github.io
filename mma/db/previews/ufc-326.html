Overview

UFC 326 is headlined by a BMF title rematch that's been eleven years in the making — Max Holloway and Charles Oliveira, two of the most entertaining fighters the lightweight division has ever seen, finally running it back at the top of the sport. The card is strong from top to bottom, with a middleweight co-main that has genuine title implications, several intriguing prospect debuts on the prelims, and a pair of veteran lightweight brawlers who figure to light up the undercard.

The elephant in the room with this main event is obvious: both Holloway and Oliveira have been stopped in the first round by Ilia Topuria. The BMF belt exists to honor fighters who entertain, but there's something a little awkward about watching two fighters who both got cracked by the same man compete for a trophy that's supposed to represent the baddest man in the sport. That said, if you're going to watch two fan favorites go to war, you could do a lot worse than this matchup — and the grappling dimension of this fight makes it genuinely compelling.

The co-main features two middleweights in freefall trying to arrest their slides: Caio Borralho coming off his first UFC loss and Reinier de Ridder coming off a retirement in the fourth round against Brendan Allen. Both need a statement, and neither can afford to look bad. The prelims have some real prospect energy — Donte Johnson continues his undefeated tear on the first UFC bout to air on CBS, Rafael Tobias makes his debut after a 15-second DWCS knockout, and Alberto Montes brings one of the more specialized submission games you'll see in a UFC debut. Good card.


Main Card Picks

Max Holloway vs. Charles Oliveira

Max Holloway (27-8) enters as the -225 favorite, defending the BMF title for the second time after beating Dustin Poirier in July 2025. Before that, he was knocked out cold by Ilia Topuria in round three. The resume at 155 is solid — Poirier, Gaethje at UFC 300, a couple of featherweight stalwarts — but Holloway's grappling at lightweight has genuinely never been stress-tested. His entire career has been fought in the standup or against wrestlers who couldn't hold him down. There's simply no data on what happens when a world-class submission grappler gets him to the mat.

Charles Oliveira (36-11) enters at +185 carrying the UFC's all-time submission record with 17 finishes on the mat. He lost to Topuria in a controversial first round at UFC 317 — the sequence matters here, because Oliveira went down to the canvas before the finishing shots, and Topuria was already passing his guard and hunting a crucifix. Oliveira chose to stand, and it ended badly. Before that, he submitted Mateusz Gamrot in round two to prove the grappling is still elite. This is a rematch eleven years in the making — they first fought in 2015, with Holloway winning via TKO due to injury. Both are at different peaks now.

The question is simple: can Holloway stay off the mat, or can Oliveira drag him into the chaos where he thrives? Holloway's volume and cardio are the best in the sport. Oliveira's finishing ability in transitions is equally elite. Something has to give.

Pick: Charles Oliveira

Method: Submission

Look at the pattern of Oliveira's losses — every meaningful defeat has come against fighters who hold a serious grappling advantage. Arman Tsarukyan dominated him wrestling. Islam Makhachev controlled him completely on the mat. And Topuria, despite the knockout result, had already passed Oliveira's guard and was hunting a crucifix before Oliveira elected to stand — that was Ilia's grappling edge forcing the standup, and the KO was the consequence. The common thread is clear: when Oliveira doesn't control the ground, he loses. Against Holloway, he controls the ground completely.

Holloway's recent fights — Poirier, Topuria, Gaethje, Volkanovski, Korean Zombie — none of them put him in real grappling danger. His mat skills at 155 are an unknown, and that's a terrible position to be in when you're stepping into an octagon with someone who has 17 UFC submission wins. Critically, Holloway doesn't have Topuria's finishing power — he's not going to crack Oliveira before the grappling threat fully materializes. Oliveira gets this to the ground, and it ends within three rounds.

Caio Borralho vs. Reinier de Ridder

Caio Borralho (17-2) is ranked inside the middleweight top 10 and suffered his first UFC loss to Nassourdine Imavov in Paris in September 2025 — a unanimous decision that snapped a four-fight winning streak and put him back at the drawing board. He enters as a heavy -275 favorite and has been vocal about wanting a statement win to get back into the title picture. The issue is that Borralho's game is built on grinding volume and relentless pressure, not necessarily elite individual skill in any area. In Paris, he looked slow, limited, and unable to impose himself on a bigger, longer fighter.

Reinier de Ridder (21-3) is a long, rangy submission grappler whose most recent outing was genuinely alarming — he retired on his stool in the fourth round against Brendan Allen in October 2025. Before that, though, he had put together four straight finishes, including a split decision over Robert Whittaker and a second-round knee to the body to stop Bo Nickal. The retirement against Allen looked damning on the surface, but reports indicate he dealt with significant health issues in camp. And critically, round one against Allen told a different story: de Ridder got his back, held him in a triangle for most of the round, and dominated completely before his gas tank apparently gave out.

Both men come in off losses and need performances that reset the narrative. The fight that matters is on the mat.

Pick: Reinier de Ridder

Method: Decision

Borralho's grappling is real — he's shown it — but de Ridder is a superior grappler at 185 pounds, and the stylistic matchup here favors the Dutchman. Borralho builds his wins on volume, pressure, and outworking opponents over three rounds. Against a healthy de Ridder who can control the mat, that game plan hits a wall. Borralho will struggle to close distance against someone de Ridder's size, and the exchanges on the ground will go de Ridder's way.

The retirement against Allen deserves scrutiny, not dismissal — but the first round of that fight shows exactly what a functional de Ridder looks like, and it's a problem for Borralho. Caio looked terrible against Imavov, and his wins over Cannonier and Craig, while solid, don't represent elite opposition. If de Ridder is physically right, he spends the majority of this fight controlling the mat and winning grappling exchanges Borralho cannot match. This is de Ridder's fight to lose.

Rob Font vs. Raul Rosas Jr.

Rob Font (22-9) is 38 years old and on the wrong side of the roster bubble. He dropped a decision to rising prospect David Martinez in September 2025, another loss in a record that has been heading in the wrong direction since the Cory Sandhagen fight in 2023. Font is a polished boxer with genuine UFC experience and a solid jab — but his grappling has been exposed at this level in a way that is hard to unsee. This fight was originally scheduled for September 2025 before Rosas Jr. withdrew.

Raul Rosas Jr. (11-1) signed with the UFC at 17, making him the youngest fighter in promotion history. Now 21, he's on a four-fight winning streak and pushing for his first official bantamweight ranking. He's framed this as a generational statement — new blood retiring old guard — and a win here would put him firmly on the map in a deep 135-pound division.

The matchup structure couldn't be clearer: Font wants distance and the jab; Rosas Jr. wants the takedown and the scramble. One of these game plans is far more likely to be successful inside a UFC octagon.

Pick: Raul Rosas Jr.

Method: Decision

Think back to the Sandhagen fight. Cody Sandhagen is not primarily a wrestler — he's a striker with unorthodox movement — and he mopped the floor with Font on the mat while dealing with an injury at the same time. Font's defensive grappling is genuinely poor, and it's been documented. Rosas Jr. doesn't need to be a submission specialist to exploit it — he just needs to get the fight to the ground, and then the blueprint writes itself.

Rosas Jr. takes Font down repeatedly, employs a lay-and-pray approach where needed, and grinds out a decision. The UFC knows exactly what it's doing booking this matchup — if Font's grappling gets exposed again, Rosas Jr. gets a highlight-reel win over a name; if Font pulls off the upset, a veteran gets a career resurgence. Either way, the structure of the matchup heavily favors the younger man. Font will have his moments on the feet. It won't be enough.

Drew Dober vs. Michael Johnson

Drew Dober (28-15) is 37 years old and was on a three-fight losing streak before stopping Kyle Prepolec at UFC Vancouver in October 2025 — a win that came with a Fight of the Night bonus and reportedly saved his roster spot. But the circumstances matter: Dober was being outworked before a low blow stopped the action, and he capitalized when play resumed. The previous three losses included a doctor's stoppage, a brutal first-round KO, and a loss to Moicano. He enters near even money at +100.

Michael Johnson (24-19), "The Menace," is on a three-fight winning streak at 39 years old — his best run in years. He knocked out Ottman Azaitar in round two and followed it with a unanimous decision over Daniel Zellhuber. He enters as the slight favorite at -120, and it's a reasonable position given his recent form. Two longtime veterans who have somehow never shared an octagon despite careers running in parallel for over a decade. Both are best standing, both have KO power, and neither wants to wrestle. This is going to be a brawl.

Pick: Michael Johnson

Method: KO/TKO

It's over for Drew Dober. Before Prepolec, he was on a three-fight losing skid where the last two came by finish — a doctor's stoppage followed by a first-round KO. He's more chinny than he used to be, and the Prepolec win didn't erase that reality. He was losing that fight before the accidental foul changed everything.

Johnson, on the other hand, looks revitalized. He's sharp, he's connecting, and his timing is that of a veteran who knows exactly how to pace a fight like this. Both men walk to the center of the octagon and start throwing. Johnson has the sharper timing right now and the more convincing recent form. He connects first, and that's how it ends.

Gregory Rodrigues vs. Brunno Ferreira

Gregory Rodrigues (18-6), ranked #13 in the middleweight division, enters as the -190 favorite with a clear motivation: avenge the first-round knockout Ferreira handed him in his UFC debut back in January 2023. Since then, Rodrigues has gone 5-1, putting together back-to-back wins over Jack Hermansson and Roman Kopylov to push back toward relevance in the 185-pound division. He was originally booked to fight Brad Tavares in that debut — Ferreira stepped in on short notice and knocked him out. Now the UFC has served up the rematch.

Brunno Ferreira (15-2) has won three straight, including a unanimous decision over Marvin Vettori in December 2025. He's been teasing a "new card" in his game ahead of this rematch — his submission grappling has been on full display in recent outings, and he holds the psychological edge of already finishing this specific opponent. The story of Rodrigues is consistent and readable: big, aggressive striker who surges forward from the opening bell with power and pressure. He either overwhelms you early or gets picked apart in later rounds. Ferreira has already seen this movie and knows how it ends.

Pick: Brunno Ferreira

Method: KO/TKO

Rodrigues is a one-note fighter — aggressive, powerful, and limited. He overwhelms the unprepared and gets exposed against anyone who can handle his pressure and counter it. Ferreira already proved he can do exactly that. The first-round knockout in their debut wasn't a fluke; it was what happens when a patient counter-striker times an aggressive brawler who telegraphs his attacks.

Ferreira enters this rematch with three more fights of experience, a submission game that's been visibly added to his arsenal, and the confidence of someone who has already finished this specific opponent. Rodrigues will do what he always does — come out hard, put the pressure on, throw big. Ferreira will time him, and the same story plays out. Different night, same result.

Prelims Picks

Cody Garbrandt vs. Long Xiao

Cody Garbrandt (14-7) was once the most electric fighter in the bantamweight division — his knockout of Dominick Cruz for the title remains one of the most complete performances in UFC history. Now 34, he's gone 3-7 in his last 10 fights, and his recent losses have been to grapplers who were able to neutralize his output and drag him into the later rounds. He enters as the +120 underdog, which is a bizarre position for a former world champion. His most recent skid: submitted by Figueiredo in round two, outworked by Barcelos on the feet. The chin has been a liability for years.

Long Xiao (27-10) is a 27-year-old Chinese bantamweight who came through Road to UFC and holds a 1-2 octagon record. He's alternating wins and losses, and his competition has not been notable. He enters as the -145 favorite based largely on his octagon record and youth. This is a crossroads fight for Garbrandt's UFC future.

Pick: Cody Garbrandt

Method: KO/TKO

Garbrandt's losses tell a consistent story: they come against wrestlers and grapplers who can neutralize his offense and outwork him over rounds. Long Xiao is not that. He's a generalist with no clear specialty — an all-around fighter without a standout weapon — and that style plays directly into what Garbrandt still does well. The one-punch power hasn't diminished, and without a genuine takedown threat to account for, Garbrandt can operate the way he's best suited to operate.

Long Xiao has not fought anyone close to Garbrandt's level of competition in his career. The UFC's intent here is obvious: if Garbrandt loses, you start building an Asian star off the win; if Garbrandt wins, a fan favorite gets back on track. The structure of the matchup favors the former champion. This ends early with Garbrandt landing something clean and the referee waving it off.

Donte Johnson vs. Cody Brundage

Donte Johnson (7-0) earned his UFC contract at DWCS in August 2025 and immediately earned a Performance of the Night bonus in his promotional debut. "Lockjaw" is 25, undefeated, and finishes everyone he fights — five first-round stoppages in his last five outings. He's the -650 favorite and fighting on what is reportedly the first UFC bout to air on CBS under the new Paramount/Skydance media rights deal. This is a showcase, not a competitive matchup.

Cody Brundage (11-8-1) is on a two-fight losing streak, most recently stopped by Cam Rowston at UFC 325 in January 2026. He holds a 5-7-1 UFC record, his roster spot is hanging by a thread, and he enters as a +475 underdog. Brundage has shown throughout his UFC tenure that he will quit under adversity. That's a fatal character trait when you're standing across from a finishing specialist on his way up.

Pick: Donte Johnson

Method: KO/TKO

Brundage is not a fighter — he quits time and time again inside the octagon. He doesn't have the chin or the will to withstand pressure from a legitimate finishing prospect. His most recent loss came against Cam Rowston, and the fact that Rowston is considered a step up in competition tells you everything about where Brundage sits in this division.

Johnson has the tools to finish people both on the feet and on the mat. Brundage is fighting to keep his job; Johnson is fighting to introduce himself to a national audience on the biggest stage of his career so far. The outcome is not in doubt. Johnson gets the finish well before the final bell, and this is almost certainly Brundage's last fight in the UFC.

Alberto Montes vs. Ricky Turcios

Alberto Montes (10-1) is one of the more specialized submission hunters to enter the UFC in recent memory. Seven of his ten career wins have come by submission, three specifically by anaconda choke — a technique he's refined to the point of being genuinely dangerous every time he touches the mat. He earned his UFC deal at DWCS 2024 with a second-round anaconda choke and makes his promotional debut here. Venezuelan-born and Miami-based, he represents a specific kind of prospect: not a well-rounded threat, but elite in his lane.

Ricky Turcios (12-5) is moving up from bantamweight to featherweight for this bout and coming off consecutive losses. The most damaging of them: submitted by Raul Rosas Jr. via rear naked choke in round two. Before that, he lost a decision to Aiemann Zahabi. His roster spot is genuinely in question — another loss while moving up in weight is a difficult argument for the UFC matchmakers to overlook.

Pick: Alberto Montes

Method: Submission

Rosas Jr. is not a good fighter. A loss to him by submission is not a ringing endorsement of anyone's grappling defense — and Turcios has that loss on his record. Before that, he lost to Aiemann Zahabi, a bantamweight who has been clinging to the UFC roster himself for years. The level of opposition Turcios has been beaten by doesn't inspire confidence heading into a debut fight with an anaconda choke specialist.

Montes has one weapon he's extremely good at, and Turcios has demonstrated he cannot handle that weapon. The matchup writes itself. Second round, anaconda choke.

Cody Durden vs. N. Tumendemberel

Cody Durden (17-9-1) has been in the UFC since 2020 and the results have been a mixed bag throughout — flashes of brilliance buried under too many losses. He's on a three-fight losing streak, with his last two finishes against him coming on the mat. He's spent this camp training with Japanese flyweight legend Kyoji Horiguchi, which he says has transformed his defensive grappling. Claims and results are different things. He enters as the +124 underdog.

Nyamjargal Tumendemberel (9-1) is a 26-year-old Mongolian who submitted Terrance Saeteurn on Road to UFC in August 2025 to earn his official UFC contract. His primary finishing tool is submissions, and his alignment with Durden's documented weakness on the mat is not subtle. He's the -148 favorite and looking for his first official UFC victory after signing his contract.

Pick: N. Tumendemberel

Method: Submission

Three-fight losing streaks in the UFC are usually diagnostic. Durden isn't the same fighter he was when he first arrived, and his grappling defense has been the hole that everyone keeps shooting at. The Horiguchi camp story is compelling on paper, but until Durden actually demonstrates improved mat defense against a live opponent under pressure, you cannot give him credit for it in advance.

Tumendemberel is built to exploit exactly the weakness Durden keeps showing. He gets the fight to the ground, and it ends in the second round. Durden's roster future is in serious jeopardy after this one.

Sumudaerji vs. Jesus Aguilar

Sumudaerji (18-7) is the first Tibetan fighter in UFC history, originally from Ngawa in Sichuan Province, and a genuine fan favorite. He's a chaos merchant — 72% of his career wins come by knockout, and he trains at Team Alpha Male in Sacramento. He's on a two-fight winning streak, but the wins haven't come against notable opposition, and his losses on the mat expose a real structural flaw: Tim Elliott and Matt Schnell both submitted him when they could drag the fight to the ground. He's the betting favorite coming in.

Jesus Aguilar (12-3) is a Mexican flyweight from Ensenada who earned his UFC deal at DWCS 2022. He bounced back from a loss to Rafael Estevam with a decision over Luis Gurule in September 2025, showing enough to stay on the roster. He enters as the +190 underdog, but he brings a functional ground game and guillotine threat that map directly onto Sumudaerji's biggest weakness.

Pick: Jesus Aguilar

Method: Decision

Sumudaerji gets lost in the chaos he creates. He goes out and brawls, he tries to land the big shot, and when it lands it's exciting — but lately the competition hasn't been distinguished enough to really validate those wins. His most recent victories came at the hands of fighters who aren't exactly household names, and he barely got the decisions.

Aguilar is not going to get sucked into a street fight. He'll stay composed, work to close the distance, and drag this to the mat where Sumudaerji has consistently struggled. Aguilar controls from there and takes a quiet decision. The chaos merchant gets neutralized.

Rafael Tobias vs. Diyar Nurgozhay

Rafael Tobias (14-1) is a 21-year-old Brazilian making his UFC debut after knocking out Rafael Pergentino in 15 seconds at DWCS Season 9. "Bipolar" trains at Chute Boxe Monstro and is on a six-fight finishing streak — 43% submission wins, 36% TKO/KO — making him a versatile finisher who can end it on the feet or on the mat. UFC named him a "Fighter on the Rise" for this card, and he enters as the underdog despite most analysts backing him heavily.

Diyar Nurgozhay (10-2) arrived in the UFC with significant expectations — 10-0 before signing, serious prospect pedigree from Kazakhstan — and has been submitted in both of his UFC appearances. Two fights, two submissions. That is a damning pattern, and it speaks directly to a structural weakness in his game against this level of competition.

Pick: Rafael Tobias

Method: Submission

Nurgozhay was supposed to be something, and he just isn't. Two submissions in two UFC fights against opponents he was expected to handle tells you everything you need to know about how his grappling holds up when things get difficult. There's also the eye-touching tic throughout his fights — a minor tell, but symbolic of a fighter who gets rattled under pressure and loses his composure.

Tobias is built to exploit exactly that hole. He finishes people with submissions and has the tools to put Nurgozhay in bad positions quickly. He gets it done in the first or second round, announces himself to the light heavyweight division with a statement debut, and Nurgozhay's future on the roster becomes a genuine question.

Gaston Bolaños vs. Jeong Yeong Lee

Gaston Bolaños (8-5) is Peruvian-born and Dublin-based, moving up from bantamweight to featherweight for this bout. He's 2-2 in the UFC, none of his wins are particularly impressive, and his losses have come by finish against opponents who are no longer on the roster. He enters as the +215 underdog — the right number for where he actually sits in this division.

Jeong Yeong Lee (11-3) is a former Road Fighting Championship featherweight champion on a two-fight losing skid inside the UFC — stopped by Hyder Amil in round one and then outpointed by Daniel Santos. Despite the losing streak, he's the natural featherweight with the experience, size, and pedigree advantage over a man moving up in weight. The original opponent for this bout withdrew due to injury; Lee stepped in.

Pick: Jeong Yeong Lee

Method: Decision

Honestly, neither of these fighters should probably still be in the UFC. But if you have to pick, Lee has the better competitive ledger. He beat Yizha — an actual striker with real technical ability — which represents the best win either of these men can point to. Bolaños' UFC victories came against fighters who are no longer on the roster, and his finishes happened to him, not for him.

Lee pulls out an uninspiring decision and survives another day. This is not a fight anyone will be talking about the next morning.

Luke Fernandez vs. Rodolfo Bellato

Luke Fernandez (6-0) is a 29-year-old from Lacey Township, New Jersey who knocked out Rafael Pergentino in 15 seconds at DWCS to earn his UFC contract. He held the CFFC Light Heavyweight Championship and trains at Dante Rivera BJJ. His finishing rate is 83% TKO/KO, and the UFC named him a "Fighter on the Rise" for this card. He enters as the +130 underdog despite being widely backed by analysts — the market hasn't caught up with the hype yet.

Rodolfo Bellato (12-3-1), ranked #23 in the division, enters as the -150 favorite on the strength of his UFC experience alone. But his last meaningful stretch has been 0-1-1 — the majority draw against Jimmy Crute and the decision loss to Navajo Stirling in September 2025. Before that, the Paul Craig fight ended in an accidental illegal upkick controversy that drained whatever momentum he had. He's ranked but fighting like someone who doesn't fully believe in himself anymore.

Pick: Luke Fernandez

Method: KO/TKO

Bellato is not the fighter he looked like when he first showed up. The Paul Craig controversy drained his momentum, the Stirling loss compounded it, and he's coming in with a winless recent skid against opponents he should have handled. He's ranked on reputation, not current form.

Fernandez is the opposite — a young finisher full of confidence, with stopping power and the kind of debut energy that translates into early aggression and clean execution. Against a Bellato who is passive and not imposing his will, Fernandez's power is the deciding factor. The finish comes early, probably in round one, and a new name announces itself in the light heavyweight division.
