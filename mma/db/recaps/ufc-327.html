Summary

UFC 327 delivered a genuinely entertaining card that will be remembered mostly for Josh Hokit flipping off Curtis Blaydes on a UFC main card and a light heavyweight title fight that lasted under four minutes — and not in the way anyone expected.


The night belonged to Carlos Ulberg, who became the new UFC Light Heavyweight Champion with a brutally efficient left check hook that put Jiří Procházka face-down on the canvas in round one. If you blinked, you missed it. And yet somehow the most talked-about part of the finish was not how clinical Ulberg was — it was how utterly preventable Procházka's knockout was. Jiri walked straight forward, hands at his sides, chin in the air, directly into a kickboxer. Again. The defenses are already coming in from his fans — that he showed mercy, that he had Ulberg hurt before the finish. Maybe. But Ulberg tore his ACL during the fight and still won the championship. Walking straight forward into a check hook with your hands down is not mercy — it's a habit. Pereira punished it twice. Ulberg punished it once. The uncomfortable truth is that Procházka's success was built on a weak era in the light heavyweight division, and every technically sound kickboxer he faces exposes the same holes. Ulberg is a dog. That's a champion.


The co-main event produced the best narrative of the night. Paulo Costa vindicated his move to light heavyweight in emphatic fashion, dropping Azamat Murzakanov with a head kick in round three and watching him go down and cower up on the canvas. Costa looked like a different fighter at 205 — not depleted, not slow, genuinely dangerous from the opening bell. Murzakanov, meanwhile, confirmed what his résumé had always suggested: he is a dangerous power puncher with elite forward pressure and very little else. He can't grapple, he can't circle, and when the pressure came back his way late in the fight, he went down and stayed there. Costa may have walked himself directly into a light heavyweight title fight.


The real show-stealer was Josh Hokit, who rearranged Curtis Blaydes's face across three rounds while also finding time to flip him off in between exchanges. Hokit has hands and he has speed, but his defense is non-existent; hands at his sides at all times. He got away with it against Blaydes. Against Tom Aspinall, that's a one-round disaster. Still, the kid earned his two performance bonuses and a White House card slot against Derrick Lewis, and the question of whether "The Incredible Hok" is legitimate now has a real answer: probably yes, with caveats.


The rest of the card had its moments. Cub Swanson went out the right way — a first-round KO of Nate Landwehr, a straight right that ended his retirement fight cleanly and sent him into the sunset on his own terms. Aaron Pico completely styled on Patricio Pitbull for the final two rounds and may actually be living up to the hype that surrounded him years ago in Bellator. The biggest upset of the night came when Vicente Luque sank a d'arce choke on Kelvin Gastelum in round one — after Gastelum had spent the entire round winning everywhere else on the feet. And the promotion added a touch of chaos by announcing Chris Padilla as the winner of his fight with MarQuel Mederos in the octagon, then quietly correcting the result to a majority draw about an hour later. That's MMA.


One note of frustration: the broadcast ran an AI-generated promo for the upcoming White House card featuring the main event fighters walking around Washington D.C., and it looked like exactly what it was — cheap, rushed, and mailed in. If you're spending sixty million on an event, spend the twenty grand to film an actual promo. It matters.


Final Score: 7.4/10



Main Card

Carlos Ulberg vs. Jiří Procházka

Result: KO/TKO, Left Hook to Ground Punches at 3:45 Round 1 of 5

Carlos Ulberg became the new UFC Light Heavyweight Champion in under four minutes, and the manner of victory said everything about both men. Somewhere early in the fight, Ulberg tore his ACL — and then kept going anyway.
Jiri walked straight forward. Hands down. Chin up. Directly into a left check hook that put him face-first on the canvas. Ulberg followed with ground punches and the referee waved it off at 3:45 of the first round.
The excuse brigade mobilized immediately. Jiri had Ulberg hurt before the finish. Jiri showed mercy to an injured opponent. Maybe some of that is true. But here's the thing about the "mercy" argument: if Jiri had done the same thing against a healthy Ulberg, the same check hook lands the same way. Walking straight forward with your hands at your sides into a technical kickboxer is not a style — it's a habit. Pereira punished it twice. Ulberg punished it once. The light heavyweight division is not that deep, and Procházka caught it at the right time. The chapter appears to be closing.
Ulberg deserves every bit of credit. He tore his ACL mid-fight and still won a UFC title. People forget that when Tom Aspinall tore his ACL against Blaydes, he went down and stayed down in the middle of the octagon. Ulberg fought through it and cracked the man with a clean left hook. That's a champion.
As for what comes next — the title picture is genuinely murky. Ulberg will be out for the foreseeable future rehabbing a torn ACL. Procházka is now coming off a loss. Ankalaev is coming off a loss. Paulo Costa just had his first fight at light heavyweight. There are no clean contenders. The most logical path is to match up four names — Jiri, Ankalaev, Costa, and Reyes — let them sort themselves out, and then feed the winners to Ulberg whenever he's back. If that takes long enough, an interim title fight is not out of the question.

Paulo Costa vs. Azamat Murzakanov

Result: KO/TKO, Head Kick at 1:23 Round 3 of 3, 11:23 Total

Paulo Costa made a statement in his light heavyweight debut, dropping Azamat Murzakanov with a head kick in round three and watching him fold on the canvas. The result wasn't even the most interesting part — it was how Costa looked getting there.
He spent the first round working body and leg kicks, moving well, staying off the fence. Murzakanov walked him down and landed his shots, but Costa was sharper and faster than anyone at 185 had ever seen him. In round two, Murzakanov started to connect more consistently and Costa began to slow, but Costa controlled the grappling exchanges and reversed a takedown attempt to hold Murzakanov against the fence. By round three, something had shifted — Murzakanov was the one fading. Costa came out aggressive, landed another head kick that buckled him, and when Murzakanov stumbled, went down, and curled up on the canvas, it was over.
The caucus region glazers who had been talking up Murzakanov off the strength of a win over a 2025 Aleksandar Rakić got the answer they deserved. Murzakanov is a one-dimensional fighter — forward pressure, big hands, and nothing else. He can't grapple, can't circle, and when the tide turned late, he went down and cowered up. Costa exposed every limitation in the book. The bigger story is what this win means for "Borrachinha" — he may have just walked into a light heavyweight title shot. With a depleted contender pool at 205, Costa's combination of power, frame, and now a statement finish makes him the most compelling option available.

Josh Hokit vs. Curtis Blaydes

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

This was supposed to be a fraud check. It was not a fraud check.
Josh Hokit fought Curtis Blaydes for three rounds, rearranged his face at least twice, flipped him off multiple times in between exchanges, and walked away with a unanimous decision and two performance bonuses. The circus act turned out to be a real fighter.
Blaydes came out doing what Blaydes does — wrestling, clinch control, wearing on his opponent — and Hokit shucked him off the first takedown attempt and immediately cracked him with a right hand that nearly put the veteran down. Blaydes's face was a mess by the end of round one. In round two, Hokit kept denying takedowns and kept landing, while Blaydes fought back and had his moments in the Thai clinch. By round three they were both just slugging until the final horn.
The real question coming into this fight was whether Hokit had the boxing to compete at this level, and the answer is yes — with an asterisk. His hands are perpetually at his sides, his defense is essentially non-existent, and he gets hit way too clean way too often. Against Blaydes, that was survivable. Against Tom Aspinall, those same habits end the night in round one. Hokit also played into the character a little too much — there were multiple moments where a finish was there, and instead of pulling the trigger he was busy showing Blaydes his middle finger. He won't get away with that at the top of the division.
Still, Hokit is right there. He's earned his spot. He's already booked to face Derrick Lewis on the White House card, which is a showcase fight for his antics and his skills, and if he wins that one cleanly, a title shot conversation becomes real. As for Blaydes — he's fought two unranked opponents in a row and deserves better matchmaking after this. Give him someone with a ranking attached to their name.

Dominick Reyes vs. Johnny Walker

Result: Decision, Split at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Thirty minutes of this card are gone and they cannot be returned.
Dominick Reyes and Johnny Walker spent three rounds doing their absolute best impression of two fighters who were terrified of each other. The first round was a cautious feeling-out period. The second round was a cautious feeling-out period. The third round was a cautious feeling-out period with slightly more leg kicks.
Both men clearly understood that they could not afford to lose this fight, and so neither of them really tried to win it. Walker circled the outside while Reyes stayed mostly stationary in the center, occasionally throwing head kicks that didn't land. Reyes surged forward and stung Walker in the second, which was as close to excitement as this fight got. By round three they were trading calf kicks and body kicks while both men looked at the clock.
Reyes got the split decision. It's hard to be too mad about the result specifically — he probably won the last two rounds — but the fight itself was one of the most boring in recent memory. These are two heavy-handed light heavyweights who both hit hard enough to end a fight in a second, and neither of them was willing to risk it. Don't run this back. Never run this back.

Cub Swanson vs. Nate Landwehr

Result: KO/TKO, Overhand Right and Right Hook at 4:06 Round 1 of 3

Cub Swanson picked the perfect night to have the best performance for his retirement fight. He walked out, pieced up Nate Landwehr against the cage, stumbled him twice, and finished him with a straight right in round one at 4:06. Clean, clinical, and final.
Swanson controlled the entire fight from start to finish. Landwehr, who was supposed to be the aggressive pressure fighter who'd make this ugly, spent most of the round getting backed up and picked apart. Swanson stumbled him in the center, followed him down briefly, let him back up, and then closed the show with a right hand that put Landwehr down for good. Landwehr tried to wrestle the referee on his way to the canvas, which was a fitting ending.
As retirement fights go, this was about as good as it gets. Swanson went out on the biggest stage of his late career, in Miami, on a numbered card, with a first-round KO finish. A Hall of Famer going out on his own terms and looking every bit the legend doing it. As for Landwehr — he's garbage, and the promotion should cut him. Get rid of him.

Prelims

Aaron Pico vs. Patricio Pitbull

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Aaron Pico completely styled on Patricio Pitbull for three rounds and may have finally delivered on the hype that surrounded him since his Bellator days.
The first round was competitive — Pitbull was active with jabs and a low kick that legitimately stumbled Pico early. Pico got a takedown and gave it back, and they traded shots with Pitbull landing a right that opened Pico's nose. Then round two started, and Pico found his groove. He started landing the 1-2 consistently, and once he got going he was nearly impossible to stop. A big uppercut hurt Pitbull badly, a follow-up counter sent him down, and by the end of the round Pico had taken over completely — dancing around the octagon, landing at will, and finishing the round with ground and pound that had Pitbull in survival mode.
Round three was essentially an exhibition. Pico was playing with his food — circling, touching Pitbull up at will, taking him down and dragging him back whenever Pitbull tried to stand. Pitbull threw a pathetic leg kick at one point in pure desperation, and his face told the whole story. Pico took the unanimous decision going away.
The hype might be real. Pico lost his first fight badly and his Bellator career never lived up to the billing, but what he showed in rounds two and three of this fight was something different. He's fast, he's technical, and he has real finishing power. Pitbull's UFC career is effectively over — he's 38 years old and just got styled on for two rounds, and it's not fun watching older fighters get put through that by young gunslingers. The next fight for Pico should be Yair Rodriguez; Yair struggled against Pitbull, and Pico just completely dismantled him.

Kevin Holland vs. Randy Brown

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Kevin Holland actually had a gameplan for once. He came out investing in low kicks, targeting Randy Brown's legs early, and it was working — Brown had a visible swell on his left calf by the end of round one. Holland also got a takedown, threatened a guillotine, and took an up kick on the way up. It was the most structured Holland has looked in years.
And then round two happened, and Brown just stopped doing anything. Holland kept spamming the leg kicks and Brown kept walking into them, barely throwing back. Holland landed two spinning elbows, threatened a guillotine again, and by the end of the round Brown was visibly exhausted and looking at the clock. A man who was supposed to be in prime fighting years had completely shut down after one round.
Round three was strange. Brown came forward but couldn't connect, while Holland fought off the back foot and stalled against the fence. There was something wrong with Brown's right hand — he never threw it. They clinched, reversed, and clung to each other until the final horn. Holland took the unanimous decision.
Sloppy fight. A prelim slot on a numbered event is about right for Holland at this stage of his career — he's not going near the title anytime soon, and neither is Brown. The loss to Gabriel Bonfim looks like it may have been the nail in the coffin for Brown's ceiling. He was main eventing a UFC event against someone with real championship aspirations, and now he's fighting Kevin Holland. The trajectory is not encouraging for a 35-year-old welterweight. At least they're both entertaining enough that people will keep watching.

Mateusz Gamrot vs. Esteban Ribovics

Result: Submission, Arm Triangle Choke at 4:19 Round 2 of 3, 9:19 Total

Who would have thought that Mateusz Gamrot would be collecting a performance bonus in 2026?
Mateusz Gamrot absolutely dominated Esteban Ribovics from start to finish — taking him down in round one, working through a crucifix for ground and pound, and spending the better part of both rounds controlling position on the mat. In round two, after denying another takedown attempt on the feet, Gamrot dragged Ribovics back to the ground, secured the back, transitioned to side control, locked up an arm triangle choke, and got the tap at 4:19.
Gamrot is a problem for anyone at lightweight without elite grappling. Ribovics never had a response for the wrestling, which was entirely predictable given that he went to war with Daniel Zellhuber and didn't look like he had any grappling to speak of. The matchmaking here was a mystery — if the goal was to build Ribovics up, there are easier ways to do it than feeding him to an elite wrestler. Gamrot is ranked seventh at lightweight and has now fought two unranked opponents in two of his last three outings, including a short-notice fight. At some point you have to give this guy a ranked opponent. Put him back in the conversation.

Tatiana Suarez vs. Lupita Godinez

Result: Submission, Rear Naked Choke at 2:29 Round 2 of 3, 7:29 Total

Tatiana Suarez had a wobble in round one and then proceeded to do what Tatiana Suarez does to everyone.
Godinez actually hurt her early — a right hand dropped Suarez and Godinez followed her down to land ground and pound from the back. Suarez survived, got back to her feet, and that was more or less the last time Godinez had anything going her way. Suarez got the fight against the cage, took her down, and spent the rest of round one in top position grinding away while Godinez held her wrist and waited for it to be over.
Round two was cleaner. Suarez dragged her to the ground again, took the back, got both hooks in, worked her arm under Godinez's chin, and got the rear naked choke tap at 2:29.
Suarez is now on a two-fight win streak after her loss to Weili Zhang and looks as sharp as ever. The question for the strawweight division is simple: if Zhang Weili comes back, she runs through everyone. If she doesn't, Suarez runs through everyone. Either way the division is going to look lopsided. As for Godinez — her last three losses have come against Dern, Jandiroba, and now Suarez, all grapplers. It's hard to imagine she thought this fight was a good idea. She should find a striker.

MarQuel Mederos vs. Chris Padilla

Result: Ends in a Draw, Majority at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

A majority draw is about as fitting an ending as you could write for a fight this chaotic — even if nobody knew it was a draw for the first hour.
Chris Padilla and MarQuel Mederos spent three rounds clinching against the fence, eye poking each other, exchanging elbows in tight, and accumulating enough point deductions to muddy any scorecard. Padilla was announced as the winner in the octagon. Then, about an hour later, the broadcast quietly corrected the result — the fight was actually a majority draw. Whether it was a Bruce Buffer blunder or someone failing to properly account for the point deductions on the scorecards remains unclear, but it was an embarrassing administrative moment for the promotion.
Padilla was the busier and more consistent fighter overall — higher output, constant teep kicks, always pressing — but Mederos had his moments, particularly with elbows in the clinch, and the point deduction Mederos received in round three for an eye poke didn't help his case. The fight had genuine entertainment value despite being messy. Both guys went the full fifteen swinging with bad intentions, and the late rounds especially felt like two men who knew the fight was close and weren't willing to leave it to the judges.
The draw stings for Padilla especially given that he missed weight and already took a financial hit. He looked good out there, but this result gives him nothing to build on officially. Neither fighter loses stock here — it was an entertaining scrap with a weird ending. Get Padilla a fight up the rankings against someone just inside or just outside the top fifteen.

Vicente Luque vs. Kelvin Gastelum

Result: Submission, D'arce Choke at 4:08 Round 1 of 3

Kelvin Gastelum was winning this fight everywhere it went — and then Vicente Luque caught him with an uppercut and sank a d'arce choke, and somehow Gastelum was tapping in round one.
Gastelum came out aggressive, backed Luque into the fence, put together combinations, and took the fight to the ground at one point before they reset. He was piecing Luque up on the feet and winning comfortably. Then Luque landed an uppercut that stumbled Gastelum and sent him to the canvas, and Gastelum went from winning the fight to surviving it in about two seconds. Luque followed him down, and even as Gastelum defended the ground and pound, Luque snaked in the d'arce choke and got the tap at 4:08.
Crazy turn of events. This was reportedly the first finish of Gastelum's career, which says something about how durable the man is after all these years. It's also a little insane that both of these guys are only 34 — they carry the energy of fighters who should be in their mid-forties. Neither of them is getting a title shot or a high-profile matchup anytime soon, but they're good enough to keep their roster spots. Luque's anaconda and d'arce are always live, even when he's losing the fight. That's the thing about submission specialists — you only need one opening.

Charles Radtke vs. Francisco Prado

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Charles Radtke and Francisco Prado put on a surprisingly scrappy opener that had more going on than the names suggested.
Prado actually looked decent early — landed a left hook that stung Radtke right out of the gate and applied pressure along the fence in round one. But Radtke answered with a takedown, took the back, and spent the rest of the round grinding away with a body triangle. Prado showed some scrambling ability and even threatened a leg lock at one point, but Radtke stayed composed and kept recovering position. In round two, Radtke got the back again in the center of the octagon, squeezed with the body triangle, and was landing body shots while Prado just tried to survive. Prado managed a reversal late and landed elbows from on top, and the canvas got painted red when Radtke's cut opened up from a shot off the bottom.
Round three had an eye poke from Prado — Marc Goddard took a point — and Radtke was threatening a rear naked choke at various points before Prado reversed late. Radtke took the unanimous decision on the cards.
The moral of Francisco Prado's UFC career has been consistent throughout: he doesn't look particularly bad, he just can't put it all together and looks small for the division. He's got four straight losses now and should be cut. He's 23, though, and a return to the regional scene to rebuild some confidence isn't the worst thing. Radtke is serviceable but what's next for him is unclear — maybe the winner of Holland vs. Brown, given he already lost to Malott.
