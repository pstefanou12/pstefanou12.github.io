Summary

There have been cards this year where the individual performances were probably better on paper, but UFC 328 delivered something different: it had an aura. From the security guards in the cage before the main event to the bad blood that had been building for months, this felt like a moment that mattered beyond just the sport.


Sean Strickland dethroned Khamzat Chimaev by split decision in a fight that went exactly the way the blueprint said it should. Survive round one, survive the grappling, make Chimaev work, and break him in the championship rounds. Chimaev had no answer for a bigger, stronger, more durable man who simply refused to fold. The boys won. And for anyone online crying robbery, go touch grass.


The co-main delivered its own epic. Joshua Van survived a first round of Taira lay-and-pray, got dropped at points, had Taira on his back and nearly finished, and then turned the entire fight on its head with a single punch in round two. What followed was five rounds of absolute carnage: Van walking Taira down, Taira refusing to die, both men's faces covered in blood by the end. The TKO stoppage in round five was early, but Taira's face was a mess. Van is no longer a paper champion. He's the real thing.


Sean Brady gave Joaquin Buckley the most comprehensive grappling clinic of the year. 81 to 7 in the striking differential says everything you need to know. Buckley's guard was non-existent and Brady had his way on the ground for fifteen minutes. Alexander Volkov got a 30-27 scorecard against Waldo Cortes-Acosta that at least two judges ought to be ashamed of. WCA clearly took round three and may have done enough in the others. The heavyweight division is backed up and the judging didn't help.


On the prelims, Yaroslav Amosov announced himself with a nasty arm triangle finish of Joel Alvarez and looks like the real deal at 170. Grant Dawson picked up a bounce-back submission win over Mateusz Rebecki, though Rebecki gifted him the finish by going for an ill-advised takedown in the third when he was up on the feet. Jim Miller, 42 years old, submitted Jared Gordon with a guillotine in round one. Pathetic from Gordon. Remarkable from Miller.


Final Score: 8.1/10



Main Card

Sean Strickland vs. Khamzat Chimaev

Result: Decision, Split at 5 Rounds, 25:00 Total

Khamzat Chimaev came out body kicking early and went straight to business, getting the takedown within seconds and plastering Sean Strickland against the fence with both hooks in. He rode Strickland's back for most of round one, hunting the rear naked choke while Strickland battled the hands. Strickland scrambled, reversed, fought into mount, and simply refused to fold. He survived.


Round two was the turning point. Chimaev, already asking questions about his own cardio, stopped shooting and tried to box. Strickland answered with clean jabs and right hands, then sprawled a takedown attempt so cleanly that Chimaev resigned himself to his back. Strickland smothered him with ground and pound. When Chimaev shot again, Strickland sprawled a second time and did it all over again. The blueprint was being drawn in real time.


Round three was a jab fest, and Chimaev looked cooked. He plodded forward, eating Strickland's shots, while Strickland moved on the backfoot and picked him apart. Both men's faces were starting to show the damage. Round four gave Chimaev some life: he gained confidence on the feet, incorporated body kicks, and managed a late takedown that kept Strickland pinned for the final minute. It was enough to steal the round. But round five was Strickland's: a takedown attempt stuffed early, Chimaev visibly tired, and Strickland piecing him up on the feet through the final bell.


Strickland took rounds two, three, and five on the split decision. Chimaev had no cardio for a five-round war against a bigger, stronger, more durable man. The blueprint was simple: survive round one, make him work, break him in the championship rounds. Strickland executed it perfectly. He is now a two-time middleweight champion, and there is a real argument that he is one of the greatest middleweights of all time. Imavov appears to be next. The pussies are online crying robbery. The boys won.

Joshua Van vs. Tatsuro Taira

Result: KO/TKO, Punches (Standing TKO) at 1

Tatsuro Taira went to work immediately, dragging Joshua Van to the canvas within thirty seconds. He cycled through mount, side control, and back to the takedown all through the opening round, just wearing on Van without doing a ton of damage. Van fought back from the bottom, landed some decent shots on the feet when they separated, but Taira controlled the round. It looked like it could be a long night for the champion.


Round two started with more trading, and then Taira went for another takedown. He got Van down in the middle of the octagon, worked into mount, and Van was grinding to survive. Then Van landed an insane shot that put Taira on the canvas, and everything changed. From that moment on, it was Van's fight.


Rounds three through five were carnage. Van stalked Taira around the octagon, landing nasty combinations, headhunting relentlessly. Taira's face was a bloody mess. He kept shooting for takedowns to survive, occasionally getting Van down, once nearly getting a triangle, once dragging him into a scramble, but Van kept getting back up and walking him back down. At the start of round five, Van was still peppering Taira with the jab, sprawled a late takedown, and cracked him with a big combination. The referee stepped in with Taira still on his feet, which was early, but Taira's face was absolutely wrecked and the outcome was not in question.


Van is no longer a paper champion. He is the real thing: a legitimate flyweight titleholder who just survived a nightmare first round and turned the entire fight on its head. Van Pantoja II is the obvious next fight and it is a massive one. Taira is only 26 and will be back in the title picture, but he will need a tune-up first.

Alexander Volkov vs. Waldo Cortes-Acosta

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Waldo Cortes-Acosta came forward from the jump, plodding toward Alexander Volkov who spent the fight on the back foot, managing distance and eating up WCA's lead leg with calf kicks. Round one was controlled, boring movement from Volkov, picking his shots and circling away whenever WCA surged. Round two saw WCA get more aggressive: a big right clipped Volkov, and at one point WCA stood in the center of the octagon and just invited Volkov to come fight him. Volkov clipped him with a left and WCA actually pretended to be hurt to draw Volkov in. It did not work.


Round three was clearly WCA's. He hurt Volkov, got momentum, landed a knee on the break, and kept pressing forward while Volkov's output declined. It was the easiest round on the card to score, and two judges gave it to Volkov anyway. The final scorecard read 30-27 across the board for Volkov, which is criminal. WCA took round three and had a case in at least one of the others.


Volkov's style is boring as hell and his title fight case is complicated by it, but there is nothing else left for him at heavyweight: the division is backed up and he deserves the shot. WCA can stay near the top of the division. Whoever wins between Hokit and Derrick Lewis could be an interesting matchup for him. The judging did nobody any favors tonight.

Sean Brady vs. Joaquin Buckley

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Both fighters were feeling each other out early when Joaquin Buckley threw a kick and Sean Brady caught it, took him down, and that was essentially the story of the fight. Brady moved into half guard, worked to mount, and started landing ground and pound while Buckley covered up and tried to survive. Brady cycled between submission attempts and positional control, and Buckley's guard was non-existent throughout.


Round two was more of the same. Brady got the bodylock, took Buckley down again immediately, moved into half guard, and had his way on the ground. Buckley somehow scrambled back to his feet at one point, charged forward, and Brady just caught him and took him right back down. Hard elbows, ground and pound, zero pushback from Buckley. Round three: Brady shot a single leg, took him down against the cage, went for an arm lock, abandoned it, went back to ground and pound. The final striking differential was 81 to 7 in Brady's favor.


Usman's win over Buckley has aged terribly. Usman did nothing with Buckley on the ground while Brady just absolutely worked him for fifteen minutes. Brady needs the loser of the next title fight or a matchup with Usman to get back in the mix. Buckley was given two shots against fighters ranked above him and got smashed both times: he needs to fight down the rankings next.

King Green vs. Jeremy Stephens

Result: Submission, Rear Naked Choke at 4:20 Round 1 of 3

Bobby Green opened with his hands low and on the backfoot, while Jeremy Stephens walked forward throwing big looping shots without much output. Stephens clinched against the cage and landed an elbow on the break. Then Bobby hit flow state: two big lefts landed flush and Stephens was hurt. The action stopped briefly for a groin shot, and when it resumed Stephens kept pressing forward.


Bobby got the takedown, rained down haymakers from the top, Stephens tried to work a kimura from the bottom, and Bobby scrambled out, got to the back, and sank in the rear naked choke at 4:20 of round one. Stephens tapped.


Great finish from Bobby, but the question hanging over this fight is what the point of it was. Bobby Green will never fight for a belt again and with competition at this level he will never be ranked again. Stephens was not UFC caliber when he was cut the first time around and nothing here suggested otherwise: time to go back to BKFC. Green deserves a prelim slot at best, not a main card spot. If you want to build someone with this kind of showcase, put Amosov and Alvarez there, put Gautier there. Use it on someone whose career trajectory can actually benefit from the exposure.

Prelims

Ateba Gautier vs. Ozzy Diaz

Result: KO/TKO, Overhand Right and Hammerfists at 1:10 Round 2 of 3, 6:10 Total

Ozzy Diaz spent round one on the back foot, running away from Ateba Gautier while Gautier walked forward landing big shots. Diaz tried a couple of single leg attempts that Gautier stuffed, and they spent some time tied up against the fence, but the round belonged to Gautier.


Round two was brief. Gautier landed some body shots, a body kick, and then cracked Diaz with an overhand left that put him down. The referee stopped it at 1:10. Early stoppage.


Gautier picks up the finish, but this one went longer than it had any right to, and the stoppage was premature. Diaz was a known quantity coming in: bona fide trash. Gautier's last few performances have not been convincing, and his Pullyaev showing was not impressive either. Unless something changes, he will never be a champion in this organization.

Yaroslav Amosov vs. Joel Alvarez

Result: Submission, Arm Triangle Choke at 1:13 Round 2 of 3, 6:13 Total

Yaroslav Amosov opened jumping around on the feet but not engaging much, while Joel Alvarez stood stoic and waited. They traded some hooks and jabs, and then Amosov went for the takedown, got Alvarez down, worked half guard, and spent the round cycling through takedowns and drags. Alvarez kept scrambling back to his feet, but Amosov kept dragging him right back down. Amosov landed punches in the clinch and threw Alvarez down at the bell.


Round two, Alvarez came out more aggressive, trying to box on the feet and put together combinations. He got Amosov on the ropes against the cage for a moment. Then Amosov caught a leg, slammed him to the canvas, immediately locked up the arm triangle, and got the tap at 1:13 of round two.


Nasty win. Amosov is a legitimate prospect at 170 who goes for the finish and gets it. Alvarez may have been the sacrificial lamb here to calibrate a prospect, but there is still a spot for him in the division. Amosov deserves a step up in competition: the winner of Muhammed vs. Bonfim makes sense, or a JDM matchup, though the latter risks ending JDM's career prematurely.

Grant Dawson vs. Mateusz Rębecki

Result: Submission, Rear Naked Choke at 4:42 Round 3 of 3, 14:42 Total

Grant Dawson shot for a takedown early, but Mateusz Rebecki scrambled back to his feet. Dawson got to the back, locked in a body triangle, and threatened the rear naked choke, but Rebecki survived and reversed position with about a minute left. Rebecki wore on Dawson up against the fence, landed some shots, and tripped him at the bell. Round one was surprisingly competitive.


Round two belonged to Rebecki on the feet. He clipped Dawson with a right that dropped him early, and Dawson looked wildly uncomfortable standing up: hands low, charging in awkwardly, eating clean shots. Rebecki dropped him again with a right hook. Dawson kept clinching to survive, and they ended the round in a headlock on the canvas.


Round three opened with Rebecki going for a takedown, which was the worst decision he could have made. Dawson won the scramble, got Rebecki's back with the body triangle, cranked at the neck repeatedly, and when Rebecki rolled trying to escape, Dawson kept the back and sank in the rear naked choke for the tap at 4:42.


Rebecki gifted Dawson this win and a finishing bonus. If he stays on his feet in the third round, he probably wins the fight. Dawson still has no chin and cannot compete with anyone credible in the lightweight division, but a bounce-back win is a bounce-back win. Good for him to get back on track.

Jim Miller vs. Jared Gordon

Result: Submission, Guillotine Choke at 3:29 Round 1 of 3

Jim Miller went forward early and Jared Gordon retreated. Miller caught Gordon with an uppercut, threw a kick, and Gordon caught the leg and took him down. Miller hit Gordon in the groin and the action paused briefly. When it resumed, Miller got Gordon into a guillotine, and Gordon tapped at 3:29 of round one.


Just about the most pathetic loss you can take: Gordon caught the kick, should have had the dominant position, and somehow ended up in a guillotine that submitted him. He lost to a 42-year-old who has been in the UFC for 19 years. Miller, meanwhile, continues to be remarkable: getting it done at 42 in the most prestigious promotion in the world is genuinely impressive, full stop.

Roman Kopylov vs. Marco Tulio

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Marco Tulio was the aggressor from the start, throwing 1-2s and landing calf kicks while Roman Kopylov spent most of round one backing up and countering. Tulio invested in the body and used his jab consistently, though the round stayed relatively even with both fighters exchanging.


Round two opened up. Kopylov figured out that when Tulio dropped his lead hand, the right hook was there, and he started landing it cleanly. Both men were bloodied. Then Kopylov landed a right hook that sent Tulio to the canvas, followed up with ground and pound, and Tulio somehow got to the fence. Kopylov clinched to close out the round.


Round three, Kopylov came out and immediately clipped Tulio with a right hook, then followed with a straight left that dropped him again. Tulio got up and they kept fighting, but Kopylov then inexplicably went for a bodylock and started grappling, burning energy for no reason. He spent the back half of the third visibly exhausted, coasting to the decision. Tulio threw everything he had at the end, but it was not enough.


Kopylov saves his UFC career with that win, but his cardio is terrible. He had two sequences where he nearly finished Tulio and could not seal it, then sat on a lead in round three instead of pressing. The jury is still out on Tulio's chin, but two straight losses put him in danger territory in the promotion.

Pat Sabatini vs. William Gomis

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

William Gomis controlled the center and went forward early while Pat Sabatini looked to close the distance. Sabatini got in close, shot a takedown, and when Gomis backed to the fence, Sabatini slammed him to the ground. Gomis popped right back up. Sabatini transitioned to the back, worked for a hook, and kept wearing on Gomis. Sabatini reversed, got reversed back, and finally put Gomis on the canvas for the last ten seconds of round one.


Round two was the same script: Sabatini clinched, went for a head choke, got a takedown, Gomis scrambled back up, Sabatini slammed him again, Gomis got back up again. Knees to the thigh, wearing on him against the cage, then separation. Gomis landed a body kick and some strikes when they separated, but Sabatini kept dragging the fight back to the fence and grinding on him.


Round three: Sabatini shot, Gomis sprawled and denied it. They traded on the feet briefly, with Sabatini visibly uncomfortable at range since Gomis is long and quick. Sabatini got back to the cage work, had the single leg, could not finish it, got Gomis down, Gomis got up. Sabatini kept going for the single but Gomis held his wrists and did nothing to create separation either. It went the distance.


Boring as hell. Sabatini ground out the win but his grappling is not dominant enough to beat the top of the division. Gomis is good, but he cannot spend fifteen minutes just holding onto someone's wrists against the cage and expect different results. The fight needed an earlier and more consistent standup call from the referee.

Baisangur Susurkaev vs. Djorden Santos

Result: Submission, Technical (Rear Naked Choke) at 4:12 Round 3 of 3, 14:12 Total

Djorden Santos came out aggressive while Baisangur Susurkaev stood there with his hands awkwardly low. Susurkaev stung him with a right hand early and kept chipping away at Santos's left calf with calf kicks. Santos tagged him back with a right hook and an uppercut on a failed takedown attempt. Susurkaev's eye started bleeding and he spent the last ninety seconds of the round walking away with his hands down. Round one was Santos's.


Susurkaev came out in round two with his guard higher. Santos kept digging with the jab, cut Susurkaev's face up more, and landed a clean elbow in close. But Susurkaev started finding his rhythm, landing an uppercut and an elbow that opened Santos up on the forehead. Round two was more competitive.


Round three, Susurkaev opened with a spinning elbow and went straight to a body lock, pressing Santos against the cage and landing knees to the thigh. They traded in the center, Susurkaev got another takedown and clinched again. Santos threatened a guillotine but could not lock it in. Susurkaev got to Santos's back, sank in the rear naked choke, and put him to sleep at 4:12.


The Chechen prospect remains undefeated, but he is not good. His hands are low, his wrestling is mid, his boxing is unimpressive, and he does not have knockout power. It genuinely feels like he fumbles his way into a finish in each outing. Santos is not a credible measuring stick at middleweight, and if Susurkaev cannot handily beat someone at this level, he has no shot against anyone in the top fifteen. He will lose his zero soon.

Jose Ochoa vs. Clayton Carpenter

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Both fighters came out swinging from the opening bell. Jose Ochoa walked forward loading up for the kill shot, investing in left body shots and teeing off whenever Clayton Carpenter was pinned against the cage. Carpenter tried two takedown attempts in the first round, both sprawled. They clinched briefly, and when they separated Ochoa teed off again and Carpenter went down. Carpenter threw up a heel hook from the canvas that Ochoa survived, and then Carpenter stood back up. Carpenter's face was a bloody mess by the end of round one.


Round two: Carpenter kept fighting but Ochoa remained the more dominant force. Ochoa walked him down with the jab, landed a big combination with a head kick against the fence, and cracked him with a spinning back elbow that Carpenter somehow ate. Carpenter took Ochoa's back on the feet briefly, went for another heel hook, Ochoa shook it off. Ochoa landed a knee to close the round.


Round three, Carpenter finally got a takedown and worked into side control, took the back, lost it. When they got back to the feet, Ochoa was back to teeing him up and walking him down for the rest of the round. Ochoa won a clear unanimous decision.


Ochoa looked good, but there is a real concern that he could not finish a fighter who was on the ropes multiple times. He is only 25 with two UFC losses coming in against Loner Kavanaugh and Asu Almabayev, both quality opponents, so give him another fight around this level or slightly better to build experience. Carpenter has now lost three straight in the UFC and barely won any exchanges tonight. He should probably get cut. And in his post-fight interview, Ochoa proposed to his girlfriend, who is currently in Brazil.
