Summary

UFC Fight Night: Evloev vs. Murphy was just another garbage card in a run of garbage cards, and if you stayed up for the main event hoping for something memorable, you were rewarded with exactly nothing.


The prelims were mostly forgettable, saved almost entirely by Mason Jones vs. Axel Sola — the undisputed best fight of the night and the only one that deserved a Performance of the Night bonus. Jones got clipped with a spinning back elbow in the first round that would have finished most fighters, but kept coming forward, broke Sola over the next two rounds, and stole a decision. That fight deserved to be on the main card. Everything around it was either boring, sloppy, or both. Nathaniel Wood vs. Losene Keita was the kind of low-output feinting contest that hardcore fans will insist was high-level chess and everyone else will fall asleep watching. Mario Pinto, who entered the night as one of the more exciting heavyweights on the roster, spent fifteen minutes hugging Felipe Franco against the fence and won a decision that felt like a punishment for both the fighters and the audience.


The main card delivered one bright spot: Iwo Baraniewski disposed of Austen Lane in 28 seconds and went home. That was the correct outcome. Everything else ranged from disappointing to actively bad. Danny Silva knocked out debuting prospect Kurtis Campbell in the second round, which was a fine result, but reminded everyone that winning on the Contender Series in pink shorts does not automatically make you a finished product. Michael Page won a 30-27 decision over Sam Patterson while spending the majority of the fight walking backwards and doing absolutely nothing — a performance so dull it made Khamzat Chimaev vs. Dricus Du Plessis look like a war. When the crowd is booing in the second round of a main card fight, something has gone catastrophically wrong. The Roman Dolidze vs. Christian Leroy Duncan fight went the distance and was unclear at best, with Dolidze apparently badly hurt at some point and spending large portions of the fight on the floor pulling guard, while Duncan let himself end up in terrible positions he had no business being in.


The main event was exactly as bad as the prelims deserved. Movsar Evloev and Lerone Murphy spent three rounds too scared to engage with each other, Evloev took a point deduction for two groin shots in the fourth, and by the time the grappling finally showed up it was too late to save the fight. Evloev won the majority decision and called for a title shot. He probably has a case. It is hard to get excited about it. The whole night felt like the product is getting worse — uninspired matchmaking, no promotion, and a roster padded with Contender Series debutants nobody cares about. The UFC needs to do better than this.


Final Score: 5.9/10



Main Card

Movsar Evloev vs. Lerone Murphy

Result: Decision, Majority at 5 Rounds, 25:00 Total

The main event of UFC Fight Night London had all the makings of a genuinely compelling title eliminator on paper — two undefeated featherweights, clear styles clash, winner gets Volkanovski. What we got instead was twenty-five minutes of two friends who clearly did not want to hurt each other. The post-fight interactions said it all.


Evloev spent the first three rounds doing almost none of the wrestling that made him the #1 contender in the first place. Murphy was winning the exchanges on the feet with cleaner boxing but wasn't doing much with it either — fighting off the backfoot, not throwing with any real intention. By the second round it was clear that there were two guys scared to fully engage, and the fight descended into Evloev spamming spinning backfists that weren't landing while Murphy walked away. Something appeared to happen to Murphy around the third and fourth rounds — he looked slowed down, possibly from one of Evloev's two groin shots, the second of which was bad enough for referee Marc Goddard to deduct a point. With the point gone, Evloev finally started wrestling in the fourth, getting Murphy down repeatedly with body locks and grinding out control. The fifth round was more of the same — Evloev in on the back, getting mount, getting dragged back up, dragging Murphy back down. Murphy landed a nice left late but Evloev shook it off.


Evloev won a majority decision and called for a title shot at 20-0. He probably deserves it. But both men looked terrible on the night — Murphy slow and throwing without intention, Evloev awkward on the feet and waiting too long to do what he does best. If they skip Evloev for Jean Silva at this point, that would be genuinely insane. But nobody should be expecting Evloev to do much with Volkanovski based on what he showed here.

Luke Riley vs. Michael Aswell Jr.

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Luke Riley was supposed to be the next big thing out of the UK, and Saturday night was meant to be his coming-out party. Instead, it was a three-round slog that Riley himself rated a 5/10 after the fight — and that feels about right.


Riley controlled the early exchanges with volume and power, winning the striking in the first and clearly hurting Aswell with a big right hand that stumbled him. But Michael Aswell Jr. kept leaning on the clinch whenever things got uncomfortable, nullifying Riley's offense against the fence and making this a grind rather than the showcase it was supposed to be. By the second round, Aswell was finding his range better in the center of the octagon, and while Riley still had the better moments, he couldn't create any real separation. The third round saw both men clearly tiring, with Riley pressing and going for the finish in the final seconds — combinations to the body up against the fence — but the stoppage never came.


Riley won the unanimous decision, and that was the right call. But he should have made easy work of Michael Aswell and didn't. His grappling isn't impressive, and whether he has genuine knockout power against fighters of real quality remains an open question. He'll get a higher-ranked opponent next — probably just outside the rankings — and that fight will tell us a lot more than this one did.

Michael Page vs. Sam Patterson

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Michael "Venom" Page has been doing interviews lately complaining that he doesn't know who he upset at the UFC to get such bad matchmaking. All you have to do is watch his fights, and everything makes complete sense.


This was one of the most unwatchable fights on the card, which is saying something given the competition. Page spent the majority of three rounds walking backwards, sticking his tongue out with his hands at his sides, and doing absolutely nothing. Sam Patterson circled around the octagon, feinted a lot, and occasionally clinched up against the fence. The crowd was booing in the second round. The commentators ran out of things to say about the actual fight and started talking about MVP's old highlight reel. Somehow, Page won 30-27 on all three judges' scorecards, which is a result that defies reasonable explanation given that he barely threw a punch for fifteen minutes.


Patterson had a four-fight first-round finish streak coming in and never looked like using any of it. Page looked like a man who either needs a knockout handed to him on a silver platter or simply will not engage. Performances like this kill the sport. MVP will almost certainly be rewarded with a top-15 ranked opponent next, which nobody on earth asked for. He should have been cut after the Ian Garry fight.

Iwo Baraniewski vs. Austen Lane

Result: KO/TKO, Left Hook to Ground Punches at 0:28 Round 1 of 3

Iwo Baraniewski landed a right hand, followed it with a big left hook, and that was the fight. Twenty-eight seconds. Austen Lane was completely out of it on the canvas, and Baraniewski walked away still in shape with nothing to show for the night except another first-round finish on his record.


Lane had the size advantage and the reach, and it meant absolutely nothing. He complained about an early stoppage afterward, which was genuinely insane — he was completely unconscious. Get Baraniewski back in there within the next couple of weeks if his hand is healthy. No need for a layoff after thirty seconds of work. The UFC should be looking at Jimmy Crute or Ion Cutelaba as next opponents — someone just outside the rankings to keep building the prospect without throwing him to the wolves. As for Lane, it is well past time to cut him. He was never good, and there is no logical reason he is still on the UFC roster.

Christian Leroy Duncan vs. Roman Dolidze

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

This fight was a mess, and the unanimous decision result left more questions than answers about both men.


Dolidze came out wrestling from the jump, taking Christian Leroy Duncan down with ease in the first round and controlling him against the fence. But something appeared to go badly wrong for Dolidze at some point — he started turning his back, dropping to pull guard, and going for leg locks like a man who had been badly hurt and had no other option. Whether it was a strategy or an injury, it looked terrible from a scoring perspective. Duncan landed a big spinning back kick and was piecing Dolidze up on the feet in the second whenever the Georgian wasn't on the floor, with Dolidze going down repeatedly and Duncan letting him back up each time.


The third round saw Dolidze grind his way onto Duncan's back with a body triangle and land ground and pound until the final bell — yet the judges still gave it to Duncan unanimously. How Duncan ended up stuck in that position for the closing stretch of the fight is a real question. His decision-making and grappling are genuinely concerning — if an Anthony Hernandez type gets in there with him, he gets finished on the ground, no questions asked. Duncan gets the win and moves forward, but the performance was far from convincing. As for Dolidze, the verdict stands: he is not a top contender and he is not a future champion. When the fight is anywhere other than the ground, he looks like a fish out of water.

Danny Silva vs. Kurtis Campbell

Result: KO/TKO, Straight Right at 0:31 Round 2 of 3, 5:31 Total

Kurtis Campbell came in unbeaten, wearing the pink shorts, fresh off a Contender Series contract, and ready to announce himself to the UFC. Danny Silva had other ideas.


Campbell actually looked the part early, shooting for takedowns repeatedly in the first round and showing the grappling credentials that made him a prospect. He got the back at one point and looked dangerous, but Silva defended the submission attempts well, reversed positions, and got back to his feet. By the end of the first round, Silva was landing good strikes and the momentum had shifted. The second round lasted thirty-one seconds. Silva clipped Campbell with a big right hand that put him down, and when Campbell bounced back up, Silva put him away for good with a straight right.


The bottom line: winning on the Contender Series and wearing pink shorts does not give you magical fighting ability. It makes you overhyped and adds value to whoever is across the cage. Campbell is 23 years old with eight fights — that is nothing. He needs time and experience before being matched up against fighters with Silva's level of seasoning. Silva, for his part, just turned away a hyped prospect after going the full distance with Kevin Vallejos last year. That is a résumé building in the right direction. Time to give him a bump up in competition, possibly even a ranked opponent.

Prelims

Mason Jones vs. Axel Sola

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

This was the best fight on the card, and it wasn't particularly close.


Sola dropped Mason Jones with a spinning back elbow in the first round — a clean, nasty shot that would have finished most fighters. Jones was dead to rights. He scrambled back up, used the fence to recover, and then did something most fighters in that position don't do: he kept walking forward. Sola sensed blood and came charging in, cutting Jones open in the process. Jones fired back with big shots of his own, and by the end of the round both men were bloodied and the crowd was alive for the first time all night.


Jones came out in the second and third rounds and broke Sola down methodically — head kicks, pressure, volume. Sola's face was gushing blood by the third, and Jones just kept walking forward and landing knees up against the fence in the closing seconds. Jones attempted his own spinning back elbows throughout, none of which landed nearly as cleanly as Sola's did in the first, and the 30-27 scorecards were a stretch given how competitive the opening round was. But they got the right winner. Jones got cut from the UFC, rebuilt on the regional scene, came back, and now just absorbed the nastiest shot of the night and came back to steal a decision. There is no quit in him. Get him on a bigger card with a higher-profile matchup. He has earned it. As for Sola — that was an entertaining fight and there is no real stock loss for the Frenchman here.

Nathaniel Wood vs. Losene Keita

Result: Decision, Split at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Nathaniel Wood won a split decision over Losene Keita, and the judges' scorecards are genuinely hard to explain.


This was a low-output, boring fight from start to finish — the kind of bout that hardcore fans will call high-level chess while everyone else quietly checks their phone. The first round was a jab fest that quickly devolved into feinting with almost no strikes landing on either side. Keita was the more aggressive fighter across all three rounds — finding his range with teeps and jabs in the second, landing a big knee that opened Wood's nose, and finishing the round with a right hand. The third was more feinting and occasional exchanges, with Keita swinging for big shots that couldn't find a home and Wood landing some nice kicks and jabs without ever decisively asserting himself.


The split decision going to Wood is baffling. Keita landed the bigger shots, was more aggressive, and looked the more dangerous fighter throughout. Wood continues his baffling run where he wins fights without doing anything particularly effective or impressive, and continues to be denied ranked opponents despite a five-fight winning streak. This fight continued to kill the prelims of this event.

Mario Pinto vs. Felipe Franco

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Before this fight, Mario Pinto was shaping up to be one of the more exciting heavyweights on the UFC roster. After it, the question is: what exactly is his skillset?


This was fifteen minutes of clinching against the fence. Pinto spent the majority of all three rounds with Felipe Franco pressed up against the cage, landing baby shots in the clinch, going for takedowns Franco repeatedly defended, and then resetting to do it all over again. The only takedown Pinto managed came in the third round, and Franco scrambled straight back to his feet. Franco, for his part, was on short notice and had very little offensively after a decent first round — a groin shot briefly stopped the action in the second, but beyond that he was mostly a passive participant, happy to survive.


Pinto won the unanimous decision, but it was the kind of win that raises more questions than it answers. If he can't get the takedown, he doesn't do anything. His striking isn't there, and the ground-and-pound that made his first two UFC wins so impressive never materialized because he couldn't get Franco to the canvas. He does not have a chance against a top-15 opponent right now. Franco went the distance on short notice, which is worth something, but whether he is UFC-caliber remains unclear. Do not give Pinto a ranked opponent next. That was a major stock loss.

Mantas Kondratavičius vs. Antonio Trócoli

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

This fight went to a unanimous decision, which says everything you need to know about how it went.


Kondratavičius came out cracking Trócoli from the opening bell, but what followed was a three-round slog defined by clinch work, fence hugging, and two fighters with no cardio desperately looking for breaks. Kondratavičius was reckless and super sloppy — just trying to starch his man with no technique or strategy — while Trócoli spent most of the fight holding on for dear life against the cage. By the second round both men were begging for pauses. Trócoli at one point simply stood in place and let Kondratavičius land heat while the referee repeatedly threatened to stop the fight if he didn't improve his position — Trócoli kind of moved, then got into a worse spot. A low blow from Trócoli stopped the action in the third, the fighters were not sent to opposite sides of the octagon as they should have been, and the officiating was a mess throughout. Kondratavičius got a takedown late and landed ground and pound to close the show.


Kondratavičius dominated and deserved the win, but he has no technique, no strategy, and no cardio — he just wants to go out and hurt people. That will hit a wall at some point. As for Trócoli, there is no more runway here. No striking, no grappling, no cardio, no heart. He is dangerous to have on a UFC roster — one bad night away from serious damage. Cut him. The fact that two judges gave him a round is one of the more baffling scorecards of the night.

Brando Peričić vs. Louie Sutherland

Result: KO/TKO, Punches at 1:48 Round 1 of 3

Louie Sutherland came out swinging and landed some big shots early, but it was clear almost immediately that his gameplan was to either knock Brando Peričić out or get knocked out himself. Unfortunately for him, it was the latter.


Sutherland rushed forward and landed a 1-2 that backed Peričić to the fence, then kept pressing and shot for a takedown that Peričić defended. But Peričić hurt Sutherland with a combination shortly after, and once Sutherland went down, the fight was over in everything but name. Peričić moved to north-south, landed big shots, circled around to the back, and finished with ground and pound. One minute forty-eight seconds.


The disappointment on Sutherland's face after the loss said it all — this is an unforgiving sport, and a swing-for-the-fences gameplan against a heavier-handed opponent is a coin flip that did not go his way. Peričić is flabby and somewhat uncoordinated, and called out a top-10 opponent afterward, which is getting well ahead of himself. He has two UFC fights. Let's see who the promotion actually gives him before drawing any conclusions.

A. Al-Selwady vs. Shem Rock

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

A. Al-Selwady won a unanimous decision over Shem Rock, and then tried to sucker punch him afterward. Class act.


Al-Selwady came out eager, landing calf kicks and moving forward with combinations while Rock spent the fight trying to make his size and reach work against a smaller, quicker opponent. The biggest problem for Rock was his own slowness — he had the physical tools in theory, but Al-Selwady was consistently slipping in, putting together combinations, and getting out before Rock could capitalize on anything. Rock landed a head kick in the first that he thought had hurt Al-Selwady, rushed in, and got dropped by a right hand for his trouble. He scrambled to a desperation takedown and survived, but it set the tone. By the second round, Rock's face was bleeding and he wasn't even trying to engage — standing in the center waiting to counter an opponent who simply wouldn't walk onto his shots. Rock's corner told him to get the fight to the ground and submit him. Rock didn't threaten a serious takedown until sixty seconds were left in the third. That tells you everything.


The fight itself was pretty bad — a guy too small to beat anyone decent in the division using his speed against a guy with no striking, coordination, or fighting instinct. Al-Selwady will get clipped by any competent striker at lightweight with a longer reach, and should seriously consider moving down. As for Rock, that was a terrible performance. He should probably be cut. And the post-fight conduct — telling his opponent "fuck off" when Rock came to say good fight, then attempting a sucker punch — is the cherry on top. Learn some respect.

Shanelle Dyer vs. Ravena Oliveira

Result: KO/TKO, Knees and Punches at 1:17 Round 2 of 3, 6:17 Total

Shanelle Dyer controlled this fight from start to finish and finished it in the second round with ground and pound.


Dyer kept the pressure on throughout the first round, consistently moving forward behind her jab while Ravena Oliveira backed up and looked hesitant. Oliveira had one moment — a right hand at the start of the second that briefly made Dyer retreat — but it was the only significant thing she did all night. Shortly after, Dyer landed a leg kick that grazed Oliveira on the chin. It didn't look like it landed flush, but a couple of toes on the jaw was apparently all it took — Oliveira wobbled, Dyer followed up, got the fight to the ground, and finished with ground and pound at 1:17 of round two.


Oliveira looked really bad out there. She was in defensive mode for nearly the entire fight, her boxing defense is not sound, and she was sloppy in the clinch. Women's MMA is a thin division, so Dyer may get a bump in competition next — but the performance itself was not particularly impressive. Her boxing defense has holes and she'll need to clean that up if she's going to make any real noise in the strawweight division.
