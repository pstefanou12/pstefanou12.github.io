Summary

UFC Fight Night: Strickland vs. Hernandez was a tale of two cards — a prelim slate that was largely a slog of forgettable decisions, saved only by a breathless Njokuani-Leal war, and a main card that steadily built momentum before delivering a genuinely satisfying main event.


The prelims were rough. Fight after fight went to the cards, with too many bouts that produced little memorable action and drained the energy out of the venue. The one exception was Carlos Leal vs. Chidi Njokuani — a pure heart-over-technique brawl where both men walked into each other's shots without a shred of self-preservation. It was the best fight on the card and deserved a Fight of the Night bonus. Until that one, the prelim portion was testing the patience of anyone watching, let alone fans paying to be in attendance.


The main card picked things up. Jacobe Smith rolled through Josiah Harrell with a crucifix TKO that raised his record to a perfect 12-0. Melquizael Costa added another first-round highlight-reel finish to his resume, dropping Dan Ige with a spinning back kick followed by ground and pound. Uros Medic put the welterweight division on notice by putting Geoff Neal out cold with a left hook inside 80 seconds — a statement win over a household name that he delivered with ice-cold efficiency.


The main event was the best thing on the card. Sean Strickland — somehow priced as a +250 underdog — methodically broke down Anthony Hernandez over three rounds, controlling the range with teeps and volume before finishing with a body shot and follow-up punches in the third. Anyone actually watching Strickland fight knew the line was absurd. He rolled with punches, dictated pace entirely, and looked every bit the top-five middleweight he is. The visible devastation on Hernandez's face at the final horn, before the stoppage came, said everything about how completely the fight had gone against him.


The card also benefited from being held in a real venue with a crowd that was audibly invested throughout. The atmosphere made even the slower moments more bearable and gave the main event the send-off it deserved. A flawed night of fights, but one with enough highlight finishes and a strong enough headliner to justify the score.


Final Score: 6.3/10


Main Card

Sean Strickland vs. Anthony Hernandez

Result: KO/TKO, Knee and Punches at 2:33 Round 3 of 5, 12:33 Total

Whatever the betting market thought going into this one, Sean Strickland was never in any serious danger. Priced as a +250 underdog against Anthony "Fluffy" Hernandez, who had arrived on the back of an eight-fight win streak and was being treated by some corners of the media as a legitimate upset candidate, Strickland fought exactly the way he always does — intelligently, patiently, and with a volume that accumulates faster than it looks.


From the opening bell, Strickland established the pace with teeps to the body and consistent jabs, keeping Hernandez at range and making him pay every time he charged forward. Hernandez pressed with combinations and tried repeatedly to close the distance and initiate takedowns against the cage, but Strickland's takedown defense held and his footwork kept him out of danger. In the first round, a big Strickland overhand right stumbled Hernandez early, and the follow-up pressure showed Strickland's instinct to hurt when he smells blood. What the highlight reels don't always show is how well Strickland rolls with incoming shots — every Hernandez combination that looked like it might land clean was subtly slipped or absorbed at an angle.


By the third round, Strickland had done enough grinding to make Hernandez's forward momentum look increasingly desperate rather than threatening. A sharp body shot buckled Hernandez, Strickland swarmed with the finish, and the fight was waved off at 2:33 of the third. The aftermath was hard to watch for Hernandez — a man who had built his streak on legitimate wins and clearly believed in his chances, sitting devastated on his stool. For Strickland, the path forward likely runs through Nassourdine Imavov next, with a title shot not far behind if Chimaev doesn't get off the sideline soon.

Uros Medic vs. Geoff Neal

Result: KO/TKO, Left Hook at 1:19 Round 1 of 3

Uros Medic made a statement. Against Geoff Neal — a veteran welterweight who had gone to the wire with some of the best in the division — Medic needed 79 seconds and one left hook to end the night. Neal had taken six months off following a brutal knockout loss to Carlos Prates, and whatever had been damaged in that fight clearly hadn't fully healed. From the opening exchanges, the two men were feeling each other out when Medic found the temple with a clean left hook that dropped Neal cold.


The finish was emphatic, and Medic's post-fight callout of Leon Edwards was delivered with the confidence of a man who genuinely believes he belongs at that level. He might be right. Medic is now on a three-fight win streak and has a flair for the theatrical — his own words in the post-fight interview made clear he's about entertainment as much as winning. For Neal, now approaching 0-5 in his last five at 35 years old, this is the kind of knockout loss that raises serious questions about whether it's time to consider retirement. The body of work is there; there's no shame in walking away from a sport that is clearly starting to take from him more than he's taking from it.

Melquizael Costa vs. Dan Ige

Result: KO/TKO, Spinning Back Kick to Ground Punches at 4:56 Round 1 of 3

Melquizael Costa is on a roll, and he's making it look easy. Against Dan Ige, the Brazilian featherweight extended his win streak to six with a spectacular first-round finish — a spinning back kick that buckled Ige, followed immediately by ground and pound that forced the stoppage with four seconds left in the opening frame. It was Costa's second consecutive first-round knockout, and it had the same quality as the last one: not a lucky punch, but a composed, confident athlete who knew exactly what he was doing.


Costa looked technically cleaner than his opposition throughout, and Ige — now 1-4 in his last five — had no answer for the Brazilian's combination striking. The fight was largely one-sided from the moment Costa started finding his range, and the finish was a thing of beauty. At this pace, the UFC will need to start giving Costa someone from the rankings sooner rather than later — Arnold Allen and Pat Sabatini have both been floated as logical next opponents, and either would tell us something meaningful about where Costa actually sits in the featherweight picture.

Serghei Spivac vs. Ante Delija

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

This was a fight that revealed more about both men than the scorecards might suggest. Serghei Spivac came in as the more seasoned UFC heavyweight and executed a jab-heavy gameplan that proved surprisingly effective — not because the jab is a devastating weapon, but because Ante Delija had no consistent answer for it across three rounds. Delija entered the fight 1-1 in the promotion and looking to build, but from round one his face was accumulating damage as Spivac found his target repeatedly.


Delija had his moments — a big right hand in the second round drove Spivac back into the fence and showed the power that made him an intriguing prospect coming in. But rather than following that moment with offense, Delija clinched, which handed the momentum back to Spivac. In the third, Spivac took the fight to the ground and controlled with elbows before Delija worked back to his feet, and the two traded on the outside to close it out. The unanimous decision win ends Spivac's two-fight losing streak, though the performance wasn't one to get excited about. Delija, meanwhile, now sits 1-2 in the UFC and needs a significant improvement to stay relevant in a heavyweight division that doesn't wait for anyone.

Jacobe Smith vs. Josiah Harrell

Result: KO/TKO, Strikes From Side Control at 3:01 Round 1 of 3

Jacobe Smith is now 12-0, and he keeps finding ways to finish people. Against Josiah Harrell — who took the fight on short notice for his UFC debut — Smith was measured in the opening exchanges, feeling out his range before landing a sharp left hand that wobbled Harrell. From that point, it was a matter of when, not if. Smith pursued the finish, took Harrell down, and worked into a crucifix before unleashing a barrage of unanswered shots that brought the referee in at 3:01 of the first.


The stoppage was arguably a touch late — Harrell had checked out before the end came — and the short-notice nature of the opponent limits what we can take from the performance. Still, Smith looks like a legitimate prospect with real finishing instincts. The ability to control from the crucifix and find the finish in that position takes skill beyond raw power. The next step should be someone with genuine UFC experience at his level, and a win there will start to paint a clearer picture. Harrell, for his part, deserves a fairer shot than a debut on short notice against an undefeated finisher — the contender series would be the right place to see what he actually has.

Michel Pereira vs. Zach Reese

Result: Decision, Split at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

This fight was a mess, and not entirely in a good way. Michel Pereira came out looking to end things early, landing a big leg kick that wobbled Zach Reese in the opening minute, and then unloading a right hand that sent Reese to the canvas. Pereira inexplicably stepped back and let Reese recover, which proved costly — Reese gathered himself, started using his own leg kicks, and began to neutralize Pereira's aggression. From there, the fight devolved into a low-output grind punctuated by eye pokes, low blows, and lengthy stoppages from Herb Dean that repeatedly killed whatever momentum existed.


By the third round, both fighters were visibly spent, trading at reduced output and picking their shots with diminishing returns. Reese landed a left hook late and briefly threatened with an anaconda choke attempt before Pereira reversed and they traded to the final horn. The split decision for Pereira was defensible — the first round was clearly his, and the next two were close enough that awarding them to Reese felt like a stretch. Still, the result sparked controversy among those who felt Reese had done more in the final ten minutes. Neither man covered themselves in glory, and the tactical limitations of both were on full display.

Prelims

Carlos Leal vs. Chidi Njokuani

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

This was the best fight on the card and it wasn't particularly close. Carlos Leal and Chidi Njokuani threw themselves at each other for three rounds with a shared willingness to absorb punishment that made for genuinely compulsive viewing. Leal entered as the aggressor from round one, walking Njokuani down and landing big combinations that forced Njokuani to the cage repeatedly. Njokuani, for his part, was clean and accurate every time he countered — a right hand that clipped Leal's chin, a knee to the face that opened visible damage — but he spent far too much of the fight moving backward rather than pulling the trigger.


The fight wore on both men as the rounds progressed. Leal's output dropped visibly in the second, and Njokuani briefly took over before Leal reasserted himself. In the third, Njokuani came out with urgency, finally walking forward and using his jab, and even put Leal down briefly with a right hand that sent him to one knee — only for Leal to bounce straight back up and keep pressing. The unanimous decision went to Leal on the basis of sustained pressure and higher damage output, but Njokuani could have won any round with a bit more trigger-pulling. The fight deserved Fight of the Night money. That it happened on the prelims and went largely unseen by casual fans was this card's biggest shame.

Alibi Idiris vs. Ode Osbourne

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Alibi Idiris was the better fighter in every dimension here, and he proved it across fifteen minutes of relentless output and pressure that Ode Osbourne had no real answer for. From round one, Idiris was everywhere — landing big right hooks on the feet, dragging the fight into heavy grappling exchanges, threatening d'arc chokes and arm triangles from mount, and somehow doing it all without appearing to tire. His cardio was the defining feature of the fight; he looked as sharp and energetic in the third round as he did in the first.


Osbourne spent the fight looking for his left hand — the knockout weapon that has ended fights at this level before — but Idiris gave him no space to load up and use it. Whenever Osbourne tried to stand and trade, Idiris dragged him into grappling. Whenever Osbourne shot a takedown in the third round, Idiris sprawled it off and reasserted control. Osbourne is durable and never looked close to being finished, but durability without offense is a losing formula. The unanimous decision was the right call. A natural next step might be to pair Idiris with Alden Coria — two fighters at similar career stages in the flyweight division with something to prove.

Alden Coria vs. Luis Gurule

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Alden Coria picked up his second straight UFC win with a clean, technical performance over Luis Gurule, and the numbers backed up what the eye test showed: Coria out-landed Gurule 79 to 50 in significant strikes, with a similar edge in shots to the head. The fight played out on the feet throughout, with Coria consistently sharper and more active, his striking cleaner and his output higher across all three rounds.


Now 2-0 in the promotion, Coria is building the right kind of momentum for a flyweight prospect — wins over credible competition, no wasted time, and a style that suggests a ceiling worth finding out about. The next few fights will be important developmental ones as he faces opponents who test him in more meaningful ways. As a short-term next step, a fight with Idiris — who competed two fights later on the same card — would make natural sense for both.

Joselyne Edwards vs. Nora Cornolle

Result: Submission, Rear Naked Choke at 2:44 Round 2 of 3, 7:44 Total

Joselyne Edwards needed a full round to get going, but once she did, the finish came quickly. The first round was an oddly passive affair from Edwards — more clinch-and-hold than the aggressive output you'd expect from a fighter with her experience — while Nora Cornolle was the more active presence, landing shoulder strikes and working in the clinch. Edwards did land a meaningful right hand that caused Cornolle to retreat, but the opening round felt like a miss.


The second round was a different fight entirely. Edwards shot in for a takedown, and though Cornolle initially made it back to the cage and her base, Edwards maintained back control and worked the position patiently. A slam brought Cornolle to the canvas — Cornolle appeared to be hurt or in serious discomfort at this point, possibly from a shoulder injury sustained on impact — and Edwards transitioned seamlessly to back control before sinking in the rear naked choke for the submission win at 2:44. Edwards, now on a four-fight win streak, avenges a 2023 loss to Cornolle and plants herself near the top of a women's bantamweight division that remains notably shallow. Whether she can challenge for a title from there remains to be seen, but the trajectory is pointing in the right direction.

Punahele Soriano vs. Ramiz Brahimaj

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

A largely forgettable bout that got interesting only in the final round. Ramiz Brahimaj controlled the early portion of the fight with takedown pressure and clinch work, landing a clean right hand that dropped Punahele Soriano to one knee in the first and continuing to work the grappling game through the second. Soriano was passive for much of the first two rounds, content to defend against the cage and wait for openings that rarely came.


The third round was the only time the fight had real energy. Soriano came out with genuine intent, landing nasty body kicks, knees to the head, and head kicks that had Brahimaj backing up and covering. Brahimaj shot for takedowns to survive, but Soriano's defense was sharper and his scrambles prevented Brahimaj from doing any real damage on the ground. The late flurry was enough to secure the round and close the unanimous decision. Soriano is now on a four-fight win streak at 33, and a step up to ranked competition is overdue — the answer of whether he's championship caliber is one that needs to start being answered. Brahimaj, meanwhile, has real holes in his game and limited upside at this stage of his career.

Jean-Paul Lebosnoyani vs. Phil Rowe

Result: Decision, Split at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

A fight where both men showed enough to keep you watching but neither showed enough to get genuinely excited. Jean-Paul Lebosnoyani was the busier and more aggressive fighter throughout, using a combination of forward pressure, takedowns, and clinch wrestling to control the pace. Phil Rowe has all the physical tools — length, power in his hands, a naturally long and rangy frame — but fights with a passivity that consistently buries him in the scorecards before he decides it's time to actually engage.


Rowe had his moments. He found his jab in the second half of round one and started landing with more authority, and a knee to the head on a Lebosnoyani takedown attempt was sharp and accurate. But those flashes were separated by long stretches of disengagement that allowed Lebosnoyani to keep accumulating output. By the third round, Lebosnoyani had Rowe's back after catching a kick and taking him down, and threatened with rear naked choke attempts and d'arc setups that forced Rowe to spend his energy defending rather than attacking. The split decision for Lebosnoyani was correct. He is a well-rounded, solid UFC middleweight who needs more time against better competition before the rankings conversation begins — Rowe's length made this a difficult matchup for him, and he won anyway.

Jordan Leavitt vs. Yadier del Valle

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Jordan Leavitt did what he does — grapple, control, grind — and walked away with a unanimous decision over Yadier del Valle that was never seriously in doubt. From the opening bell, Leavitt brought the fight to the clinch and against the cage, using his size and wrestling to keep del Valle stuck in half guard and mount for long stretches of each round. He wasn't threatening any damaging submissions or landing significant ground and pound, but he was doing just enough to prevent the referee from standing them up and accumulating the control time that wins rounds.


Del Valle had his moments — a successful sprawl in the second, a knee to the chest in the third that briefly reversed positions — but he was never able to make anything happen once Leavitt had established top position. The decision was the right call, even if the performance raised more questions than it answered about both men. Leavitt has legitimate grappling credentials but nothing threatening enough on the mat or the feet to suggest a serious ceiling in the division. Del Valle, for his part, was badly outmuscled — if he can't beat Leavitt, the UFC roster at lightweight is going to be a very short stay.

Carli Judice vs. Juliana Miller

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Carli Judice outworked Juliana Miller over three rounds to earn a unanimous decision win, with the edge coming primarily through higher output and more consistent striking. Miller spent much of the fight attempting to close distance and initiate grappling exchanges, but Judice was the sharper and busier striker on the feet, dealing out the more meaningful damage across all three rounds and doing enough to make the final scorecards uncontroversial.
