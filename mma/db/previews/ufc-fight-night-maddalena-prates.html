Overview

UFC heads Down Under for UFC Fight Night 275 at RAC Arena in Perth, Western Australia — and the card is built for the Australian market in the most blatant way possible. Half the fighters on this card either live in or train out of the region, and the UFC has clearly engineered a homecoming showcase. The question is whether the fights themselves hold up once you get past the narrative packaging.

The main event is genuinely compelling. Jack Della Maddalena is the former welterweight champion — won the belt from Belal Muhammad in a Fight of the Year candidate, then got dominated by Islam Makhachev in his first title defense. Now he's back in Perth, fighting in front of his people, trying to remind the division who he is. His opponent, Carlos Prates, is the most dangerous striker in the welterweight division right now — back-to-back knockouts of Geoff Neal and Leon Edwards on the same night JDM lost his title. That is a hell of a calling card. This is a real fight with real stakes, and whoever wins likely gets a title shot next.

The co-main event is a different kind of story. Beneil Dariush is a +350 underdog — the biggest of his career — against rising star Quillan Salkilld, who was named the 2025 UFC Newcomer of the Year and has finished three of his four Octagon appearances in the first round. Dariush was knocked out in 16 seconds by Benoit Saint-Denis at MSG in November, and the news that he's carrying an undisclosed injury into this fight doesn't inspire confidence. This has the look of a torch-passing moment whether Dariush is ready to accept it or not.

Beyond the top two fights, the card is a mixed bag. Tuivasa is on a six-fight losing streak and openly admitted his job is on the line. Rahiki's original opponent Jenkins pulled out injured, leaving a 4-2 regional debutant to take his place on the main card. Gaziev, a top-15 heavyweight, reportedly dropped 40+ pounds ahead of this fight — a deeply strange thing for a man competing at 265 lbs. Perth is a good fight town, and the crowd will make almost anything electric. But there are some charity cases buried in this lineup.


Main Card Picks

Jack Della Maddalena vs. Carlos Prates

Jack Della Maddalena (18-3) is the former UFC welterweight champion, having claimed the belt from Belal Muhammad in a Fight of the Year performance at UFC 315 before dropping a lopsided title defense to Islam Makhachev at UFC 322. He's a high-volume, technically precise striker who has never been dropped in 23 Octagon rounds, landing 5.57 significant strikes per minute while absorbing just 3.84. He's back home in Perth and his coach publicly stated the UFC ignored their preferred opponent to make this match — meaning JDM didn't choose this fight.

Carlos Prates (23-7) is the most dangerous striker in the welterweight division right now. He knocked out Geoff Neal and Leon Edwards in back-to-back fights — the Edwards finish coming via straight left at 1:28 of the second round on the exact same card where JDM lost his title. He's No. 5 ranked, 21 of his 23 wins have come by KO or submission, and he's already calling for the title shot a win here would earn him. His only UFC loss came against Ian Garry. He comes in as the slight betting underdog but carries one-shot knockout power in either hand.

Pick: Carlos Prates

Method: KO/TKO

A lot of people are picking JDM here, and it's hard to understand why. Look at his two biggest wins — Gilbert Burns and Belal Muhammad, both primarily grapplers. He wasn't outboxing world-class strikers; he was grinding out decisions against guys who wanted to wrestle. Now he's stepping in with the most lethal striker in the division, and the assumption is that this time it's different. It isn't. Prates's only UFC loss came against Ian Garry — a short-notice replacement — and he still took it to the fifth round. JDM is not going to be able to hang with Prates on the feet, and when Prates finds his rhythm he has an answer for everything. Look for this to end in round three.

Beneil Dariush vs. Quillan Salkilld

Beneil Dariush (23-7-1) is coming off one of the worst knockouts of his career — dropped in 16 seconds by Benoit Saint-Denis at MSG in November, faceplanting flat on the canvas on live television. It's the third time in four fights he's been stopped violently in the first round. He's fighting as a +350 underdog, the biggest of his career, and revealed hours before the fight that he's carrying an undisclosed injury. He believes his grappling will be the difference and that the fight will hit the ground regardless of what Salkilld wants.

Quillan Salkilld (11-1) was named the 2025 UFC Newcomer of the Year and has earned it — he's 4-0 in the Octagon with three first-round finishes, including a head kick KO of Nasrat Haqparast and an RNC of Jamie Mullarkey. He's 26 years old, has been fighting at a relentless pace, and a win over Dariush sends him "head-first into the top 10" by his own description. He comes in as a -450 betting favorite, and rightfully so.

Pick: Quillan Salkilld

Method: KO/TKO

Anyone picking Dariush here is absolutely out of their mind. Yes, Dariush can grapple — but so can Salkilld, and it's his primary skill set. On top of that, Dariush has become dangerously chinny. He face-planted flat on the canvas after Saint-Denis tapped him at MSG in November, and he's been stopped in the first round three times in four fights. He'd need a flawless performance — total grappling dominance, not a single clean strike landing on his head — to survive this, and that's before accounting for the undisclosed injury. Salkilld is coming off a KO of the Year contender and has looked lethal in every UFC outing. First round knockout.

Tim Elliott vs. Steve Erceg

Tim Elliott (21-13-1) has been rattling around the flyweight division for over a decade and is still dangerous — his quirky footwork, relentless pace, and elite wrestling make him a nightmare stylistic matchup for clean technical fighters. He's ranked No. 11 and just submitted Kai Asakura with a guillotine in August 2025. At +195, he's the underdog, but his physical strength and chaos factor are never to be underestimated.

Steve Erceg (13-4) is ranked No. 12 and is fighting at home in Australia. His 4-3 UFC record is a deeply misleading number — those three losses came against Alexandre Pantoja (title fight), Brandon Moreno, and Kai Kara-France. He's beaten genuinely elite competition to a decision on multiple occasions and snapped his losing skid with a win over Ode Osbourne in August 2025. He's the -250 favorite and the cleaner technical striker of the two.

Pick: Steve Erceg

Method: Decision

Erceg is the classic example of a fighter who got fast-tracked and paid for it. He went from a promising prospect straight into a title shot against Pantoja, then faced Kara-France and Moreno back-to-back — and looked lost in all three. But here's the thing: he arguably beat Pantoja, and the losses to elite opposition at the wrong stage of his development tell a different story than his record suggests. The bounce-back win over Osbourne seems to have restored some confidence, and fighting in front of his home crowd only helps.

Meanwhile, Elliott's momentum is built on a last-minute guillotine against Kai Asakura — a fighter the jury is very much still out on. Elliott's chaos and wrestling are real, but Erceg is the sharper, cleaner fighter at this stage. Elliott's luck has run out. Erceg gets it done over the distance.

Marwan Rahiki vs. Ollie Schmid

Marwan Rahiki (8-0) made a violent first impression in his UFC debut just six weeks ago, breaking Harry Hardwick's jaw in the second round en route to a Fight of the Night bonus. The French prospect is undefeated with finishes throughout his record, and his original opponent Jack Jenkins withdrew injured — meaning Rahiki is getting a short-notice replacement for his second Octagon outing.

Ollie Schmid (4-2) is stepping in on four days' notice for his UFC debut. He trains at City Kickboxing alongside Israel Adesanya and recorded a 15-second spinning backfist KO at Shuriken Fight Series 23 last month. He's 4-2 as a professional, and fans were openly baffled when the signing was announced — analysts pointed out that none of his wins came against opponents who had positive records at the time. This is a short-notice debut on the UFC main card against a fighter three months removed from a Fight of the Night bonus.

Pick: Marwan Rahiki

Method: KO/TKO

Rahiki is a genuinely sick fighter — nonstop action, violent tendencies, and a growing track record of hurting people badly. Although he represents Morocco, he's based in Australia, and this is effectively a homecoming for him in front of a crowd that wants to see him perform. Schmid is a sacrificial lamb. The UFC is setting Rahiki up for a statement KO in front of a home crowd and Schmid drew the short straw. The City Kickboxing environment is elite, but four days' notice into a UFC debut against an undefeated prospect on a war path is a tough ask for anyone.

Shamil Gaziev vs. Brando Peričić

Shamil Gaziev (14-2) is ranked No. 15 at heavyweight and enters this fight coming off a first-round knockout loss to Waldo Cortes-Acosta in November 2025. He's 36 years old and has now been stopped by KO twice in his last four fights. A pre-fight report noted he dropped 40+ pounds in camp — an alarming detail for someone competing at heavyweight. When Gaziev has faced legitimate upper-tier opposition, he's fallen short consistently.

Brando Peričić (6-1) is the Australian hometown fighter who has gone 4-0 in the UFC with four first-round finishes — five knockouts and one submission across his entire UFC run, none of them going past the opening round. He's 31 years old and just knocked out Louie Sutherland in 1:48 six weeks ago. The UFC has called him "a man in a hurry" and the bookmakers agree, making him the slight favorite at -155 over a ranked veteran.

Pick: Brando Peričić

Method: KO/TKO

Gaziev is a blob who has consistently run into trouble against anyone with legitimate skills. His losses to Waldo Cortes-Acosta and Jairzinho Rozenstruik say far more than any of his wins — he cannot get it done against quality opposition. Add in the bizarre 40-pound weight drop in a heavyweight camp and questions about his mental state after back-to-back knockouts, and this is not a man who looks ready to turn things around.

Peričić isn't just another big body — he's a finisher with real skills who moves well and hits with bad intentions. He's not a complete blob in the way Gaziev is, and he's been destroying people from the jump since entering the UFC. He goes out there and smokes Gaziev, R1 or R2.

Tai Tuivasa vs. Louie Sutherland

Tai Tuivasa (14-9) has lost six fights in a row. He hasn't won since 2022, and he's been brutally transparent about it — admitting pre-fight that "my ass is on the line." His last performance against Tallison Teixeira was particularly deflating: no finish, just a demoralized fighter going through the motions over three rounds. His original opponent Sean Sharaf withdrew with a fractured nose in training, meaning Tuivasa now faces a last-minute replacement in what may be the final fight of his UFC contract.

Louie Sutherland (10-5) is 0-2 in the UFC. Both losses came quickly — a heel hook from Valter Walker and a TKO from Brando Peričić just six weeks ago. He's on eight days' notice and is a genuine underdog at +180. For Tuivasa, this is the opponent you need to beat if you're ever going to beat anyone.

Pick: Louie Sutherland

Method: Decision

Tuivasa's last outing was bad in a way that goes beyond just losing — he looked completely demoralized, like a man fighting out of obligation rather than belief. Six-fight losing skids don't just happen in MMA; they're usually a signal that a fighter is over the hill, and at 31 years old with that run of results, Tuivasa fits the profile. It can be a tough pill to swallow, but them's the breaks.

Sutherland is much closer to his prime and, while he's 0-2 in the UFC, both of those losses came against legitimately dangerous fighters in Walker and Peričić. Tuivasa is neither of those things at this stage of his career. Sutherland grinds this out to a majority decision, and Tuivasa walks off into the Australian sunset.

Prelims Picks

Cam Rowston vs. Robert Bryczek

Cam Rowston (14-3) is nicknamed the "Battle Giraffe" for a reason — he's 6'3" with a 75-inch reach, has evolved into a ground-and-pound finisher over the last year, and is on a six-fight win streak. His last three wins have all come inside the distance, including back-to-back stoppages of Cody Brundage and Andre Petroski inside the Octagon. He's 2-0 in the UFC, fighting at home, and is the -180 betting favorite. The UFC is clearly invested in building him up.

Robert Bryczek (18-6) is 1-1 inside the UFC after stopping veteran Brad Tavares in September 2025 for his first promotional win. The Polish middleweight is mechanically sound with heavy hands — Rowston himself acknowledged his "Polish power" and expects him to come forward. At +145, he's a legitimate upset candidate with the right shot.

Pick: Cam Rowston

Method: Decision

Rowston has been on a hot streak and the promotion is clearly invested in him — he's getting fed opponents designed to showcase what he can do. Bryczek, on the other hand, has had a mixed bag since joining the UFC, is 35 years old, and has been plagued by inactivity. The loss to Potieria aged poorly. Rowston is younger, hungrier, and has more momentum. This fight will be harder than the Brundage outing — Bryczek hits harder — but Rowston's reach, output, and top-position finishing ability should be enough to see him through over three rounds.

Junior Tafa vs. Kevin Christian

Junior Tafa (6-5) is 2-5 inside the UFC after moving down from heavyweight to 205 lbs. He's lost four of his last five, and his two most recent losses came by submission — a notable and concerning trend for someone whose entire identity is knockout power. His brother Justin won't be in his corner, which he acknowledged pre-fight stings. The crowd in Perth will be behind him, and one clean shot can still end any fight.

Kevin Christian (9-3) earned his UFC contract with a triangle armbar of Francesco Mazzeo on DWCS, then lost his UFC debut to Billy Elekana via RNC in November 2025. It's worth noting: both Tafa and Christian have lost their most recent fights to Elekana. Christian is the submission specialist of the two, with triangle armbars and RNCs spread across his recent form.

Pick: Junior Tafa

Method: KO/TKO

Look, both of these guys are bums — they're both coming off a loss to the same guy in Billy Elekana. But Tafa still has knockout power, and his loss to Elekana was not as alarming as Christian's. At 205 lbs, one clean punch from Tafa ends this cleanly, and that's his most realistic path. Christian can submit people, but he needs to get there — and Tafa landing something big before that happens is a real possibility. Tafa gets it done inside three rounds.

Jacob Malkoun vs. Gerald Meerschaert

Jacob Malkoun (9-3) came back from a nearly two-year layoff in January 2026 with a unanimous decision over Torrez Finney and has now strung together back-to-back wins. He's 5-3 inside the UFC, has never been submitted in his career, and comes in as the -1100 betting favorite — one of the most lopsided lines on the card.

Gerald Meerschaert (37-21) is 38 years old, on a four-fight losing streak, and reportedly missed weight by four pounds for the first time in his career heading into this fight. His last three losses all came by stoppage. He holds the UFC middleweight record for submission wins with 12, but that distinction feels like a relic at this point — he's been choked out, knocked out, and outpointed in consecutive outings. He's +700 on the books.

Pick: Jacob Malkoun

Method: KO/TKO

What is Meerschaert doing in the UFC at this point? He's always been a journeyman who got by on submission trickery, and now he's 38, riding a four-fight losing skid, and missing weight for the first time in his career. Everything about this situation — the weight miss, the losing streak, the age — screams that he's checked out and on his way to the door.

Malkoun has never been submitted, which neutralizes the one weapon that made Meerschaert dangerous, and the veteran's striking has been getting him stopped recently. Malkoun is going to starch him.

Colby Thicknesse vs. Vince Morales

Colby Thicknesse (8-1) is 2-1 in the UFC, bounced back from a loss to Aleksandre Topuria with a clean decision win over Josias Musasa at UFC Perth in September 2025 — on this exact same stage. He trains at a high level alongside Alexander Volkanovski and comes in as the -130 favorite with the home crowd behind him again.

Vince Morales (16-10) has gone 0-3 in the UFC. Every one of his Octagon appearances has ended in a unanimous decision loss — to Taylor Lapilus, Elijah Smith, and Raul Rosas Jr. He's lost every UFC fight he's ever taken, all by the same method, and the judges haven't been warming to whatever he's putting out there. His wrestling and Peruvian Necktie game are his best tools, but he hasn't been able to convert them into results at this level.

Pick: Colby Thicknesse

Method: Decision

Why is Vince Morales still in the UFC? He's been signed since 2024 and done nothing but lose — three fights, three identical unanimous decisions going the wrong way. The UFC is feeding him to Thicknesse on his way out the door. Thicknesse doesn't have a standout specialty, but he's the right fighter at the right time and has already proven himself on this stage. Decision.

Wes Schultz vs. Ben Johnston

Wes Schultz (8-3) made his UFC debut in February 2026 and got knocked out by Damian Pinas in the first round — got clipped by a jab-cross and just folded. He's a submission specialist with some length (6'2", 77" reach) and genuinely dangerous grappling tools — Suloev stretches, guillotines — but he needs to survive the stand-up to get there, and that's been the issue.

Ben Johnston (5-1) is making his UFC debut at 35 years old after serving as the Eternal MMA champion and going 4-1 under that banner. He trains under Eugene Bareman at City Kickboxing and has reportedly sharpened his striking under that system. He's on a four-fight win streak, is the -150 betting favorite on home soil, and his recent finishes have all come by RNC.

Pick: Ben Johnston

Method: KO/TKO

Schultz just doesn't have it. In his UFC debut against Damian Pinas, he kind of just gave up and fell to the canvas off a jab — that's not a confidence-inspiring performance heading into fight number two. He's a pure grappler, but Johnston isn't going to let him grapple. Johnston's City Kickboxing striking will keep Schultz on the outside and off-balance, and when Schultz tries to force the grappling, Johnston will be ready. Eventually Johnston clips him with something clean, and that'll be all she wrote.

Jonathan Micallef vs. Themba Gorimbo

Jonathan Micallef (9-1) is 2-0 in the UFC with two convincing wins — a decision over Kevin Jousset and a second-round RNC of Oban Elliott in January 2026. He's the Australian "Captain," hunting submissions in every fight, and holds a significant size advantage over Gorimbo at 170 lbs. He comes in as the -245 favorite.

Themba Gorimbo (14-6) got submitted in 52 seconds by Vicente Luque — an anaconda choke — at UFC 310, exposing a glaring vulnerability in his grappling defense. He then lost a decision to Jeremiah Wells in November 2025. He's 4-3 in the UFC and his main weakness is precisely what Micallef targets for a living.

Pick: Jonathan Micallef

Method: Submission

The further you get down this card, the more it becomes clear that the UFC is aggressively rebuilding the Oceania market following the decline of Adesanya and Whittaker. Micallef is another layup in that project. Gorimbo doesn't have impressive wins, and his kryptonite — grappling — is exactly what Micallef does best. The one caveat: Micallef did struggle early on the feet against Oban Elliott, so Gorimbo needs to keep this standing to have any chance. If it hits the mat, it's over. It will hit the mat. Micallef locks in the submission win.

Kody Steele vs. Dom Mar Fan

Kody Steele (7-1) is making his second UFC appearance after losing his debut to Rongzhu via decision in February 2025. He's a credentialed wrestler who works heavy from top position and lands elbows — the prototypical grappler who beats you with controlled positional pressure. He's the slight underdog, likely hurt by the home crowd factor working against him.

Dom Mar Fan (9-2) won the Road to UFC tournament with a decision over Sang Uk Kim at UFC 325 in January 2026 and is riding a five-fight win streak. The "Street Buddha" is fighting at home in Perth and has confidently predicted that once he reads Steele's timing, "it won't look close." Three of his five wins came by decision, with one quick RNC finish mixed in.

Pick: Kody Steele

Method: KO/TKO

Everything coming out of Road to UFC has been terrible, and Dom Mar Fan is no exception. Winning a regional tournament and stringing together decisions doesn't mean you're ready for this level, and Steele is exactly the kind of scrappy, physical wrestler who exposes that. Steele is going to drag this to the ground, control it, and find the finish. The "it won't look close" prediction from Fan is bold — too bold for someone facing a credentialed wrestler who's been here before.
