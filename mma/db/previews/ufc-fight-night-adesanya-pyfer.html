Overview

UFC lands in Seattle this Saturday for a card headlined by a fight that asks an uncomfortable question: is Israel Adesanya cooked? Three straight losses — the last a second-round knockout by Nassourdine Imavov — have stripped away whatever goodwill a four-year title run earned. The man who once looked untouchable at 185 is now 36, slowing down, and being matched against an up-and-coming prospect with serious finishing instincts. This is either Adesanya's last stand or the clearest sign yet that it's time to walk away.

The co-main is a rematch with a role-reversal that couldn't be more dramatic. Five years ago, Alexa Grasso beat Maycee Barber and went on to become flyweight champion. Now Barber is riding a seven-fight winning streak and enters as a -160 favorite, while Grasso is on the first two-fight skid of her career and hasn't looked sharp offensively in over a year. Time does funny things in MMA.

Beyond the headliners, the card has genuine intrigue. Michael Chiesa is taking one last walk in his retirement fight, and the UFC is giving him a hometown sendoff in the Pacific Northwest. Lerryan Douglas is making his UFC debut against Julian Erosa in what should be a banger. Mansur Abdul-Malik, one of the most quietly exciting unbeaten prospects in the promotion, takes on the kickboxing credentials of Yousri Belgaroui in a stylistically compelling middleweight clash. And Terrance McKinney is on the card, which means at least one fight will be over before you finish your first beer.


Main Card Picks

Israel Adesanya vs. Joe Pyfer

Israel Adesanya (24-5) enters this fight on a three-fight losing streak that has fundamentally rewritten his legacy. He surrendered the middleweight title to Sean Strickland via unanimous decision, got submitted by Dricus Du Plessis in the fourth round of their rematch, and was knocked out in the second round by Nassourdine Imavov in his only outing of 2025. Now 36 years old, the questions about diminishing reflexes and a compromised chin are no longer speculation — they're the story of every fight. His footwork and counter-striking are still elite when firing, but opponents have increasingly figured out how to walk through his offense and make him pay.

Joe Pyfer (15-3) is everything Adesanya's recent opponents have been: young, physical, and ascending. "Bodybagz" is ranked #14 at middleweight and riding a three-fight winning streak with wins over Kelvin Gastelum (decision), Marc-André Barriault (first-round KO), and Abus Magomedov (second-round RNC). He has heavy hands and improving wrestling — a combination that has given Adesanya problems repeatedly. Pyfer came up short against Jack Hermansson two years ago and has looked like a different, more complete fighter since. This is his biggest opportunity yet.

Pick: Joe Pyfer

Method: Submission

Win streaks mean something in MMA. Losing streaks do too. It's extremely hard to back a fighter on a three-fight skid unless there are extenuating circumstances — and for Adesanya, there aren't any. Every fight lately has followed the same script: Izzy looks good until he doesn't, and then he loses. Pyfer, on the other hand, is a prospect who had a taste of the rankings, came up short against Hermansson, and has spent the last two years proving he's not going back to that level. He has legitimate grappling to go with his power, which matters here. Pyfer will come out swinging and force Adesanya to fight off the back foot. Starting in the second round, he'll lean on the grappling, and Adesanya won't be able to hold off the attack. Submission, somewhere in the middle rounds. This is a tough one to watch for fans of the sport — Adesanya is a true legend — but the slide looks terminal.

Alexa Grasso vs. Maycee Barber

Alexa Grasso (16-5-1) and Maycee Barber (15-2) first met over five years ago at UFC 258, where Grasso earned a unanimous decision win. The trajectories since could not be more different. Grasso went on to become flyweight champion, submitted Valentina Shevchenko in one of the sport's great upsets, and was considered one of the best pound-for-pound fighters in the world. Then the wheels came off. She dropped the title back to Shevchenko in a grinding decision at UFC 306, then lost a forgettable unanimous decision to Natalia Silva in May 2025 — a fight that should have been a favorable striker matchup. Grasso landed just 18 significant strikes in 25 minutes against Shevchenko. This is the first two-fight losing streak of her career, and it's raised real questions about where she is physically.

Barber, meanwhile, has been on an absolute tear. The 27-year-old is riding a seven-fight winning streak and has evolved from a raw pressure fighter into a confident, well-rounded contender. She called for this rematch immediately after her decision win over Karine Silva at UFC 323, and it was granted. She enters as a -160 favorite, which tells you everything you need to know about how the roles have reversed.

Pick: Maycee Barber

Method: Decision

After the Shevchenko trilogy, Grasso hasn't looked the same. In that final fight, Shevchenko went for the win and got it by grinding out a lay-and-pray decision. The follow-up loss to Natalia Silva was even more alarming — that should have been a favorable striker matchup for Grasso, and she still couldn't impose herself. Those two fights revealed that Grasso isn't physically dominant enough to hang with the top dogs at flyweight anymore. Barber coming in on a seven-fight win streak isn't going to gamble on a spectacular finish — she'll be methodical, impose her size and physicality, sit on Grasso for the duration, and grind out the decision.

Michael Chiesa vs. Niko Price

Michael Chiesa (19-7) is making the final walk of his career, and the UFC is sending him out the right way — in the Pacific Northwest, roughly four hours from his hometown of Spokane Valley, Washington. It's as close to a hometown fight as the promotion can give him. Chiesa arrives on a three-fight win streak with finishes of Tony Ferguson and Max Griffin sandwiching a decision over Court McGee. At 38 years old and still stringing wins together, it's a genuinely impressive final chapter — even if the competition hasn't exactly been murderer's row.

Niko Price (16-10) stepped in on short notice after Carlston Harris withdrew, which more or less tells you what you need to know about where Price is in his career. He's on a three-fight losing streak, was finished in his most recent outing by Nikolay Veretennikov in February 2026, and carries a UFC record of 8-10. Price has always been dangerous given his unorthodox style, but he's a fighter who is clearly on the wrong side of his career.

Pick: Michael Chiesa

Method: Decision

It's genuinely wild that 38-year-old Chiesa is on a three-fight win streak in 2026, but here we are. He's been the beneficiary of favorable matchups for a while now, and this one is no different. Niko Price is dogshit — riding a three-fight losing streak, with his most recent loss coming via vicious knockout just last month. If Carlston Harris hadn't pulled out, Price probably gets cut from the UFC. Chiesa won't have the power or the finishing grappling to put Price away, but he'll outpoint him comfortably. Expect a sloppy fight where both guys gas after the first round, with Chiesa doing just enough to get the decision.

Julian Erosa vs. Lerryan Douglas

Julian Erosa (31-12) is a divisional mainstay who has put together an 8-4 run in his third UFC stint, racking up finishes over Darren Elkins, Christian Rodriguez, and Ricardo Ramos along the way. He's coming off a decision loss to Melquizael Costa and fighting on home turf — he's from Yakima, Washington — which means he'll be extra fired up. Erosa is a submission threat at all times and loves chaotic, ugly exchanges.

Lerryan Douglas (13-5) is making his UFC debut after earning his contract on Dana White's Contender Series with a 36-second KO of Cam Teague. He's on a five-fight win streak, is a former LFA featherweight champion, and is as hyped a debutant as the 145-pound division has seen in a while. Douglas is a hard puncher with a legitimate finish rate — exactly the kind of guy Erosa loves to fight, and exactly the kind who can make Erosa pay for recklessness.

Pick: Lerryan Douglas

Method: KO/TKO

There's real hype around Douglas coming into this one, and Erosa simply isn't good enough to derail it. Douglas will come out and scratch Erosa in the first round.

Mansur Abdul-Malik vs. Yousri Belgaroui

Mansur Abdul-Malik (9-0-1) is one of the most intriguing unbeaten prospects in the UFC. Every professional win has come by stoppage, and his UFC run — three wins and an overturned draw — has featured increasingly dominant performances. The UFC has gotten behind him, but most of his opponents have been feeder-level competition. This is the first fight where someone can legitimately make that argument stick.

Yousri Belgaroui (9-3) is not your typical UFC debutant. A former Glory kickboxer with elite-level striking credentials, he's one of Alex Pereira's main training partners — which tells you the level he's operating at on the feet. He made his UFC debut with a TKO of Azamat Bekoev and is riding a four-fight win streak. There's a massive difference between good MMA strikers and elite kickboxers: the technical precision, the defense, the combination variety. Belgaroui brings all of that.

Pick: Yousri Belgaroui

Method: Decision

Abdul-Malik has been fed cans since joining the UFC, and this is where that catches up to him. If he goes out there and tries to strike with Belgaroui, he's going to get slaughtered on the feet — elite kickboxers operate at a completely different technical level than MMA strikers, and Belgaroui's background puts him in rare company. Abdul-Malik's only real path is to incorporate grappling early and test whether Belgaroui's takedown defense holds up under sustained pressure. That's a question worth watching. But this feels like a rude awakening for a prospect the UFC has been protecting.

Terrance McKinney vs. Kyle Nelson

Terrance McKinney (17-8) holds a record that defies belief: every single one of his 17 wins and 8 losses has ended inside the distance. He has the shortest average fight time of anyone in UFC lightweight history. McKinney is fighting on home turf — he confirmed the fight on social media saying he's "fighting in my home state" — and is coming off a first-round submission loss to Chris Duncan in December 2025 that snapped a two-fight win streak. The loss doesn't change what McKinney is: pure chaos, all the time.

Kyle Nelson (17-6-1) quietly turned his UFC career around after starting 1-4 in the promotion. He's gone 4-1 in his last five, including a one-sided decision over Matt Frevola most recently. Nelson is more methodical than McKinney and has shown he can grind out decisions, but he's never been tested by anyone with McKinney's explosiveness.

Pick: Terrance McKinney

Method: KO/TKO

McKinney is all action, all the time — it doesn't matter who he fights. Nelson just isn't good enough to hang with him. He hasn't been able to make it competitive against top competition in the division, and McKinney is a step above the Canadian. McKinney's going to charge forward, crack Nelson early, and finish the job in the first round.

Prelims Picks

Ignacio Bahamondes vs. Tofiq Musayev

Ignacio Bahamondes (17-6) is stepping in on three weeks' notice, but this isn't a desperation booking — he's a legitimate lightweight who has fought solid competition in Rafael Fiziev, Jalin Turner, and Manuel Torres. The Chilean is a creative, rangy striker with dangerous submissions from unorthodox positions, and he's coming off a decision loss to Fiziev that snapped a three-fight finishing streak.

Tofiq Musayev (22-6) is a former RIZIN champion with 20 finishes in 22 career wins, including 18 knockouts — one of the most prolific KO artists at lightweight. The only problem: his UFC debut was a disaster. He was submitted by Myktybek Orolbai via kimura in the first round last June, and the only reason he's still in the promotion is that Orolbai missed weight. The two also fought on the same card in Baku last June — Bahamondes dropping a decision to Fiziev, Musayev getting tapped. Now they're paired together, which has an odd redemption arc energy to it.

Pick: Ignacio Bahamondes

Method: Submission

Musayev has no business being in the UFC. He's 36, lost his debut to Myktybek Orolbai, and the only reason he's still here is that Orolbai missed weight. Bahamondes has fought genuinely competitive lightweights in Fiziev, Turner, and Torres. Musayev hasn't faced anyone at that level inside the Octagon. "Nacho" is going to blast through this fraud and submit him in the first or second round.

Chase Hooper vs. Lance Gibson Jr.

Chase Hooper (16-4-1) is fighting in Washington state for the first time since 2018, which makes this a homecoming of sorts. He had built a five-fight winning streak at lightweight — beating the likes of Jim Miller, Clay Guida, and Viacheslav Borshchev — before Alexander Hernandez stopped him via first-round KO last August. About 75% of his stoppages come by submission, and he's been one of the more impressive grappling-based lightweights on the roster since moving up to 155.

Lance Gibson Jr. (9-2) has a compelling backstory — born in Seattle, son of Canadian MMA pioneer Lance Gibson Sr., making them only the third father-son duo in UFC history. He gave a decent account of himself on incredibly short notice against King Green in his UFC debut, dropping a split decision. Now with a full camp, he'll be looking to show what he's actually capable of. He has submission skills of his own with RNC and guillotine finishes on his record.

Pick: Chase Hooper

Method: Decision

Lance Gibson Jr. is trash. He lost to Bobby Green when Green took the fight on short notice, and it wasn't even close. Chase Hooper is better than Bobby Green in terms of grappling aptitude. Hooper will latch onto Gibson like a parasite, threaten submissions all night, and grind out the decision — even if he can't quite get the finish.

Marcin Tybura vs. Tyrell Fortune

Marcin Tybura (27-10) has been a fixture in the UFC heavyweight division for the better part of a decade, fighting everyone from Alistair Overeem to Tai Tuivasa. He's a technically sound fighter with legitimate wrestling and submission skills, and he's alternated wins and losses against quality competition throughout his career. He's coming off a first-round KO loss to Ante Delija in Paris last September and will be looking to bounce back.

Tyrell Fortune (17-3) is making his UFC debut on a three-fight win streak. The Portland, Oregon native is a former NJCAA and NCAA Division II wrestling champion who also spent time on the U.S. national wrestling team — elite credentials on paper. He trains alongside surging contender Waldo Cortes-Acosta and enters as a slight betting favorite. The question is where the UFC found this guy, because he was cut from the PFL before landing here.

Pick: Marcin Tybura

Method: Decision

Where does the UFC get these guys? Fortune was cut from the PFL — he has no business being here. Tybura isn't world-beater material either, but he has far more relevant experience and meaningful wins on his résumé than anything Fortune has done. This is going to be a long, sloppy heavyweight decision that Tybura grinds out on experience alone.

Casey O'Neill vs. Gabriella Fernandes

Casey O'Neill (10-2) is ranked #11 at flyweight and returning after over a year away from competition. She's a technically precise striker who trains at Tiger Muay Thai and looked good in her decision win over Luana Santos at UFC 305 in August 2024. Her last three fights tell a concerning story though: a loss to Ariane Lipski via armbar, a loss to Jennifer Maia on the cards, and then the Santos win — with both losses coming against fighters who have since been cut from the UFC.

Gabriella Fernandes (11-3) is ranked #15 and on a three-fight winning streak with wins over Julija Stoliarenko, Wang Cong — a highly touted prospect with a win over Valentina Shevchenko in Muay Thai — and Carli Judice. Fernandes has been active, sharp, and improving.

Pick: Gabriella Fernandes

Method: Submission

O'Neill is 1-2 in her last three fights, and both of those losses came against fighters who were subsequently cut from the UFC. That's a damning indictment of where she is right now. Add over a year of inactivity on top of that, and the rust concerns are real. Fernandes is active, on a three-fight streak, and beat Wang Cong — a legitimate prospect. The edge in activity and recent form goes to Fernandes, and she'll exploit it with a submission finish.

Navajo Stirling vs. Bruno Lopes

Navajo Stirling (8-0) is a New Zealand product of City Kickboxing — the same gym as Israel Adesanya and Carlos Ulberg — and is 3-0 in the UFC after wins over Tuco Tokkos, Ivan Erslan, and Rodolfo Bellato. The undefeated light heavyweight has been dominant in all three promotional appearances, scoring a unanimous decision in each. He's one of the more intriguing prospects in a division that desperately needs them.

Bruno Lopes (14-2) went 1-1 in his UFC stint: a unanimous decision win over Magomed Gadzhiyasulov in his debut, followed by a first-round knockout loss to Dustin Jacoby in May 2025. Jacoby exposed Lopes's chin, and that's a hard thing to unsee when evaluating him going forward.

Pick: Navajo Stirling

Method: Decision

Stirling is hot coming into this fight after a dominant decision over Rodolfo Bellato. Lopes has some experience but Jacoby made quick work of him, and he hasn't faced top-level competition since joining the UFC. Stirling will piece Lopes up on the feet and come away with a dominant 30-27 decision.

Ricky Simón vs. Adrian Yanez

Ricky Simón (22-7) is a classic bantamweight gatekeeper who calls Vancouver, Washington home — practically a local for this card. He's fought a who's who of the division with wins over Merab Dvalishvili, Jack Shore, Javid Basharat, and Raphael Assuncao. He's coming off a decision loss to Raoni Barcelos in November 2025, stepped in on short notice here, and his wrestling remains elite: at least two takedowns in 10 of his last 12 fights.

Adrian Yanez (17-6) was red-hot when he entered the UFC, rattling off five straight victories. He's gone 1-4 since, with losses to Rob Font, Jonathan Martinez, and Daniel Marcos revealing the limits of what he can do against fighters who neutralize his striking. His 81% takedown defense looks impressive on paper but is inflated by weak competition — Marcos took him down twice in four attempts, and Marcos is not a wrestler.

Pick: Ricky Simón

Method: KO/TKO

Simón is a classic gatekeeper — he fights tough competition, usually comes up short against the elite, but turns away everyone who isn't clearly better than him. Yanez fits squarely in that category: his losses to Marcos, Martinez, and Font don't stack up against anyone on Simón's résumé. Simón is fighting in front of his home crowd, and the wrestling is going to be the deciding factor. Yanez's takedown defense won't hold up.

Alexia Thainara vs. Bruna Brasil

Alexia Thainara (13-1) is ascending fast. She earned her UFC contract on Dana White's Contender Series in September 2024, debuted on seven days' notice at UFC Fight Night: Evloev vs. Murphy in March 2025 and submitted Molly McCann in the first round, then followed it up with a unanimous decision over Loma Lookboonmee in September. She's 2-0 in the UFC and on an 11-fight win streak. The only loss on her entire record came six years ago — a guillotine choke finish by one Bruna Brasil at Thunder Fight 20 in 2019. So yes, this is technically a rematch.

Bruna Brasil (11-6-1) is fighting for the second time in under two months, having just dropped a decision to Ketlen Souza on February 7. She stepped in as a late replacement after the original opponent withdrew. Brasil holds the historical win over Thainara, but she's gone 2-3 in her last five and has been on a losing run that makes that 2019 result feel like ancient history.

Pick: Alexia Thainara

Method: Decision

Thainara is a hot prospect who has been making waves since her debut. Her win over Loma Lookboonmee is more impressive than anything on Brasil's recent résumé, and Brasil is less aggressive and less dynamic than Thainara at this stage of both their careers. Brasil is also fighting twice in two months on a losing skid. Thainara controls this fight and gets the decision.
