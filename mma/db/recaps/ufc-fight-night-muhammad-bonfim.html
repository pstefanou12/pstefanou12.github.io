Summary

UFC Fight Night: Muhammad vs. Bonfim was a pretty solid Apex card. There were genuine stakes on the line, reasonable matchups across the board, and a handful of clean finishes courtesy of some favorable mismatches. Not a show that will be remembered for years, but one that delivered on most of what it promised.


The biggest storyline coming out of this night has nothing to do with who won. It is what Belal Muhammad's performance means for everything that came before him. Belal held UFC gold, looked untouchable against Leon Edwards, and was supposed to be among the elite of the welterweight division. Now he is on a three-fight losing streak, getting walked down and picked apart by Gabriel Bonfim for 25 minutes without ever seriously threatening a takedown. A serious fall from grace. And if that is where Belal is at right now, it raises some uncomfortable questions about the generation he came up in. Was Usman ever that good? Was Leon any good? Was Colby? The new wave of welterweights is significantly better than the old guard, and Friday night made that uncomfortably clear.


The one real gripe with the card is organizational. The UFC has two Apex shows flanking the White House card next weekend, and both have solid matchups worth watching. The smarter move would have been to combine them into one real event, take it on the road, and give fans something memorable. A Japan Fight Night with Muhammad, Kape, Horiguchi, Allen, Ziam, Ewing, Bia Mesquita, and Luana Santos would have been a legitimately stacked card. Instead, we get two competent Apex shows in the same week. Fewer events with more weight behind each one is always the better product.


Final Score: 7.1/10



Main Card

Gabriel Bonfim vs. Belal Muhammad

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 5 Rounds, 25:00 Total

Belal Muhammad came in as the former UFC Welterweight Champion, making this a de facto number one contender fight with major title implications. After losing the belt, Belal needed a statement win to prove he still belonged at the top of the division. Gabriel Bonfim was looking to announce himself as a legitimate title threat.


From the opening bell, Bonfim was the aggressor, pressing forward and forcing Belal to operate off the cage. Belal found early success with a sharp right hand that stung the Brazilian, but it would prove to be one of the few times he landed with authority. Bonfim quickly settled into a rhythm, clipping Belal with left hooks and systematically shutting down whatever offense Belal tried to generate. By the end of round one, Belal's output had dropped to nearly nothing.


The second round offered a brief window of hope. Bonfim's pace dipped, his output fell off, and Belal landed some clean shots including a nice left hand. If he was going to turn the tide, getting the fight to the ground was the obvious path. He tried exactly that to open the third, shooting for a takedown immediately out of the corner, but Bonfim used the cage to stuff it cleanly and they reset.


From there, it got ugly. Bonfim cracked Belal so hard at one point that his mouthpiece went flying across the octagon. Herb Dean stopped the action to retrieve it. Belal's face was getting progressively bloodier as the rounds wore on, and the grappling that built his entire career simply never materialized, whether because he couldn't get it going or chose not to for reasons that remain unclear. In rounds four and five, Belal was just getting walked down and absorbing punishment without offering any meaningful response.


Bonfim won a clear unanimous decision, and the bigger question is what this means for Belal. He is now on a three-fight losing streak, and if his contract were up right now, it is hard to imagine the UFC offering him a renewal. A serious fall from grace for a man who held championship gold not long ago. It also forces an uncomfortable question about the generation he came up in: Belal mopped the floor with Leon Edwards, but now he is getting picked apart by the next wave. Was Usman ever really that good? Was Leon? Was Colby? The new welterweights are on a different level, and that reality hit hard on Friday night. As for Bonfim, it is hard to know exactly what to make of him yet. Give him a real top-5 opponent and find out.

Brendan Allen vs. Edmen Shahbazyan

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Brendan Allen came in on a solid winning streak and was looking for a result that could push him into title contention. Edmen Shahbazyan, once one of the most hyped prospects in the middleweight division, was trying to prove he still belonged at this level.


Both fighters spent round one feeling each other out, though Allen was the quicker one to establish himself, stinging Shahbazyan with sharp shots on the feet. Edmen looked slow and lethargic from the jump, getting caught clean whenever he moved forward. He stuffed Allen's takedown attempt and landed some shots toward the end of the round, but the first clearly belonged to Allen.


The second continued along the same lines. Shahbazyan was largely stationary, standing in front of Allen and letting him dictate the terms of the fight. His footwork was non-existent. Allen cut Edmen over the eye with an elbow and kept landing cleanly from both the clinch and at range. Edmen got off a decent jab and a nice right, and the two traded on the feet, but every time Allen connected, Edmen's reaction was noticeably more pronounced.


In the third, Allen just walked Shahbazyan down and kept piecing him up. Both men were getting bloodied up by this point, and Allen added some nasty elbows in the clinch before landing a takedown in the final moments. Edmen scrambled back to his feet and the two were swinging wildly in the last ten seconds.


Allen got the unanimous decision, though the win came with some caveats. His kicks didn't look particularly sharp, and he couldn't get his grappling going consistently. Still, he is on a three-fight win streak and has earned a real test. With Caio out of his scheduled fight with Pyfer, the options are there: if Allen is healthy, the Pyfer fight makes sense. If the DDP matchup is available, take that. He needs a top-ranked opponent at this point, and he has done enough to deserve one.


As for Shahbazyan, this was a tough watch. He tires quickly, his footwork is poor, his knockout power is nowhere near what the hype suggested, and he doesn't have any meaningful grappling to fall back on. For a fighter who was supposed to be a future champion not long ago, the current reality is a hard one to reconcile.

Tom Nolan vs. Fares Ziam

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Fares Ziam came in riding a win streak looking to keep momentum going. Tom Nolan was the relatively unknown quantity, but he made his presence felt from the jump.


Nolan opened with some heavy body kicks, but Ziam was quick to drag the fight to the ground, taking Nolan's back early in the first. Nolan worked his way to the cage and got back upright, then completely flipped the script: he body locked Ziam and slammed him to the canvas, worked from inside guard with Nolan keeping him pinned up against the fence, then picked him up and dumped him a second time after Ziam briefly scrambled and got to the back. Ziam tried to slam Nolan in return but couldn't off-balance him, and Nolan stalled out the rest of the round in a body lock.


In the second, Ziam landed a clean 1-2 early, but Nolan answered with a knee and kept pressing. The fight ended up back on the ground after Ziam spun for an elbow and somehow went down in the process, leaving him on the canvas with Nolan on top landing good elbows. Ziam attempted a kimura from the bottom, made it to the cage, and got back to his feet, though Nolan controlled the clinch throughout. Ziam tried to close the round strong but whiffed on the key shots.


The third followed the same pattern: Nolan shutting down Ziam's striking attempts and repeatedly getting him back to the cage and the canvas. Ziam managed to lift Nolan and get him down twice, but struggled to control him from top position and fumbled the position back to Nolan both times. The fight ended with the two of them scrambling on the canvas and neither man with a clear advantage.


Nolan picked up the unanimous decision and looked solid doing it. He is not particularly dominant in any one area, but he is genuinely good all around, which makes him dangerous. Watching this fight makes you wonder what is going on with fighters like Michael Chandler and Paddy Pimblett sitting in the rankings. Both Nolan and Ziam look like they beat those guys. As for Ziam, this was a tough loss that snaps his win streak and likely pushes him back down the rankings to rebuild.

Bryce Mitchell vs. Santiago Luna

Result: Submission, Arm Triangle Choke at 4:52 Round 3 of 3, 14:52 Total

Santiago Luna deserves credit before anything else: he stepped in on a week's notice and put his undefeated record on the line. That kind of willingness to take a fight on short notice against a veteran like Bryce Mitchell is admirable. The fight itself, however, was not.


Mitchell got the takedown in the first round after Luna initially denied it, and from there the round turned into an extended grappling grind. Mitchell moved through side control and into mount, threatened a rear naked choke from the back, and landed some elbows late. The problem was that he was not really doing anything with those dominant positions. Luna was landing small strikes from the bottom and Bryce was not responding. Not dangerous, not finishing, just holding.


The second round was more of the same. Mitchell dragged the fight back to the canvas, Luna scrambled on top, Bryce threatened a triangle from the bottom and Luna abandoned the position to let him back up. Some scrappy exchanges on the feet before Mitchell got it back to the ground again and moved back into side control. Nothing happened.


The third opened with some actual chaos, and Luna briefly turned the tables, getting Mitchell down and threatening a d'arc choke that Mitchell had to work to survive. After that, Bryce got body to body and proceeded to do absolutely nothing with it. The referee probably should have stood them up. Mitchell eventually worked to the back, threatened a neck crank, abandoned it, and then had Luna completely laid out on the canvas without throwing a single meaningful piece of ground and pound. He finally locked up an arm triangle and got the tap with eight seconds left in the round.


Mitchell got the win, but that was a painful fight to watch. Incredibly boring, insanely uneventful, and a reminder that the UFC does not need another lay-and-pray fighter taking up roster space. Luna showed real heart stepping up on short notice, and he gave Mitchell genuine problems throughout. He deserves another shot in a fight he actually has time to prepare for.

Iwo Baraniewski vs. Junior Tafa

Result: KO/TKO, Leg Kick and Ground Strikes at 1:25 Round 1 of 3

Iwo Baraniewski came in with a reputation for finishing fights fast and a perfect round-one finish streak to protect. Junior Tafa was the latest name on the list trying to survive past the opening bell.


The fight started with both men feeling each other out, Baraniewski shooting in briefly for a clinch before resetting. Then it ended. A low kick from Baraniewski completely snapped Tafa's leg, and Baraniewski followed up immediately with ground and pound to force the stoppage at 1:25 of the first round.


The round-one finish streak stays alive, and Baraniewski continues to look like a real problem for the light heavyweight division. He needs to be put in front of an actual ranked opponent so we can find out where his ceiling is. As for Tafa, he will head back to Australia and wait for the next Aussie card to roll around.

Prelims

Alessandro Costa vs. Matt Schnell

Result: KO/TKO, Punches at 2:32 Round 1 of 3

Both fighters were tentative early, but Matt Schnell was the slightly more active one, advancing forward and throwing feints. Alessandro Costa caught him clean, and the referee stepped in to stop the fight at 2:32 of the first round.


This one came with an asterisk. Schnell went down and got up quickly, which raised the question of whether the stoppage was premature. We have seen Schnell genuinely comatose inside the octagon before, but on this night he was back on his feet fast. Herb probably should have let him eat a couple more hammer fists for good measure before waving it off. That said, Schnell was not advancing the position from the bottom, and it was genuinely hard to tell whether he was out or just rocked.


A nice win for Costa regardless of the circumstances. As for Schnell, the stoppage controversy does not change much. He is fucking trash, and no amount of ref charity was going to change where his career is headed.

Marcus McGhee vs. John Yannis

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Marcus McGhee came in looking like a man who has been fighting at a high level for a long time. John Yannis came in looking like someone who has not.


McGhee controlled the center of the octagon from the opening bell, staying composed and picking his spots while Yannis scrambled around the cage. McGhee threw a couple of spinning back kicks, landed some solid combinations, and generally looked the more polished and technical fighter. Yannis had his moments: he stung McGhee with a straight right in the first and cracked him again with a right hook early in the second. He even had McGhee briefly on the back foot against the fence in round two after some big shots. But he could not sustain the pressure, and McGhee's combination work kept the scorecards tilted his way.


By the third, Yannis turned timid. He controlled the center but stopped committing, stopped going for it, and let McGhee continue to work on the outside and pick up points. A late takedown attempt from McGhee was shrugged off easily, but it barely mattered at that point.


McGhee took a clear unanimous decision. He looks technically sharp and experienced, and it is time to find out what he can do against ranked competition. A matchup with Song Yadong or Payton Talbott makes sense as the next step. Yannis just did not have the experience or the finishing ability to close the show when he had McGhee hurt.

Edgar Cháirez vs. Bruno Silva

Result: Submission, Rear Naked Choke at 4:13 Round 1 of 3

The "Charles Johnson KO curse" had been following Bruno Silva around for a while, with the theory being that anyone Johnson knocked out was destined to get finished next. Silva came in looking to debunk it. He did, technically, though not in the way he would have wanted.


Both men traded on the feet early, with Edgar Cháirez immediately imposing a serious reach advantage. Cháirez landed a nice right hand that stumbled Silva, who recovered quickly. A jab put Silva on the canvas briefly before he got back to his feet. Cháirez continued walking Silva down and controlling the center, and then landed a right that put Silva down for good. He followed to the ground and locked in a neck crank submission to finish the fight at 4:13 of the first round.


Cháirez is long, dangerous, and now has a submission finish to go alongside his striking. He is going to be a problem for the flyweight division for a long time, and he deserves a bump in competition immediately. Someone in the 10-to-15 ranked range makes sense, and a Moreno matchup could be a natural next step. As for Silva, the curse is technically broken, but he is 36 years old in a division that is not kind to aging men. It is hard to know what the path forward looks like from here.

Chelsea Chandler vs. Priscila Cachoeira

Result: Submission, Armbar at 3:42 Round 1 of 3

Priscila Cachoeira came out aggressive, moving forward and getting Chelsea Chandler up against the fence early, landing some bombs in the process. Chandler went down to the canvas and was throwing kicks up from the bottom before the referee stepped in and made her get back to her feet.


From there, the fight flipped completely. Chandler wobbled Cachoeira and put her on the canvas, worked to the back, and started looking for the rear naked choke. Cachoeira survived somehow, but Chandler transitioned to a pseudo mount, then quickly moved to an armbar and got the tap at 3:42 of the first round.


Hard to take much away from this one. Chandler looked terrible on the feet, genuinely bad, but the moment she got the fight to the ground it was completely one-sided and game over. It might be time for Cachoeira to seriously consider hanging it up. That was a tough loss, and the ground game was just nowhere close to being competitive.

Joanderson Brito vs. Jordan Leavitt

Result: Submission, Front Choke at 4:19 Round 1 of 3

Jordan Leavitt came in as a grappler whose entire game plan was built around getting the fight to the ground. That made what happened next all the more damning.


Leavitt spent the early portion of the round trying to grapple, getting Brito up against the fence and working for control in over-unders. Joanderson Brito reversed the position and separated, then started walking Leavitt down and landing clean shots on the feet. Leavitt shot for a takedown, and Brito locked in a ninja choke and got the tap at 4:19 of the first round.


Brito looked sharp in all areas. He held his own on the ground against a dedicated grappler and finished the fight with a submission. That is a well-rounded performance and he deserves a step up in competition. As for Leavitt, that was bad. When your entire identity as a fighter is grappling and you get submitted by a striker, it is time to have some serious conversations. It might be time to look at retirement.

Jeisla Chaves vs. Yuneisy Duben

Result: Decision, Split at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

The size and length disparity between these two was obvious from the first exchange. Jeisla Chaves had a clear reach advantage, and Yuneisy Duben had to cover a lot of ground just to get in range, which meant she was eating shots along the way.


In round one, Chaves controlled the range and pieced Duben up on the feet. Duben went for a takedown, Chaves got to the fence and returned to her feet quickly, and they went back to trading. Chaves landed a jab, then some nasty strikes, and when Duben tried an overhand right, Chaves absorbed it with a smile. Duben went for another takedown and ate a tomahawk elbow on the way in before they clinched up against the cage.


In the second, Duben managed to get the fight to the fence and pushed for takedowns, but Chaves defended and stayed upright. When Chaves did shoot, she got Duben to the canvas, worked to the back, and went for a neck crank. Duben fought the hands, Chaves abandoned the submission attempt, and continued landing ground and pound from the back.


The third was more of the same on the feet before Chaves landed a right hand that put Duben on the canvas briefly. Duben got back up, Chaves dragged her down again, worked to the back and threatened a rear naked choke. Duben scrambled, reversed, and slammed Chaves down with about two minutes left, but could not do much damage from top position, and Chaves pulled her tight to neutralize whatever ground and pound Duben tried to land.


Chaves picked up the split decision, and she showed some genuine skills: good length, dangerous on the feet, and capable of getting the fight to the ground. The weaknesses are real though. Her submission game is thin and she looks completely out of her element when she has to finish on the canvas. She also kept looking to her corner for direction throughout, which reads as someone still very green. As for Duben, she just looked small out there. She probably needs to move down a weight class. The flyweights are simply too big for her.

Ketlen Souza vs. Ariane Carnelossi

Result: KO/TKO, Head Kick at 1:34 Round 1 of 3

Ariane Carnelossi came out aggressive and moving forward, pushing Ketlen Souza up against the fence and looking to impose herself early. She was doing decent work, getting her offense going and controlling the early stages of the round.


Then Souza bided her time, clawed back some space, stung Carnelossi on the feet, and put her out cold with a head kick at 1:34 of the first round. Nasty finish.


Carnelossi looked good early but could not capitalize on her aggression, and that was all Souza needed. One opening, one head kick, lights out. Clean, brutal, and efficient.
