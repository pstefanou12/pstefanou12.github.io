Overview

UFC Fight Night: Muhammad vs. Bonfim is a solid mid-tier card built around a genuinely compelling welterweight main event and a frustrating amount of mismatched booking underneath it. It is the kind of show that rewards watching because the main storyline actually means something, even if the promotional logic for several fights on this card is difficult to square.

The headline is Belal Muhammad, the former UFC welterweight champion who has fallen on hard times. After a dominant title defense over Leon Edwards in July 2024, Muhammad dropped the belt to Jack Della Maddalena and then added a decision loss to Ian Machado Garry in November 2025, sending him into this fight on a two-fight slide and outside the title picture. His opponent, Gabriel Bonfim, arrives with four straight wins and real momentum, but there are legitimate questions about the quality of those performances. His split decision win over Stephen Wonderboy Thompson is widely considered a robbery, and his second-round TKO of Randy Brown was called early by many observers. Muhammad needs this win to stay relevant; Bonfim needs it to force a title shot conversation. The razor-thin odds reflect how genuinely even this matchup is on paper.

Underneath the main event, Brendan Allen faces Edmen Shahbazyan in a fight that says more about the UFC's matchmaking priorities than it does about where Allen stands. After stopping Reinier de Ridder and knocking off Marvin Vettori, Allen deserves at minimum a title eliminator. Instead he gets Shahbazyan, a guy who got submitted by Gerald Meerschaert a year and a half ago and whose recent wins have come against lower-tier opposition. If Allen wins, which he should, he needs to be given Pyfer or Du Plessis next. Anything less would be hard to justify.

Elsewhere on the main card, Iwo Baraniewski is becoming must-watch television. The undefeated Polish light heavyweight has finished his last three opponents in under two minutes combined: 28 seconds over Austen Lane, 89 seconds over Ibo Aslan, and 20 seconds over Mahamed Aly. Junior Tafa is his opponent here, a short-notice replacement stepping in off consecutive submission losses. Expect this to be brief and violent. Farès Ziam also continues to be poorly served by the matchmakers, sitting on a six-fight winning streak but once again facing an unranked opponent in Tom Nolan instead of a top-15 name that would prove his place in the division.

The prelims offer a couple of fights worth tracking. Alessandro Costa is in on nine days' notice to face a declining Matt Schnell, whose three recent choke losses point to a technical vulnerability that Costa is built to exploit. At bantamweight, Marcus McGhee handles a short-notice John Yannis in what should be a clean bounce-back performance after his loss to Petr Yan. The card closes out with a pair of women's fights: Jordan Leavitt should smother Joanderson Brito in a style matchup that plays directly to his strengths, and Yuneisy Duben is an interesting live dog against the unbeaten but unconvincing Jeisla Chaves.


Main Card Picks

Belal Muhammad vs. Gabriel Bonfim

Belal Muhammad (24-5) enters this fight at a crossroads. The former UFC welterweight champion won the title with a dominant decision over Leon Edwards in July 2024 but lost it to Jack Della Maddalena at UFC 315 and followed that with a decision loss to Ian Machado Garry in November 2025. Now ranked #5, a loss here would push him entirely out of the title conversation. His opponent, Gabriel Bonfim (19-1), has won four straight, most recently finishing Randy Brown with a clinch knee in round two. His only UFC loss came to Nicolas Dalby in 2023; since then he has been precise and dangerous.

The matchup is classic wrestler versus striker. Muhammad will look to close distance, secure clinch ties, and grind Bonfim into the fence. Bonfim's path requires disciplined footwork, clean exits from the clinch, and landing power shots in space. The odds are razor-thin, reflecting how even this looks on paper.

Pick: Belal Muhammad

Method: Decision

Bonfim's recent momentum comes with a serious asterisk. His split decision win over Stephen Wonderboy Thompson is widely considered a robbery, and his second-round TKO of Randy Brown was called early by a significant portion of observers. That controversy makes the hype around him feel unjustified and suggests he is getting bumped in competition before it has been fully earned. Stylistically, Bonfim is primarily a striker, and that is the game he will want to play against Muhammad.

Muhammad, however, is a well-rounded veteran who earned a UFC title with a dominant win over Leon Edwards. His recent losses have not been blowouts. He went five rounds with Della Maddalena and nearly stole the decision, and Garry has since proven himself to be one of the best welterweights on the planet. Neither loss reflects a fighter in serious decline. Since moving training camps, Muhammad has shown sharper discipline and fewer mental errors. He will mix in his wrestling consistently, and Bonfim, with no real history of shutting down high-level grappling, will find himself reacting rather than dictating. Once the takedown threat is established, Muhammad's striking output becomes cleaner as Bonfim focuses on defending the level change. Veteran craft, control time, and a well-rounded gameplan carry Muhammad to a dominant decision.

Brendan Allen vs. Edmen Shahbazyan

Brendan Allen (26-7) is ranked #4 at middleweight and is on a two-fight win streak after a rough three-fight skid. He stopped Reinier de Ridder at the end of round four and beat Marvin Vettori on the scorecards before that. His BJJ black belt and ability to take backs remain his sharpest weapons, and he has publicly stated he is gunning for a Sean Strickland title rematch after this. His opponent, Edmen Shahbazyan (16-5), was once seen as a prodigy before a string of losses derailed his trajectory. He has since put together three straight wins, including a first-round ground-and-pound stoppage of André Muniz at UFC 320 in October 2025. He has 13 career KO/TKO finishes and real hand speed. His one recent vulnerability was a submission loss to Gerald Meerschaert in August 2024.

The fight hinges on whether Allen can weather Shahbazyan's early striking and get to positions he controls. Shahbazyan carries one-punch KO power and if he catches Allen in a bad exchange in the first round, an upset is on the table. But once Allen closes distance and gets to the mat or the clinch, his back-taking and submission threat become overwhelming.

Pick: Brendan Allen

Method: Decision

The matchmaking here reflects poorly on the UFC. Allen took a tough fight against de Ridder on three weeks' notice, finished him, and the best the promotion could offer as a reward is Shahbazyan, a fighter outside the top rankings whose most credible recent loss came via submission to Meerschaert. It is not a fight that moves the needle toward a title shot, and it is hard to see how Shahbazyan wins it.

Shahbazyan's style is straightforward: he goes in early, throws with bad intentions, and tries to end things in the first round. Allen has been caught early before, against Chris Curtis and Sean Strickland earlier in his career, but he has learned. He went five rounds with Nassourdine Imavov, who knocked out Israel Adesanya, and showed he can survive adversity and execute a game plan over distance. With the training environment he is now in alongside Muhammad, Allen has cut down on the mental lapses that got him in trouble. If Shahbazyan does not get him out of there early, Allen will drag this fight to the mat, pin him down, work his back-taking sequences, and grind out a dominant decision. The most logical outcome for Allen after this is a matchup with Pyfer or Du Plessis for a legitimate title eliminator shot.

Fares Ziam vs. Tom Nolan

Farès Ziam (18-4) has put together one of the more quietly impressive runs at 155 lbs, winning six straight with a mix of distance kickboxing, chain wrestling, and violent elbow work in the clinch. His recent wins over Nazim Sadykhov (elbows and punches, round two), Mike Davis, and Matt Frevola (knee to the head) show a fighter who has expanded his game beyond the pure striker he once was. Ranked in the top 15, he is overdue for a matchup that tests his ceiling. His opponent, Tom Nolan (10-1), is a 26-year-old Australian on a four-fight winning streak with a mix of finishes and decisions since his only career loss, a first-round KO to Nikolas Motta in January 2024. His RNC of Charlie Campbell in September 2025 showed grappling ability on top of his size and reach advantages.

Ziam's six-fight streak and all-around evolution make him a clear favorite. Nolan brings youth, momentum, and a 74-inch reach that could create problems at range. The question is whether Nolan's experience base is deep enough to handle a Ziam who attacks from everywhere.

Pick: Farès Ziam

Method: KO/TKO

This matchup continues a pattern: Ziam keeps winning and the UFC keeps feeding him opponents who are not ranked. Nolan is a solid prospect, but he is greener, less experienced at the top level, and being asked to step up significantly against a fighter with six straight wins and a fully evolved skillset. The gap in experience and versatility is real.

Ziam does not have one path to victory, which is what makes him dangerous for an unproven opponent. He can box at range, attack in the clinch with elbows, and chain into wrestling when the moment calls for it. Nolan will have a difficult time building a consistent game plan against that kind of variety. Expect Ziam to find the finish in the first three rounds, either through accumulated damage on the feet or a sharp clinch finish. If he does, the UFC needs to put him in against Moicano, Ortega, or Chandler next. Ziam has the tools to be a genuine star in France and in the division, and the matchmakers need to stop protecting the top 10 from him.

Bryce Mitchell vs. Santiago Luna

Bryce Mitchell (18-3) made his bantamweight debut in July 2025 with a unanimous decision win over Said Nurmagomedov, a grappling-heavy fighter who had competed in Abu Dhabi. His identity has always been deep wrestling and suffocating top control, and the move to 135 lbs appears to have given him a size and strength edge. He has just one KO in 21 professional fights, so the path to victory is almost always through grappling. His opponent, Santiago Luna (8-0), jumped at this assignment on 9-10 days' notice and is 2-0 in the UFC with finishes. He is unbeaten, carries real finishing ability across positions (KOs and submissions), and has a four-inch reach advantage over Mitchell. He is publicly unbothered by the short camp.

Mitchell's wrestling experience and UFC track record are the main advantages here. Luna brings raw upside, a longer reach, and the confidence of a young unbeaten fighter. The short-notice camp is the biggest variable on Luna's side, specifically in terms of specific preparation against Mitchell's wrestling.

Pick: Bryce Mitchell

Method: Decision

Luna has looked impressive in two UFC appearances, but nothing on his résumé suggests he can handle the kind of wrestling Mitchell brings. Nurmagomedov, who Mitchell beat at bantamweight, is a serious grappler in his own right and competed at the highest levels of submission grappling. Luna is stepping in on under two weeks' notice, and while his confidence is not a concern, the preparation gap against a specialist like Mitchell is.

Mitchell will get the wrestling going early and use his top control to blanket Luna for the majority of the fight. Luna has reach and striking ability, but Mitchell is disciplined about cutting angles and closing distance without walking straight into counters. Once he gets Luna down, the fight slows to his tempo. This will not be a spectacular performance, but Mitchell does not need it to be. He will grind out a clear decision and continue building his case at 135.

Iwo Baraniewski vs. Junior Tafa

Iwo Baraniewski (8-0) has been a first-round wrecking ball since arriving in the UFC. He knocked out Austen Lane in 28 seconds, stopped Ibo Aslan in 89 seconds, and before that finished Mahamed Aly in 20 seconds on the Contender Series. Every single finish has come via ground-and-pound in the first round. The 18-win streak covers his entire professional career across all promotions without a single blemish. His opponent, Junior Tafa (7-5), stepped in on short notice to replace Billy Elekana, one of the men who beat him. He picked up a first-round ground-and-pound win over Kevin Christian in May 2026, but arrives here with consecutive submission losses before that and a 1-3 record in his last four fights. He carries genuine KO power and is tough, but the short-notice camp and the submission losses are significant red flags.

This fight is unlikely to see a scorecard. Baraniewski's finishing rate and the pace at which he ends fights make a decision almost implausible. Tafa's only realistic path is catching Baraniewski with something significant on the feet in the opening minutes before the fight reaches the mat.

Pick: Iwo Baraniewski

Method: KO/TKO

Junior Tafa is not equipped for this matchup. He has been submitted in back-to-back fights, is taking this on short notice, and is stepping in against arguably the most dangerous finisher in the light heavyweight division right now. Baraniewski does not just win, he annihilates, and he has done it in under 90 seconds in three straight UFC appearances. The ground-and-pound is extraordinary and the finishing instinct is elite.

Tafa is tough and has puncher's equity in any fight, but that window is small and Baraniewski does not give opponents time to establish anything. Once this hits the mat, it is over. Expect a first-round TKO, quick night at the office for the Polish destroyer.

Prelims Picks

Matt Schnell vs. Alessandro Costa

Matt Schnell (17-10) has gone 1-4 in his last five fights, with three of those losses coming by choke submission: a guillotine from Joseph Morales, another guillotine from Christian Guzman, and a ninja choke from Cody Durden. His lone win over Jimmy Flick in April 2025 was a decision. The pattern of neck-and-head-position submissions is not an anomaly at this point; it is a persistent structural problem. His opponent, Alessandro Costa (15-5), is in on nine days' notice replacing Imanol Rodriguez. His most recent win was a liver shot TKO over Stewart Nicoll in April 2026, a clean finishing performance. He has dangerous power in his hands and on the ground. Both fighters have a win over Jimmy Flick, which provides an indirect comparison point.

Costa's short-notice camp is the only question mark. Otherwise, his physical finishing threat maps directly onto Schnell's documented weaknesses in close-range exchanges.

Pick: Alessandro Costa

Method: KO/TKO

Schnell announced his retirement after his loss to Cody Durden in 2024, and unfortunately for him, the comeback has not gone well. He is chinny, aging, and does not have a dominant enough skill set in any one area to paper over those deficiencies. Three choke losses in recent fights point to a fighter whose neck position and defensive awareness have deteriorated.

Costa is coming in on short notice but arrives with genuine finishing power and a clean win just two months ago. He will clip Schnell early, get the fight to a position where he can unload, and finish with ground and pound. Not a fight that goes deep into the rounds.

Marcus McGhee vs. John Yannis

Marcus McGhee (10-2) is 4-1 in the UFC with his only loss coming to Petr Yan in July 2025, a decision in which he was competitive through the first round before Yan's quality told. Yan has since regained the UFC bantamweight title, which ages that loss well. McGhee returns here after an 11-month layoff and was originally booked against Jakub Wiklacz before that fight fell through. He is a heavy -450 favorite. His opponent, John Yannis (10-4), is 1-1 in the UFC: a first-round elbow finish of Jamie Siraj in April 2026 followed his submission loss to Austin Bashi. He is taking this fight on short notice and it represents a significant jump in competition.

McGhee is the superior fighter on paper. The 11-month layoff and the late change of opponent introduce rust as a variable, but Yannis's own submission loss to Bashi, a fighter early in his UFC career, points to real vulnerabilities that McGhee can exploit.

Pick: Marcus McGhee

Method: KO/TKO

Yannis losing by submission to Austin Bashi in round one is the number that matters most here. Bashi was barely established in the UFC at that point, and a first-round submission loss to him suggests Yannis has meaningful grappling deficiencies. McGhee is a far more complete and experienced fighter.

McGhee is 36 but has only 12 professional bouts, meaning the odometer is low. He went the distance with Petr Yan, took the first round against arguably the best bantamweight in the world, and still looked sharp doing it. His four-fight win streak before the Yan loss showed a fighter who finishes, with wins over Jonathan Martinez, Gaston Bolaños, and JP Buys. Yannis is also stepping in on short notice into a significant skill gap. McGhee will impose his wrestling, limit Yannis's offensive opportunities, and get the finish early.

Bruno Silva vs. Edgar Cháirez

Bruno Silva (15-8-2) is ranked #15 at flyweight and has been inconsistent, going 2-3 in his last five with wins sandwiched between losses. His most recent fight was a split decision loss to Charles Johnson in March 2026. He averages over two takedowns per fight and his wrestling and body lock game give him a consistent positional path regardless of how the striking is going. His opponent, Edgar Cháirez (13-6), is on a two-fight win streak: an RNC over CJ Vergara in March 2025 and a split decision over Felipe Bunes in February 2026. He has multiple submission finishes and brings real grappling danger himself.

Both fighters share the same recent common opponent in Joshua Van: Cháirez lost a decision to Van in September 2024, Silva was TKO'd by Van in June 2025. Both fighters operate primarily on the mat, which sets up a grappling-heavy fight where a single positional mistake could end it for either man.

Pick: Bruno Silva

Method: Decision

The common opponent data with Joshua Van is worth examining, but only to a point. Van has since become the flyweight champion and beaten some of the best in the division, so losses to him at this stage of his career carry limited negative weight for either fighter. Silva was stopped late in the third, Cháirez lost on the scorecards, which are different circumstances but both explainable against a champion-level talent.

What matters more is the resume depth. Silva has fought significantly better opposition throughout his UFC career and has been competitive with ranked flyweights consistently. Cháirez's wins have come against lower-tier names. Silva's takedown volume and body lock control give him a reliable route to winning rounds even when the striking is not there. He will mix his wrestling with his striking better than Cháirez can match and accumulate enough control time for a clear decision.

Chelsea Chandler vs. Priscila Cachoeira

Chelsea Chandler (6-4) is 2-3 in the UFC and on a two-fight losing streak: a decision loss to Yana Santos and a first-round KO loss to Joselyne Edwards in April 2025. Her jiu-jitsu background is her clearest path in any fight, and it is directly relevant here given her opponent's history. Her opponent, Priscila Cachoeira (13-8), known as "Zombie Girl," is also on a two-fight skid: a first-round KO loss to Joselyne Edwards and a unanimous decision loss to Klaudia Sygula in February 2026. She has four career submission losses and tends to leave openings when throwing combinations. Both fighters lost to Joselyne Edwards, with Cachoeira's loss being more recent.

This is a fight for UFC survival for both women. The odds are near coin-flip at -124 / +104, reflecting how evenly matched their respective slumps have been. Chandler's submission game against Cachoeira's documented submission vulnerability is the key dynamic.

Pick: Chelsea Chandler

Method: Decision

Cachoeira is pushing 38 and arriving on a two-fight skid that includes a KO loss and a decision loss. Her loss to Sygula in February 2026 suggests she has lost a step: Sygula is a solid fighter but not someone a healthy, sharp Cachoeira should be losing to cleanly on the scorecards. The end of her UFC run may be approaching.

Chandler is not in great form either, but she is younger and her jiu-jitsu background creates a real threat against an opponent with four career submission losses. Cachoeira's forward pressure and punching aggression is the counter path, but if Chandler can avoid the early exchanges and use her grappling, she accumulates enough over three rounds to win the decision.

Jordan Leavitt vs. Joanderson Brito

Jordan Leavitt (13-3), nicknamed "The Monkey King," is on a two-fight win streak at featherweight: an anaconda choke finish of Kurt Holobaugh in 99 seconds and a unanimous decision over Yadier del Valle in February 2026. More than half his wins come by submission. The UFC put together a "Beware the Monkey King" promotional piece for him ahead of this event, suggesting real investment in his trajectory. His opponent, Joanderson Brito (18-5-1), is 6-3 in the UFC and coming off back-to-back decision losses to William Gomis and Pat Sabatini before rebounding with a UD over Isaac Thomson in December 2025. His striking is his primary weapon and he knows exactly what Leavitt is going to try — Brito has stated publicly that the game plan is clear.

The matchup is a clean style split: Leavitt shoots for takedowns and hunts submissions, Brito wants to keep it standing and land clean strikes. Brito's recent losses on the scorecards add pressure on him to string consecutive wins together.

Pick: Jordan Leavitt

Method: Decision

Leavitt looked dominant against del Valle at featherweight largely because of the size and strength advantage the weight class provides him. A similar dynamic is likely here. More importantly, Brito's loss to Pat Sabatini is a significant red flag: Sabatini's style is built on the same smothering, grappling-heavy pressure that defines Leavitt's game. If Brito could not solve Sabatini, there is little reason to believe he solves Leavitt.

Leavitt will close distance, get his takedowns, and use his size and top pressure to limit Brito's offensive output. Brito will have his moments on the feet and may score in spots, but he has not shown the wrestling defense needed to keep this fight standing consistently. Leavitt grinds out a decision through control time and positional dominance.

Jeisla Chaves vs. Yuneisy Duben

Jeisla Chaves (6-0), known as "A Braba," enters this fight unbeaten with wins across decisions and finishes. Her most recent UFC outing was a split decision over Sofia Montenegro in September 2025, a performance that was not dominant — Montenegro gave her real problems, and the split nature of the decision shows Chaves can be pushed. She is a heavy favorite (-395 to -430) based on her unbeaten record. Her opponent, Yuneisy Duben (6-1), is coming off a first-round head kick KO loss to Carli Judice in March 2025. Before that loss she had three consecutive wins, two of them by KO/TKO including a head kick of her own. She is a +300 underdog but carries real one-shot finishing power.

Chaves is the clearer all-around fighter. Duben's path is narrow but real: she has demonstrated she can end a fight instantly on the feet, and Chaves's unconvincing split against Montenegro shows she is not untouchable.

Pick: Yuneisy Duben

Method: KO/TKO

Duben's loss to Carli Judice should not be written off as a sign of decline. Judice has proven herself to be a legitimate contender with real aspirations, so the loss has aged about as well as it possibly could. More importantly, getting finished early in your career by a fighter of that quality tends to be instructive rather than damaging. It teaches you what level is required and sharpens your awareness in positions where you previously got hurt.

Chaves is unbeaten but her split over Montenegro was a warning sign. She has not been tested by a striker with Duben's finishing instincts. Duben showed against Shannon Clark and others that she can put fighters away with a single shot. If she lands clean early, this fight is over before Chaves can establish her rhythm. Taking the upset here at plus money.

Ketlen Souza vs. Ariane Carnelossi

Ketlen Souza (16-6) bounced back with a unanimous decision win over Bruna Brasil in February 2026 after consecutive split decision losses to Angela Hill and Piera Rodriguez. She has a submission finish on her recent record (RNC over Yazmin Jauregui in September 2024) and has shown she can integrate grappling effectively with her striking. Her opponent, Ariane Carnelossi (15-4), is coming off a rear naked choke loss to Talita Alencar in November 2025. Her most recent notable win was a stoppage of Piera Rodriguez via intentional headbutt in 2024. Both she and Souza have fought Rodriguez, providing an interesting comparison point: Carnelossi beat Rodriguez, Souza lost to her.

Souza is on the right trajectory coming off a win; Carnelossi's submission loss to Alencar is her most recent result and a concern. Both are decision-heavy fighters, so this likely goes three full rounds.

Pick: Ketlen Souza

Method: Decision

Souza showed against Bruna Brasil that she can impose her will with a grappling-forward style, and the win was clean enough to suggest she is in decent form. Carnelossi just got choked out by Talita Alencar, which adds to a pattern of submission vulnerability in her recent record. Souza's RNC over Jauregui is directly relevant experience.

This fight will likely be determined by who controls the clinch and the mat. Souza's blend of grappling and striking, and her ability to integrate takedowns into her game plan, gives her a reliable route to winning rounds. Carnelossi is capable on the feet but has not shown she can solve this type of pressure fighter. Souza wins over the distance.
