Summary

UFC Seattle delivered exactly what this sport is supposed to look like. There was magic in the air at Climate Pledge Arena — a crowd locked in from the first prelim to the final buzzer, and enough blood, highlight reels, and controversy to fill a week's worth of discourse. Hands down the best card of the year, and a legitimate candidate for one of the better Fight Night events in recent memory. The only thing the UFC could have done better was promote it more.


The night's real standout was Yousri Belgaroui, who dismantled Mansur Abdul-Malik with the kind of sharp, technically precise striking that exposes just how wide the gap is between trained combat sports athletes and guys who learned to box in an MMA gym. He systematically ate up Abdul-Malik's left leg in the first, weathered a desperate grappling scramble in the second, and put him away with a knee to the head in the third. He didn't get a bonus. Meanwhile, Terrance McKinney did McKinney things — head kick, ground and pound, fight over in 24 seconds — and also got passed over. And Lance Gibson Jr., who looked like a different fighter from his 2025 Bobby Green debacle, finished Chase Hooper clean. The undercard was stacked.


Alexa Grasso issued a "y'all must have forgot" reminder to the flyweight division with one of the slickest transitions you'll ever see: a left cross that sent Maycee Barber's eyes rolling, seamlessly converted into a rear naked choke before Barber could even process what hit her. Insane finish. She earned her way back into the title conversation, though she'll need a win or two before another Shevchenko run gets scheduled.


Ignacio Bahamondes and Tofiq Musayev turned the octagon floor into a crime scene, leaving a sea of blood and a referee who apparently checked out for the second round. Musayev grabbed the fence not once but twice to prevent a takedown — blatantly — and then added a headbutt from mount just to cover his bases. No point deduction. No reset. No justice. Bahamondes and his team should file an appeal, because what happened in that second round was a robbery committed in front of a live audience.


The main event told a story that's becoming painfully familiar. Israel Adesanya was sharp in round one — calf kicks, precision movement, systematically breaking down Joe Pyfer's base — and had the better of round two's early exchanges. Then, inexplicably, he dropped his hands and stood in front of Pyfer like he was waiting for a bus. Pyfer took him down, worked to back mount, and finished at 4:18. Whether it was exhaustion, a loss of fire, or something harder to quantify, it felt less like a defeat and more like a resignation letter. Adesanya was classy in the post-fight interview, but his comment that he's "not done" is concerning. There aren't many meaningful doors left open for him at 36, and every fight from here takes a toll you can't get back.


A word on the bonuses, because the UFC deserves to hear it. The organization is pulling $1.1 billion annually from Paramount — roughly $30 million per event if you average it out, though obviously not every card is equal. On a night this loaded, the bonus distribution should reflect what actually happened on the card. Grasso's finish earned it. The Bahamondes-Musayev war earned it. But Pyfer? He was losing that fight. He got handed the number four ranked middleweight in the world — a 36-year-old on a three-fight skid — and got rewarded for capitalizing when said former champion dropped his hands. Meanwhile Belgaroui is out here beating the breaks off his opponent on the undercard and getting nothing. Frankly, Belgaroui probably beats both Adesanya and Pyfer. Let that marinate.


One more grievance: the judging. Ricky Simón's face came out of that fight looking like abstract art, and yet the result was a majority draw. The only round you could legitimately give Simón was the second — all three judges gave him the first, which is insane. Adrian Yanez nearly stopped the fight at the end of the third, and without that late barrage would have walked away with a loss by unanimous decision. Meanwhile, the Tybura-Fortune card came back with a 30-27 for Tybura so delusional that Bruce Buffer had to walk back into the octagon to correct it. Tyrell Fortune had Marcin Tybura in a crucifix in round one. Sort yourselves out.


None of that takes away from what Seattle delivered. This is what UFC Fight Nights should look like. Hold onto your seats — Moicano vs. Duncan is up next week.


Final Score: 7.6/10


Main Card

Joe Pyfer vs. Israel Adesanya

Result: KO/TKO, Punches From Back Mount at 4:18 Round 2 of 5, 9:18 Total

Israel Adesanya entered Seattle on a three-fight losing streak, carrying the weight of a legacy that most fighters would have quietly retired on by now. Joe Pyfer, the hard-hitting middleweight prospect, entered as the aggressor with something to prove — and prove it he did, though not quite in the way the narrative expected.


Adesanya controlled the first round the way only Adesanya can: calf kicks landing with regularity, breaking down Pyfer's base, and sharp countering that made Pyfer second-guess every charge. Pyfer had his moments — landing some big right hooks and a pair of takedowns — but the round belonged clearly to Izzy. The early going of round two looked much the same. Pyfer was already tiring, and Adesanya was still picking him apart with his footwork and timing.


Then, inexplicably, Adesanya dropped his hands. Pyfer charged forward, unloaded, and dragged Adesanya to the canvas. He climbed to back mount, landed punches from above, and the referee waved it off at 4:18 of round two.


The finish was clinical, but the lead-up raised more questions than it answered. Adesanya had Pyfer right where he wanted him for a round and a half, then essentially donated the second half of the fight. Whether that's the result of gas, a loss of competitive fire, or something else entirely is a question only Izzy can answer — and his post-fight comment that "he's not done" didn't inspire confidence. At 36, on a four-fight skid, there are no high-profile positions left to put him in that don't come with serious risk to his long-term health. He was a class act at the microphone. But it might be time to hang it up.


As for Pyfer — the win is real, but the glaze is getting thick. He beat a 36-year-old former champion running on fumes and has now parlayed that into top contender conversation. Does he beat Brendan Allen? Caio Borralho? Dricus du Plessis? He doesn't beat any of those top-three guys right now. The opportunity was favorable, and he made the most of it. That's the most accurate read on Saturday night.

Alexa Grasso vs. Maycee Barber

Result: KO/TKO, Left Cross at 2:42 Round 1 of 3

Alexa Grasso came into Seattle needing a statement, and she delivered one in under three minutes.


The opening exchanges saw Maycee Barber pushing the pace aggressively, coming forward with energy and volume. Grasso, collected and measured, let her come. Then she threw a left cross — clean, precise — and Barber's lights flickered. Rather than swarm with punches like most fighters would, Grasso transitioned directly into a rear naked choke as Barber crumpled, locking it up before the referee had a chance to intervene. It was one of the slickest strike-to-submission sequences you'll ever see at this level.


Grasso should be back in the title conversation after this — not immediately, but a win or two against top-five flyweights makes a strong case. Another Shevchenko bout isn't the right call yet, but give her a ranked opponent and let her build.


As for Barber — she came in riding a lengthy win streak, which in these shallow female divisions can sometimes say more about the matchmaking than the fighter. She's not bad, but she ran into a former champion who reminded everyone why she held that belt in the first place. Tough break, and her cup size isn't going to carry her past the Grassos of the division.

Michael Chiesa vs. Niko Price

Result: Submission, Rear Naked Choke at 1:03 Round 1 of 3

This one was over before you could finish unwrapping a snack. Niko Price got Michael Chiesa to the ground, Chiesa immediately worked to the back, sunk the rear naked choke, and it was done in just over a minute.


The finish was clean, but nothing about this fight sat right. How does someone as bad as Niko Price still have a UFC contract? That was embarrassing. If he doesn't retire, keep him on the roster but stop booking him — some fighters reach a point where giving them matchups is doing everyone a disservice.


And then there's the elephant in the room: the end of Chiesa's career has the faint smell of something shady. Why was he handed such a steady stream of favorable matchups at the end? Nobody's a Chiesa fan, nobody was asking for these fights — yet somehow Anthony Smith and Nate Diaz get thrown to killers at the end of their runs while Chiesa gets the Niko Prices of the world. It worked out for his record. Whether that's a coincidence or something else, the stench is hard to ignore.

Lerryan Douglas vs. Julian Erosa

Result: KO/TKO, Punches at 3:33 Round 1 of 3

Julian Erosa is a veteran who gives everything he has every time he steps in the cage, and Saturday was no different — it just wasn't enough.


Lerryan Douglas was relentless from the opening bell. Calf kicks had Erosa limping within the first 90 seconds, and a big shot early sent him crashing into the fence. Erosa bounced back, surged forward, had some moments — but Douglas kept finding him with stiff rights that dropped him repeatedly. Erosa kept getting up. The referee kept letting it go. Douglas kept swinging. Eventually, the math caught up: too much heat, not enough left in the tank to absorb it.


Erosa gave it everything he got, and the ref gave him every opportunity to turn it around. That's the story of his career — heart that outpaces the toolkit. Tough break for the vet, but thems the breaks. As for Douglas, he looked like a legitimate threat here. Let's get him in front of a real contender and see what he can do.

Yousri Belgaroui vs. Mansur Abdul-Malik

Result: KO/TKO, Clinch Knee at 3:39 Round 3 of 3, 13:39 Total

Yousri Belgaroui put on a masterclass in what happens when you bring a real striker into a cage with someone who's still learning how to use their hands.


From round one, the blueprint was clear: eat up the left leg. Belgaroui methodically chopped away at Mansur Abdul-Malik's calf with kick after kick until Abdul-Malik could barely plant his left foot. By the end of the first, he was surging forward desperately just to stay upright. In the second, Abdul-Malik adapted — clinching and shooting to take away the distance — and actually got Belgaroui down and worked to his back. But he rode too high, Belgaroui escaped, and the striking resumed. A big right sent Abdul-Malik crashing to the canvas, and only the bell saved him. In the third, with nowhere to hide and nothing left in the legs, Abdul-Malik absorbed a knee to the head and it was over.


Belgaroui is a reminder of the levels that exist in combat sports. The MMA strikers who come up without serious boxing or kickboxing backgrounds simply cannot hang with guys who've spent years in dedicated striking gyms — and Belgaroui made that case emphatically. Get him in there against a legitimate contender next: Abus Magomedov, Shara Bullet, Roman Dolidze — any of those would be a real test. As for Abdul-Malik, find him an easier opponent and let him get back on track.

Terrance McKinney vs. Kyle Nelson

Result: KO/TKO, High Kick and Hammerfists at 0:24 Round 1 of 3

Don't blink.


Terrance McKinney landed a head kick on Kyle Nelson, followed him to the canvas, and hammered him with ground and pound until the referee intervened — all in 24 seconds. It was violent, it was clean, and it was completely on-brand for a man who seems physically incapable of putting on a boring fight.


You genuinely feel for Nelson here. The disappointment on his face at the end said everything — he came to fight, got the bell, and then it was over before his corner could even get nervous. That's just what happens when you're standing across from McKinney. Some guys lose fights; Nelson just got erased from the evening's timeline. The man is a highlight reel with a UFC contract, and every card he's on is better for it.

Prelims

Tofiq Musayev vs. Ignacio Bahamondes

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

This fight had everything: blood, chaos, fence grabs, a headbutt, and a referee who apparently decided the rulebook was more of a suggestion.


Both men came out swinging from the jump, with Ignacio Bahamondes throwing heat and Tofiq Musayev happy to trade and mix in clinch work with knees to the thighs. The real fireworks came in round two. Bahamondes landed a clean right that sent Musayev to the canvas — and as Bahamondes moved in for the takedown, Musayev grabbed the fence. Twice. Blatantly. The kind of grab that should stop the action and reset the position — it didn't. Musayev got back to his feet, landed a big elbow that opened Bahamondes up badly, then added a headbutt from mount for good measure. Still no penalty. By the end of round two there was a sea of blood on the octagon floor and a gaping injustice left unaddressed. Round three was more of the same grind, with Bahamondes reversing to back mount in the dying seconds and hunting a rear naked choke — too little, too late.


Musayev won the fight on the scorecards, and one judge even had a 10-8 round for him — the same round where he grabbed the fence twice and headbutted his opponent. There was no point deduction, no positional reset, nothing. The right call here is for Bahamondes's team to file a formal appeal, because what happened in round two materially changed the outcome of the fight. Bahamondes also needs to stop defaulting to pulling guard and fishing for submissions when things get hard — it didn't work, and it cost him. But even accounting for that, he was robbed.

Lance Gibson Jr. vs. Chase Hooper

Result: KO/TKO, Knees at 2:56 Round 1 of 3

This was supposed to be a get-right fight for Chase Hooper — a chance to shake off a tough stretch and remind the UFC he still belonged. It did not go that way.


From the opening exchanges, Lance Gibson Jr. was sharp and dangerous. Hooper went in for an early takedown and ate elbows on the way in. He backed off, tried his kicks, tried the clinch — and Gibson clipped him with a nasty elbow that had Hooper wobbling on his feet. A head kick followed, Hooper went down, and it was over.


Gibson looked like a completely different fighter from the guy who got handled by Bobby Green in 2025. Clean finish, composed performance — credit where it's due.


As for Hooper — that's two rough ones in a row, and this was supposed to be the easy one. He's young, he's got a family to provide for, and the future is genuinely unclear. A UFC cut isn't inevitable, but you can't keep losing to guys at this level and expect the promotional patience to last indefinitely. He couldn't get past Lance Gibson Jr. on what was supposed to be a layup. At some point, the hard conversation about whether MMA is the right sport has to be had.

Tyrell Fortune vs. Marcin Tybura

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Three rounds of two out-of-shape heavyweights running on fumes — and then a scorecard disaster to close it out.


Tyrell Fortune came out of the gate with the better of round one, getting Marcin Tybura to the canvas early and locking up a crucifix to land elbows. Tybura somehow escaped, but Fortune had clearly done the most meaningful work of the fight. From round two on, both men were gassing badly — low output, lots of clinching against the fence, minimal offense, and a pace that felt less like a UFC fight and more like two guys quietly agreeing to survive. Round three delivered more of the same: position reversals along the fence with neither man willing or able to do anything about it.


Then the scorecard drama. Bruce Buffer announced a unanimous decision win for Tybura — with one judge's card reading 30-27. Thirty to twenty-seven. For the man who spent round one in a crucifix getting elbowed. Buffer had to march back into the octagon to correct himself; Fortune was the actual winner. People online were apparently still arguing Tybura won the fight, which is genuinely baffling.


None of it matters much, because both of these guys are cooked. The extra weight is killing their cardio, their output is shot, and there's no clear path forward for either of them. Hit the gym, hit the scale, or hit the exit.

Casey O'Neill vs. Gabriella Fernandes

Result: KO/TKO, Punches at 3:11 Round 1 of 3

Short and not particularly sweet.


Casey O'Neill hurt Gabriella Fernandes with a left hand early in round one, followed up with combinations, and the referee stepped in at 3:11. Fernandes's eyes went to the back of her head — she was out before she hit the canvas.


O'Neill then proceeded to scream at the top of her lungs for reasons that weren't entirely clear, which was its own kind of entertainment. The finish itself was clean; the celebration was a bit much. Neither of these women is particularly interesting at this stage of their careers, and what happens next for both of them is a wait-and-see.

Navajo Stirling vs. Bruno Lopes

Result: KO/TKO, Punches and Elbows From Mount at 4:05 Round 2 of 3, 9:05 Total

Navajo Stirling got the stoppage win, but left more questions than answers.


Round one was a low-output feeling-out process, with Stirling controlling range and landing a 1-2 here and there while Bruno Lopes kept spamming spinning back elbows like he had a quota to fill. Stirling caught a leg kick but stayed on his feet and prevented the takedown. Nothing particularly convincing from either man.


Round two opened with Lopes catching Stirling coming in with a shot to the chin, then continued the spinning elbow parade. Stirling landed a clean right that dropped Lopes, followed him to the ground, threatened a guillotine, and when Lopes went back down, Stirling was on top landing punches and elbows until the referee stopped it.


The win is the win, but the performance didn't inspire confidence. When Stirling was trying to put Lopes away on the ground, he looked slow — not Pereira-pouncing-on-a-wounded-opponent slow, but genuinely measured and cautious in a way that suggested he wasn't fully comfortable closing out fights. Lopes is garbage — no real boxing, no real grappling — so the bar for looking good here was low. Stirling cleared it, barely. He called out Ion Cutelaba post-fight, which is a reasonable ask. He might win that one. But the hype around Stirling isn't real yet, and depending on who they put in front of him next, he could get stopped.

Ricky Simón vs. Adrian Yanez

Result: Ends in a Draw, Majority at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Adrian Yanez won this fight. The scorecards say otherwise. Both things are true simultaneously, and that's the state of MMA judging in 2026.


The first round was a feeling-out affair with both men establishing range — Yanez circling on the outside and picking with combinations, Simón walking him down and trying to close the distance. Yanez was the more effective striker, stinging Ricky Simón repeatedly. Somehow, all three judges gave the round to Simón. The second round gave Simón his clearest moment: a jab into a takedown, some elbows from guard, and enough aggression to claim the frame. That's the one round you can legitimately put in his column.


Then came the third. Yanez turned it on — going forward, landing clean, and picking Simón apart. Simón's face was a mess, blood streaming from a cut over his left eye. Yanez smelled it and kept pressing. He landed a clean right that had Simón in survival mode, nearly stopped the fight at the horn with a barrage that left Simón's face looking like it had been through a meat grinder. The clock saved him. The result came back a majority draw.


A majority draw. The man who needed a trip to check for a broken orbital somehow didn't go home with a loss. Without that third-round finishing flurry — which nearly ended the fight and should have convinced every judge in the building — Yanez would have lost by unanimous decision. It's insane. It's indefensible. It's MMA judging. The only consolation is that this fight was genuinely entertaining, and a rematch is warranted — one where Yanez finishes the job early instead of leaving it to the scorecards.

Alexia Thainara vs. Bruna Brasil

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

The opener was a reasonable scrap on the feet with a whole lot of nothing on the ground.


Alexia Thainara was the aggressor from bell to bell — plodding forward, throwing kicks and hands, and repeatedly taking the fight to the canvas. She got takedowns in all three rounds, controlled mount, and landed elbows. Bruna Brasil landed some bright moments — a spinning back kick, a body kick here and there — but spent most of the fight either getting walked down or lying on the bottom of mount doing very little about it.


The striking was at least active, but whenever Thainara got the fight to the ground it turned into a slog. A "can opener" attempt in round one, some elbows, and a lot of Brasil just holding on — it wasn't pretty. Thainara won clearly, but she didn't look like anything special doing it. She's reckless, lacks a defined specialty, and wins by wanting it more against low-output opponents. That works until it doesn't — someone who can actually punch back will snipe her.


Brasil is harder to figure out. She doesn't box particularly well, she doesn't grapple particularly well, and she showed no fire or urgency at any point during the fight. She looked like she didn't want to be there. Whatever her specialty is supposed to be, it wasn't on display Saturday night.
