Summary

UFC Fight Night Allen vs. Costa was a card you could have comfortably slept through without missing much, and the 6.2 rating tells you everything you need to know.


The back end of the main card saved the event from total irrelevance. Doo Ho Choi proved the Korean Zombie's faith was well-placed, breaking Daniel Santos with a body shot in the second round to collect his third straight win at 35 years old. Juan Diaz exposed Malcolm Wellmaker's grappling deficiencies in methodical fashion, choking out the man who arrived in the UFC on back-to-back first-round KO highlights. And in the main event, Arnold Allen went five rounds with Melquizael Costa and showed he still has it, controlling the fight with his grappling, ground and pound, and sharper striking to cruise to a unanimous decision.


The rest of the card was largely filler. The Bukauskas vs. Edwards light heavyweight co-main was a snoozer, with both men standing around and occasionally landing a punch as if they'd agreed beforehand to keep things forgettable. Vieira and Cavalcanti had a similarly uninspiring outing where neither fighter appeared to want to win. The Erslan-Tokkos prelim had a wild first round before flatlined completely when both guys ran out of gas and Erslan spent the remainder lying on top of his opponent.


On the brighter side, Khaos Williams kept his hype train rolling with a first-round KO and a post-knockout backflip, Cody Brundage once again refused to die in the UFC, and Benardo Sopaj earned a stoppage over Cuamba even if the ref should have intervened three minutes earlier. The biggest talking point of the night may have had nothing to do with the fights: Dana White announced the Conor McGregor vs. Max Holloway fight during the Francis Ngannou walk out, turning a mid-tier card into a backdrop for a blockbuster announcement.


If you had plans Saturday night, you made the right call.


Final Score: 6.2/10



Main Card

Arnold Allen vs. Melquizael Costa

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 5 Rounds, 25:00 Total

Arnold Allen vs. Melquizael Costa headlined the card, and Allen came in with a point to prove. Coming off a loss to Giga Chikadze, the English featherweight needed a convincing performance to re-establish himself in the top-ten conversation. Costa, the Brazilian knockout artist, entered the fight as a credible threat, having made his name blitzing opponents in the first round.


From the opening bell, Allen set the tone with his wrestling. A left arm sent Costa to the canvas early, and Allen followed him down, quickly transitioning to the back and landing ground and pound. Costa recovered and got creative with back-takes of his own, and the first round turned into a chaotic scramble-fest with both men cycling through positions. Allen came out ahead.


Rounds two and three were more of the same: Allen controlling the grappling exchanges, landing his jab consistently on the feet, and stuffing Costa's offense. Every time Costa surged forward with big punches, Allen either slipped the shots or dragged the fight back to the mat. By the third round Allen was entering flow state, tagging Costa more freely and landing punishing ground and pound from dominant positions. A pseudo crucifix sequence late in the round showcased just how far ahead Allen was in the wrestling department.


Costa had his moments in rounds four and five, most notably landing some left hands that briefly shifted momentum in the fourth, but Allen kept dragging him back down and grinding him out. By the fifth, Costa was getting wobbled on the feet and tripped back to the canvas repeatedly. Allen held his back and stalled out the final minutes, comfortable in his lead.


Allen still has it. The win puts him right back in the featherweight mix and the fight to make is Allen vs. Diego Lopes, or if Lopes doesn't cooperate, Allen vs. Steve Garcia if Garcia keeps winning. As for Costa, this loss was educational. He spent five rounds getting taken into deep waters by a complete fighter and couldn't rely on early knockout power to bail him out. A step back down in competition to accumulate more rounds would do him good.

Doo Ho Choi vs. Daniel Santos

Result: KO/TKO, Body Shot to Ground Strikes at 4:29 Round 2 of 3, 9:29 Total

Doo Ho Choi returned to the octagon against Daniel Santos, and this one had the makings of a firefight from the jump. Santos came out swinging for the bleachers in the first round, landing a flurry of uppercuts that bloodied Choi's nose and backed him up. Choi wasn't fazed. He denied a takedown attempt with the fence, landed a clean right hook that hurt Santos, and began dictating the pace from the center of the octagon. Santos kept pressing, landing an uppercut that sent Choi back significantly, but Choi kept fighting through it and denied another takedown to close out the round.


The second round is where Choi took over. He landed a calf kick that stumbled Santos early, and from there it was just a matter of time. Choi worked his way back to the center, landed knees in the clinch, and started breaking Santos down piece by piece. Then came the finish: Choi walked Santos back against the fence and crumbled him with a body shot, following up with ground strikes until the ref stepped in at 4:29 of the second round.


Crazy finish, and Choi made the Korean Zombie proud. Three straight wins at 35 years old is no small thing, and Choi has earned himself a ranked opponent. He called out Pitbull after the fight, and that's a matchup worth making. The time to find out how high Choi's ceiling is at featherweight is now.

Juan Díaz vs. Malcolm Wellmaker

Result: Submission, Rear Naked Choke at 4:08 Round 2 of 3, 9:08 Total

Malcolm Wellmaker arrived in the UFC as one of the more electric prospects on the roster, knocking out his first two opponents in the first round with vicious right hooks. Then reality hit. Juan Diaz exposed exactly what happens when Wellmaker can't just clip a guy early and call it a night.


Wellmaker looked sharp in the opening minutes, moving well and landing his jab and body kicks with authority. Then Diaz got a bodylock and dragged him to the cage, and suddenly the fight looked completely different. Diaz tried to trip him, dragged him down to the canvas, and started wearing on him. Wellmaker survived and got back to his feet, and Diaz even dropped him with a right hand late in the first round before a sketchy knee with one second left forced a review. They just restarted the round and ended it.


By the second, Wellmaker's output had dried up. Diaz got another bodylock, dragged him back down, and this time there was no escape. Diaz worked to the back, got a hook in, transitioned to rear naked choke, and that was it at 4:08 of round two.


Who would have seen this coming. Two first-round KO highlights, and now on a two-fight losing streak, submitted in the second. Wellmaker doesn't have grappling to speak of, and his boxing only works when he's got a can he can just clip badly. The disappointment on his face said everything. Tough reality of combat sports. Great win for Diaz.

Modestas Bukauskas vs. Christian Edwards

Result: Decision, Split at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Modestas Bukauskas vs. Christian Edwards was the light heavyweight co-main event, and it was exactly the kind of fight that gives the division a bad reputation.


Both men came out selecting their shots carefully in the first round, which is a generous way of saying they barely did anything. A few body kicks, a clinch here and there, a couple of clean punches, and that was it. Round two had slightly more action, with Bukauskas landing a big right hook that hurt Edwards and forced him to clinch, but Edwards came back and neither man could build any sustained offense. By the third, they were back to standing across from each other and occasionally throwing something. Edwards went for a takedown against the cage, grabbed a clinch, got an elbow, and that was about the extent of it. He whiffed a head kick. Bukauskas landed a few punches. The round ended.


Bukauskas saying post-fight that he was happy to put on an exciting fight for the fans is genuinely comical. They weren't mixing anything together, didn't appear to have any real strategy, and spent most of three rounds standing around. Bukauskas took the split decision, but neither man distinguished himself here.

Benardo Sopaj vs. Timmy Cuamba

Result: Submission, Rear Naked Choke at 2:25 Round 2 of 3, 7:25 Total

Benardo Sopaj and Timmy Cuamba put together a fight that had a good result buried inside a refereeing disaster.


The first round was competitive. Cuamba controlled the center early, landed some nice body shots, and both men traded combinations without much separating them. Then Sopaj got to work: he changed levels, wore on Cuamba against the fence, took his back with both hooks in, and nearly finished him with a neck crank. Cuamba barely survived to the bell.


The second round started with a groin shot on Sopaj, pausing the action briefly. When they resumed, Cuamba kept investing in body shots, but Sopaj stunned him and followed him to the canvas. What followed was one of the worst referee performances of the night. Sopaj unloaded on a clearly compromised Cuamba with everything he had, Cuamba's face getting completely destroyed, and the ref just watched. He finally intervened only when Sopaj sunk in the rear naked choke and Cuamba tapped immediately, which itself tells you how bad things had gotten: you only tap that fast to a choke when you're already seriously compromised. The fight should have been stopped on strikes several minutes earlier.


Sopaj gets the win and deserves credit for the finish, but the stoppage was inexcusable. Cuamba took punishment that never needed to happen.

Khaos Williams vs. Nikolay Veretennikov

Result: KO/TKO, Right Hook at 3:31 Round 1 of 3

Khaos Williams wasted no time. From the opening exchanges, he was landing clean rights on Nikolay Veretennikov, and when he hurt him with a right hand, Williams was all over him. A couple more combinations and it was done, right hook finish at 3:31 of the first round. Williams capped it off with a backflip post-knockout that was as clean as the finish itself.


Williams is rolling, and the backflip is the least surprising part of his act at this point. The next step is straightforward: put him in with someone just outside the rankings and see how he handles a step up in competition. If he passes that test, then we can start talking ranked matchups. The hype is real, but it needs to be stress-tested.

Prelims

Ivan Erslan vs. Tuco Tokkos

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Ivan Erslan and Tuco Tokkos put together one of the more schizophrenic prelim fights on the card: a wild, entertaining first round followed by two rounds of near-complete cardio collapse.


The first round was genuinely fun. Erslan came plodding forward behind his jab and right hand, stinging Tokkos with an uppercut that put him down momentarily. Tokkos got creative, shot for a takedown to survive, and somehow Erslan fumbled the finish and let him back to his feet. Then the momentum swung completely: Tokkos found his rhythm, walked Erslan down, and nearly finished him against the fence late in the round. Both men were swinging hard and both were very hittable.


Then round two arrived and the gas tanks hit empty. Tokkos shot for a takedown, completely whiffed it, and ended up pulling guard. Erslan landed some ground and pound from side control without doing much damage. Tokkos kept trying to get back to his knees, Erslan kept him down, and the round ended with neither man doing much of anything. Round three was more of the same: Erslan lying on top of Tokkos on the ground, doing just enough to prevent a standup, while Tokkos tried and failed to create any offense from the bottom.


No cardio, no fun. Both guys were slugging out of their minds in round one and had absolutely nothing left after that. Erslan gets the decision win, and it matters: without it, he was probably getting cut. He said he wants a new contract, and honestly, if the price is right, it's worth keeping him around. In the first round, he will make it entertaining.

Tommy Gantt vs. Artur Minev

Result: KO/TKO, Punches From Back Mount at 2:51 Round 2 of 3, 7:51 Total

Tommy Gantt made his UFC debut against Artur Minev, and if you wanted a snapshot of who Gantt is as a fighter, this was it: he's going to wrestle you relentlessly until you break.


The first round was almost entirely spent in the clinch against the cage. Gantt shot for takedowns repeatedly, Minev used the fence to deny them time after time, and neither man got much going. Gantt kept dragging him to the fence, landing shoulder strikes and knees to the body in the clinch, and grinding away. Minev was surprisingly durable and disciplined in his defense, getting right back to his feet every time Gantt managed to get him down. The round went nowhere, but Gantt was clearly in control of where the fight took place.


The second round is where the blueprint finally paid off. Minev kept denying takedowns with the fence, but the volume of attempts wore him down. Gantt finally got him to his knees, started landing knees from the back, slammed him back down when he got up, and then flattened him out on the canvas. From there it was serious ground and pound until the ref stepped in at 2:51 of round two.


Good debut from Gantt, but he's not going to surprise anyone going forward. Everyone knows exactly what he's going to do: overwhelm you with grappling until you break. Minev, on the other hand, deserves credit. He took the fight on short notice, denied Gantt's gameplan for the better part of two rounds, and showed enough to warrant at minimum a shot at the Contender Series.

Ketlen Vieira vs. Jacqueline Cavalcanti

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Ketlen Vieira and Jacqueline Cavalcanti put on a fight where neither fighter appeared to want to win, and Vieira somehow got her hand raised anyway.


The first round was a low-output feeling-out session. Vieira landed some clean 1-2s, Cavalcanti relied on teeps and calf kicks, and not much of consequence happened until Cavalcanti landed a nice 1-2 to close out the round. Round two saw Vieira attempt to grapple, dragging Cavalcanti down to the canvas and landing some elbows, but the output on the ground was minimal. She wore on Cavalcanti more than she damaged her, while Cavalcanti did very little from the bottom to get back to her feet.


Then came round three, and Vieira essentially took it off. Cavalcanti was the more active fighter, landing a clean 1-2, digging to the body, and entering something resembling flow state by the end, tagging Vieira at will. Vieira barely threw anything. It was an absolutely baffling gameplan to effectively concede the third round when the fight was close, and she could easily have lost the first as well.


Vieira wins these close fights by doing just enough and then getting the benefit of the doubt on the scorecards. She fights with low output and doesn't go for finishes on the ground. She won this one, but just barely, and only because Cavalcanti was also content to be on her back in the second and not do much about it. Neither fighter wanted it badly enough.

Cody Brundage vs. Andre Petroski

Result: KO/TKO, Left Hook to Ground Strikes at 0:44 Round 2 of 3, 5:44 Total

Andre Petroski and Cody Brundage opened with a fairly unremarkable first round of feeling each other out. Both men landed some right hands, Brundage lunged in with a couple of his own, and then Petroski shot for a takedown. Brundage jumped a guillotine but didn't have the position right, and Petroski ended up in side control with Brundage locking on a headlock of his own. Neither submission went anywhere. Petroski eventually settled into landing elbows and ground and pound to close out the round.


Round two ended quickly. Brundage clipped Petroski with a left hook that put him on the canvas, then followed him down and opened up with serious ground and pound until the ref finally stepped in. Worth noting: it was a late stoppage. The ref could have stepped in at any point and let it go longer than it needed to.


Whenever you think Brundage is about to get cut from the UFC, he goes out there and squeaks out a win. Impressive in the most grudging sense of the word. That said, he's so shot at this point it's hard to get excited about it. The guy keeps surviving, but nobody is watching Cody Brundage fights expecting to be entertained.

Alice Ardelean vs. Polyana Viana

Result: Submission, Calf Slicer at 4:36 Round 2 of 3, 9:36 Total

Alice Ardelean came out on the front foot from the jump and never let Polyana Viana get comfortable. The fight opened with a brief stoppage when Viana complained of an eye poke, but the replay showed it was a fist, and the action resumed. Whatever the replay showed, Viana's left eye was already bloodied early and it didn't improve her night.


Ardelean kept advancing, landing big rights that stumbled Viana and eventually had her circling the cage with nothing to offer. Viana spent the first round on the back foot, getting tagged repeatedly without any real answer.


The second round saw Ardelean take the fight to the ground, landing on top while Viana locked on a body triangle from the bottom to prevent any damage. It worked briefly, but Viana's defensive position became her own undoing: Ardelean was able to use the body triangle against her, applying pressure to Viana's knee and forcing the tap from a calf slicer at 4:36 of round two.


Polyana Viana is honestly terrible. She resigned herself to the bottom position from the moment the fight hit the ground and then handed Ardelean the submission by putting herself in a bad spot. She's now on a four-fight losing streak, and it is genuinely time to pack it up. As for Ardelean, she's on a three-fight win streak at 34 years old. Give her a real step up in competition, because she won't be around forever.

Luis Gurule vs. Daniel Barez

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Luis Gurule and Daniel Barez traded momentum throughout three rounds in a fight Gurule ultimately controlled, even if he left a finish on the table more than once.


The first round was entertaining. Both men were light on their feet and trading early, with Gurule putting together some nice combinations and walking Barez down. Then something unexpected happened: Barez went for a takedown and somehow transitioned to the back, getting both hooks in. Gurule reversed and attempted an anaconda choke that nearly finished the fight, Barez's face turning purple before he managed to survive. They got back to the feet and Gurule continued to press.


Round two was a seesaw. Barez landed a right hook that stumbled Gurule and briefly took over, walking him down and finding success with his right hand over Gurule's lead hand. Then Gurule came back, hurt Barez with a right of his own, and slammed him to the ground with a suplex. With Barez on the ropes and getting tagged at will, Gurule kept clinching instead of staying outside and putting him away. It was baffling. He had the finish there multiple times and kept grabbing the clinch instead of sealing the deal.


By the third, Barez had nothing left. His shots had no sting, his speed was gone, and Gurule was landing whatever he wanted. Barez hung in there, but it was academic.


Good win for Gurule, but why wasn't he going for the finish when he had the chances? Barez, at 37 and on a two-fight losing streak fighting once a year, may want to start thinking about retirement.

Nicolle Caliari vs. Shauna Bannon

Result: Submission, Arm Triangle Choke at 3:08 Round 3 of 3, 13:08 Total

Nicolle Caliari opened the card against Shauna Bannon in a strawweight bout, and it was a technical win for Caliari, even if she left a lot on the table on the ground.


Caliari was the aggressor from the start, surging forward and taking the fight down repeatedly across all three rounds. The problem was that once she got there, she struggled to do much with the position. She was mostly lying on top of Bannon, unable to pass the guard or create real damage, while Bannon landed elbows and upkicks from the bottom. Caliari got to north-south, threatened a kimura in round one, and kept busy enough to win the frame.


Round two followed the same pattern. Caliari got Bannon down, but Bannon was active from the bottom, landing elbows and upkicks whenever Caliari postures up. The pace picked up on the feet late in the round, with Caliari landing on the break before they went back to the ground.


Round three was where it ended. Caliari jabbed Bannon's eye and followed up with an elbow on the same spot, swelling it up. She got into half guard, didn't do much with it, and then started threatening an arm triangle. Bannon tapped at 3:08 of round three.


Nice win for Caliari, and an unexpected technical one for a fighter coming down from a higher weight class. That said, she does very little with her top position, and Bannon was extremely diligent about the chest hold to limit damage. For Bannon, it's two straight losses by submission, and she looked pretty bad out there. She shouldn't be cut from the UFC just yet, but she needs to figure something out.
