Overview

UFC Vegas 116 is a solid mid-tier Apex card with a genuinely intriguing main event and a few compelling secondary storylines, even if the overall fight card leans toward the thinner side. The headliner has real title stakes attached to it — whoever walks out of the Apex with a win between Aljamain Sterling and Youssef Zalal is almost certainly next in line for a shot at featherweight gold — which gives the main event the weight it deserves. Sterling, the former bantamweight champion, has been methodically building his featherweight résumé since moving up, while Zalal has been riding a wave of momentum off a string of finishes against opponents whose shelf life has to be questioned. The winner earns the right to call themselves the #1 contender; what they'll actually have proven about their quality is a separate discussion.

The co-main event features Norma Dumont, who has quietly assembled one of the longest active win streaks in the women's bantamweight division, against a surging Joselyne Edwards who has finished every single opponent in her current four-fight run. The contrast in styles — Dumont's methodical point-scoring versus Edwards' genuine finishing power — makes this one of the more watchable fights on the card, even if the judges seem to have a standing reservation for Dumont regardless of how close the action gets.

Below the top two bouts, there's genuine intrigue in the Rafa García vs. Alexander Hernandez lightweight clash, where Hernandez brings a four-fight win streak and a very public controversy into the cage — he was reportedly implicated in the UFC 324 betting scandal before being cleared, and has since been speaking out about fighter protections in the sport. Adrian Luna Martinetti makes his UFC debut fresh off what DWCS called one of the greatest fights in the show's history, although his opponent Davey Grant is the type of crafty veteran who knows exactly how to take the fire out of a hyped-up prospect.

The preliminary card has its share of short-notice drama — Cody Durden stepped in on six days' notice, and former Bellator man Lucas Brennan signed on five days' notice for what is essentially a filler spot that was added late to shore up a thin card. Neither should be competitive on paper, but short-notice fights have a way of producing chaos.


Main Card Picks

Aljamain Sterling vs. Youssef Zalal

Aljamain Sterling (25-5-0) brings championship pedigree to this featherweight main event — a former UFC bantamweight champion who has gone 2-1 since moving up to 145 lbs, beating Calvin Kattar and Brian Ortega by decision around a unanimous decision loss to the unbeaten Movsar Evloev, then bouncing back with a technical fall stoppage of Benson Henderson in February. He's ranked #5 and a win here puts him in a title eliminator at minimum.

Youssef Zalal (18-5-1) is the hottest fighter on this card — eight straight wins, five fights back in the UFC since re-signing, four finishes, most recently an armbar on interim title challenger Josh Emmett in 98 seconds. He's ranked #7 and the slight -120 favorite. But the question worth asking is: who, exactly, has he beaten? His biggest wins are over the sun-setting versions of Emmett and Kattar — names that carry weight on paper but not necessarily as a barometer of elite featherweight competition.

The stylistic matchup is striker vs. grappler with title implications on both sides. Sterling will close distance and impose his world-class defensive grappling; Zalal hunts submissions from both top and bottom, so the ground is not automatic safe territory for either man. The winner is almost certainly next in line for featherweight gold.

Pick: Aljamain Sterling

Method: Decision

Real talk — who has Zalal actually beaten? His best wins are over the dead corpses of Emmett and Kattar, fighters well past their primes whose names look better on paper than they reflect about Zalal's actual level. Sterling, for all the questions about inactivity and moving up a division, gave Movsar Evloev — one of the most legitimate top-ten featherweights in the world — all he could handle, then came out and styled on Brian Ortega for five full rounds. That's championship-level performance against championship-level opposition.

Zalal is not going to be ready for Sterling's grappling IQ. Sterling has spent his entire career mastering the clinch, controlling positions, and making elite fighters look ordinary on the mat. And on the feet, his boxing has always been underrated — he can handle himself in stand-up exchanges long enough to dictate where the fight goes. I don't see a world where Zalal gets the win here. Sterling by decision.

Norma Dumont vs. Joselyne Edwards

Norma Dumont (13-2-0) has become one of the most reliable decision grinders in the women's bantamweight division. Ranked #3 and on a six-fight win streak over genuine competition — Karol Rosa, Chelsea Chandler, Germaine de Randamie, Irene Aldana, and most recently a split decision over Ketlen Vieira — all by decision. That split over Vieira raised eyebrows; most observers felt Vieira won that fight clearly, but Dumont walked out with the win anyway. The judges seem to have a standing reservation for her regardless of how the action unfolds. She enters as the -155 favorite.

Joselyne Edwards (17-6-0) is on a four-fight finish streak that has forced the division to take notice. Ranked #11, all four wins have come by stoppage — two KOs, two submissions — including first-round hooks on Chelsea Chandler and a second-round rear naked choke on Nora Cornolle in February. She has the finishing tools to score an upset, but she hasn't faced anyone close to Dumont's level of competition.

The defining tension is Dumont's disciplined volume and point-scoring against Edwards' genuine finishing power. Dumont's 57% takedown accuracy and 69% takedown defense make her difficult to put in danger on the ground, but if Edwards lands the big shot and drags Dumont into chaos, she has the upside to steal it.

Pick: Norma Dumont

Method: Decision

After Dumont's last win, it's clear she will never lose a decision in the UFC as long as she keeps fighting. She visibly lost to Ketlen Vieira — most people watching that fight saw it clearly — and somehow got her hand raised anyway. That's the reality of fighting Dumont: you have to finish her, because the judges aren't going to give it to you on points.

I'm not sold on Joselyne Edwards as a fighter. I'm not sure how good she actually is, and she hasn't faced competition anywhere near the level Dumont has been beating. Dumont has wins over former title contenders in Irene Aldana; Edwards has been finishing mid-tier opponents. This goes to the scorecards, and a sketchy 29-28 in Dumont's favor is the only realistic outcome.

Rafa García vs. Alexander Hernandez

Rafa García (18-4-0) is on a two-fight win streak after a brutal ground-and-pound TKO loss to Grant Dawson in October 2024. He's bounced back with a decision over Vinc Pichel and a third-round elbow stoppage of Jared Gordon. Training out of Bloodline MMA in California, García has promised a war and guaranteed finish intentions — his grappling-heavy style built on pressure and cardio makes him a dangerous three-round proposition.

Alexander Hernandez (18-8-0) is riding the longest win streak of his UFC career at four fights, with consecutive stoppage wins over Chase Hooper and Diego Ferreira highlighting the run. He walks in with notable off-cage noise — Hernandez was reportedly implicated in the UFC 324 betting scandal that scrapped a previous fight of his, though he has forcefully and publicly stated he was falsely accused and has been speaking out about the lack of fighter protections in the system. Whatever the full story is, he has a point to prove inside the cage.

The matchup has legitimate Fight of the Night potential — two fighters in excellent form, both willing to engage, with García's cardio and grappling pressure testing whether Hernandez's streak holds up against a more complete wrestler.

Pick: Alexander Hernandez

Method: KO/TKO

Hernandez has paid his dues and is now in the form of his career. Four straight wins — the longest run of his UFC tenure — and he has finished fights with real authority. The stoppage wins over Hooper and Ferreira show that his striking has genuine bite behind it. He has matured into a fighter who knows how to close the distance and put opponents away.

García is a solid fighter, but Hernandez has faced considerably stronger competition over this win streak. More importantly, Hernandez carries more finishing power in his hands — García tends to grind and accumulate damage over three rounds, while Hernandez looks for the knockout. He lands clean in the first two rounds and puts García down. KO/TKO.

Davey Grant vs. Adrian Luna Martinetti

Adrian Luna Martinetti (17-1-0) arrives with the kind of DWCS hype that makes you nervous for him. The 30-year-old Argentinian won his UFC contract in October 2025 with a victory over Mark Vologdin described as one of the greatest fights in the show's history — a chaotic war that had the crowd on its feet. He's on a 15-fight win streak, has never been stopped professionally, and brings genuine submission finishing instincts into his octagon debut.

Davey Grant (15-8-0) is 40 years old, battle-tested through over a decade in the UFC, and coming off a guillotine choke loss to Charles Jourdain in October that snapped a two-fight win streak. He's been vocal about wanting to put on an entertaining fight and earn a Fight of the Night bonus. His inverted triangle choke finish of Raphael Assunção proves there's still finishing ability in those hands.

This is the classic UFC baptism-by-fire scenario — an unproven talent with excellent credentials against a crafty veteran who knows every shortcut and pressure point inside the octagon. The biggest unknown is how Martinetti handles the pressure of his debut.

Pick: Davey Grant

Method: Decision

The Contender Series fight that made Martinetti famous hasn't aged particularly well. His opponent that night, Mark Vologdin, made his UFC debut last weekend against Castaneda and was styled on for three rounds, getting a majority draw only because of a point deduction to Castaneda. That's who Martinetti went to war with for his defining moment. The hype may be significantly out in front of the reality.

This is too much, too soon for the prospect. Grant has fought top UFC competition — Charles Jourdain is a legitimate, dangerous fighter — and he's crafty enough not to get drawn into the kind of firefight that flatters Martinetti's game. Grant keeps his distance, fights smart, and goes to a decision victory. The kid's moment will come, but not today.

Montel Jackson vs. Raoni Barcelos

Montel Jackson (15-3-0) is ranked #14 at bantamweight and trying to shake off a split decision loss to former champion Deiveson Figueiredo in October. His seven career knockouts and the 18-second KO of Da'Mon Blackshear make him one of the most dangerous one-punch weapons in the division. He enters as the betting favorite — a reflection of how seriously oddsmakers take that power even after a loss.

Raoni Barcelos (21-5-0) is the story of a fighter refusing to let his UFC career die quietly. At 38, he's won four straight after back-to-back losses had him on the edge of release — and three of those four wins are decisions over legitimately ranked opponents in Payton Talbott, Cody Garbrandt, and Ricky Simón. His fight IQ, volume striking, and pacing have all improved markedly over this run.

Jackson's one-punch KO power versus Barcelos' four-fight decision mastery sets up the central tactical question: does Barcelos commit to his volume game and risk running into something that ends the night instantly?

Pick: Raoni Barcelos

Method: Decision

Montel Jackson is a one-trick pony. His trick is knocking people out early. If that doesn't happen, he turns into a paint-drying decision machine who loses on the scorecards — that's the consistent pattern.

Jackson is also coming off a loss to Deiveson Figueiredo, and Figueiredo hasn't even looked that sharp at 135 over the past couple of years. Barcelos is a much more complete bantamweight in terms of size, grappling, and in-fight intelligence. He'll incorporate wrestling to neutralize Jackson's power, control the pace over 15 minutes, and win a clear decision.

Marcus Buchecha vs. Ryan Spann

Marcus Buchecha (5-2-1) is arguably the greatest Brazilian jiu-jitsu competitor in the sport's history — and the evidence is piling up that translating that to MMA is a different problem than anticipated. He's gone 1-1-1 in his last three, with a decision loss to Martin Buday and an unusual unanimous draw against Kennedy Nzechukwu. The MMA game is exposing the gaps in his all-around game.

Ryan Spann (23-11-0) has reinvented himself as a heavyweight after a long light heavyweight career. His guillotine choke over Łukasz Brzeski last July shows he carries submission danger of his own, and his record — violent wins and losses that tend to come fast — makes him both a threat and a liability. He's the +105 underdog despite being the more well-rounded MMA fighter in this matchup.

If Buchecha can get the fight to the mat and grind through prolonged exchanges, his submission game is theoretically among the most dangerous in MMA history. But the cage is not a jiu-jitsu tournament, and Spann's physicality and guillotine threat ensure the ground is no automatic safe haven.

Pick: Ryan Spann

Method: KO/TKO

Buchecha is a garbage MMA fighter. He is one-dimensional, his striking is limited, and he has almost no submission wins despite being the greatest submission grappler of his generation — because MMA is not jiu-jitsu, and he still hasn't figured that out. He's been losing to fighters he should be finishing on the mat. Spann isn't a great fighter either, but he has shown real flashes — his win over Dominick Reyes wasn't a fluke, and his guillotine game is legitimate enough to slow down takedown attempts from Buchecha.

More importantly, Spann will be considerably faster and more explosive in the octagon. He has real knockout power and the physical tools to land something clean before Buchecha can impose his game. Spann by KO/TKO in the first or second round.

Prelims Picks

Rodolfo Vieira vs. Eric McConico

Rodolfo Vieira (11-4-0) is "The Black Belt Hunter" — a world-class BJJ practitioner who has built his UFC career around arm triangles and mat control. He's finished Derek Brunson and Armen Petrosyan with first-round arm triangles, beat Tresean Gore by decision, then ran into Bo Nickal and got head-kicked unconscious in the third round of their November fight. At 36, absorbing that kind of damage late in a fight is not something you shake off easily.

Eric McConico (10-4-1) has gone 2-2 in his last four UFC appearances, and both of his losses came by knockout — a pattern that suggests his chin is a real concern when things land clean. He's a well-rounded fighter with wrestling credentials, but Vieira's submission dominance on the mat makes this a difficult stylistic problem.

If the fight goes to the ground, Vieira's arm triangle is almost certainly waiting. McConico's path is landing the kind of heavy hands that exposed Vieira against Nickal — exactly the type of striking Vieira's defense has struggled with.

Pick: Rodolfo Vieira

Method: Submission

Vieira took a brutal head kick knockout from Bo Nickal, and at 36, that kind of damage is not easy to come back from. But McConico is not Bo Nickal. He doesn't have that level of wrestling expertise, that level of striking precision, or that kind of physical dominance. Both of these fighters are journeymen at this stage, but Vieira has a clearly superior skill set for this specific matchup — elite grappling against an opponent whose losses have come from being hit, not from being submitted.

Vieira gets McConico to the mat early and finishes with the arm triangle in the first round. It's his highest-percentage weapon and McConico has no particular answer for it.

Sedriques Dumas vs. Jackson McVey

Sedriques Dumas (10-4-0) is on a two-fight losing streak — both by stoppage — and urgently needs a win to stay relevant in the middleweight division. He was guillotined by Donte Johnson in the second round and TKO'd by Michał Oleksiejczuk in the first. A concerning finish rate is developing against him. Before those losses he'd gone 2-1, but the pattern of getting stopped is hard to ignore.

Jackson McVey (6-2-0) started his UFC career with three quick submission wins, then promptly got submitted himself in back-to-back fights — armbarred by Brunno Ferreira, rear naked choked by Zach Reese. His submission defense is a documented problem, but his own grappling attack has legitimate bite.

Both fighters are fighting for their roster spots with no margin for a third consecutive loss. Dumas' striking advantage should be significant if he keeps it standing; McVey needs to scramble into grappling exchanges to neutralize the physical disadvantage.

Pick: Sedriques Dumas

Method: KO/TKO

Both of these guys are garbage, but Dumas is the less garbage option. His last fight against Donte Johnson was bad, but he took that one on short notice and looked noticeably out of shape — that context matters. McVey is simply not UFC caliber. He got exposed the moment he faced opponents with real submission games, and Dumas, whatever his flaws, is the more experienced and more physically imposing fighter here. Dumas gets the TKO win near the end of the second round.

Mayra Bueno Silva vs. Michelle Montague

Mayra Bueno Silva (10-6-1) is on a four-fight losing streak that has her firmly on the wrong side of momentum. Three of those losses are decisions, which suggests she's still competitive enough to go the distance but can no longer impose her submission game at the level needed to win. She's shifted training camps away from American Top Team — where she and tonight's opponent once trained together — moving through the Fighting Nerds and other gyms looking for whatever she's lost.

Michelle Montague (7-0-0) is undefeated and the clear -355 favorite. The New Zealand-born fighter continues to train at ATT and has built her UFC career on a rear naked choke game — four of her five recorded UFC wins have come by RNC. She's beaten increasingly credible opposition and enters this as genuinely the toughest test of her career.

The shared ATT history adds a unique wrinkle: these two trained together routinely, which means neither has many secrets from the other. Whether that benefits the more motivated, better-performing fighter or gives Bueno Silva an edge from knowing exactly what's coming is the one open question here.

Pick: Michelle Montague

Method: Decision

Bueno Silva is in a bad place and doesn't look like she's getting out of it anytime soon. When you drop four in a row and you're getting out-grappled at your own game — the submission art that defined your entire career — that's a very bad sign. MMA is fickle: when you lose it, you lose it.

Montague is physically stronger, better conditioned, and currently operating at a completely different level than her former training partner. Bueno Silva gets out-muscled over three rounds and Montague takes the decision.

Jafel Filho vs. Cody Durden

Jafel Filho (17-4-0) is a first-round submission machine. His UFC wins include a kimura on Clayton Carpenter, a rear naked choke on Ode Osbourne, and an arm triangle on Daniel Barez — all in the opening round. He also beat ranked contender Amir Albazi by decision before dropping one to Allan Nascimento, then rebounded with that kimura of Carpenter. He's a prohibitive favorite around -500.

Cody Durden (17-10-1) is on a four-fight losing streak and stepped in on six days' notice as a replacement for Lucas Rocha. His last four losses include an anaconda choke from Nascimento — the same man who beat Filho — plus two other stoppages and a decision. His takedown defense sits at 61% and his offensive grappling is essentially non-existent against a fighter of Filho's caliber.

This fight has one dominant narrative: if Filho gets the takedown, it's over quickly. Durden's only path is landing something fight-ending on the feet before the grappling exchanges begin.

Pick: Jafel Filho

Method: Submission

Durden hasn't been able to put it together for a while and there's no reason to think this weekend breaks the skid. When a fighter strings together four consecutive losses, there's usually a real reason — and Durden's submission defense being as porous as it is against the premier submission specialist at 135 pounds is about as bad a matchup as you can draw. Filho mixes in the grappling, gets the fight where he wants it, and finishes within the distance. Submission.

Francis Marshall vs. Lucas Brennan

Francis Marshall (9-3-0) has been a decent-but-unspectacular UFC lightweight, splitting his six Octagon appearances at 3-3. He bounced back from a split decision loss to Mairon Santos with a first-round rear naked choke of Erik Silva in February. He's the -600 favorite, and for once that line is telling an accurate story.

Lucas Brennan (11-2-0) is making his UFC debut on five days' notice after being signed as a last-minute filler for a card that needed it. Ten of his eleven wins have come by finish, including eight first-round submissions, and he's on back-to-back rear naked choke wins in his most recent outings. But this fight was added late specifically to save a thin card — the circumstances of the booking tell you everything you need to know about where Brennan sits in the promotional hierarchy.

Brennan's submission-heavy style mirrors Marshall's own grappling leanings, which means this fight will spend time on the mat. The key question is whether Brennan's submission acuity from outside the UFC translates immediately against a tested UFC fighter on five days' prep.

Pick: Francis Marshall

Method: KO/TKO

There's no good explanation for why Brennan is in the UFC. He was cut from the PFL, he doesn't have the credentials to justify an Octagon spot on merit, and the only reason he's here is that the UFC couldn't fill this card any other way. Marshall has legitimate wins and has faced actual UFC competition. This is a layup. Marshall finishes him.

Max Griffin vs. Victor Valenzuela

Max Griffin (20-12-0) is 39 years old and coming off consecutive losses — a split decision to Chris Curtis and a rear naked choke stoppage by Michael Chiesa in the third round. His nine career knockouts prove he's always dangerous, but the overall trend line of 20-12 with losses accelerating against better competition tells a clear story. The end may be near.

Victor Valenzuela (13-4-0) makes his UFC debut after winning an interim Fury FC belt and scoring a second-round TKO over Yusaku Kinoshita in Tokyo in March. The 32-year-old Chilean has diverse finishing methods — leg kicks, knees, straight KOs — and enters as the -140 favorite, a notable endorsement of his prospects from the oddsmakers for a debuting fighter against a UFC veteran.

Griffin's best hope is grappling exchanges and veteran experience; Valenzuela's is landing the varied striking that has finished opponents in multiple different ways. The veteran-vs-hungry-debuting-prospect dynamic rarely favors the veteran when the age gap is this significant.

Pick: Victor Valenzuela

Method: KO/TKO

Another head-scratching matchup that probably shouldn't be in the UFC. Griffin is 40 years old, coming off two straight losses, and one of those losses came at the hands of Michael Chiesa — a fighter whose calling card is more "boogeyman" reputation than transcendent skill. When Chiesa is ending your night in the third round, you don't have many miles left on those tires.

Valenzuela is hungry, entering his prime at 32, and has the finishing instincts to capitalize on Griffin's declining defensive athleticism. He comes in and seizes the biggest opportunity of his career with a KO finish inside the distance.

Talita Alencar vs. Julia Polastri

Talita Alencar (7-1-1) is a multiple-time BJJ world champion at gi and no-gi across colored and black belt divisions — her grappling credentials are among the best in the strawweight division, period. She's gone 2-2 in her last four UFC fights, with the most recent result a third-round rear naked choke finish of Ariane Carnelossi that showed her submission game remains sharp when the fight reaches the later rounds.

Julia Polastri (14-5-0) is a Muay Thai-based Brazilian whose head kick and punches TKO over veteran Karolina Kowalkiewicz in October was the best performance of her UFC career. She rebounded from a decision loss to Lupita Godinez with that finish and enters here with striking confidence and legitimate one-shot power.

BJJ world champion versus Muay Thai-based striker — Polastri's early-round danger on the feet against Alencar's submission threat growing as the fight progresses. Whoever can impose their preferred range controls this fight.

Pick: Talita Alencar

Method: Decision

After fighting for her roster spot in her last outing, Alencar is looking like a much more cohesive fighter. She is an accomplished grappler on a level few strawweights can match, and in her last fight she showed she can put it all together for a full 15 minutes. She'll be aggressive from the opening bell, use her grappling to dictate the pace and the range, and dominate over the distance on the way to a clear decision win.
