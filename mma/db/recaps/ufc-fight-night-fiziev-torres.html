Summary

The UFC's return to Baku over-delivered on the finishes while leaving a sour taste courtesy of what may be the most nakedly biased refereeing performance in recent memory. The matchups on paper were thin in places, and the card lacked the star power of a true destination event, but the actual fights were fast, mostly decisive, and produced genuine highlights throughout. It was a card that fought above its weight class.


The night started in scandal. The opener between hometown fighter Tahir Abdullayev and Jefferson Nascimento was a masterclass in home-crowd officiating that would embarrass most athletic commissions. The referee scolded Nascimento for moving too much, warned him to engage before the third round (which directly led to him getting taken down), and then stopped the fight with Abdullayev burying his forehead into Nascimento's chest without throwing a punch. This was preceded by a parade of unchecked fouls in Abdullayev's favor: a fence grab that neutralized a takedown, a phantom low blow complaint that halted the action, and a post-bell punch that went unpunished. It was the worst stoppage in recent memory, and the officials who enabled it were a disgrace to the Azerbaijani commission. Anyone defending the call as remotely acceptable is simply not watching objectively. Nascimento deserves a Contender Series slot and a full training camp before anyone draws conclusions about his UFC ceiling.


The finishes came in bunches everywhere else. Nursulton Ruziboev executed a perfect gameplan against Andrey Pulyaev, immediately dragging the fight to the ground and squeezing until Pulyaev was out cold before the referee arrived. AbdulRakhman Yakhyaev landed one right hand eight seconds into his fight with Julius Walker and the evening was already over for Walker. Daniil Donchenko dropped Theodor Berggren with a nasty head kick in round two. Kaan Ofli produced the biggest individual upset of the night, dropping Javier Reyes with a right hand before finishing with an arm-triangle choke. And Abusupiyan Magomedov confirmed that Michal Oleksiejczuk's recent win streak was built on nothing of substance, finishing him with a guillotine choke in the opening round after a head kick and a stiff elbow.


The main event was the highlight of the card. Rafael Fiziev, who had looked like a man in gradual decline after the Ruffy loss, reminded everyone that he is still a dangerous lightweight in front of his hometown crowd. He controlled the first round on the ground, and then opened the second with a spinning wheel kick that sent Manuel Torres crashing to the canvas and finished the job with ground and pound. Torres is young and will be fine, but this was Fiziev's night without question. Next up: Moicano or Hooker, or the loser of whatever the UFC books in two weeks.


The co-main was the competitive low point. Michel Pereira had Sharabutdin Magomedov hurt on the ground in round one and looked like he was about to finish the fight, then spent the next two rounds doing virtually nothing as his gas tank hit empty right on schedule. Magomedov took a unanimous decision on activity and minimal output. Two things are now clear: Pereira after round one is a completely different fighter than Pereira in round one, and Magomedov is not a championship-caliber fighter. He has a glass eye that prevents him from competing in the United States, and he did not produce a single highlight against an opponent who ran out of gas. At some point, the prelims are the appropriate home for a fighter at this level.


Asu Almabayev was outstanding, finishing Charles Johnson with a kneebar in round three after three rounds of sustained grappling control. Johnson is aging out of the contender pool and showed grappling vulnerabilities that are not getting better. Ikram Aliskerov outclassed Brunno Ferreira completely across three rounds in a fight that raised genuine questions about why Ferreira is still on UFC contracts. The Camilo-Sadykhov upset silenced the arena and confirmed that Sadykhov's hype was never backed by the results needed to justify it.


The broadcast worked. The earlier time slot was an improvement over the late-night international card format, the commentary team is measurably better than the PPV crew, and the UFC's commitment to running lean, advertisement-light international events continues to make these cards more watchable than their domestic equivalents. Paul Felder is the best pure commentator in the organization right now. Michael Bisping is better than his reputation suggests and is a clear upgrade over Joe Rogan recycling the same four observations and Daniel Cormier sounding like he is watching highlights rather than the live fight.


Final Score: 7.1/10



Main Card

Rafael Fiziev vs. Manuel Torres

Result: KO (Spinning Wheel Kick and Punches) at R2 0:15

From the opening bell, Rafael Fiziev surged forward with the aggression of a man fighting in front of his hometown crowd for the first time in years. Manuel Torres countered well early and managed to stay upright, but Fiziev dragged him to the canvas and spent the rest of the first round grinding him against the cage. Torres scrambled back to his feet and caught Fiziev with a knee to the groin that briefly halted the action, and the two spent the back half of the round trading in the center without either man establishing real separation. Fiziev got another trip late that put Torres down momentarily, but round one was largely about Fiziev imposing his will without landing a definitive statement.

The second round lasted fifteen seconds. Fiziev opened with a spinning wheel kick that landed flush and sent Torres crashing to the canvas, and a few punches on the ground was all that was needed to bring in the referee. It was an extraordinary finish from a fighter who has spent most of the past three years losing close fights to elite competition.

Fiziev bounced back from his TKO loss to Mauricio Ruffy in emphatic fashion and looked like the most dangerous version of himself all night. He is still a serious problem for anyone in the top ten of the lightweight division. Moicano or Hooker makes sense as the logical next step, or he gets the loser of whatever the UFC lines up in the coming weeks. Torres is young, talented, and will be fine. This was just not his night.

Sharabutdin Magomedov vs. Michel Pereira

Result: Decision (Unanimous) at R3 5:00

Michel Pereira had Sharabutdin Magomedov dead to rights in the first round and then squandered it completely. Pereira landed a big shot early that put Magomedov on the canvas, followed him down with ground and pound, and was landing elbows from on top while Magomedov could only attempt up kicks from his back. Herb Dean stopped the fight to warn Magomedov about grabbing Pereira's hair, which somehow did not result in a point deduction. Magomedov survived, scrambled to top position late in the round, and ended up landing his own ground and pound before the bell. Round one was chaotic, eventful, and the only reason this fight has a rating above a five.

Rounds two and three were a collective study in inactivity. Pereira had nothing left. He wandered around the octagon, threw the occasional jab or right hand, went for a couple of takedowns that Magomedov sprawled on comfortably, and contributed almost nothing offensively. Magomedov was not much better, landing a head kick in the second, pointing to the center of the octagon to complain about Pereira's movement, and then doing the bare minimum to stay ahead on the cards. He also poked Pereira in the eye in the third round without receiving a point deduction, following a pattern established by the earlier hair-grab warning that Herb Dean seemingly forgot about immediately. Magomedov landed a knee on the break and coasted to the decision.

Pereira gassing out after round one is as predictable as anything in this sport, and watching it happen again here was genuinely embarrassing. He had the fight finished and let it slip entirely because he could not sustain five minutes of activity. As for Magomedov: the infatuation with him as a contender is baffling. He is not a championship-caliber fighter, he cannot compete inside the United States due to his prosthetic eye, and he produced zero highlights against an opponent who had already run out of gas. Put him back on the prelims and let the results dictate the conversation rather than the reputation.

Matheus Camilo vs. Nazim Sadykhov

Result: TKO (Punches) at R1 1:31

Nazim Sadykhov came out aggressive from the opening bell, marching forward and pumping calf kicks at the Brazilian underdog with the confidence of a -260 favorite who had already mentally won the fight. Then Matheus Camilo landed a right hand, Sadykhov went down, the referee stopped it, and the Baku arena went completely silent.

This was the upset of the card and a reminder that hype built on regional wins against limited competition has a way of cashing out badly when the margins tighten. Sadykhov had been treated as a significant lightweight prospect, but he is now on a two-fight losing skid and his UFC caliber is genuinely in question. Camilo was doing essentially nothing in that round when the finish came, which is not quite the same as saying he looked good. He is 25 years old, on a two-fight win streak, and the correct path forward is building him up gradually against lower-ranked opponents before the promotion starts handing him names. He is a work in progress, not a finished product.

Asu Almabayev vs. Charles Johnson

Result: Submission (Kneebar) at R3 3:33

The size difference was immediately apparent when Asu Almabayev and Charles Johnson stepped into the octagon together. Johnson, standing 5'9" at flyweight, looked like a different species. Both men exchanged inside leg kicks early before Almabayev caught a kick and dragged Johnson to the mat, where Johnson's long legs gave him just enough guard to frustrate the Kazakh's attempts to advance. Almabayev threatened a guillotine against the cage without being able to lock it in, worked Johnson away from the fence, found side control, and when Johnson scrambled back to his feet, immediately tripped him back down. The first round was a sustained grappling clinic from Almabayev, even if he could not finish.

The second round produced the highlight of the fight. Almabayev was chipping away with calf kicks and clean shots when he landed a spinning back kick directly to the body that dropped Johnson immediately. Somehow no point was deducted. Johnson recovered, put together a good combination on his feet, and made it competitive, but Almabayev kept finding takedowns, kept taking the back, kept chain wrestling Johnson back to the canvas whenever he threatened to stand. The round ended with Almabayev on Johnson's back.

In the third, Almabayev secured the takedown again and found himself briefly caught in a Johnson triangle attempt from the bottom, which he survived by staying composed until Johnson abandoned the submission. From there it was only a matter of time. Almabayev smothered Johnson, moved to the back with a body triangle, and finished with a kneebar that got the tap.

Almabayev looked outstanding across all three rounds and is now 7-1 in the UFC with his only loss to Manel Kape. A matchup with Kyoji Horiguchi makes sense as the logical next step. Johnson is getting older and less athletic than he used to be, and his grappling vulnerabilities are not a recent development. He has a couple more fights in him but the window is closing.

Ikram Aliskerov vs. Brunno Ferreira

Result: Decision (Unanimous) at R3 5:00

Ikram Aliskerov was the aggressor from the first bell, moving forward with calf kicks and takedown attempts while Brunno Ferreira tried to use the cage to neutralize the pressure. Aliskerov stayed persistent, eventually hauling Ferreira to the mat, and when Ferreira scrambled back to his feet, Aliskerov was already attached to his back. A low blow pause briefly interrupted the action, but Aliskerov came right back with a 1-2, threatened a flying knee, and finished the round with another takedown and smothering ground-and-pound from on top.

Aliskerov looked like he tired slightly in the second, which gave Ferreira a window that he largely failed to use. Aliskerov still landed a knee to the head, threatened multiple flying knees that forced Ferreira to defend, and kept getting the fight to the ground. He moved to north-south position, worked to the back, and when Ferreira managed to stand back up, Aliskerov shot a single leg and put him right back down. The third round was more of the same: Aliskerov piecing Ferreira up on the feet, then dragging him to the ground, getting the body triangle, threatening the rear naked choke, and when that did not materialize, just wearing on Ferreira in full mount until the final horn.

Ferreira was outclassed in every department across all fifteen minutes. He lost the striking, he lost the wrestling, and whatever submission game he was supposed to bring never materialized. It is genuinely unclear what version of Brunno Ferreira still deserves to be competing at this level after a performance like that. Aliskerov, on the other hand, is now on a three-fight win streak in the middleweight division and has earned a ranked opponent. Someone in the top ten would be an appropriate next assignment.

Abus Magomedov vs. Michal Oleksiejczuk

Result: Submission (Guillotine Choke) at R1 3:25

Both men felt each other out to start, but Abusupiyan Magomedov quickly established that he was the better striker. A head kick landed clean, an uppercut stung Michal Oleksiejczuk visibly, and Magomedov clinched and started working knees from the over-under position before Oleksiejczuk separated. Then Magomedov landed an elbow that put the Polish fighter on the canvas, followed him to the ground, and locked in a guillotine choke that put Oleksiejczuk out. Magomedov held on longer than necessary while the referee was pulling him off, which was a piece of work on its own, but the fight was well and truly over.

The result confirmed what the preview suggested: Oleksiejczuk's three-fight win streak was built against fighters who had no business threatening a veteran like Magomedov, and treating that run as evidence of genuine resurgence was a mistake. Magomedov's only real limitation is his cardio, and in a fight that ended inside three and a half minutes, that limitation was never tested. He is now back on a winning track and deserves another shot up the rankings. A Bo Nickal matchup would be the kind of interesting stylistic test that actually tells us something about where he fits.

Prelims

Farman Hasanov vs. Eric Nolan

Result: Decision (Unanimous) at R3 5:00

Farman Hasanov established his gameplan immediately: take Eric Nolan down, keep him down, and repeat. Every time Nolan threatened to get back to his feet in the first round, Hasanov was already dragging him back to the canvas. He wore Nolan against the cage, hauled him to the center of the octagon, and kept pressure on whenever Nolan tried to give up his back and crawl toward the fence. It was not pretty, but it was effective.

The second round exposed Hasanov's biggest limitation. He kept getting takedowns, kept grinding, but his gas tank started betraying him and Nolan began to tee off when they were on the feet. Hasanov gassed noticeably in round three, barely moving at times and not even pressing for the immediate takedown that had defined his earlier rounds. He still found clinches, still managed to drag Nolan down, and landed enough baby ground-and-pound from the back to keep the judges on his side. Nolan, for his part, was too paralyzed by the threat of another takedown to really open up on the feet when he had the chance.

Hasanov won, but the style has a hard ceiling. His cardio is a genuine problem, and any fighter with better defensive wrestling and halfway decent striking will eat him alive. Nolan does not look like someone who belongs in the UFC at this point in his career. One more fight to see if he can turn it around, but the bar to clear to stay on the roster is not being met right now.

AbdulRakhman Yakhyaev vs. Julius Walker

Result: KO (Punches) at R1 0:08

AbdulRakhman Yakhyaev landed one right hand on the opening bell, Julius Walker went down, and that was it. Eight seconds.

This is getting to a point where it becomes genuinely irresponsible to keep feeding Yakhyaev opponents who cannot test him. He could have fought again the next day at this pace. The UFC needs to drop him into an actual ranked fight and find out what he is made of, because watching him dispatch cans in under thirty seconds is not a sustainable showcase for anyone involved. Walker was just in the wrong place at the wrong time. It happens.

Nursulton Ruziboev vs. Andrey Pulyaev

Result: Technical Submission (Neck Crank) at R1 3:58

Nursulton Ruziboev came in noticeably lighter on his feet than Andrey Pulyaev, who plodded around flat-footed and invited the clinch. Both men felt each other out early before Pulyaev threw a body kick that Ruziboev caught, immediately converting it into a takedown attempt. Pulyaev managed to get back upright initially, but Ruziboev yanked him away from the cage, brought him to the ground, and immediately took his back with both hooks in. From there it was a matter of time. Ruziboev landed ground-and-pound, went for a rear naked choke and briefly abandoned it to readjust, then squeezed with everything he had. Marc Goddard waited a beat too long to stop it, and Pulyaev was already out cold when the fight was waved off.

The gameplan was executed perfectly. Ruziboev's team correctly identified that Pulyaev was most dangerous on the feet and made sure the fight never stayed there. The moment it hit the ground, it was over almost immediately. Ruziboev is a legitimate dark horse in the middleweight division: he is long, athletic, has genuine finishing ability from both striking and grappling, and now has a clear argument for a ranked opponent. After a performance that clean, he has earned it. Pulyaev is on a two-fight losing streak and needs a win the next time out or the roster spot becomes very difficult to justify.

Kaan Ofli vs. Javier Reyes

Result: Submission (Arm-Triangle Choke) at R1 4:16

The reach and size advantage that Javier Reyes brought into this fight was apparent immediately, but Kaan Ofli neutralized it with footwork that Reyes simply could not match. Reyes plodded forward while Ofli moved fluidly, picking his spots, landing an early right hand that stumbled Reyes and then closing the distance for a clean uppercut. Ofli was consistently lighter on his feet and far more dangerous on the counter throughout the round.

Then Ofli landed another right hand that put Reyes on the canvas. The follow-up ground and pound was ferocious enough that most referees would have stopped it, but when they did not, Ofli transitioned seamlessly to an arm-triangle choke and got the tap. Reyes had the genetics for this sport and none of the execution. He looked completely overmatched from the first exchange to the last.

This was a comfortable result on paper despite the upset tag. Ofli's footwork was the story of the fight, and his ability to switch between striking and grappling mid-sequence is what makes him a dangerous fight for anyone on the featherweight prelims.

Daniil Donchenko vs. Theodor Berggren

Result: TKO (Head Kick and Punches) at R2 1:35

The first round was a genuinely competitive exchange. Daniil Donchenko leaned on his leg kicks to control the distance and set up his offense, while Theodor Berggren showed enough on his UFC debut to suggest he belongs at this level. The round was back and forth and gave no obvious indication of what was coming.

The second round shifted. Berggren started moving forward more aggressively, pushing Donchenko onto the back foot and making him work defensively. Then Donchenko landed a head kick that dropped Berggren immediately, and the follow-up punches brought in the stoppage. Nasty finish from a fighter who just wants to scrap at all times.

Berggren actually looked good enough in there to merit another shot in the promotion. He was making Donchenko uncomfortable when the kick landed, and a debut loss against someone on a six-fight win streak is not the kind of result that should end a UFC run. Donchenko continues to build his case as a legitimate welterweight to watch.

Early Prelims

Jean Matsumoto vs. Bekzat Almakhan

Result: Decision (Unanimous) at R3 5:00

Both fighters spent the early exchanges feinting and circling without committing, but Bekzat Almakhan established himself as the cleaner striker almost immediately. He landed a 1-2 in the first round that put Jean Matsumoto on the canvas, and while Matsumoto bounced back up quickly, the power differential was already obvious. Matsumoto responded by pressing for a takedown against the cage, eventually getting it and landing some elbows from top position, but Almakhan's strikes were doing the real damage whenever they were on the feet.

The second round continued the same pattern. Almakhan was keeping his distance and picking Matsumoto apart with straight punches, while Matsumoto kept throwing hooks that brought him in too close and made him easy to counter. A straight right from Almakhan stung Matsumoto badly and opened a cut above his eye. Matsumoto kept shooting for takedowns that Almakhan was regularly able to prevent with the cage.

The third round featured a phantom low blow call that stopped the action despite the replay showing no contact, and the fight played out in the same general rhythm as the first two, with Matsumoto active but outgunned whenever it came to actual damage.

Then the judges gave it to Matsumoto unanimously, and it is hard to explain. Almakhan was the one landing the shots that mattered. Matsumoto was busy, but busy is different from effective, and the scorecards did not reflect what actually happened in there. Almakhan needs to be far more active and not allow opponents to outwork him on volume the way Matsumoto did, because he will keep losing decisions like this one. As for Matsumoto: another low-ranked opponent to build on before anyone considers matching him up against a genuine contender. Someone like O'Malley would hurt him badly at this stage.

Tahir Abdullayev vs. Jefferson Nascimento

Result: TKO (Punches) at R3 4:28

This fight did not happen in a vacuum, and the officiating made sure of that. Tahir Abdullayev opened the first round flatfooted in the center of the octagon while Jefferson Nascimento circled and threw jabs to the body. Abdullayev landed an overhand right and some clinch uppercuts when he trapped Nascimento against the cage, but Nascimento showed good takedown defense when he stuffed a shot and used the fence. At that point Abdullayev complained loudly that Nascimento had grabbed his gloves and that a low blow had landed. The replay showed neither happened. The referee, nonetheless, gave the complaint serious attention.

The second round belonged to Nascimento. He landed a clean 1-2, threw calf kicks, connected with a right hand, and was generally the more accurate and active fighter. Abdullayev complained again that Nascimento was running away, and the referee stopped the fight to scold Nascimento for his movement. To cap the round, Abdullayev landed a punch after the bell with no consequence.

Then, before the third round began, the referee had a direct conversation with Nascimento instructing him to engage more. Nascimento changed his approach, walked forward, and got taken down. Abdullayev landed ground and pound from inside the guard, passed to side control, moved back to full guard, and landed a few more shots, at which point the referee stopped the fight while Abdullayev's head was buried into Nascimento's chest with no punches being thrown.

It was the worst stoppage in recent memory, and it capped the most nakedly biased sequence of refereeing decisions in a single UFC fight in a very long time. Every call in this bout went against Nascimento: unchecked fouls by Abdullayev, phantom infractions used to stop momentum, a pre-round instruction that directly shaped the outcome, and a stoppage that made no sense by any standard definition. The people willing to defend any part of this officiating as acceptable are simply not watching objectively. Nascimento deserves a Contender Series slot, a full training camp, and a do-over in an environment where the rules apply equally to both fighters.
