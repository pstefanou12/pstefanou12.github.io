Summary

For an Apex card, UFC Vegas 115 delivered just about everything you could ask for: finishes, controversy, upsets, and a main event that doubled as a career revival. The Meta APEX's increased attendance continues to pay dividends — the energy in the building makes these fights feel like they actually matter, and the fighters respond to it accordingly. Score this one a 6.9, which is about as high as an Apex card goes.


The main event was a full-on "you must have forgot" moment for Renato Moicano, who came out fighting like a man who knew retirement was the alternative. Down two straight and reportedly ready to hang it up if he lost, Moicano submitted his own training partner Chris Duncan with a rear naked choke in the second round. Below that, Ethyn Ewing delivered the performance of the night, systematically breaking down Rafael Estevam over three rounds before finishing him with a liver shot — an outright masterclass from a guy who is rapidly stealing hype from fighters who were supposed to be the future of the bantamweight division. Meanwhile, Virna Jandiroba bounced back from her title shot loss with a dominant decision over Tabatha Ricci, whose gameplan amounted to standing in front of a BJJ black belt and hoping for the best.


The biggest shock of the night came buried in the prelims: Tresean Gore — a man who had no business still being in the UFC before this fight — guillotined Azamat Bekoev in the third round in a career-resurgent performance. Bekoev looked like a shell of himself and Gore took full advantage. Elsewhere, Alice Pereira put Hailey Cowan to sleep with a nasty knee KO in round two, and Darrius Flowers finished a clearly injured Lando Vannata — though the more disturbing story there was the corner that sent a man with a broken rib back out for more punishment rather than just taking the loss. Also worth noting: the split decision that handed Kai Kamaka III a win over a Dakota Hope who had outworked him for most of the fight is one of the more baffling scorecards of the year.


There are still valid questions about the booking. Creating fights between two debutants on short notice — skipping the Contender Series entirely — raises legitimate concerns about whether the UFC is so strapped for available fighters to fill Paramount card slots that they're just handing out contracts to warm bodies. Some fights had no business being on this card. But for an Apex night, this one delivered where it counted.


Final Score: 6.9/10



Main Card

Renato Moicano vs. Chris Duncan

Result: Submission, Rear Naked Choke at 3:14 Round 2 of 5, 8:14 Total

Renato Moicano came into this main event with his back against the wall — down two straight, fighting his own training partner, and reportedly ready to walk away from the sport if he lost. He did not look like a man in crisis. From the opening bell, Moicano was the aggressor, plodding forward and landing a big right hand that stumbled Chris Duncan early. The first round was competitive, with both fighters landing on the feet and Duncan's nose already bleeding by the final bell.


The second round was where Moicano put it together. He peppered Duncan with his jab, opened up the right hand again — this time dropping Duncan clean — and quickly swarmed with hooks, landing on the back with a body triangle locked in. From there, Moicano unloaded ground and pound before sinking in the rear naked choke to get the tap at 3:14 of round two.


It was a "you must have forgot" performance from Moicano, the kind a fighter produces when the lights finally come on after a rough stretch. From the post-fight interview, it became clear this was effectively his last run — he said he would have retired if he lost. He'll keep fighting until he loses again, but for one night he looked every bit like the fighter who pushed Makhachev at UFC 311. Duncan, for his part, looked awful — though it's always a strange dynamic when you're fighting someone you share a gym with. A Jalin Turner rematch feels like the most logical next step for Moicano.

Virna Jandiroba vs. Tabatha Ricci

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Virna Jandiroba came into this fight needing a bounce-back after losing a vacant UFC Strawweight title bout to Mackenzie Dern, and she got it — decisively. From the opening minute, Jandiroba dragged Tabatha Ricci into her world and never let her out. She secured a takedown in the first, dragged Ricci back down when she stood up, and eventually got to her back on the canvas. Ricci survived the round, but Jandiroba had completely controlled the grappling from start to finish.


The second round was more of the same. Jandiroba put Ricci on the mat immediately, wore on her from top position, and kept the fight exactly where Ricci didn't want it. The brief exchanges on the feet were largely uneventful — and the moments where Ricci chose to clinch up against the fence were especially puzzling given she was losing every single grappling exchange. In the third, Ricci finally managed a takedown and found herself in mount, but Jandiroba reversed position and bounced back to her feet. Jandiroba shot for another takedown late and attacked the neck, but Ricci defended it well enough to hear the final bell.


Jandiroba won a comfortable unanimous decision, and it wasn't particularly close. Ricci simply had no answer for the grappling and nothing really worked for her in this matchup — she needs to go back to the drawing board. For Jandiroba, the win reestablishes her at the top of the division, though one more fight feels right before she gets another title shot. The winner of Tatiana Suarez vs. Lupita Godinez the following weekend looks like the most likely path.

Abdul Rakhman Yakhyaev vs. Brendson Ribeiro

Result: Submission, Rear Naked Choke at 2:52 Round 1 of 3

It was not as clean as his previous two UFC appearances, but Abdul Rakhman Yakhyaev got the job done. Brendson Ribeiro actually caught him early — landing a right hand that buckled Yakhyaev's knees and then following up with another shot that stumbled him again. For a moment, it looked like the unbeaten prospect might be in trouble. Yakhyaev shot for a takedown in desperation but was denied.


The turning point came when Ribeiro threw a head kick, Yakhyaev caught it, and dragged him to the canvas. From there, it was textbook: elbows to the body, wearing Ribeiro down, and eventually taking the back. Every time Ribeiro worked back to his feet, Yakhyaev simply dragged him back down. The rear naked choke sank in and Ribeiro tapped at 2:52 of round one.


Yakhyaev is still green — getting clipped multiple times early by Ribeiro made that obvious — and the UFC needs to resist the urge to fast-track him against ranked opponents. If they give him someone with real striking ability before he's ready, it could end badly. Ribeiro drops to three straight losses and his UFC future is in serious doubt.

Ethyn Ewing vs. Rafael Estevam

Result: KO/TKO, Left Hook to the Liver at 1:44 Round 3 of 3, 11:44 Total

Ethyn Ewing came into this fight with momentum off his stunning UFC debut win over Malcolm Wellmaker — and he made a statement that no one can chalk up to a one-off. Against a 14-0 Rafael Estevam who had never been finished professionally, Ewing delivered a systematic, three-round dismantling that was as methodical as it was impressive.


The first round set the tone. Ewing moved forward, landed leg kicks at range, and stuffed two takedown attempts from Estevam — the second one followed immediately by a big combination that punished Estevam for shooting. In the second, Ewing got Estevam against the cage and started teeing off. Estevam's face began to show the damage, and he had no real answer — just surviving without mounting any meaningful offense of his own. By the third round, Estevam was already a bloody mess. Ewing stumbled him with a right hand, kept breaking him down, and then put him away with a liver shot that sent Estevam to the canvas at 1:44 of round three.


A real masterclass. Ewing has good striking and has now shown legitimate wrestling defense — he may be able to compete with some real names at bantamweight. The hype that was attached to Wellmaker last fall is now fully transferring to Ewing, and rightfully so. For Estevam, the weight miss likely seals his fate — he will probably be cut regardless of how close the fight was, and this one wasn't close at all.

Tommy McMillen vs. Manolo Zecchini

Result: KO/TKO, Punches and Knees at 3:57 Round 1 of 3

Tommy McMillen made his UFC debut with all the subtlety of a bar fight. From the opening bell, he was hurling haymakers with zero regard for range or strategy, charging straight into Manolo Zecchini's striking lane despite being the significantly longer fighter. Zecchini actually surged forward early, but McMillen ate it and kept coming, landing a big left hand that put a welt on the side of Zecchini's face. A knee to the groin paused the action briefly, and then McMillen went right back to throwing chaos — eventually stringing together a combination that put Zecchini out on his feet. Herb Dean stepped in to stop it at 3:57 of round one.


The finish was fine. The approach was not. McMillen spent the whole round fighting in close range with a man he had a massive physical advantage over — constantly giving Zecchini a puncher's chance he had no business receiving. His corner shouting "chaos, chaos" during the fight about sums up the gameplan. If this is how McMillen fights every time out, a legit featherweight will clean his clock. He's another douchebag who will get completely exposed the moment he steps up in competition.

José Delano vs. Robert Ruchała

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

José Delano made his UFC debut against a man who needed a win to keep his roster spot, and the fight played out over three competitive rounds that were closer than the scorecards suggested. Robert Ruchała was the more active fighter early, firing head kicks and spinning backfists — the latter of which was nowhere near landing — and generally controlling the pace on the feet in the first. Delano was timid initially, but the volume wasn't translating into damage.


The second round shifted things. Delano started finding his range, landing a nasty left hand that busted up Ruchała's face, and began piecing him up more consistently as the round wore on. He even tripped Ruchała over by catching his heel on a kick. In the third, Ruchała came out with more intent — landing head kicks, straight shots, and spinning back kicks with more authority. Delano seemed overwhelmed by the volume at points. Then Ruchała grabbed the fence with both hands on a Delano takedown attempt, costing him a point from referee Keith Peterson — though the point deduction ultimately didn't change the outcome. Delano clinched and closed out the round.


The judges gave it to Delano unanimously, but one judge scoring it 30-27 for Delano is a stretch — even his corner didn't think he won all three rounds. Delano looked like a fish out of water in the early going before finding his footing, and the point deduction didn't end up mattering. One other gripe: taking a point and then resetting the fighters in the same position — giving Ruchała back the spot he fouled to escape — seems like the wrong call. Either deduct the point or give the position. Not both.

Prelims

Thomas Petersen vs. Guilherme Pat

Result: Decision, Majority at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Two rounds of not much, followed by a wild third — that was Thomas Petersen vs. Guilherme Pat in a nutshell. Pat moved forward to start the fight while Petersen circled and backed up almost the entire first round. Pat landed a big calf kick; Petersen clinched when he could and tried a trip that barely got Pat to his knees before he popped back up. The second round followed the same script: some clinch work, a jab from Pat that stung Petersen, and not much else worth writing home about.


Then the third round happened. Petersen shot for a takedown, clipped Pat badly against the cage, and Pat went down. Petersen followed with nasty ground and pound that nearly finished it, but Pat somehow survived and made it back to his feet — only to get clipped again and go down a second time. Petersen followed up again with more ground and pound, though he was visibly tiring and the output dropped as the round wore on. Pat endured and the fight went to the judges.


Two judges gave Petersen two rounds, which is a bit generous given he did essentially nothing for the first two-thirds of the fight. The third round was legitimately spectacular and carried him to the majority decision, but if Petersen had that in him all along, it's fair to wonder what he was doing in rounds one and two.

Alessandro Costa vs. Stewart Nicoll

Result: KO/TKO, Left Hook to the Liver at 4:56 Round 2 of 3, 9:56 Total

Alessandro Costa looked every bit the seasoned veteran in this one. Stewart Nicoll came out aggressive and spamming spinning attacks from round one — spinning backfist after spinning backfist, with no real setup and no real damage. Costa, meanwhile, kept his composure, landed some sharp calf kicks early, and picked his spots. By the end of round one, Costa had hurt Nicoll with a body kick and Nicoll was backpedaling.


The second round was a clinic in staying calm when a guy with no gameplan is swinging wildly. Costa worked Nicoll against the cage, got a takedown, and got to his back. Nicoll shook him off and got back to his feet, but the damage was accumulating. Costa then landed something that hurt Nicoll badly and started teeing off before going for a takedown. He tried to take the back and sink a choke, Nicoll escaped, and then Costa ended it emphatically — a left hook to the liver that made Nicoll face-plant the canvas. Herb stepped in immediately at 4:56 of round two.


Liver shots are nasty. Exhibit A. Costa looked like a composed, experienced fighter who knew exactly what he was doing against an inexperienced guy who thought spinning backfists were a gameplan. Three straight losses for Nicoll in the UFC — that is almost certainly it for him.

Darrius Flowers vs. Lando Vannata

Result: KO/TKO, Hammerfists at 0:52 Round 2 of 3, 5:52 Total

Darrius Flowers vs. Lando Vannata started as a scrappy lightweight fight and ended as a cautionary tale about corner negligence. Vannata came out moving and landed a 1-2 early, briefly getting Flowers to the ground before he scrambled back up. Vannata got a trip takedown mid-round, found mount, and was on top when Flowers threatened an armbar — Vannata somehow escaped and they returned to the feet. Then, in the closing seconds of the round, Flowers landed a brutal slam that sent Vannata crashing to the canvas and followed up with ground and pound. The round ended, but Vannata looked like a man who had just broken a rib.


He came out for round two anyway. Flowers walked him down, shot a takedown of his own, and the moment it went to the ground Vannata covered up immediately — he was in too much pain to fight. Herb stepped in to stop it at 0:52 of round two.


The main takeaway here has nothing to do with either fighter's performance. It has everything to do with the corner that watched Vannata take a devastating slam, saw their guy wince through the break, and sent him back out there like a lamb to slaughter. You already lost the fight. Protect your guy. Vannata is 34 years old, now on a three-fight losing skid, and will almost certainly be cut. Don't risk irreparable damage for a win you're not getting. As for Flowers — nice finish, but the post-fight mic celebration was a little much for a guy who just beat a broken man.

Alice Pereira vs. Hailey Cowan

Result: KO/TKO, Knee at 4:24 Round 2 of 3, 9:24 Total

Alice Pereira vs. Hailey Cowan was not a long fight, but it was eventful enough. Both women came out swinging in round one, and Cowan actually looked decent early — landing a clean punch that rocked Pereira and a knee to the head in the final seconds of the frame. She went for a takedown mid-round and Pereira popped back up easily. Heading into round two, it felt like Cowan might have a real chance.


She did not. Cowan got Pereira to the ground partway through the second, but Pereira neutralized any ground and pound by pulling Cowan close to her body. When they got back to their feet, Pereira ended it immediately — a nasty knee that put Cowan out cold on the canvas. The KO came at 4:24 of round two.


Cowan had one decent moment in the entire fight and never inflicted any serious damage. She shouldn't be in the UFC. Pereira, on the other hand, looks promising — she took a shot and kept her composure, then found the finish. The one area she needs to develop is her grappling, because the women's bantamweight division at the top is deep enough that striking alone won't be enough. That said, the division is complete chaos, and you genuinely never know who is going to rise in it.

Tresean Gore vs. Azamat Bekoev

Result: Submission, Technical (Guillotine Choke) at 3:27 Round 3 of 3, 13:27 Total

Nobody saw this coming. Tresean Gore walked into this fight as a massive underdog on the verge of getting cut, and he walked out with one of the more stunning upsets on a Vegas card this year. Azamat Bekoev was supposed to be the guy the UFC was building — the former LFA champion on a long win streak who needed a rebound performance. He looked like a completely different fighter.


The first round was competitive. Both men traded leg kicks early, Gore landed a nice body kick, and then — the moment of the fight — Gore landed a spinning elbow that hurt Bekoev badly. Bekoev shot for a desperate takedown but was denied. They traded back and forth through the rest of the round with Bekoev landing a combination late. The second round saw Bekoev drop Gore with a big right and follow with ground and pound, but Gore got back up and answered with a right of his own. The grappling went back and forth with both men scoring takedowns.


By the third, it was clearly Gore's fight. He was the fresher man, walking Bekoev down on damaged legs while Bekoev stumbled around the octagon. Gore took Bekoev down, got into mount, and started landing elbows. Then he sank in a guillotine choke and put Bekoev to sleep at 3:27. Insane. Bekoev looked like a one-trick pony — get you early or he's done — and Gore exposed him completely. It was a career-resurgent win for Gore, though let's not get carried away: he's still cringe and annoying, and he probably won't go far at this level.

Dione Barbosa vs. Melissa Gatto

Result: Decision, Majority at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Dione Barbosa vs. Melissa Gatto was a messy three rounds, made messier by an illegal shot that should have had bigger consequences. The first round was a back-and-forth grappling exchange — Gatto secured a takedown and got into mount before Barbosa threatened an armbar to close it out. Neither woman looked dominant.


The second round took a controversial turn. After Barbosa scrambled to top position, she stood up and landed a soccer kick squarely to a grounded Gatto's head area. The shot was clearly illegal — Gatto's opponent was grounded, it was obvious — and the referee waved off the action. Gatto was out cold on the canvas. The fight was ultimately restarted with Barbosa docked one point. One point. For nearly decapitating an opponent with an illegal kick. It should have been at minimum two points, if not an outright DQ. But what do I know.


With the restart, the fight continued. Barbosa got more takedowns, smothered Gatto against the cage, and landed a spinning elbow that looked sharp. The third round was a scrambled mess of reversals and position changes, with Barbosa generally in control but neither woman doing anything decisive. Barbosa won a majority decision that, illegal shot aside, reflected the actual fight — Gatto had no business being in there with her. Gatto is 29 and still hasn't figured out what she's doing in this division.

Kai Kamaka III vs. Dakota Hope

Result: Decision, Split at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Dakota Hope fought like a man possessed for fifteen minutes and somehow walked away without the win. From the opening bell, Hope was surging forward, landing big combinations and heavy knees in the clinch against Kai Kamaka III. In the first round, Hope staggered Kamaka, followed him to the ground with ground and pound, and Kamaka barely survived — getting back to his feet and using the clinch to slow things down. It was a dominant round for Hope.


The second was more of the same. Hope kept advancing with bad intentions, landing the better shots whenever they were in open space. Kamaka's primary response was to clinch and hope the activity slowed — which it did somewhat, but Hope was still landing the cleaner punches when they traded in the center. The third round saw both men begin to tire, with Hope slowing noticeably — but Kamaka showed no urgency whatsoever, fighting as if he had already banked enough to win on the cards, even after getting his ass kicked for two rounds. Hope put together another big combination late and got a takedown with twenty seconds left.


The judges gave it to Kamaka by split decision. How? Hope nearly finished him in the first, outworked him throughout, and Kamaka did nearly nothing for fifteen minutes except clinch and land shots in the pocket that, maybe, looked more effective than they appeared on screen. It was an insane decision. Hope is entertaining, took the fight on a couple days' notice, and should absolutely be kept — imagine what he does with a full training camp. Kamaka keeps his UFC dream alive, but if he thinks clinching and hoping for the best is a long-term strategy, he's going to get picked apart by anyone with real ability at this level.
