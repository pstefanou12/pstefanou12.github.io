Summary

UFC Fight Night: Emmett vs. Vallejos was probably the best card the UFC has put on so far this year — which says something, though it's genuinely unclear whether that's a compliment to the card or an indictment of everything else in 2026. Either way, the fights delivered.


The two legitimate gripes are that there were no high-stakes matchups and the whole thing was buried in the Apex. The most high-profile fight on the card was the main event, and even that will just move its winner toward another ranked opponent. Kevin Vallejos is entertaining, he's got a fan base, and he's good enough to be on a PPV or a live Fight Night event — not in a warehouse. There were other matchups on the card that also had no business being in the Apex: Bia Mesquita is a female bantamweight who could be a major problem for a lot of contenders, and she's opening a prelim card in front of nobody. The matchmaking didn't deserve all that much praise here — a lot of what made this card good was luck. The matchmakers thought Holloway vs. Oliveira would be a banger and that was a complete dud. Good UFC events in 2026 might just be random chance at this point.


The name to walk away from this card with is Marwan Rahiki. He is lengthy, quick, must-see TV, and has the potential to be a real star in this promotion. His performance against Hardwick was a statement. Outside of that, the main event delivered shock value, the prelims were competitive, and the night felt like a sleeper card that punched well above its billing.


Final Score: 6.9/10



Main Card

Kevin Vallejos vs. Josh Emmett

Result: KO/TKO, Punches, Knees, and Elbows at 3:33 Round 1 of 5

Kevin Vallejos walked Josh Emmett down from the opening bell and never let him breathe. Emmett moved around the octagon early trying to create angles, but Vallejos was relentless in cutting off the cage and landing combinations. A knockdown came quickly, Emmett bounced back to his feet, and then Vallejos put him down again. This time he followed to the ground and unloaded, and Emmett was done — TKO finish, round one, 3:33.

What was wild about this was the timing. Ilia Topuria went the full distance with Emmett. Vallejos just walked through him in less than four minutes. People will have short-term memory on this and act surprised, but the reality is that Emmett was never as good as the hype suggested. He is a one-trick pony — knock out whoever walks stupidly into his pocket — and beyond that punch, he has nothing. Poor striking defense, no technical skills, no wrestling, no jiu-jitsu. Three straight losses now at 41 years old. It's time to hang it up. As for Vallejos, the next logical step is a rematch with Jean Silva, and the UFC should get him out of the Apex for it.

Gillian Robertson vs. Amanda Lemos

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Gillian Robertson controlled this fight through grappling over three rounds, though it never got particularly exciting. Amanda Lemos spent the first round defending takedown attempts and trading in a stalemate near the fence, with Robertson trying to drag the fight to the ground and Lemos looking to land on the way in. The second round was Robertson's best, getting the takedown, passing to side control, and threatening a kimura from north-south before working back to a dominant position. The third was more of the same — Robertson grinding on Lemos, eating up time, denying any meaningful offense.

Pretty boring fight overall. Just grappling exchanges without anything remarkable to write home about. Robertson came out of the fight calling for a title shot, pointing to her five-fight win streak, which is fair — but Zhang Weili has to be next in line for the belt. Robertson's run is real, it's just hard to get excited about a path to the championship that runs through fights like this one.

Jose Delgado vs. Andre Fili

Result: Decision, Split at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Jose Delgado came out using kicks to set the range, but Andre Fili caught him early. A big right hand in round one put Delgado down, and though he scrambled back to his feet with a desperation takedown, the damage was done — his output cratered for the rest of the frame and Fili cruised to a clear first round, capping it with a takedown of his own.

Delgado showed mental toughness coming out composed in round two, going back to his kicks and staying at range. He managed a takedown and landed elbows from mount, but Fili got back to his feet quickly. The problem for Delgado was consistency — whenever Fili connected, it was effective, and Fili controlled the fight with selective, well-placed shots. The third round was more active for Delgado, landing a big head kick and winning on output, which was enough for a split decision. Tough loss for Fili given he had Delgado in deep trouble in the first and never finished it. Delgado talks about being the best in the world, but he's got a long way to go — he looked timid after absorbing that early right hand and needs to learn how to push through adversity.

Marwan Rahiki vs. Harry Hardwick

Result: KO/TKO, Corner Stoppage at 5:00 Round 2 of 3, 10:00 Total

Marwan Rahiki put on an absolute clinic. The first round was a good exchange with both fighters throwing with intent, but the second round was something else entirely. Rahiki cracked Harry Hardwick with a vicious head kick that hurt him badly, then poured on combinations to the head and body that had Hardwick surviving on instinct alone. A desperate takedown attempt from a hurt Hardwick didn't save him — Rahiki reversed into mount and the damage was enough that the corner stopped it between rounds after Hardwick's jaw was broken.

Rahiki is nasty. He's lengthy, quick, and can take your body apart from distance. His ability to work the full range of the human frame — legs, body, head — with that kind of precision is rare. Hardwick showed more tonight than in his debut, but he was completely outclassed and he knows it. He should get another shot in the UFC to show what he's got, but he was getting carved up out there. Rahiki has every tool to be a big star in this promotion.

Ion Cutelaba vs. Oumar Sy

Result: Submission, Mounted Guillotine Choke at 4:24 Round 1 of 3

Nobody saw this coming. Ion Cutelaba and Oumar Sy felt each other out early before Sy went for the clinch. Cutelaba tripped him to the mat, and what followed was a wild scramble — Sy nearly reversed into mount, but Cutelaba won the scramble and landed on top. Sy tried to pull Cutelaba close and neutralize the ground and pound, but Cutelaba worked to a mounted guillotine and forced the tap at 4:24 of round one.

Great win for Cutelaba, embarrassing performance from Sy. That's really all there is to say about it.

Charles Johnson vs. Bruno Silva

Result: Decision, Split at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

This fight spent most of its time finding creative ways to not happen. Bruno Silva kept initiating clinches against the fence, Charles Johnson was fighting off the back foot and landing the occasional knee off the break, and between the low blows and head clashes that kept stopping the action, rounds one and two were more disruption than actual combat.

The third round finally delivered, with both fighters throwing to hurt each other late. The split decision for Johnson was a bit of a surprise — it was hard to see what he was doing enough of to clearly beat Silva — but the judges saw it differently. Johnson has now stopped Josh Van and Lo'ner Kavanagh, called out a top five opponent, and probably deserves exactly that given those credentials. Some guys build a case in the background and Johnson has quietly put together a legitimate one.

Prelims

Myktybek Orolbai vs. Chris Curtis

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Three rounds of Myktybek Orolbai taking Chris Curtis down and grinding on him. Every round opened the same way: both fighters feeling each other out, Orolbai shooting in on a single leg, and then Curtis spending the rest of the frame trying to get back to his feet while Orolbai dragged him back down. In round one, Orolbai got the back, flattened Curtis out on his stomach, and landed ground and pound — a lot of those shots landed suspiciously close to the back of the head, but the referee let it go. Same story in rounds two and three, with one violent slam to emphasize the point. Orolbai threatened a face crank in round one and generally just wore on Curtis without doing much to finish the fight.

Pretty lame fight overall. Curtis's corner kept telling him to put pressure on Orolbai and take away the takedowns, and Curtis never did it — every round he stood flat-footed and let Orolbai shoot in unopposed. This is probably the end for Curtis. He's 38, moved down from middleweight to fight at welterweight, and couldn't compete with a natural lightweight. He doesn't have the grappling to survive that kind of attack, and his striking didn't look good enough to make up for it. Orolbai needs to work on finishing fights — that was dull.

Vitor Petrino vs. Steven Asplund

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Vitor Petrino and Steven Asplund had themselves an entertaining slobberknocker while making each other's cardio look terrible. Asplund came out throwing jabs and moving, but Petrino was just dropping bombs from the jump — countering everything with big hooks that opened a cut under Asplund's eye early. Petrino's takedowns weren't landing, but his hands were. The round ended with Asplund stunning Petrino, following to the ground, landing some ground and pound, and Petrino scrambling back to his feet — a wild round with momentum swinging both ways.

By round two, both guys were visibly gassing. Petrino caught Asplund off balance and got to the back, tried to drag him down and go for a face crank, but Asplund shook it off. From there it was two tired men throwing slow, lethargic combinations at each other. The third round was more of the same — both fighters in genuine distress, Asplund's face bright red and gushing blood, Petrino walking away instead of pressing advantages. Asplund got dropped by a leg kick late but finished the fight swinging. Petrino got the nod on the cards. He's explosive and has real power, but his cardio is a serious problem — he was a ghost after round one. If he can put together three full rounds, he could be a legitimate contender. Asplund needs to fix the lunging jab habit — he's leaving himself open for exactly the kind of counters Petrino was landing all night.

Elijah Smith vs. Su Young You

Result: Submission, Rear Naked Choke at 1:04 Round 2 of 3, 6:04 Total

Su Young You won round one by just wearing on Elijah Smith — getting to the back, getting hooks in, threatening a guillotine, and dragging Smith back down every time he tried to stand. It was a controlled, systematic performance. The only problem was that You has no striking, knows it, and his whole strategy appeared to be grinding an opponent down over 15 to 25 minutes while never actually threatening a submission.

Round two turned quickly. You shot in for a takedown, Smith shook it off, landed a big right, and knocked You to the ground. He followed with nasty ground and pound, got to the back in the scramble, sank the rear naked choke, and got the tap at 1:04 of round two. When Smith looked good, he looked dangerous. The biggest takeaway from this fight: whenever a Road to UFC fighter is matched against anyone from outside that bracket, just bet on the opponent.

Manoel Sousa vs. Bolaji Oki

Result: KO/TKO, Straight Right at 4:12 Round 3 of 3, 14:12 Total

Manoel Sousa dropped Bolaji Oki in round one with a big right hand and followed with heavy ground and pound — Oki had no business surviving that. He did, somehow, scrambling back to his feet and reversing position. Sousa kept hunting a kimura off the fence that made increasingly little sense as the round wore on, and Oki took back control from the clinch to finish the frame on top.

Round two was Oki's — he landed a right hook that wobbled Sousa, started taking over on the feet, and began controlling the clinch. But the third round ended it all with Sousa landing a nasty straight right that put Oki out cold at 4:12. Entertaining fight overall. Oki is lucky he survived the first — and ultimately it didn't matter because the finish came anyway. Sousa is a solid prospect for a debut, but he gets hit too easily and makes some questionable decisions in there. Getting picked apart by someone like Oki, who doesn't have a reputation as a striker, is a concern. Oki is now 0-2 in the UFC — impressive roar-back in round two, but it wasn't enough.

Eryk Anders vs. Brad Tavares

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Three rounds of Eryk Anders shooting for takedowns and pressing Brad Tavares against the cage. Anders was going to the well constantly with his wrestling, and the first round had a mixed report card — couldn't finish the takedowns, but landed a sharp elbow in the clinch that stung Tavares, then closed the round with a right hand that dropped him. Tavares was shelling up to survive the final seconds. Round two was more dynamic, with Tavares landing a big right of his own to send Anders to the canvas — but Anders pulled guard, gave up the back, rolled out of it, and eventually finished on top with Tavares pinned against the fence.

The third was Anders grinding forward, finally getting the takedown, landing shots from side control and on the feet, and just outworking Tavares to a unanimous decision. Anders announced his retirement after the fight, which is honestly the right call — and going out on a win is the way to do it. He always had the athleticism but was content to grind rather than try to finish, and at this stage of his career against ranked opponents, that wouldn't cut it anyway. As for Tavares, his striking is decent but nothing special, his grappling isn't strong, and it's genuinely unclear what the next move looks like. Neither of these guys can beat ranked opponents.

Bia Mesquita vs. Montse Rendon

Result: Submission, Rear Naked Choke at 2:07 Round 1 of 3

Montse Rendon came charging forward at the opening bell trying to impose pressure, and Bia Mesquita answered immediately with a left hand that hurt Rendon and sent her retreating to the cage. From there it was over quickly — Mesquita landed big shots on the feet, took Rendon down, worked to full mount, and when Rendon gave up the back, the rear naked choke was there. Tap at 2:07 of round one.

Mesquita was nasty, but that was expected going in. The bigger issue is what comes next — this woman should not be getting buried in an Apex prelim. She is too good for this. Put her in a packed arena, give her the UFC marketing machine behind her, and start feeding her ranked opponents. Women's bantamweight is a shallow division, and Mesquita looks like someone who can beat the vast majority of the top 15. She's not ready for Joselyne Edwards yet, but give her Macy Chiasson or Ketlen Vieira and see what happens. Rendon will be back — just don't book her against a hot undefeated prospect right out of the gate.

Hecher Sosa vs. Luan Lacerda

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

For a second fight on an Apex card, this one went crazy. Hecher Sosa and Luan Lacerda went at each other with bad intentions for three rounds — Lacerda working leg kicks and takedowns, Sosa throwing heavy leather and looking to finish. The first round had it all: Lacerda getting Sosa to the mat twice, Sosa landing elbows off his back to create separation, a big knee to the face on the break, and a right hand from Sosa that stunned Lacerda and sent him backing up.

Round two saw Sosa's nose open up early and start pouring blood, but he stayed dangerous — landing a flying knee and some heavy combinations that had Lacerda backing up. Lacerda kept shooting and kept getting stuffed against the fence as Sosa neutralized him there. The third round had both fighters visibly gassed, the shots crisper earlier in the fight than what was on display now. Sosa kept spamming spinning backfists that weren't landing — never connected, and it's a questionable choice to commit to a shot that clearly isn't working. Lacerda's face was gushing blood by the end, and the fight closed with them slugging against the fence. Sosa got the nod in his debut, but he needs to improve if he wants to stick in the promotion — he gets hit too easily and makes low-IQ decisions. Lacerda is now 1-3 in the UFC, but this guy is a scrapper and worth tuning in to on a prelim card.

Piera Rodriguez vs. Sam Hughes

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Piera Rodriguez walked Sam Hughes down for three rounds and made it look easy. The first round had a moment or two where Hughes tried to counter with big shots and even stunned Rodriguez briefly with a right, but Rodriguez recovered immediately and kept coming forward, finishing the round with a sharp combination. Hughes tried to grapple her way out of the pressure — using the fence to deny Rodriguez's takedown attempt, throwing vicious knees to the body in the clinch in round two — but Rodriguez was landing too consistently and the damage was accumulating on Hughes's face by round's end.

By the third round, Hughes had stopped trying to counter entirely — she was just backing up and getting hit. Rodriguez was throwing nasty combinations every time she engaged, and Hughes had no answers. One-way traffic from start to finish. Rodriguez looked dominant throughout and the scorecards were never in doubt. Hughes looked like a fish out of water in there — it's unclear what direction her UFC career heads after this.
