Summary

UFC 326 was another disappointing entry in what is shaping up to be one of the worst years in recent UFC history. There were some bright spots — a couple of nasty first-round stoppages on the undercard and a submission finish that showed some real promise — but two lay-and-pray performances on the main card killed whatever momentum the night had built, and the main event ended with fans booing in the arena. That about sums it up.


The main event between Charles Oliveira and Max Holloway was a showcase of the UFC's most annoying habit: building a fighter's résumé exclusively against opponents that suit their style, then being surprised when that fighter gets exposed. Max has fought boxers his entire UFC career. The moment he stepped in against a world-class grappling specialist, he had nothing. Five rounds of Charles taking him down, passing his guard, and riding out a decision. The crowd booed. They were right to.


The Gregory Rodrigues and Drew Dober first-round stoppages were the highlights of the night — two KOs that gave the crowd something to actually react to. Alberto Montes also looked sharp on the prelims with a quick anaconda choke finish that suggested there's real upside there. But the Oliveira–Holloway main event and Raul Rosas Jr. grinding out a three-round lay-and-pray against Rob Font dragged the whole card down.


To make things worse, during the event the UFC announced the White House Main Event card headlined by Ilia Topuria vs. Justin Gaethje and Alex Pereira vs. Cyril Gane. The fights are fine, but they feel like compromises. Topuria vs. Arman is the fight everyone wants. Pereira vs. Jones is the fight that would actually feel special. Instead we're getting Gaethje — who has no business fighting for a title in 2026 — and Gane because apparently Jon Jones doesn't exist. The rest of the card is a joke: Bo Nickal vs. Kyle Daukaus years after Nickal declared he'd be champion, O'Malley vs. Zahabi, and Michael Chandler on a marquee card. The announcement landed with a thud. Every card this year has felt like the UFC is phoning it in now that the Paramount money is in the bank.


The BMF title needs to go away. It gave the UFC an excuse to put on a non-title main event that ended in five rounds of lay-and-pray, and somehow the winner got to call it the fight of the night. If that belt exists to honor the baddest man on the planet, the person wearing it shouldn't be winning by out-grinding someone for 25 minutes. 2026 is almost through Q1 and the UFC has yet to put on a single undisputed title fight or a card that felt genuinely special. The fans booing at the end of UFC 326 weren't wrong.


Final Score: 6.1/10



Main Card

Charles Oliveira vs. Max Holloway

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 5 Rounds, 25:00 Total

Charles Oliveira bear-hugged Max Holloway from the opening bell, took him down immediately, passed his guard, and worked to Max's back. That set the tone for the next 25 minutes. Max had his submission defense to thank for not getting finished — he fought the hands, changed positions, and survived — but he had no answer for the takedowns and no ability to get back to his feet. Round after round, Charles out-grappled him. Round 2 featured a beautifully timed takedown off a front kick. Round 3 was more of the same. By Round 4, Charles had Max in a crucifix and was landing strange heel shots while Max sat there watching the clock. Round 5, Max scrambled up to mount at one point, but Charles didn't care — Max wasn't doing any damage from there anyway. They stood and traded for the last five seconds. The crowd was absolutely justified in booing loudly at the end — you pay for a BMF title fight and you get 25 minutes of grappling control. Not exactly what was advertised.


The main takeaway here is that the UFC very clearly protects their favorite fighters. Max has been fed boxers for virtually his entire career — guys who will stand and trade with him. The moment he steps in against a grappling specialist, he is completely outclassed. It wasn't close. It wasn't competitive. Charles calling this the fight of the night is a definite stretch — it was a grappling clinic, not a fight. A fighter who wins via lay-and-pray doesn't deserve to wear the BMF title.


This is a bad spot for both fighters going forward. Max can't go near the top of the lightweight division — Islam and Arman are pure grapplers who would do the exact same thing to him, just faster. Still, it must sting that Gaethje gets a title fight while Max needs to fight down the rankings. There's an argument that this could be the end of Max Holloway's career as a relevant contender. As for Charles, the next matchups that make sense are Arman vs. Paddy and Charles vs. BSD — let the winners sort it out from there for a shot at the belt.

Caio Borralho vs. Reinier de Ridder

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

From the opening exchanges, Caio Borralho was landing the better shots. He tagged Reinier de Ridder with big rights early in round one, sending RDR to the canvas at one point before he bounced back up. De Ridder's primary response was to clinch Borralho against the cage and wear on him, but he wasn't doing serious damage there — just surviving. By round three, Borralho secured a takedown, grounded RDR, and held control. RDR made a late push trying to get a finish, landing some combinations, but Borralho answered with a big right hand that buckled him near the end of the fight. Borralho's shots were cleaner and heavier throughout, and the decision reflected that.


This would have been a more interesting fight over five rounds — Borralho probably works to a finish if given more time. As it stands, it's a solid bounce-back win for Borralho after his loss to Imavov. The middleweight division is in a bit of a standstill with Chimaev sitting at the top, so Borralho will likely need two more wins before getting a real title shot. Tough break for RDR — a year ago it looked like he was on a fast track to the belt, and now he's going to have to fight his way back up the rankings, possibly against someone with serious knockout power.

Raul Rosas Jr. vs. Rob Font

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Raul Rosas Jr. knew exactly what he was going to do before this fight started, and Rob Font apparently had no answer for it. Rosas got Font to the ground in round one off a single leg and just wore on him — not doing meaningful damage, not threatening submissions, just lying on him and letting the rounds tick by. Font scrambled in round two, briefly threatening a triangle, but Rosas kept his composure, worked out of it, and got back on top. Same story in round three. It was classic lay-and-pray from start to finish. Font had some moments on the feet where he landed jabs and combinations, but he had nothing once it hit the ground, and that's where this fight lived.


Another dull fight. Rosas needs to develop his striking in a serious way — it's hard to imagine him doing anything against someone like O'Malley except getting knocked out. He is a grappling-dependent fighter in a division full of well-rounded guys, and that ceiling is going to be painfully obvious the moment he steps up in competition. As for Font, he should probably hang it up. He's 38 years old, has dropped two in a row, and isn't improving on the weaknesses that keep getting exposed. It's not clear where you go from here.

Drew Dober vs. Michael Johnson

Result: KO/TKO, Straight Left at 1:53 Round 2 of 3, 6:53 Total

Michael Johnson looked crisp, fast, and sharp in round one — peppering Drew Dober with jabs and clean combinations while Dober struggled to find a home for his shots. Johnson was clearly winning the fight on the feet. Dober landed a right hand late in the first that briefly stumbled Johnson, but it wasn't enough to shift the momentum. Round two opened much the same way, with Johnson continuing to outwork Dober on the feet. Then Dober landed a clean right hand and sent Johnson to the shadow realm.


Nasty finish for Dober — he was not winning that fight, but he got it done. Both of these guys are always entertaining and looked good in there, but neither is going anywhere near a title. From the looks of it, Johnson would have most likely made his way to a comfortable decision victory if he'd stayed out of trouble. These are exactly the kind of guys the UFC should keep active with fun matchups — they belong on a fight night main card and would deliver every time.

Gregory Rodrigues vs. Brunno Ferreira

Result: KO/TKO, Straight Right at 1:47 Round 1 of 3

Gregory Rodrigues played the early exchanges off the back foot, letting Brunno Ferreira advance forward. Then Robocop landed a massive right hand that sent Ferreira stumbling to the ground, and it was over.


The poetic symmetry here is hard to ignore. In their first fight, it was Rodrigues advancing forward who got caught and stopped. Tonight, Ferreira advanced and got caught and stopped. Both of these guys are genuinely solid fighters and this result doesn't diminish Ferreira's standing — he can bounce back and fight his way back up the rankings. Cannonier would make a sensible next matchup for him. As for Robocop, the fight that makes the most sense next is Fluffy — both guys are on similar schedules, they match up interestingly in terms of style, and the rankings make the fight easy to put together.

Prelims

Cody Garbrandt vs. Long Xiao

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

A messy, foul-filled fight from start to finish. Round one had Cody Garbrandt landing a nasty elbow that put Long Xiao on the canvas, but Xiao bounced up almost immediately. Xiao also landed a head kick early that was arguably the most damaging shot of the entire fight — more so than anything Garbrandt managed all night. Round two was low output from both sides, with Xiao doing slightly more and a headbutt late stopping the action. Round three was defined by illegal shots — Xiao landed a low blow to open, the action resumed, and then he immediately landed another one that had Garbrandt doubled over and throwing up in the octagon. Herb Dean docked a point. The action resumed again, and Xiao landed yet another low blow in the clinch — Herb took a second point. With two points deducted and needing a finish, Xiao stood in the center of the cage and invited Garbrandt to come fight. Garbrandt spent the rest of the round running, happy to let the clock expire. Somehow, the judges gave it to Garbrandt unanimously.


Garbrandt looked awful, and the scoring was wild. Two of the three judges gave Garbrandt round one — the head kick Xiao landed early in that round was more devastating than anything Garbrandt threw all night. But the most outrageous part was one judge handing Garbrandt round three, despite Garbrandt doing absolutely nothing in that round except run away and survive. Even accounting for the two point deductions, Xiao should have won this fight 28-27. That's some crazy scoring. It blows my mind that this man was ever a UFC champion.

Donte Johnson vs. Cody Brundage

Result: Decision, Split at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Cody Brundage got a takedown early in round one but did absolutely nothing with it — just held Donte Johnson against the fence while Johnson worked back to his feet. Johnson started going to work on the feet after that, before Brundage cracked him with a stiff right hand that briefly stumbled him. Round two was where things got genuinely embarrassing for Brundage — his strategy appeared to be sitting on the ground and holding Johnson's wrists while Johnson stood above him. Not threatening a submission. Not working for position. Just holding wrists. After the fighters stood up, there was an eye poke on the separation, and then more of the same. Round three was timid from both sides — Johnson walking Brundage down and landing straight rights, Brundage tagging him occasionally with stiff lefts, neither fully committing. Johnson took the split decision.


Donte Johnson has got some serious work to do. He constantly walks forward with his chin up in the air and his boxing defense is nonexistent. His grappling is also suspect — getting taken down and controlled by Cody Brundage is a major red flag. He's nowhere near ready for ranked competition and needs more time against cans to get his reps in. As for Brundage — sitting on the ground holding your opponent's wrists while he stands above you is not a fighting strategy. If you could throw knees to a grounded opponent, Brundage would have been removed from the building. He is not a fighter. Cut him.

Alberto Montes vs. Ricky Turcios

Result: Submission, Technical (D'arce Choke) at 0:40 Round 2 of 3, 5:40 Total

Alberto Montes made quick work of Ricky Turcios. Round one saw Montes landing big shots on the feet, with Turcios visibly hurt by a front kick to the body — he spent the remainder of the round running away and retreating into the fence. Round two opened with Turcios shooting for a takedown, Montes immediately sinking in the anaconda choke, and Turcios going to sleep at the 0:40 mark.


An absolutely nasty finish. Realistically, Turcios never had a chance — he couldn't strike with Montes, couldn't grapple with him, and his only successful moments in the entire fight were the brief periods he managed to hold Montes against the fence. That's three losses in a row for Turcios and he is 1-3 in his last four. Cut him. Montes, on the other hand, has some real Topuria aura about him — slick grappling, nasty striking, now 10-1 with a sharp UFC debut. Give him one more fight and start matching him against ranked guys to see what he's really got.

N. Tumendemberel vs. Cody Durden

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Garbage fight. Three rounds of both guys scrambling for takedowns, reversing positions, and chasing submissions that never came close to finishing. The most damaging strikes in the entire fight were some elbows N. Tumendemberel landed from mount. Cody Durden was active off his back with submission attempts — a knee bar in round two, an arm triangle in round three — but none of them were close. The most notable moments of the fight had nothing to do with fighting: Tumendemberel tried to land strikes after the bell in both round two and round three, with the ref having to physically pull him off the second time.


That's four losses in a row for Durden. That has to be it for him in the UFC. Tumendemberel now sits on a two-fight win streak, but he's also just not very good. Hard to know what's next for either of these guys.

Sumudaerji vs. Jesus Aguilar

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Sumudaerji used his range masterfully across all three rounds, keeping Jesus Aguilar at distance and picking him apart with head kicks and combinations. Aguilar spent most of the fight lunging into punches that weren't even close to landing — just punching hot air. Sumudaerji got some takedowns in when he wanted them, moved well, and never looked in danger. By the end, Aguilar's nose was cut and bleeding, and the two of them were swinging in the center of the octagon in a closing sequence that felt way more dramatic than the fight deserved.


Pretty dull performance from Sumudaerji despite the dominant win. When you're that much better than your opponent, just finish him — what are you waiting for? Aguilar has no striking and no grappling, so life is going to be tough for him in the UFC. Sumudaerji is now on a three-fight win streak and a matchup at the bottom of the rankings or just outside them seems like the right next step.

Diyar Nurgozhay vs. Rafael Tobias

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Diyar Nurgozhay controlled this fight with his big left hands and body kicks for most of three rounds, with Rafael Tobias struggling to find consistent offense on the feet and resorting to clinching. The officiating was a story in itself — Nurgozhay landed a low blow in round one that Herb Dean largely waved off, letting the fight continue almost immediately. Later in the same round, Nurgozhay had a blatant fence grab that directly prevented a Tobias takedown. Herb's response was to say "don't grab the fence" and move on — no point deducted. Round two saw Tobias land a big shot that briefly stunned Nurgozhay, took him down, and started landing nasty ground and pound — but Nurgozhay reversed, got to mount, and started landing his own. Tobias got a takedown with fifteen seconds left in round three, but it was far too little too late.


Both guys looked extremely low level, and it is genuinely perplexing what gets you into the UFC these days. Tobias has absolutely nothing on the feet and nothing special on the ground. Nurgozhay has some power shots but zero grappling. Nurgozhay should have lost at least one point in this fight given the low blow and the fence grab — the fact that he didn't is a Herb Dean special. Not that it would have changed anything, because Tobias is just not good enough to capitalize regardless.

Rodolfo Bellato vs. Luke Fernandez

Result: KO/TKO, Left Hook to Ground Punches at 2:42 Round 1 of 3

Luke Fernandez came out aggressive from the opening bell, throwing right hands and a knee to the face right away. Rodolfo Bellato was holding his own early — landing body shots and staying in the fight — but Fernandez continued to push the pace, landing an uppercut and walking Bellato to the fence. Then Bellato tagged Fernandez with a big left hook that put him on the canvas, followed up with nasty ground and pound, and finished the fight.


The commentary treated this like a one-sided beatdown, but the fight was more competitive than they made it sound — Bellato was landing meaningful shots before the finish and was very much in the fight. That said, this was still an underwhelming debut from Fernandez. Bellato is nothing special, and he couldn't get it done. For Bellato's part, getting tagged that cleanly by a debuting fighter is a concern — you can't be absorbing shots like that and then hoping the finish bails you out every time.
