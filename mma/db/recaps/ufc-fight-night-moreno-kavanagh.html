Summary

UFC Fight Night: Moreno vs. Kavanagh was a serviceable card that delivered entertainment in spurts but never felt like anything more than a mid-tier fight night. The fights were mostly action-packed, but the event suffered from a glaring lack of star power and no real divisional stakes anywhere on the bill. Brandon Moreno, once a beloved champion, headlined as a compromised version of himself, getting carved up by a Dane most people had never heard of before fight week. The prelims churned out some fun finishes, but the larger problems with this card — and with the UFC's 2026 calendar — are impossible to ignore.


The big picture problem is the UFC's scheduling chaos. During this very card, RAF 6 was happening in Tempe, Arizona, with Arman Tsarukyan grappling with Aljamain Sterling — a former champion who publicly complained he can't get fights. Meanwhile, the UFC's competing card had Marlon "Chito" Vera, a fighter on a four-fight losing streak, as its co-main event. The fact that a former champion like Aljo can't get booked while Chito gets a co-main event slot in 2026 is a symptom of something deeply broken. Aljo wasn't even the co-main event on the wrestling card — he was buried. If the UFC can't put on events with more name recognition than RAF, it's over. If instead of a Jones vs. Pereira White House main event we get Kayla Harrison vs. Amanda Nunes or some similar nonsense, the calendar is in genuine crisis. When RAF 6 has more star power than a UFC Fight Night, that's not a sign of a healthy organization. Absolutely pathetic.


Still, credit where it's due: there was non-stop action on this card. The Kareckaite-Tarin fight was a throwback brawl, the Gandra finish was early and violent, and Santiago Luna showed off the full toolkit that almost took him to the Arizona State wrestling room and the Mexican Olympics. The main event's first two rounds were genuinely exciting before Moreno had to resort to stalling against the cage just to survive. It just all felt like a warm-up for something that never arrived.


Final Score: 6.8/10


Main Card

Lone'er Kavanagh vs. Brandon Moreno

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 5 Rounds, 25:00 Total

The main event was billed as a triumphant homecoming for Brandon Moreno, the former flyweight champion fighting in front of a rabid Mexico City crowd. His opponent, Lone'er Kavanagh, was a Dane that most fans couldn't pick out of a lineup before fight week. The first two rounds turned out to be genuinely exciting — Kavanagh operating at range, consistently landing cleaner shots while Moreno struggled to close the distance. In round two, Kavanagh had wobbled Moreno with a big right hand, and it was remarkable that the former champion was still standing.


As the fight wore on, Kavanagh's calf kicks did serious structural damage to Moreno's legs — the welts were visibly grotesque by the championship rounds. With his mobility compromised, Moreno had no option but to resort to the clinch against the cage, smothering Kavanagh and waiting for Herb Dean to separate them. Kavanagh repeatedly broke free and landed shots on the break, including a nasty elbow, but his conditioning started to fade in the later rounds. By round five, it was a battle of two compromised fighters — Moreno's face gushing blood, Kavanagh running on fumes.


Kavanagh is a lot better than advertised — no one should have expected this performance from him. But his cardio is a major question mark going forward; he got very tired as the fight progressed. As for Moreno, the picture is bleak. He got seriously messed up in this fight, and the truth is his best days are behind him. There's no version of the next chapter where Moreno is reclaiming title contention. The question isn't what's next for him — it's whether there should be a next at all.

David Martinez vs. Marlon Vera

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

The co-main event featured Marlon "Chito" Vera entering on a three-fight losing streak against a younger, more dynamic David Martinez. For two rounds, it was the same Chito we've been watching for years — flat-footed, plodding, accumulating damage while hoping to land that one big shot. Martinez bounced around the octagon, circling away from Chito's attempts and tagging him consistently with his speed advantage. In round two, Martinez hit Chito with a big head kick that sent him to the canvas — Chito just resigned himself to the bottom and continued eating punishment before Martinez let him back up.


By the third round, Martinez was gassing, and Chito started to track him down and connect more consistently. It made the scorecards closer than they had any right to be, but the damage from the first two rounds was done. Martinez got the unanimous decision.


For the past two-to-three years, it has been the same story fight after fight with Chito, and he continues to not address the obvious problems with his fighting style. He's slow, he's not active enough, and everyone in the division now knows the blueprint: circle away, rack up damage, and coast through the third. What makes it maddening is not just the losses — it's the booking. The UFC keeps putting Chito in three-round co-main events, which is the absolute worst format for his fighting style. Put him in five-round Apex main events and he at least has a path — he could go down two rounds and still win a decision. In a three-rounder, he's always destined to lose. Last night, Martinez won the first two and faded in the third, which in a five-round fight would have given Chito a fighting chance. Instead, he was cooked from the jump. That's on his team too — if you aren't getting new representation and a new game plan after four consecutive losses, what are you doing? Four losses in a row. At some point, the UFC has to make the call and cut him. Martinez, for his part, should get a bump in competition — a matchup with Song Yadong seems like a natural next step.

King Green vs. Daniel Zellhuber

Result: KO/TKO, Punches at 4:55 Round 2 of 3, 9:55 Total

Daniel Zellhuber, the young Mexican prospect on a two-fight losing streak, came in against King Green — a man who is damn near forty years old and who nearly had his head taken off by Mauricio Ruffy less than a year ago. This should have been a winnable fight. Instead, Zellhuber was way too timid on the feet throughout, walking Green down but never pulling the trigger with any authority. Green's hands were embarrassingly low, as always, but his hand speed made up for every defensive shortcut. He was consistently active, tagging Zellhuber up throughout round one while Zellhuber kind of just followed him around, throwing kicks at the legs and body but never committing upstairs.


In round two, Green got a takedown and mounted Zellhuber. Zellhuber threatened a choke from his back, but Green looked fine and started landing knees to the body. They got back to the feet, Green landed a big right that stumbled Zellhuber, and then followed with the finishing combination to get the TKO at 4:55 of round two.


How bad is Daniel Zellhuber? A young, local fighter fighting in front of his home crowd just got knocked out by a near-forty-year-old who was nearly decapitated a year ago. Zellhuber is now on a three-fight losing streak — at this point you have to start asking whether this level is simply too much for him. The UFC may have an answer for him soon, and it won't be a fun one. Green, on the other hand, is now on a two-fight win streak, which is genuinely wild given the Ruffy beating. Bobby Green refuses to die. More power to him.

Edgar Cháirez vs. Felipe Bunes

Result: Decision, Split at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

This fight came down to a classic dilemma: a dominant wrestler who needed to wrestle going up against a striker who could actually strike. Felipe Bunes solved the riddle in round one, getting Edgar Cháirez against the fence and taking his back with a body triangle. Bunes worked methodically, landing 12-6 elbows to Cháirez's shoulder while Marc Goddard repeatedly warned him about using the top of the octagon. Cháirez had absolutely nothing for him on the mat — he just let it happen for five minutes and managed to flip Bunes at the bell, which was far too late to matter.


Round two swung the other way. Cháirez managed to keep Bunes at range and started connecting more cleanly from the outside, winning most of the exchanges on the feet — though it was a lackluster round overall. By the third, Cháirez was throwing everything with obvious urgency: crisp body combinations, constant forward pressure, landing real damage even as his face was getting progressively more busted up. Bunes, inexplicably, decided to strike with him.


The split decision for Cháirez was somewhat surprising given how thoroughly his face had been rearranged by the final horn, but not impossible to understand depending on how you scored round three. The bigger question is why Bunes abandoned his grappling after round one — Cháirez had zero answers for him on the mat, so why start trading on the feet? That's the blueprint staring you in the face and he just walked away from it. For Cháirez, the lesson is simpler: you cannot allow someone to have their way with you for an entire round. What are you doing letting that go on for five minutes?

Imanol Rodriguez vs. Kevin Borjas

Result: KO/TKO, Overhand Right to Ground Strikes at 4:21 Round 2 of 3, 9:21 Total

Imanol Rodriguez came within a whisker of an early exit in this one. Kevin Borjas landed a massive right hand early in round one that had Rodriguez in serious trouble, following up with nasty knees and big shots that looked like the end was imminent. Rodriguez somehow kept his composure, found a takedown, clawed his way to mount, and survived the round. It was one of those moments where you genuinely wonder how someone is still upright.


The second round told a very different story. Borjas had nothing left in the tank — his output dropped dramatically, and Rodriguez started landing more confidently on the feet. He found a left hand that stumbled Borjas, followed him to the fence, and got the TKO finish at 4:21 of round two.


One of the more dramatic momentum reversals on the card. Rodriguez was dead to rights in round one and still came back to win, which shows something real. But the bigger story is Borjas's cardio — the guy had Rodriguez wobbling and completely ran out of gas before he could close the deal. At 1-4 in his last five, Borjas needs a win in his next fight or the UFC is going to move on. Rodriguez called out Josh Van after the win, which is a fun callout but an ambitious one for someone who just earned their first UFC victory — fight your way up the rankings before getting anywhere near that conversation.

Santiago Luna vs. Angel Pacheco

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Santiago Luna was originally headed to wrestle at Arizona State and compete for Mexico in the Olympics before the UFC came calling. Against Angel Pacheco, he made clear that both paths were understandable — because the guy can flat out fight. From the opening bell, Luna walked Pacheco down with constant pressure, mixing crisp combinations with elite-level wrestling. He took Pacheco down, got to mount, dealt out ground and pound, and then got right back up to do it again on the feet. Pacheco's jab worked early, but that was essentially the entirety of his offensive toolkit for the entire fight.


In round two, Luna threw Pacheco to the canvas with a suplex-style throw, mounted him, and then landed a knee as they separated back to the feet. By round three, Pacheco was clearly fading — his output had fallen off completely while Luna kept coming. Luna even shot for a guillotine late and dragged Pacheco to the ground with it — Pacheco somehow fought out, only for Luna to start landing elbows from the bottom. From top, from bottom, on the feet, on the ground — Luna had answers everywhere.


About as dominant a unanimous decision as you'll see. Pacheco had absolutely nothing for him — the jab worked for half a round and that was it. Luna called out Alexandre Topuria after the win, which is a fun callout, but that fight isn't happening — Topuria vs. Mitchell is the bigger, more logical matchup the promotion needs to make first. Luna deserves a genuine bump in competition, but let's walk before we run. As for Pacheco, three losses in a row including a Contender Series defeat is a pretty clear signal. Losing on the Contender Series and then going on a three-fight skid in the UFC is not a recipe for long-term roster survival. Time to move on.

Prelims

Ryan Gandra vs. José Medina

Result: KO/TKO, Punches at 0:41 Round 1 of 3

José Medina didn't last long. Ryan Gandra came out with immediate, suffocating pressure and had Medina finished in just 41 seconds — walking him down next to the fence and unloading until the referee stepped in. Medina was eating serious punishment, fell hard, and had no response. Clean stoppage. The fact that Medina protested it afterward was a little rich.


Gandra is a legitimate prospect: immediate violence, no hesitation, efficient and decisive. The key is to develop him properly — gradually increasing his levels of competition rather than jumping him up too quickly. As for Medina, this is five losses in a row, including his fight on the Contender Series — where he lost and still somehow walked away with a UFC contract. Who would have thought that losing on the Contender Series wouldn't be a good signal for future UFC success? Cut him.

Ailín Pérez vs. Macy Chiasson

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

This one was a slog. Ailín Pérez controlled the grappling across all three rounds against Macy Chiasson — getting takedowns, taking back position, and grinding out positional dominance without ever threatening any serious damage or submissions. There was a genuinely questionable moment in round two where the ref stood them up right after Pérez had just landed some clean shots from mount. Regardless, the pattern held all night. Pérez wrestled, Chiasson tried to stay upright, and in the third Chiasson went for a takedown attempt instead of looking for a finish — which told you everything about where the fight stood.


Dull fight. Neither fighter really engaged the other in any meaningful way — no devastating ground and pound, no serious submission attempts. It came down entirely to control time. Pérez is now on a six-fight win streak, which is genuinely impressive, and a matchup with Ketlen Vieira seems like the natural next step. Chiasson is now on a three-fight losing streak, which is concerning, but she'll likely keep her roster spot given the UFC's perpetual shortage of female fighters.

Cristian Quiñonez vs. Kris Moutinho

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Cristian Quiñonez walked into this fight as a -700 favorite over Kris Moutinho. At that price, you go out there and end the night early. What actually happened was one of the more uninspiring performances you'll see from a heavy favorite. Quiñonez controlled the fight — yes — but his method was to jab Moutinho, drag him to the ground, threaten submissions that never materialized, and coast for three rounds while Moutinho shambled forward like a zombie who forgot what round it was. The jab was effective enough to bloody Moutinho's nose and score the rounds, but Quiñonez was significantly bigger. He never had Moutinho in any real danger, never threatened a serious submission, and got progressively more tired as the fight went on — while Moutinho was busy grabbing the fence and attempting kneebars from the bottom.


Terrible fight overall. Quiñonez clearly won, but this is a pathetic performance for a -700 favorite — he should have had Moutinho out of there in the first round. Quiñonez is still terrible; I don't see him beating anyone in the UFC with any real skill. As for Moutinho, the experiment is officially over. He's not good enough for this level, showed nothing in this fight to suggest otherwise, and should be cut immediately.

Javier Reyes vs. D. Silva de Andrade

Result: KO/TKO, Punches From Back Mount at 4:59 Round 1 of 3

D. Silva de Andrade is 40 years old and a career journeyman. Javier Reyes was supposed to dispatch him cleanly. That is not what happened. After a slow, low-output opening, de Andrade landed a huge right that sent Reyes to the canvas — then followed with a big left that had him backing up to the fence in serious trouble. Reyes somehow kept his composure, circled away, and regrouped. When Reyes eventually landed his own big right, de Andrade went down, and Reyes followed him to the canvas, took the back, and started dealing out punishment from back mount. Herb Dean let it go for a couple of minutes, then stepped in and stopped it with literally one second left in the round.


That stoppage is confusing. Herb let de Andrade absorb two to three minutes of serious punishment from back mount with no meaningful defense, and then decided the one-second mark was the right moment to intervene. Either stop it earlier or let the round end on its own. As for Reyes — how do you nearly get knocked out by a 40-year-old journeyman in round one? He weathered the storm and got the finish, but this is not the kind of performance that inspires confidence. De Andrade is now on a two-fight losing streak at 40 years old — it's time for him to retire. Nobody wants to watch a 40-year-old getting beaten up repeatedly. Good scrap while it lasted, though — this one had non-stop action throughout.

Regina Tarin vs. Ernesta Kareckaite

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Regina Tarin stepped up on three days' notice after Montenegro dropped out for undisclosed reasons. Ernesta Kareckaite had every preparation advantage in the world. It didn't matter. From the opening bell, Tarin stunned Kareckaite and the two women were immediately trading in the center of the octagon. Tarin was landing knees and elbows in the clinch; Kareckaite was throwing with bad intentions at range. The first round ended with both women tangled against the cage, already marked up.


Round two saw Kareckaite do more damage — she landed a head kick that genuinely staggered Tarin, followed with a combination that looked potentially fight-ending, but Tarin kept going. Kareckaite got a takedown at the end of the round and did absolutely nothing with it. By round three, both women were swinging for the finish in an all-out brawl that felt like the last 30 seconds of Holloway-Gaethje stretched out for an entire round.


Tarin is legit. She came in on three days' notice and laid it all on the line against a fighter who should have had every advantage. Kareckaite realistically should have run through her — that she didn't is partly a credit to Tarin, and partly a product of the altitude. Mexico City sits above 7,000 feet, and being a local fighter likely gave Tarin a meaningful physiological edge that explains why she didn't fade the way a three-day-notice fighter normally would. As entertaining as this fight was — and it was a great fight — neither woman came close to a finish despite landing volume throughout all three rounds. Women's MMA has its limitations, and the lack of knockout power remains a persistent issue.

Francis Marshall vs. Erik Silva

Result: Submission, Rear Naked Choke at 2:29 Round 1 of 3

Erik Silva came out swinging — literally. He opened the fight with a spinning head kick right out of the gate that landed on Francis Marshall, who shrugged it off and kept moving. Props for the aggressive intent. Marshall then landed an eye poke that paused the action briefly, before shooting a double leg and getting the fight to the canvas. Silva threatened a guillotine on the way up, but Marshall stuffed it, returned him to the mat, and started working from half guard. He threatened an arm triangle, Silva scrambled back to half guard, and then in the ensuing scramble Marshall got to the back and sunk the rear naked choke for the finish at 2:29 of round one.


Silva wasn't on Marshall's level, and that's the bottom line. Three losses in a row to start a UFC career — that should be it for him. Marshall, meanwhile, bounces back from the Santos loss in clean fashion. He's young, talented, and still just scratching the surface as a prospect. Good win, and there's plenty of reason to be optimistic about where his career is headed.

Damian Pinas vs. Wes Schultz

Result: KO/TKO, Jab-cross at 2:30 Round 1 of 3

That was one of the worst performances from Wes Schultz imaginable. His apparent game plan was to walk into Damian Pinas's shots, go down to the canvas, and lure Pinas into his guard. These are middleweights — what type of game plan is that? After biting on the bait the first two times, Pinas stopped following Schultz down and started welcoming him back to his feet instead. Pinas landed a nasty 1-2 that put Schultz on the canvas, followed with some ground and pound, then cracked him with a brutal uppercut that sent him down again. At that point Schultz had been dropped at least three times and the referee had seen enough.


Massive stock drop for Schultz — it's unclear what his UFC future looks like after a performance like that. As for Pinas, the finish is a finish, but these are middleweights — he couldn't get a vicious, clean knockout over a terrible prospect with a game plan this bad? KOs should be very easily achievable at this weight class against someone this incompetent. Not exactly a ringing endorsement. Still unclear what's next for him on the booking calendar.
