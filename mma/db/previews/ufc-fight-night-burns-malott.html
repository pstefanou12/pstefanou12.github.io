Overview

UFC Winnipeg is a home show first, a fight card second. The UFC needed a Canadian market event and built accordingly — stacking the prelims with regional-circuit fighters who wouldn't sniff an Apex card, signing Canadians off the regional circuit purely to fill seats, and propping the whole thing up with a main event that is itself a mismatch dressed up in headline billing. Most Fight Night cards held at the Apex in Las Vegas carry deeper, more credible matchmaking than what's on offer here. That says a lot.

The headliner — Gilbert Burns vs. Mike Malott — tells the story of the whole event. Burns is a fighter on a four-fight skid who has looked increasingly shot since taking catastrophic damage at UFC 299, and Malott is a serviceable welterweight who is getting fed a name at exactly the right moment in that name's decline. The co-main, Jasmine Jasudavicius vs. Karine Silva, is a legitimately interesting ranked flyweight matchup and the best fight on paper top to bottom. Below those two, Mandel Nallo vs. Jai Herbert stands out as the most compelling sleeper on the card, and Kyler Phillips vs. Charles Jourdain has the potential to be a fun bantamweight scrap. Everything else is filler.

The prelims feature UFC debuts from fighters who bypassed the traditional feeder programs entirely — signed because they're Canadian, or because the card needed bodies. Julien Leblanc is a 34-year-old who was handed a UFC contract without going through DWCS or TUF. Tanner Boser was previously cut from the promotion and is back because Winnipeg will cheer for him. The matchmaking here isn't about building the division — it's about selling tickets. Watch the top two fights, enjoy the chaos if Barbosa or Saricam gets going early, and don't expect much else.


Main Card Picks

Gilbert Burns vs. Mike Malott

Gilbert Burns (22-9) was once a legitimate welterweight title contender — one of the best grapplers in the division, a submission threat every time he hit the mat, and a guy who gave Kamaru Usman everything he could handle. That version of Burns is gone. Since eating a catastrophic beating from Jack Della Maddalena at UFC 299, Burns has looked like a different fighter. Sean Brady, who is not a striker, pieced him up on the feet. Michael Morales knocked him out cold in the first round. Four straight losses, each one exposing the same problem: Burns can no longer keep up athletically at 170 lbs, and when he can't impose his grappling, he has no answers.

Mike Malott (13-2-1) steps into his first UFC main event in front of his home country on a three-fight win streak. He's a well-rounded welterweight with clean boxing and a solid grappling base — not a world-beater, but a capable, physical fighter who is being handed Burns at the exact right moment in Burns's career decline. His second-round knockout of Charles Radtke and tactical decision over Kevin Holland show he can win in multiple ways. Malott doesn't need to be exceptional here — he just needs to be what he is against a version of Burns that is no longer what he was.

Pick: Mike Malott

Method: KO/TKO

The MMA community carries a lot of respect for Gilbert Burns, and rightly so — his career résumé earned it. But those days are behind him. The UFC 299 fight with Della Maddalena was a turning point: Burns absorbed an enormous amount of damage, and he has not looked the same since. Brady, a wrestler and grinder, was beating him on the feet. Morales dropped him in the first round without much trouble. Burns's grappling, which was always his path to victory, requires him to close distance and control scrambles — but when he's getting hurt on the feet, that path closes. Malott will win the striking exchanges, keep Burns from establishing his offense on the ground, and get the finish in the second or third round. Burns is done at the top level, and this fight will make that clear.

Kyler Phillips vs. Charles Jourdain

Kyler Phillips (12-4) was one of the more exciting young fighters in the bantamweight division a few years ago — unorthodox movement, creative striking, legitimate wrestling pedigree. He's now dropped two straight decisions to Rob Font and Vinicius Oliveira, and neither loss has aged particularly well. Phillips hasn't looked like the prospect he was projected to be, and he enters this fight as a slight underdog needing a statement performance to stay relevant in the top 15.

Charles Jourdain (17-8-1) has found a new gear since dropping to bantamweight from featherweight. Where he used to be a high-volume brawler at 145, he's become a more composed, precision-based fighter at 135 — and his grappling has quietly become a genuine weapon. His last two wins both came via guillotine choke, which is a dangerous combination against a wrestler like Phillips who likes to initiate contact. Fighting close to home in Winnipeg, Jourdain enters as the -150 favorite on a two-fight win streak. He's bigger for the weight class and has grown into 135 in a way that Phillips, a natural bantamweight, hasn't been able to match recently.

Pick: Charles Jourdain

Method: Decision

Charles Jourdain's career resurgence at bantamweight is real. He's not just winning — he's winning with more variety and composure than he ever showed at featherweight, and two consecutive guillotine finishes signal a fighter who has added genuine submission danger to his toolkit. Kyler Phillips's recent losses to Font and Oliveira weren't close, and neither opponent is a world-beater — that's a bad sign for where Phillips is in his development. Jourdain will be able to incorporate grappling into this fight in ways that Phillips won't have a clean answer for, and his size advantage at the weight class is a real factor. Expect Jourdain to control pace and range over three rounds and take a clear decision.

Mandel Nallo vs. Jai Herbert

Mandel Nallo (14-3) makes his UFC debut as the oldest fighter ever signed off Dana White's Contender Series — a 38-year-old Brazilian who has put together a remarkable five-fight win streak, with every single one of those finishes coming in the first round. KOs, rear naked chokes, leg kicks — he finishes in different ways, which makes him genuinely dangerous early. The UFC clearly views this as a layup debut for a feel-good story, but the matchup they made may not be as soft as they think.

Jai Herbert (13-6-1) is exactly the kind of fighter whose record looks worse than it is. Scroll through his UFC losses and you'll find Ilia Topuria, Renato Moicano, Fares Ziam, and Ľudovít Klein — none of these are charity opponents. Herbert is a long, powerful striker who has been consistently matched against elite competition throughout his UFC tenure. He's coming off a 13-month layoff after a split decision loss to Chris Padilla, and while the layoff is a legitimate concern, so is the quality gap between everyone Nallo has ever faced and everyone Herbert has fought inside the octagon. The UFC thinks they're giving Nallo a layup. They may have miscalculated.

Pick: Jai Herbert

Method: Decision

Look past Jai Herbert's record and look at the names. Topuria. Moicano. Ziam. Klein. Every single one of those fighters is a level above anything Mandel Nallo has seen in his entire career. Nallo's five-fight finishing streak is impressive on paper, but the competition level simply hasn't been there. The UFC is packaging this as a showcase for their new DWCS signing, but Herbert is not the soft touch they're making him out to be. His length, power, and experience against high-level strikers give him the tools to survive the opening round — and if this fight gets out of the first, Nallo's path to victory shrinks considerably. Herbert is the craftier, more experienced fighter against better competition. He survives the early pressure and grinds out a decision.

Jasmine Jasudavicius vs. Karine Silva

Jasmine Jasudavicius (14-4) is the best fighter on this card not named Burns, and the argument can be made she's better than him at this point in both their careers. The Canadian (#7 ranked) built herself into a genuine flyweight title contender with four straight wins — including a first-round rear naked choke over former champion Jéssica Andrade — before getting clipped and stopped by Manon Fiorot in October 2025. That loss is more forgivable than it looks: she got caught, went down, and was back on her feet immediately. It wasn't a beatdown, it was a shot that landed clean. Now she's back home in Canada, in front of a crowd that will be loud for her all night, looking to reassert herself as a title contender.

Karine Silva (19-6) arrived in the UFC with considerable hype a few years back, and that hype was never fully justified. She's a finish-or-lose-a-decision fighter who doesn't have the cardio to sustain pressure for a full three rounds, a fact that was exposed painfully in her decision loss to Viviane Araujo at UFC 309. Silva alternates wins and losses, most recently dropping a unanimous decision to Maycee Barber in December 2025. She brings a solid jiu-jitsu background, but getting into a grappling fight with Jasudavicius — one of the best clinch submission hunters in the division — is not the path to victory.

Pick: Jasmine Jasudavicius

Method: Submission

The hype around Karine Silva was always a few steps ahead of the results. She's a finisher when she's winning and a decision loser when she's not — and the cardio issues that derailed her against Araujo will show up again here against a fighter who is relentlessly physical and thrives in the clinch. Jasmine Jasudavicius's loss to Fiorot doesn't change what she is: an elite grappler with suffocating clinch work and the submission instincts to capitalize on any moment of exposure. Silva's jiu-jitsu won't save her in a position battle against someone at Jasudavicius's level. Jasudavicius gets the fight where she wants it and finishes by submission in front of a hometown crowd that will be on their feet.

Thiago Moisés vs. Gauge Young

Thiago Moisés (19-9) is a career UFC journeyman who has been floating around the lightweight division for years without ever breaking through. He's fought elite competition — Islam Makhachev, Benoit Saint-Denis — and gotten the results you'd expect. His most recent outing was a first-round knockout loss to Jared Gordon in May 2025, and at 9-7 inside the UFC, he's the kind of veteran the promotion keeps around to give younger fighters a recognizable name on their record. There's nothing left in the tank here in terms of upward trajectory.

Gauge Young (10-3) is 25 years old and still visibly improving fight to fight. He averages over 5.7 significant strikes per minute with 51% accuracy and has five first-round finishes on his résumé. He dropped a decision to Evan Elder in April 2025 but bounced back with a clear win over Maheshate in August. Young is exactly the type of fighter this card needed more of — a legitimate prospect with upside — and he's being handed a favorable matchup to showcase what he can do.

Pick: Gauge Young

Method: Decision

Thiago Moisés has been in the UFC long enough that the results tell the story clearly: he's not a top-15 lightweight, and the fights where he's looked competitive are behind him. There is no other sport where the young eat the old as efficiently as MMA, and Gauge Young is exactly the kind of hungry, improving 25-year-old that makes veterans like Moisés look their age. Young's striking volume and pace will be a problem all night, and Moisés's submission grappling — his best attribute — requires him to survive on the feet long enough to get there. Young controls the stand-up, wins the round-by-round battle, and takes a decision.

Prelims Picks

Dennis Buzukja vs. Márcio Barbosa

Dennis Buzukja (12-5) has gone 1-4 in his last five UFC fights, including getting guillotined in under two minutes by Christian Bobe in his most recent appearance. With a 2-4 record inside the promotion and a history of being vulnerable to early pressure and first-round finishes, he is a poor stylistic matchup against the man across from him. Buzukja brings decent striking volume, but volume doesn't matter much if you're not standing up past round one.

Márcio Barbosa (17-2) arrives in the UFC on a four-fight win streak, with all four victories coming by first-round knockout. Sixteen of his seventeen career wins have ended inside the distance. He is a -440 favorite for good reason — this is one of the most lopsided matchups on the card, and the UFC is deploying a legitimate finishing machine against a fighter who has already proven he can be stopped early. Barbosa's UFC debut is less a question of whether he wins and more a question of how quickly.

Pick: Márcio Barbosa

Method: KO/TKO

Márcio Barbosa finishes fights. Four in a row, all in the first round, and he's being handed Dennis Buzukja — a fighter with a documented tendency to get stopped early against pressure opponents. Buzukja hasn't done anything in the UFC to suggest he has the chin or the tools to weather what Barbosa brings. Barbosa connects early and gets the finish inside two rounds. This is a showcase bout, and it'll look like one.

Robert Valentin vs. Julien Leblanc

Robert Valentin (10-6) had his moment in the spotlight on TUF 32, advancing to the middleweight finale and taking on a coaching role with Team Grasso. He has not been able to translate any of that into octagon success. On a three-fight losing streak that includes a first-round stoppage by Ateba Gautier, Valentin looks like a fighter who thrived in the TUF environment and has been exposed at the UFC level ever since. His imposing frame and grappling tools are real, but they haven't worked consistently against UFC-caliber competition.

Julien Leblanc (10-2) is a 34-year-old middleweight from Gatineau, Quebec who was handed a UFC contract without going through DWCS or TUF. The cynical read — and it's the correct one — is that the UFC needed Canadian bodies to fill out this card and Leblanc was available. He's a southpaw with physical pressure, heavy punches, and some grappling ability, and he's on a five-fight win streak on the regional circuit. But the regional circuit is not the UFC, and bypassing the feeder programs entirely to land a spot on a televised card raises obvious questions about whether he belongs here.

Pick: Robert Valentin

Method: KO/TKO

This matchup exists because the UFC needed a Canadian fighter on this card, not because Julien Leblanc earned his way here through the established pathways. A 34-year-old signed directly off the regional circuit, bypassing DWCS and TUF entirely, is a significant red flag about his readiness for this level. Robert Valentin has his problems — the three-fight losing streak is real — but he has UFC experience and physical tools that should be more than enough against a regional fighter making his octagon debut. Valentin gets the finish.

Tanner Boser vs. Gokhan Saricam

Tanner Boser (22-10-1) was previously cut from the UFC, went to UAE Warriors to rebuild, and is now back in the octagon because Winnipeg will cheer for him. That's an honest summary of how this fight got booked. The "Bulldozer" is a crowd-pleasing heavyweight with real finishing power, but he was let go from the promotion for a reason, and the two-fight win streak he put together outside the UFC came against UAE Warriors-level competition. His homecoming energy is real; his path to victory against a legitimate prospect is shakier.

Gokhan Saricam (11-2) makes his UFC debut on a three-fight win streak, with his last two fights ending in first-round stoppages. The Turkish heavyweight is part of a new generation of big men — mobile, high-output, with serious punching power. His Bellator/PFL run showed he can compete and finish at a credible level. He enters as a -155 favorite, and together these two fighters combine for 19 career knockdowns — which is a polite way of saying this fight is unlikely to go the distance.

Pick: Gokhan Saricam

Method: KO/TKO

Tanner Boser was cut from the UFC once already. The UFC doesn't usually bring fighters back because they've gotten better — they bring them back because they need something, in this case a recognizable Canadian name. Boser is a fun fighter to watch, but he's being matched here against a genuinely dangerous prospect in Gokhan Saricam, who has the footwork and finishing power to make life very difficult for a plodding heavyweight. Saricam's back-to-back first-round stops suggest he's hitting his stride at exactly the right time. He gets Boser out of there early and announces himself as a heavyweight to watch.

Melissa Croden vs. Darya Zheleznyakova

Melissa Croden (7-3) is the hometown fighter here, fighting in Canada off the back of a decision loss to Luana Santos in December 2025. Prior to that she'd won three straight, including a third-round TKO of Tainara Lisboa, showing she has the physical tools to grind opponents down over the course of a fight. "Scare" is a pressure fighter — relentless forward movement, top-game grappling — and she'll have a packed arena behind her.

Darya Zheleznyakova (10-2) is a technical striker who likes to work behind her jab and keep fights standing. She bounced back from a first-round submission loss to Ailín Pérez by decisioning Melissa Mullins in June 2025 — avenging an earlier career loss — and enters on a one-fight win streak. Her 50% takedown defense rate is a real vulnerability against a pressure grappler, and she's shown before that she can struggle when opponents take the fight to the mat and get physical.

Pick: Darya Zheleznyakova

Method: Decision

Neither of these women are known for their grappling, and this fight figures to stay on the feet for the most part. On the feet, Darya Zheleznyakova is the more technical and consistent striker. Melissa Croden has the home crowd advantage and the grinder's mentality, but Zheleznyakova has shown more in the UFC and has the tools to control range and outpoint her over three rounds. Zheleznyakova takes a decision.

JJ Aldrich vs. Jamey-Lyn Horth

JJ Aldrich (14-7) is a decade-long UFC flyweight veteran who has gone 10-6 inside the promotion since 2016. She's not a world-beater, but she's a consistent, experienced fighter who knows how to game-plan and execute at the UFC level. She broke a two-fight losing streak with a decision win over Andrea Lee in March 2025 and brings the kind of octagon IQ that gives newcomers problems regardless of physical talent.

Jamey-Lyn Horth (9-2) is the Canadian betting favorite in her home country, riding back-to-back wins including a first-round TKO of Tereza Bledá in December 2025. The Squamish, BC native has described feeling "like a white belt again" after working with new training partners this camp — which suggests genuine technical growth heading in. A win over Aldrich would push her into the top-15 flyweight rankings, giving this fight real positioning stakes for her.

Pick: JJ Aldrich

Method: Decision

Jamey-Lyn Horth is the crowd favorite and the momentum fighter, but momentum built on regional circuit wins and early UFC outings only goes so far. JJ Aldrich has been here before — she's beaten the type of fighter Horth is at this stage of her career, including a win over Vanessa Demopoulos, who Horth herself beat on the way up. Experience is the difference in a close fight, and this figures to be a close fight. Aldrich's octagon IQ edges it over three rounds.

John Castañeda vs. Mark Vologdin

John Castañeda (21-8) has publicly admitted he's fighting for his job at UFC Winnipeg. That level of candor is rare and tells you everything about where his career stands. On a two-fight losing streak at 34 years old, the veteran bantamweight is running out of runway. The fight was changed to a 139 lb catchweight after Vologdin missed weight, which adds another layer of chaos to an already unstable situation for Castañeda going into fight week.

Mark Vologdin (12-4-1) is a 26-year-old Russian trained at Allstars in Stockholm with a Kyokushin karate background and a reputation as a scrapper. He earned a UFC contract off Dana White's Contender Series in October 2025 — despite losing his fight to Juan Adrian Luna Martinetti — because both men put on enough of a show to get rewarded. Missing weight in his UFC debut is not a great first impression, but the fact that he came in heavier suggests he may be the bigger, stronger man on fight night.

Pick: Mark Vologdin

Method: KO/TKO

Mark Vologdin got a UFC contract because he went to war on the Contender Series and impressed Dana White even in a loss. That's the profile of a guy who brings aggression and finishing power every time he walks into the cage. John Castañeda is fighting for his job, on a two-fight skid, and now dealing with a catchweight situation where his opponent came in heavier. Vologdin is going to be aggressive from the opening bell, and Castañeda doesn't have the momentum or the physical edge to hold him off. Vologdin gets the early finish and makes a statement in his debut.

Jamie Siraj vs. John Yannis

Jamie Siraj (14-3) is "The Gremlin" — a long-time regional circuit veteran making his UFC debut after overcoming serious health setbacks to get here. His record features 11 finishes across 14 wins, and his last two fights both ended via rear naked choke in the second round. He's a submission-first fighter who creates danger in scrambles and transitions, and his path to the octagon has been the kind of story the UFC loves to put in front of a crowd.

John Yannis (9-4) made his UFC debut in August 2025 and was submitted by Austin Bashi in the first round, exposing a clear grappling vulnerability at the top level. Before that loss he'd rattled off three consecutive knockouts, so the striking power is real — but his debut showed he can be completely overwhelmed on the mat by a skilled grappler. He's now being matched against a fighter whose entire identity is built around submissions. It's hard to ignore the pattern.

Pick: Jamie Siraj

Method: Submission

John Yannis was submitted in the first round of his UFC debut by Austin Bashi, and Bashi is a green fighter himself. That's not a good data point. Now Yannis steps in against Jamie Siraj, whose whole game is built around putting people on the mat and finishing with the rear naked choke — the exact method Yannis was finished by in his debut. Yannis has knockout power, but he needs to keep this standing to use it, and Siraj is going to make that very difficult. Siraj gets the takedown, works to the back, and taps Yannis out. An unfortunate matchup to end the card — why is a fighter who was cut from the UFC on a card in front of a packed arena? — but Siraj should make quick work of it.
