Overview

UFC Freedom 250 is being held on the South Lawn of the White House to celebrate President Trump's 80th birthday and the United States' 250th anniversary. Strip away the political theater and what you have is one of the better fight cards the UFC has put together all year. Two title fights, a loaded main card, and a genuine superstar at the top of the bill. This one actually delivers on the spectacle.

The headline is simple: Ilia Topuria is the best fighter in the world, and Sunday is the latest opportunity for someone to prove otherwise. Justin Gaethje won the interim lightweight title and gets his third crack at undisputed gold at 37 years old. For Gaethje, this is almost certainly his last real shot. Topuria has finished Volkanovski, Holloway, and Oliveira in succession, all inside three rounds, and enters as a massive -750 favorite with a public prediction of a first-round finish. The question is not whether Topuria wins but whether Gaethje can survive long enough to make it a fight.

The co-main event is the most genuinely uncertain fight on the card. Alex Pereira is vacating his light heavyweight title to chase a belt in a third UFC weight class, something no fighter has ever accomplished. He comes in at roughly 232 lbs against Ciryl Gane at 247.5 lbs, a 43-pound gap that has created near-even odds and real uncertainty. Gane has hinted he may not keep this on pure striking, which would make Pereira's path to victory significantly more complicated. The winner gets an undisputed title shot against Tom Aspinall.

Josh Hokit is the most compelling new character in the heavyweight division, and this card is his coming-out party. His war with Curtis Blaydes at UFC 327 set the record for most significant strikes in a heavyweight contest (351 combined), and both men shared an ambulance to the hospital afterward. He is now being given Derrick Lewis on the biggest stage the UFC has ever used. Lewis holds the UFC record for most KOs with 16 and is dangerous in any fight, but he is 41, coming off a loss that looked like a quit, and his grappling has always been the hole in his game. Hokit exploits exactly that.

The rest of the main card fills in the gaps nicely. Mauricio Ruffy is a genuine star taking on a Michael Chandler who keeps getting big opportunities he has stopped being able to cash in on. Bo Nickal brings elite wrestling credentials against a Kyle Daukaus who punched his way back into the UFC and is not short on confidence. And Diego Lopes vs. Steve Garcia is a featherweight collision between two finishers with legitimate claims to be near the top of the division.


Main Card Picks

Ilia Topuria vs. Justin Gaethje

Ilia Topuria (17-0) enters as the undisputed lightweight champion on one of the most dominant title run stretches the UFC has seen in years. He finished Alexander Volkanovski, Max Holloway, and Charles Oliveira in back-to-back title fights, all by knockout, all inside three rounds. He moved up from featherweight after winning that belt and has not skipped a beat. He enters this fight as a -750 favorite and has publicly predicted a first-round finish.

Justin Gaethje (27-5) is 37 years old and holds the interim lightweight title, setting up this unification bout. This is his third shot at undisputed UFC gold, having previously lost title fights to Khabib Nurmagomedov in 2020 and Oliveira in 2022. He comes in on a two-fight win streak with decisions over Rafael Fiziev and Paddy Pimblett, but his last three fights against elite competition have all gone the distance. He carries the highest volume output on the card at 7.38 significant strikes per minute and has 20 career KO wins, but the core problem is that he gets hit. He got hit by Oliveira, by Chandler, by Poirier, by Holloway, by Fiziev, and even by Paddy Pimblett, who does not have genuine knockout power. You simply cannot afford to get hit by Topuria.

Topuria's counter-punching precision in close quarters is the most dangerous weapon in the division. Gaethje's path involves pressing the pace and hoping to impose his volume before Topuria catches him clean, but Topuria has made a habit of catching bigger, harder opponents coming in. Whether Gaethje can survive the early rounds and grind through the fight is the central question, though the answer feels increasingly obvious looking at recent film.

Pick: Ilia Topuria

Method: KO/TKO

Topuria is the superstar of the UFC right now. He has the skills, the swagger, and the rare ability to manifest exactly what he says he will do inside the octagon. Unfortunately for Gaethje, Topuria is probably the best fighter in the world at this moment, and there is no particular area of the game where Gaethje has an edge.

The fundamental problem is that Gaethje gets hit. It does not matter who is across from him: he ate shots from Oliveira, from Chandler, from Poirier, from Holloway, and even from Paddy Pimblett, who has no KO power to speak of. That tendency becomes a death sentence against Topuria, who has finished his last three opponents and possesses both nuclear power and impeccable boxing mechanics that allow him to set traps and complete long combinations with precision.

Gaethje will be more cautious than Oliveira was, who charged into Topuria's fire in the first round. This is likely his retirement fight, and he knows it, so he will be strategic and keep his distance early. Topuria will probe with combinations in round one without fully committing, giving Gaethje a false sense that he can stand and trade. Then, in round two or three, Topuria finishes the combination and bounces Gaethje's head off the canvas. The only real pressure on Topuria going in is that expectations are so high that anything short of an early finish will be read as underperformance. But even in a longer fight, this has a Topuria masterclass written all over it.

Alex Pereira vs. Ciryl Gane

Alex Pereira (13-3) is vacating his light heavyweight title to compete at heavyweight, chasing a belt in a third UFC weight class, something no fighter has ever done. His record speaks for itself: 11 of his 13 wins have come by knockout, and his kickboxing background makes him dangerous in any standup exchange. After losing a decision to Magomed Ankalaev, he came back and knocked Ankalaev out in the first round of their rematch. He came in at roughly 232 lbs at his last fight night.

Ciryl Gane (13-2) enters on a two-fight win streak and has never been stopped by strikes in his MMA career. His only losses are a decision to Francis Ngannou and a first-round guillotine to Jon Jones. He weighed in at 247.5 lbs, a 43-pound gap over Pereira that is the central narrative of this fight. Gane has hinted pre-fight that he may not keep this purely on the feet, suggesting he could look to grapple and neutralize Pereira's power. The winner faces Tom Aspinall for the undisputed heavyweight title.

The near-even odds reflect genuine uncertainty. Gane's footwork, technical striking, and sheer size give him a credible path to outwork Pereira over five rounds, especially if he is willing to mix in grappling. Gane has never been knocked out by strikes but has a history of choking in the biggest moments of his career, coming up short against Jones, Ngannou, and Aspinall when it mattered most.

Pick: Alex Pereira

Method: KO/TKO

Pereira is that guy. He was the first middleweight in UFC history to beat Adesanya, only a year into his UFC career. He then became light heavyweight champion and went on one of the best runs in recent history, with four title fights in eleven months. He has an extraordinary ability to perform when the stakes are highest, and that dog in him has never shown any sign of leaving.

Gane is a slightly underrated fighter given his skillset, but he has a documented habit of choking in the biggest moments of his career. He choked against Jones, he choked against Ngannou, and he choked against Aspinall. Those are three consecutive failures when the lights were brightest, and that pattern matters here.

Grappling will not be the deciding factor. Neither man is particularly elite on the ground, so this will come down to kickboxing, and in a kickboxing fight Pereira has the power advantage. Gane is a volume striker who moves well and works behind a jab, but he does not carry genuine one-punch knockout power, and his boxing is relatively basic: jab, straight right, circle away. Pereira is a far more complete boxer with the finishing instinct and the stamina to get there late if he needs to. The weight gap is real, and Gane is better prepared to carry it at this weight class, but Pereira's power and output do not diminish the way Gane's mobility will as the fight progresses. The longer this goes, the more Gane slows down, the more Pereira catches him with a left hook, and Gane picks up the first TKO loss of his career.

Sean O'Malley vs. Aiemann Zahabi

Former bantamweight champion Sean O'Malley (19-3) lost his belt to Merab Dvalishvili and then got finished in the rematch via north-south choke in round three. He bounced back with a unanimous decision over Song Yadong in January 2026 and now needs to make a statement to stay in the title conversation. He has said publicly that he cannot afford a boring win and has to finish Zahabi, acknowledging Zahabi's durability may require him to land a knockout shot multiple times before the fight stops.

Aiemann Zahabi (14-2) arrives on a seven-fight win streak that includes genuine scalps: Marlon Vera, Jose Aldo, and Pedro Munhoz. Eight of his 14 wins have come in the first round, and he carries a legitimate first-round finishing threat. He enters as a +300 underdog and has pointed to his mental edge as a differentiator, noting that he has been winning consistently while O'Malley came off back-to-back losses to Merab. This fight also carries a Canada vs. USA storyline on an overtly patriotic card.

Zahabi's seven-fight run is real, but he has not faced anyone at O'Malley's level. His most recent fight was a split decision over a Chito Vera who is well past his prime and whose only game plan is to walk forward and absorb punishment. O'Malley danced around Chito effortlessly. Zahabi's aggressive first-round instincts could create early danger, but O'Malley's counter-striking and footwork are tailor-made for opponents who come forward without elite level.

Pick: Sean O'Malley

Method: KO/TKO

This matchup has O'Malley masterclass written all over it. Zahabi has not done anything in the UFC that would suggest he can beat a true top contender, and O'Malley is exactly that. A seven-fight win streak sounds impressive until you look at who it was built on. His most recent win came over the corpse of Chito Vera in a split decision, when all Chito does is plod forward and get hit. O'Malley made Chito look silly. Expect the same here.

O'Malley is at his best on the feet with space to move, and Zahabi's forward pressure style plays directly into his counter-striking game. When Zahabi tires in the second round, O'Malley catches him clean and puts him out cold.

The one interesting angle on this fight is the setup. The UFC is clearly positioning O'Malley for another title shot, and if he puts on a spectacular performance with a good promo at the White House, that conversation starts immediately. But if the performance is underwhelming, the right next step is O'Malley vs. Umar for the number one contender spot, not a direct rematch with Merab.

Derrick Lewis vs. Josh Hokit

Josh Hokit (9-0) is the most exciting new heavyweight in the division and this card is his official coming-out party. At UFC 327, he went to war with Curtis Blaydes in a fight that set the all-time record for significant strikes landed in a heavyweight bout (351 combined). Both men were loaded into the same ambulance afterward, with Blaydes leaving with a fractured orbital and nose. Hokit won by unanimous decision and earned his spot on this card directly because President Trump and Joe Rogan asked why he was not already on it. He is a former All-American heavyweight wrestler and standout running back at Fresno State who also spent time with the San Francisco 49ers before finding his way to MMA. He enters as a -360 favorite.

Derrick Lewis (29-13) is a 41-year-old legend who holds the UFC record for most knockouts in history with 16. He was added to this card at the explicit request of Trump and Rogan after Hokit's Blaydes performance made noise. He comes off a TKO loss to Waldo Cortes-Acosta in January 2026 that looked like he quit in the octagon. Lewis is dangerous in any heavyweight fight because of his one-punch KO power, but his defensive liabilities and near-total absence of a grappling game have always been the holes in his armor.

Hokit's wrestling is the defining matchup dynamic. Lewis's biggest deficiency has always been grappling, and Hokit's most dangerous weapon is exactly that, mixed with relentless ground and pound. Lewis needs to land something clean early before Hokit closes the distance.

Pick: Josh Hokit

Method: KO/TKO

Anyone complaining about Josh Hokit being on this card needs to pick a lane. The same people who groan about the UFC being boring are the ones dismissing a 9-0 undefeated heavyweight who just had a Fight of the Night war with a former title challenger. Pick a side.

Hokit is a super athlete. All-American wrestler, college football standout at Fresno State, a stint with the San Francisco 49ers, and now an undefeated MMA record capped by one of the best heavyweight performances in recent memory. He is durable, relentless, and mixes grappling with ground and pound seamlessly.

On the other side, Lewis is a meme lord with nuclear power and not much else at this stage of his career. His last fight against Waldo Cortes-Acosta was embarrassing: it looked like he quit in the octagon and got finished. His grappling has always been the hole in his game, and that is Hokit's primary weapon. Hokit takes Lewis down early and finishes with ground and pound. This is the perfect stage for Hokit to announce himself, and hopefully he has a poem ready to recite at the White House afterward.

Mauricio Ruffy vs. Michael Chandler

Mauricio Ruffy (13-2) is a 29-year-old Brazilian knockout artist with 12 KO wins and a finishing rate that makes him must-watch every time he steps in the octagon. He bounced back from a rear naked choke loss to Benoit Saint-Denis with a second-round TKO over Rafael Fiziev in January 2026. He has spent time training with Alexander Volkanovski to shore up his ground game and has teased he has something "special" planned for Chandler. He is a genuine rising star in the lightweight division.

Michael Chandler (23-10) has lost three straight UFC fights, including a third-round stoppage loss to Paddy Pimblett in April 2025 that was as bad a result as his record shows. His long-awaited matchup with Conor McGregor never materialized, and this is his return to action against a younger, more dangerous striker. Chandler's wrestling and experience are the only credible path in this fight, and he has previewed the matchup by saying he has studied Ruffy extensively and believes his wrestling and veteran savvy will be the difference.

Ruffy showed a real vulnerability on the ground against Saint-Denis, which is the one angle Chandler could exploit. However, Chandler is not BSD, his chin has been tested repeatedly in recent fights, and Ruffy's striking is a genuine threat. If Chandler tries to cover distance in the opening exchange and trade on the feet, it ends badly for him.

Pick: Mauricio Ruffy

Method: KO/TKO

This matchup is frustrating because Chandler has been handed super fight after super fight in the UFC without delivering, and he keeps getting rewarded with another one. He is not a top-15 lightweight anymore, and the results have made that abundantly clear. Ruffy, on the other hand, is a genuine star with incredible striking ability.

The smart play for Chandler would be to grapple, since Saint-Denis exposed that Ruffy can be outworked on the ground. But Chandler is not that disciplined. He will try to close the distance in the opening exchange, look for the big knockout moment, and walk into one of Ruffy's highlight-reel finishes. Chandler's chin has taken serious damage over the course of his career, and Ruffy hits hard enough to exploit that.

After this fight, Chandler should retire. If he does fight again, he needs to be matched outside the rankings because he is no longer top-15 caliber. And if Ruffy wins, the UFC should resist the urge to immediately hand him a title shot. A Chandler win is not what it used to be. Give Ruffy the winner of McGregor vs. Holloway, or put him in with Oliveira, and then we can start talking about title shots.

Bo Nickal vs. Kyle Daukaus

Bo Nickal (8-1) is a three-time NCAA wrestling national champion and U23 world champion who has been trying to translate that pedigree into a UFC title run. His career has had two distinct phases: an early stretch where he looked cautious and uncomfortable in the octagon despite his undefeated record, and then the first loss of his career to Reinier de Ridder via body shot in May 2025. He came back from that with a vengeance at MSG, outclassing Rodolfo Vieira on the ground early before finishing with a devastating head kick. That was the promise of Bo Nickal. He enters as a -330 favorite in the only all-American matchup on the card.

Kyle Daukaus (17-4) is on his second UFC stint after going 2-4 in his first run and getting cut. He returned to CFFC, won the middleweight championship there, and fought his way back into the promotion. In his second UFC run he has gone 4-0 with first-round finishes of Michel Pereira and Gerald Meerschaert, both in under a minute. He is a well-rounded veteran with submissions and striking, and he has said publicly that "Bo Nickal was looking for an easier fight." He is not wrong that he presents a genuine test, but the level of his recent victims is worth noting.

Nickal's elite wrestling is the central question of this fight. If he can get Daukaus down and control him, his grappling advantage should be decisive. Daukaus is a submission specialist who has shown he can finish in the clinch and on the feet, but his second-run wins have come against lower-tier opposition, and Nickal represents a meaningful step up.

Pick: Bo Nickal

Method: Submission

Daukaus is quietly the second-best opponent Nickal has faced, behind only de Ridder. He has fought real fighters in Michel Pereira, Brandon Allen, Roman Dolidze, Kevin Holland, and Gerald Meerschaert. His two wins in this second UFC run were against Pereira and an aging Meerschaert, and while those were somewhat soft opponents for this level, he dispatched both in under a minute. He has a real skillset, with submissions, striking, and the kind of veteran savvy that gives prospects trouble.

But Nickal is going to be keen to grapple early, and it is going to be too much. The MSG performance against Vieira showed exactly what Nickal looks like when he is locked in and comfortable: he controls the grappling from the start and finishes with authority. Daukaus will not be able to hold him off long enough. Nickal gets the submission in round one.

After this, Nickal needs to stay active. Two more fights this year is the right call: get him back in September, then early next year, and start making a real charge toward the title.

Diego Lopes vs. Steve Garcia

Diego Lopes (27-8) has been living at the top of the featherweight division for the past two years. He challenged Alexander Volkanovski for the featherweight title twice, losing both by unanimous decision, and sandwiched a TKO win over Jean Silva between those title shots. He enters this fight as the more experienced and technically complete fighter, and has stated pre-fight that Garcia "has the perfect style for me," suggesting he sees Garcia's aggression as something he can exploit rather than something he needs to survive.

Steve Garcia (19-5) has been on an absolute tear with seven straight wins, six of his last seven opponents finished by KO. He knocked out Calvin Kattar and David Onama in his two most recent fights and brings the kind of blitzing, high-pressure style that has overwhelmed everyone he has faced on this run. He enters as a significant underdog against a fighter with two title fight appearances on his resume, and this is the biggest test of his career by a considerable margin.

Garcia's early blitzes are genuinely terrifying and are the primary danger for Lopes in the opening minutes. But Lopes has fought at the highest level of the featherweight division and has the cardio, chin, and technical base to weather the storm. Garcia has not faced anyone close to Lopes's level, and how he handles a fighter who does not fold in the first round is the real unknown.

Pick: Diego Lopes

Method: KO/TKO

Both Lopes and Garcia are legitimate studs, and this fight has been genuinely difficult to call. Garcia's seven-fight win streak is the kind of run that only serious fighters put together in the UFC, and it deserves respect. But the competition gap is real. Lopes has beaten Brian Ortega, Dan Ige, and Jean Silva and gone the distance twice with Volkanovski. Garcia has not faced anyone at that standard.

Lopes is good on both the feet and the ground, has power in his hands, a legitimate chin, and genuine finishing ability from multiple positions. Garcia is another knockout artist, but one peg below. Watching Garcia's fight with Kattar back, the win was built on ticky-tacky striking, not the kind of sustained pressure that will work against a fighter of Lopes's caliber. When Garcia blitzes here, Lopes is going to be ready for it, and that aggression plays directly into Lopes's counter-striking game. Garcia slips up and gets knocked out inside two rounds.
