Overview

UFC Vegas 114 is not a stacked card by any measure, but it has a handful of genuinely interesting fights and a main event with a compelling narrative — a generational clash between one of featherweight's all-time greats and the division's hottest young gun. That storyline alone is worth tuning in for.

The main event pits a 41-year-old Josh Emmett — 1-4 in his last five, fielding retirement questions, still swinging for title relevance — against Kevin Vallejos, the Argentine firecracker who went 3-0 in his first UFC year with three finishes, capped by a spinning backfist KO of Giga Chikadze. Reportedly the largest age gap in UFC main event history at 16-plus years. It has a real passing-of-the-torch feel to it.

Below the main event, Brad Tavares has a shot at history — one win would give him sole possession of the all-time UFC middleweight wins record, surpassing Michael Bisping's 16. The catch is that his opponent Eryk Anders is exactly the kind of explosive brawler who can end a fight before the record even becomes relevant. The co-main between Amanda Lemos and Gillian Robertson is a clean striker-vs-grappler matchup where both the path to victory and the path to upset are obvious. And Bia Mesquita, the 10-time BJJ world champion, continues her UFC run in a fight where she is an overwhelming favorite for good reason.

This is a card with some real moments scattered across it if the right fights deliver. The main event in particular should be fun — Vallejos brings the kind of volume and urgency that will force Emmett to prove he still has enough left.


Main Card Picks

Josh Emmett vs. Kevin Vallejos

Josh Emmett (19-6) is the UFC featherweight all-time leader in knockdowns, a legitimate former title contender, and one of the most dangerous punchers at 145 lbs. He is also 41 years old and has gone 1-4 in his last five fights, with losses to Lerone Murphy (decision), Youssef Zalal (first-round armbar), Ilia Topuria (decision), and Yair Rodriguez (triangle choke). The Murphy loss showed that he no longer has the speed or striking sharpness to beat elite fighters on the feet; the Zalal loss showed he can't compete with the best on the ground either. He's stepping up as the card's headliner, but the skid is real.

Kevin Vallejos (17-1) is 24 years old, born December 8, 2001, and went 3-0 in his first year in the UFC with three finishes. He capped 2025 with a second-round spinning backfist KO of Giga Chikadze, a result that vaulted him into the featherweight top 15 and made him the most talked-about young featherweight on the roster. He presses a relentless pace — 5.78 significant strikes per minute — and has 12 career KOs, 8 in the first round. This is reportedly the largest age gap in UFC main event history at 16 years and 9 months. It has a passing-of-the-torch quality to it.

Pick: Kevin Vallejos

Method: Decision

Emmett's only real path to a win here is a knockout, and that only happens if Vallejos unwisely walks into the pocket and gets clipped. Everything else favors Vallejos. The Murphy loss was telling — Emmett looked slow and couldn't land anything meaningful against a guy who was happy to just box him at range. Vallejos is faster, younger, and hits just as hard. He is going to be moving constantly — striking, retreating, not standing in front of Emmett and trading — and that movement will keep him off the knockout line while his volume accumulates over three rounds. Emmett cannot change levels to chase a finish and he can no longer impose his will on the feet. Vallejos by decision is the safe and obvious bet.

Amanda Lemos vs. Gillian Robertson

Amanda Lemos (15-5-1) is ranked #5 at strawweight and is genuinely one of the few fighters in the division who can end a fight with a single shot — she lands with 55% accuracy and carries real power for the weight class. She's dropped two of her last three though, including being outgrappled by Tatiana Suarez and dominated by Weili Zhang in a grappling-heavy matchup. The pattern is clear: Lemos beats fighters she is significantly better than, and she gets exposed when the opponent can either outbox her or take the fight to the ground. This bout was originally scheduled for December 2025 but was pulled after she suffered a mouth injury.

Gillian Robertson (16-8) is ranked #8 at 115 lbs and is on a four-fight winning streak, her most recent win a second-round TKO of Marina Rodriguez via punches from back control. She is one of the most relentlessly active grapplers in the division — her entire game is built around clinch, takedowns, and hunting submissions from any position. The UFC has been framing this as a potential springboard to title contention for her.

Pick: Gillian Robertson

Method: Decision

Lemos's offense is built around the threat of standing power, but her offensive grappling is not strong enough to keep this fight standing if Robertson decides to take it down. And Robertson will decide to take it down — that is her entire identity as a fighter. Lemos has already shown she has no answer for top-level grapplers, and the Suarez and Zhang losses weren't close. Robertson is on a four-fight streak for a reason: she is getting better and her grappling is a genuine weapon at this level. She is good enough to take this fight, and she will beat Lemos over three rounds by simply imposing her game plan from the opening bell. Lemos's power is always dangerous, but Robertson doesn't need to stand in front of her.

Andre Fili vs. Jose Delgado

Andre Fili (25-12) is 35 years old and has been a UFC featherweight journeyman for years. He's a fluid southpaw-switch hitter who relies on his jab, feints, and leg kicks, and he never developed consistent finishing power. His last win was a split decision over Christian Rodriguez in August 2025. He has the experience and the ring generalship, but the clock is running out on his ability to compete with the newer generation.

Jose Delgado (10-2) is 27 and had an immediate impact in the UFC with back-to-back first-round finishes of Connor Matthews and Hyder Amil before losing a close decision to Nathaniel Wood. That Wood fight was competitive — Delgado arguably could have won — and it showed he can hang with the division's better fighters. He carries real power, sharp shot selection, and lateral footwork that makes him difficult to time.

Pick: Jose Delgado

Method: Decision

Fili has been a journeyman for a long time within the UFC and hasn't been able to put together a meaningful win streak in years. At 35, this is exactly the kind of fight where the new guard steps in and takes the win over the veteran who is on the back nine of his career. Delgado is a solid prospect with nice striking and real power — his loss to Wood was either extremely close or arguably a robbery, not a sign of a fundamental ceiling. He goes the distance here and takes a clear decision.

Marwan Rahiki vs. Harry Hardwick

Marwan Rahiki (7-0) is a 23-year-old Australian making his UFC debut off a Dana White's Contender Series appearance where he finished via combination knockout in round two. Unbeaten across seven professional fights with a high finishing rate — he is one of the most hyped young featherweights entering 2026, and he enters as a -275 favorite.

Harry Hardwick (13-4-1) is the English veteran looking for his first UFC win after being knocked out by a calf kick in his promotional debut against Kaue Fernandes. He was experienced enough on the regional circuit, but his UFC debut showed vulnerability to power shots and leg attacks. It is genuinely unclear why the UFC brought him back.

Pick: Marwan Rahiki

Method: KO/TKO

Given how Hardwick's first UFC fight went, both of these guys are effectively making their debut in the promotion this weekend. On the regional scene, Rahiki has consistently been able to finish his opponents; Hardwick has not shown the same ability, and his UFC debut showed real vulnerability to power. Rahiki has more success, more finishing ability, and more upside. Hardwick gets knocked out.

Oumar Sy vs. Ion Cutelaba

Oumar Sy (12-1) is 3-1 in the UFC, with his only loss a decision to Alonzo Menifield. He bounced back immediately with a first-round TKO of Brendson Ribeiro at UFC Paris via elbows and punches from back mount, reestablishing himself as a rising divisional name. He blends long-range striking with opportunistic grappling and brings measured composure to every fight.

Ion Cutelaba (19-11-1) is 32 years old and has 13 career knockouts and 15 first-round finishes. He is an absolute chaos merchant — his explosiveness makes him live in any exchange, and he has the ability to end a fight before an opponent settles in. His record against elite competition is poor, but against mid-tier opponents he remains a genuine danger. Both of these fighters are, frankly, not elite light heavyweights.

Pick: Oumar Sy

Method: Decision

This comes down to picking your poison between two mediocre light heavyweights. Cutelaba is not UFC caliber at this stage of his career. Sy's only loss comes at the hands of Menifield, who is a legitimate fighter — that is a better résumé than anything Cutelaba has shown recently. Sy will manage the chaos, use his composure and footwork to stay off the knockout line, and grind out a decision. He might get a finish, but he is not so much better than Cutelaba that I'd count on it.

Bruno Silva vs. Charles Johnson

Bruno Silva (15-7-2) was originally scheduled to fight Lone'er Kavanagh, who withdrew to headline a different card. Silva dropped two in a row to Joshua Van and Manel Kape before rebounding with a third-round rear naked choke of Hyun Sung Park in October 2025. He has publicly stated he is chasing a $100,000 performance bonus here, which signals a finishing mindset.

Charles Johnson (18-8) stepped in as a late replacement after Kavanagh withdrew. He carries a significant size and reach advantage — sources cite a 5-inch height and reach gap in his favor — and brings high-volume striking and durability. He was knocked out by Alex Perez in January 2026, the first KO loss of his career. That is a concerning turnaround.

Pick: Bruno Silva

Method: KO/TKO

Why is Charles Johnson coming back so soon? He got knocked out by Alex Perez in January — the first KO loss of his career — and now he is fighting again six weeks later. That is an ill-advised turnaround, and fighters who take their first career knockout and turn around immediately are almost never ready to absorb more shots in the cage. Silva does not even need to throw heat here — whatever he lands is going to have a disproportionate effect on an opponent whose chin was just cracked for the first time. This looks like the end of Charles Johnson. Silva gets the KO.

Prelims Picks

Chris Curtis vs. Myktybek Orolbai

Chris Curtis (32-12) is 38 years old and one of the UFC's most crowd-pleasing brawlers — he connects at 5.98 significant strikes per minute and has never been taken down in his UFC career. He has fought legitimate fighters at middleweight: Roman Kopylov, Brendan Allen, Kelvin Gastelum. He loses fights, but he does it against real competition, and he has the experience and durability to keep going.

Myktybek Orolbai (15-2-1) finished Jack Hermansson with a first-round KO in his most recent fight, which sounds impressive on paper. The context matters though — that Hermansson came off one of the most violent knockouts in recent UFC memory, when Gregory Rodrigues destroyed him in July 2025, and then turned right back around to fight Orolbai in October and got starched again. That win should not be weighted as highly as the odds suggest. Orolbai enters as a heavy -300 favorite.

Pick: Chris Curtis

Method: Decision

Orolbai has not fought very good competition within the UFC, and that Hermansson win came against a fighter who was already shot and took an ill-advised turnaround after a brutal stoppage. Curtis, on the other hand, has fought genuine middleweights in Kopylov, Allen, and Gastelum. His experience at this level gives him a real edge, and the fact that he has never been taken down in the UFC limits Orolbai's best path to a win. Curtis stays on the feet, lays hands on him, and takes the decision. The -300 line on Orolbai is too steep given that résumé.

Vitor Petrino vs. Steven Asplund

Vitor Petrino (13-2) made what he himself called a "wise decision" in moving from light heavyweight to heavyweight in 2025, going 2-0 in the division with a first-round rear naked choke of Austen Lane and a third-round KO of Thomas Petersen. He enters unbeaten at heavyweight and is one of the cleaner technical strikers in the division's prospect tier.

Steven Asplund (7-1) has personal motivation here — Petrino defeated his teammate Thomas Petersen by KO in October 2025, and Asplund stated he believes he can "chin" Petrino. He is on a four-fight win streak with stoppages in each, and he has positioned himself as a high-volume, durable underdog. He is also a genuine character who could develop into a fan favorite with his never-ending cardio and his personality.

Pick: Steven Asplund

Method: KO/TKO

Petrino is not a knockout artist and his cardio is a question mark at heavyweight. Asplund, on the other hand, has never-ending output and the kind of forward pressure that can wear fighters down late. The only way this fight goes is Asplund walking Petrino down, landing in volume, and breaking him in the third round. The personal stakes add real intensity, and Asplund is the kind of fighter who fights harder when the motivation is there. Taking the upset here.

Elijah Smith vs. Su Young You

Elijah Smith (9-1) is 2-0 in the UFC with a brutal slam KO of Toshiomi Kazama as his most recent win. At 5'9" with a 71-inch reach, he carries significant physical advantages at 135 lbs and has already called You "easy to read" in fight week media. He is a rising prospect who finishes fights.

Su Young You (16-3) is 2-0 in the UFC with back-to-back unanimous decisions over AJ Cunningham and Long Xiao. Both wins came by decision and both opponents are forgettable. You is undersized at bantamweight, has sound defensive fundamentals, and is extremely difficult to finish — but the level of competition he has faced so far does not instill confidence that he is ready for a step up.

Pick: Elijah Smith

Method: KO/TKO

The talent that comes out of East Asia in the UFC has rarely translated into elite performance at the promotional level. You came through the Road to UFC, his two wins are over forgettable Asian opponents, and his one legitimate test — AJ Cunningham — also ended in a boring decision. Smith has the physical advantages, the finishing ability, and the upside to blow this fight open. You's decision-oriented style is not going to survive three rounds of Smith's athleticism and power. Smith gets the stoppage.

Bolaji Oki vs. Manoel Sousa

Bolaji Oki (10-3) is a UFC veteran with a 2-2 promotional record. Born in Brussels of Nigerian heritage, he enters as the +220 underdog. His most recent loss was to Mason Jones, who is a legitimate lightweight — not a soft mark. He brings UFC experience and the adjustments that come with having already navigated the promotional environment.

Manoel Sousa (13-1) is making his UFC debut with 11 finishes in 13 wins, an exceptional finishing rate on the regional circuit. He is a former opponent of Mauricio Ruffy and carries the credentials of a fighter who finishes fights. The -275 line reflects real confidence in his ability. He did pick up a loss against Archie Colgan, who is now signed in the PFL — not exactly a world-beater.

Pick: Bolaji Oki

Method: Decision

There is no obvious explanation for why Sousa is such a large favorite here. His regional résumé has some nice knockouts but also a loss to Colgan, who is a perfectly fine fighter but not someone whose win over you should be generating -275 odds in a UFC debut. Oki, meanwhile, has more UFC experience and has fought better competition within the promotion. He has earned his wins the hard way, and I think he turns away Sousa in an upset decision. This is a value play.

Brad Tavares vs. Eryk Anders

Brad Tavares (21-11) tied Michael Bisping's all-time UFC middleweight wins record at 16 with his April 2025 decision over Gerald Meerschaert, then dropped a third-round TKO to Robert Bryczek in September 2025. One win here gives him sole possession of the record — the central storyline of the fight. He is a disciplined kickboxer who builds behind his jab and calf kicks, but he is 38 years old and the losses have been piling up.

Eryk Anders (17-9) is also 38 years old and also coming off a loss, stopped by Christian Leroy Duncan in August 2025 after a highlight-reel ground-and-pound TKO of Chris Weidman the previous December. Both of these guys are near the end of their careers, have been on win-one-loss-one patterns for a while, and have both beaten Chris Weidman recently. This is reportedly the final fight of Anders's current UFC contract.

Pick: Eryk Anders

Method: KO/TKO

Neither of these men should still be in the UFC at this stage of their careers. That said, looking at recent results, there is more of a prime Eryk Anders that still exists than there is a prime Brad Tavares. Anders stopped Weidman cold with ground and pound and has the explosive power to end any fight in any round. Tavares is more done — the Bryczek loss was a third-round TKO, and he has been getting finished by fighters he has no business being finished by. Anders gets the KO within two rounds before the record becomes relevant.

Bia Mesquita vs. Montse Rendon

Bia Mesquita (6-0) is a 10-time Brazilian jiu-jitsu world champion making a seamless transition into MMA. She submitted Irina Alekseeva with a second-round rear naked choke in her UFC debut and enters as a massive -550 favorite. Her BJJ credentials make her an elite submission threat the moment the fight hits the ground.

Montse Rendon (7-1) has all seven of her wins by decision. She is a durable, disciplined fighter who grinds out rounds rather than finishing. Every single win she has ever had has gone to the judges, and she is now stepping in against one of the most accomplished grapplers to ever enter the octagon.

Pick: Bia Mesquita

Method: KO/TKO

Rendon does not know what she is getting herself into. Mesquita is going to be extremely aggressive from the opening bell — she will take Rendon down and work until the referee pulls her off. Rendon has never been finished in her career, but she has also never faced a grappler anywhere near this level. This will be one-way traffic and will not go the distance. Mesquita earns the finish.

Luan Lacerda vs. Hecher Sosa

Luan Lacerda (13-3) is a UFC veteran who has had a rough go in the promotion, going 1-2 in his first three bouts with his only win coming over Saimon Oliveira — a fighter who has done nothing but lose since joining the UFC. He built some momentum with a second-round armbar submission of Oliveira at UFC Rio in October 2025. He enters as the +200 underdog despite being the more experienced man in the cage.

Hecher Sosa (14-1) is making his UFC debut on an 11-fight win streak with 9 finishes during that run. The Lanzarote, Spain-based fighter punched his Contender Series ticket with a decision over Mackson Lee and carries significant finishing momentum into this debut.

Pick: Hecher Sosa

Method: Decision

It is hard to predict how a debutant will adjust to the UFC level, but going with Sosa here. Lacerda has had a rough UFC run and his only win is over a fighter who is essentially a promotional stepping stone. It is not certain Sosa is UFC caliber, but it is certain Lacerda is not. Sosa will not be in danger from Lacerda on the feet and should be able to outbox him for three rounds and take the decision.

Piera Rodriguez vs. Sam Hughes

Piera Rodriguez (11-2) is on a two-fight winning streak with wins over Josefine Knutsson and Ketlen Souza and enters as the -155 favorite. She won their first meeting via three-round unanimous decision in October 2022 and brings well-rounded striking with good takedown defense to this rematch.

Sam Hughes (11-6) has mounted a three-fight winning streak since her last loss, and her most recent win — a second-round rear naked choke of Shauna Bannon — showed she has added a submission dimension to her game that did not exist in 2022. Both fighters have evolved, which adds genuine uncertainty to the rematch.

Pick: Piera Rodriguez

Method: Decision

The endless parade of split decisions in women's MMA is maddening — every one of these fights ends on a razor-thin margin and it is nearly impossible to pick with confidence. In their first fight, Rodriguez got her hand raised. There is no obvious reason why the dynamic changes enough in this rematch for Hughes to flip the script, even accounting for her improvement. Rodriguez takes it the same way she did the first time — by decision.
