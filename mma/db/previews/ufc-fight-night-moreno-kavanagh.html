Overview

The UFC returns to Mexico City to tap into the Mexican market again, and once more the result is a card that is heavy on local fighters and light on genuine star power. A significant chunk of the roster is making their UFC debuts off the regional scene or the Contender Series, and the matchups that remain fail to generate any real buzz. UFC Mexico City is shaping up to be another disappointment — the kind of card that hardcore fans watch out of obligation rather than excitement.

The main event itself is a perfect encapsulation of the problem. Brandon Moreno is fighting a short-notice replacement in Lone'er Kavanagh, who was training to face Bruno Silva — not a former two-time flyweight world champion. To make matters worse, the next UFC flyweight title fight has already been announced, meaning this bout carries essentially zero divisional stakes beyond Moreno's personal narrative. It is a feel-good homecoming masquerading as a meaningful contest.


Main Card Picks

Brandon Moreno vs. Lone'er Kavanagh

Brandon Moreno (23-9-2) is a former two-time UFC flyweight champion fighting for his career legacy on home soil. The Mexico City crowd adores him, which makes this the perfect setting for a redemption arc — except the opponent he was given makes it feel hollow. Moreno was devastatingly TKO'd by rising star Tatsuro Taira in December 2025 and now needs a statement performance to re-enter the title picture at 32. Anything less could signal the beginning of a permanent decline at the championship level.

Lone'er Kavanagh (9-1) is a 26-year-old English prospect who stepped in on short notice after Asu Almabayev pulled injured from the card. The problem is Kavanagh was preparing to fight Bruno Silva — not a former two-time world champion. He is coming off his first professional loss, a knockout, has only three UFC bouts on his record, and is now expected to compete at the highest level against the biggest name in Mexican MMA, in Moreno's home country. The short notice alone would be damaging enough. The mismatch in experience and pedigree makes this almost farcical.

Pick:

Method:

The reality of this situation is that Kavanagh shouldn't be in the cage with Moreno — full stop. Moreno is a veteran who has had multiple close, grueling championship fights with Alexandre Pantoja and beaten Deiveson Figueiredo multiple times. Whatever his recent form looks like, his ceiling is worlds above anything Kavanagh has encountered. Kavanagh has three UFC fights and came in on short notice from a training camp built around a completely different opponent at a completely different level. He is walking into the lion's den unprepared, in the lion's backyard, off a knockout loss. Moreno does not need to do anything special here — he just needs to be himself, and Kavanagh will not be able to handle it. This gets stopped.

Marlon Vera vs. David Martinez

Marlon "Chito" Vera (23-11-1) is in a full-blown career crisis. The durable Ecuadorian veteran is on a three-fight losing streak, his UFC title shot against Sean O'Malley feels like ancient history, and at 33, his window to recapture relevance is closing fast. The core problem with Vera has always been the same: he is an extremely slow, low-output fighter who sits in front of opponents, walks forward, accumulates damage, and hopes to land a big shot late. At the top of the division, that plan gets you beaten every time. There is no evidence from any of his recent fights that he has made the adjustments necessary to change that pattern.

David "Black Spartan" Martinez (13-1) is everything the fight needs and nothing Chito wants to see across the cage. The 27-year-old Mexican kickboxer trained as a physician while simultaneously building an MMA career — a detail that tells you everything about his work ethic and intelligence. He holds a World Games gold medal, is 2-0 in the UFC including a stunning upset over veteran Rob Font, and enters in front of a home crowd that will be electric for him. He is the hottest prospect in the bantamweight division, and a win over Vera would push him into the rankings conversation immediately.

Pick:

Method:

Chito's only path to victory is a knockout, and there is zero data in his recent performances to suggest he can land one. He couldn't finish Zahabi, and he got completely schooled by an out-of-his-prime Figueiredo. His management's decision to book him in a three-round fight — rather than pushing for a five-round Apex main event where he would have maximum time to hunt for that finish — makes no strategic sense. If Vera is serious about this being a must-win fight, fighting three rounds is the worst format for his game plan.

Martinez has the boxing, the footwork, and the cardio to evade Chito's plodding offense for fifteen minutes. He will circle, use angles, and accumulate volume while Vera walks into the center hoping something miraculous happens. Three rounds is not nearly enough time for Chito to catch him. Martinez takes a clean decision.

Daniel Zellhuber vs. King Green

Daniel "Golden Boy" Zellhuber (15-3) arrived in the UFC as one of the most hyped prospects on the roster, but back-to-back losses have stalled his trajectory significantly. At 26, he is a 6'1 athletic specimen with genuine knockout power and a physical therapy degree — one of the more interesting background stories in the lightweight division. Fighting on home soil against a 39-year-old veteran, the stakes are clear: a loss here would be a serious blow to any timeline that has him competing at the top of the division.

King Green (33-17-1, 1 NC) is one of the most battle-hardened gatekeepers in the game. He legally changed his name to "King" in 2024, which is perhaps the most on-brand move imaginable for someone who has been in 50-plus professional fights. He is durable, experienced, and capable of causing problems for younger fighters. He also has been brutally finished by Mauricio Ruffy, Paddy Pimblett, and Jalin Turner in recent years, and just went to a decision with Lance Gibson Jr. — a fighter who has absolutely no business being in the UFC.

Pick:

Method:

Green has hit a ceiling in the lightweight division and cannot get past a certain caliber of fighter. He has the miles on the clock of someone who has been doing this for two decades, and his recent run of brutal finishes tells a story. He is here as a name-brand gatekeeper designed to test a younger fighter, not to win.

Zellhuber has a real chin, genuine power, and a 6-inch reach advantage he can exploit all night. Green fights with his hands down and relies on veteran reflexes that have been declining for years. Zellhuber will land enough clean shots over the course of the fight that Green eventually gets put away — likely by TKO in the third round when the accumulated damage catches up to him.

Edgar Cháirez vs. Felipe Bunes

Edgar "Puro Chicali" Cháirez (12-6, 1 NC) is the UFC's most recognizable Mexican flyweight — a reliable submission specialist from Mexicali who bounced back from a rough stretch with a first-round finish in March 2025. He is a consistent presence in the flyweight division and has the kind of well-rounded game that makes him dangerous to most opponents at this level. Fighting at home gives him an added dimension of crowd energy that flyweight fighters rarely get to tap into.

Felipe "Felipinho" Bunes (14-8) is a 36-year-old BJJ black belt who named his son "Miguel Gohan" after the Dragon Ball Z character — which tells you everything about what kind of person he is. His grappling skills are the foundation of his entire game. The problem is those skills have not translated against UFC-caliber competition, where opponents have been able to keep the fight standing and neutralize his ground game entirely. He travels to Mexico as a significant underdog with an uphill task.

Pick:

Method:

Cháirez has more success inside the UFC than Bunes, who has struggled to impose his grappling on anyone at this level. Bunes' entire game relies on getting the fight to the mat and working his BJJ — but there is no evidence he can do that against a fighter of Cháirez's caliber. If Bunes cannot get the takedown, he is fighting on Cháirez's terms on the feet, where Cháirez has the advantage. Cháirez wins a clear decision.

Imanol Rodriguez vs. Kevin Borjas

Imanol "Himan" Rodriguez (6-0) is making his UFC debut with arguably the most terrifying finishing rate on the entire roster — he has stopped every single professional opponent and has not been taken past the second round since his very first fights. Training at AKA alongside Daniel Cormier gives him elite coaching pedigree to go along with the natural finishing instincts. His only competitive blemish is a split-decision exhibition loss to Joseph Morales on The Ultimate Fighter — and Morales has since beaten Alibi Idiris and Matt Schnell, which recontextualizes just how close that fight actually was. Imanol can go the distance. He just rarely needs to.

Kevin "El Gallo Negro" Borjas (10-4) is 1-3 in the UFC and quickly running out of chances. The Peruvian striker has shown he can compete at this level on occasion, but consistency has been impossible to find. Being matched against an undefeated prospect on his UFC debut, in that prospect's home country, with a raucous crowd behind him, is about as difficult a draw as the matchmakers could have arranged.

Pick:

Method:

This matchup is being put on the main card to build Imanol Rodriguez, and that is exactly what is going to happen. Borjas is a bona fide can crusher who has not scored a knockout since joining the UFC and is 1-3 in the promotion — he is not UFC caliber, and the UFC's matchmakers know it. Rodriguez has been stopping everyone in front of him on the regional scene and the Contender Series with a ruthless efficiency that speaks for itself.

Rodriguez finishes this fight, and probably early. Borjas will not bring anything Imanol has not already seen and solved.

Santiago Luna vs. Angel Pacheco

Santiago "Borderboy" Luna (7-0) is the youngest male fighter on the UFC roster at 21 years old and one of the most compelling human interest stories in MMA. A three-time Mexican national Greco-Roman wrestling champion whose parents were deported when he was young, forcing him to cross the border daily to attend school — the nickname "Borderboy" carries genuine weight, not marketing fluff. He accepted his UFC debut on 10 days' notice and stopped his opponent in the first round. He carries a 100% finish rate, fighting at home in front of a crowd that will treat him like a future champion.

Angel Pacheco (7-3) is 34 years old and has not won inside the UFC. He did not win on the Contender Series either — his notable loss there coming against Caoloan Loughran, who was subsequently cut from the UFC and went on to lose in the PFL. He has also been plagued by inactivity. This matchup represents a significant step up against a heavily hyped prospect on home soil.

Pick:

Method:

Pacheco is not UFC caliber. His defining loss is to a fighter who was cut from the promotion — that says everything. Luna is a legitimate prospect with a genuine wrestling base and the mental fortitude of someone who has been navigating difficult circumstances his entire life. The gap in quality is real and significant.

Pacheco is tough enough to survive, which is why this lands as a decision rather than a finish. But Luna controls this from start to finish. This will almost certainly be Pacheco's final appearance in the promotion.

Prelims Picks

José Medina vs. Ryan Gandra

José "Chicho" Medina (11-6) is on a three-fight losing streak in the UFC. He did not even win his Contender Series bout — which is the minimum bar to clear before getting a UFC contract in the first place. He represents the rare Bolivian presence at this level, but his record in the promotion speaks for itself.

Ryan "Problema" Gandra (8-1) is a Brazilian prospect making his UFC debut after actually winning on the Contender Series — the crucial difference between these two fighters' paths. He has an impressive regional record and arrives with real momentum.

Pick:

Method:

Medina is terrible at this level. He couldn't beat a Contender Series opponent and has gone on a three-fight skid in the UFC since. Gandra is a fresh prospect who did what Medina never could — he went out, won his Contender Series fight, and earned his spot. There is nothing complicated about this one. Gandra will kill him.

Macy Chiasson vs. Ailín Pérez

Macy Chiasson (10-5) is the TUF 28 winner and biology graduate who has flashed genuine elite potential — her TKO of Mayra Bueno Silva was a statement. But chronic weight-cutting issues have become a serious, recurring problem. Missing weight at 137.5 lbs in her last fight is not a small thing at 33, and it raises real questions about her ability to consistently compete at bantamweight going forward.

Ailín "Fiona" Pérez (12-2) is quietly one of the most dangerous fighters in women's bantamweight. The Argentinian single mother — who has openly discussed using OnlyFans income to fund her MMA career and leans fully into the "Fiona" persona (princess at weigh-ins, ogre in the cage) — is on a five-fight win streak and is a legitimate title contender in waiting. She enters as a significant favorite.

Pick:

Method:

Despite the weight-cutting concerns and Pérez's impressive recent run, Chiasson has a more complete game. She can strike and submit — Pérez is a primarily grappling-dominant fighter who has been able to smother and overwhelm opponents with her physical style. Chiasson is not the kind of opponent Pérez can simply bulldoze into the fence and drag down at will.

Chiasson has more paths to victory. If Pérez cannot dominate in the grappling — and the evidence suggests she won't be able to here — she is fighting on Chiasson's terms. Decision goes to Chiasson.

Cristian Quiñonez vs. Kris Moutinho

Cristian "Problema" Quiñonez (18-5) is on a two-fight losing streak, has not competed since 2024, and has been submitted by rear naked choke in both losses. That is not a coincidence — it is a glaring, persistent vulnerability that opponents have figured out and are actively targeting. He is 1-2 in the UFC and fighting in his home region, but his resume at this level has been thin.

Kris Moutinho (14-7) is a journeyman with a compelling story — he went 0-3 in his initial UFC stint, built a five-fight regional win streak, earned a second chance, and then got knocked out in the first round of his return. This is his last shot. The New England brawler fights with genuine heart, but his path to staying on the roster runs through a fighter with abysmal ground defense.

Pick:

Method:

Let's be honest about what this fight is: two fighters who probably should not be on a UFC card, competing in a bout that does not warrant most fans' time. But a pick is a pick.

Quiñonez has genuinely abysmal grappling — the two rear naked choke losses are not flukes, they are the natural result of a fighter who cannot defend submissions. Moutinho knows this is his final opportunity and will play it safe. Quiñonez's only chance is a knockout on the feet, but Moutinho will take it to the mat, negate the striking, and grind out a finish. Second or third round, rear naked choke — the exact way Quiñonez has lost before.

D. Silva de Andrade vs. Javier Reyes

Douglas "D'Silva" Silva de Andrade (29-6) is a Brazilian veteran with nearly three decades of professional fights who is now making a weight class move to featherweight at 40 years old — a clear sign that the grind of making 135 lbs has become unsustainable. He is coming off a loss to Miles John and has not fought in two years. This feels like a late-career reinvention that may or may not stick.

Javier "Blair" Reyes (22-5) is a 32-year-old Colombian making his UFC debut after winning on the Contender Series. He brings an impressive regional record and represents a genuine threat to any fighter who is transitioning weight classes and returning from a two-year absence.

Pick:

Method:

Everything points against Silva de Andrade here. He is 40 years old, has not competed in two years, just took a loss, and is making a weight class transition. Reyes is 32 and entering his prime — younger, hungrier, and making his debut in a fight that appears heavily in his favor on paper.

Neither fighter appears to be exceptionally dominant in one particular area, but Reyes will simply be the sharper, more energetic fighter across three rounds. Experience goes to Silva on paper; relevance and timing go to Reyes in reality. Decision to the Colombian.

Ernesta Kareckaite vs. Sofia Montenegro

Ernesta "Heavy-Handed" Kareckaite (6-1-1) is a physical, punishing Lithuanian ranked #25 in the women's flyweight division. Her controversial split decision win over Carli Judice was messy, but it showed she can compete with legitimate contenders at this level. She carries a nickname that promises violence and has earned her place on the UFC roster.

Sofia "La Bruja" Montenegro (6-2) is an Argentinian making her UFC debut after losing via split decision on the Contender Series. "The Witch" brings dramatic flair to fight week, but walking into the UFC off a Contender Series loss is never an easy spot. Women's flyweight fights often play out in low-output grinding affairs where a couple of calf kicks can swing a judge's opinion on a round — not because the fights are genuinely close, but because nothing happens.

Pick:

Method:

It is hard to pick someone making their UFC debut who already failed to win on the Contender Series over a fighter who has multiple UFC appearances and a win to her name at this level. Kareckaite has the proven UFC experience and has demonstrated she can hang with division contenders.

The split decision pattern in women's flyweight does not necessarily mean fights are close — it means not much happened and the judges found ways to disagree. Kareckaite controls this one and takes the decision.

Erik Silva vs. Francis Marshall

Erik "King" Silva (9-3) is a 38-year-old Venezuelan who has lost both of his UFC fights. He brings physical aggression and a style designed to disrupt more technically refined opponents, but the results at this level have not been there. At 38, he is running out of runway to establish himself.

Francis "Fire" Marshall (8-3) is ranked #65 in the UFC featherweight division and is looking to climb. He is a younger, well-rounded prospect who has faced legitimately better competition since joining the UFC — Marion Santos, William Gomis, and an uncompromised Isaac Dulgarian are all significantly better opponents than anyone Silva has fought in his entire career.

Pick:

Method:

Silva is 38, 0-2 in the UFC, and this fight will not be any different. The gap in competition level between these two fighters is stark — Marshall has been tested against quality UFC-caliber opposition while Silva's resume simply does not hold up to that standard.

Marshall is a younger prospect with a clear quality advantage. He takes the decision without drama.

Wes Schultz vs. Damian Pinas

Wes "Party Time" Schultz (8-2) makes his UFC debut after winning his Contender Series bout with a Suloev stretch, showing the grappling-heavy style that defines his game. The concern with Schultz is a separate Contender Series appearance where he was finished in the first round by Mansur Abdul-Malik — a result that highlighted how he struggles against opponents who are physically imposing and dangerous.

Damian "The Baba Yaga" Pinas (8-1) hails from the tiny island of Aruba and carries the aura of someone who can genuinely end a fight with one shot — the kind of presence that Ngannou had or that Ciryl Gane carries, where you feel the knockout threat before a single punch is thrown. He arrives with near-perfect record and the momentum of a genuine prospect.

Pick:

Method:

Schultz has shown he struggles against physically imposing opponents, and Pinas is exactly that kind of fighter. The concern about how Pinas handles a dedicated grappler is valid, but it assumes Schultz gets to establish his wrestling game in the first place — which is not going to happen against a striker of this caliber.

Pinas will connect before Schultz can set up a takedown. When a striker with legitimate knockout power lands clean on a grappler who telegraphs his setups, it ends quickly and violently. Pinas finishes him.
