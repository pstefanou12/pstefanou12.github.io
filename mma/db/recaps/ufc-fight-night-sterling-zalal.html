Summary

Another Apex card, another forgettable Saturday night. UFC Vegas 116 lands at a 5.2/10 — and that's being somewhat generous given how lifeless the prelims were. From the first fight to the co-main event, the card felt like background noise you'd put on while folding laundry. Nothing mattered, nothing popped, and the handful of upsets on the undercard were interesting more for what they said about the losers than for the quality of the fights themselves.


The co-main event was supposed to be the bridge between the dead prelims and the main event. It wasn't. Joselyne Edwards and Norma Dumont put on a boring, ticky-tacky three rounds that at least ended with the correct result — Edwards got the unanimous decision, and Dumont's genuinely baffling post-fight reaction told you everything about how delusional she's been about her own performances. Good riddance to whatever Dumont title run might have been.


The main event saved the card from being a total disaster. Aljamain Sterling put on a five-round grappling clinic that systematically dismantled Youssef Zalal and definitively answered the question of who the better fighter was. The moral of the story was clear: wins over 2025 Josh Emmett and Calvin Kattar aren't what they used to be. Zalal was overrated, and Sterling exposed him over 25 minutes of increasingly one-sided control. Sterling called for a title shot afterward. Movsar Evloev has to be next in line — but knowing how the UFC works, Jean Silva is probably the call.


Two brutal weekends in a row for the promotion. Chimaev vs. Strickland is in two weeks, though that card looks just as thin outside the headliner. At this point you just have to hope the main event delivers.


Final Score: 5.2/10



Main Card

Aljamain Sterling vs. Youssef Zalal

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 5 Rounds, 25:00 Total

Aljamain Sterling came into this one with something to prove, and he proved it comprehensively over five rounds. The fight started with both men feeling each other out, Sterling circling off the back foot while Zalal pressed forward looking to impose his striking. Sterling's answer was the takedown, and once he got the first one — pinning Youssef Zalal against the cage and then kicking his legs out from underneath him — the blueprint for the entire fight was established.


Rounds two and three went back and forth more than the scorecards probably reflected. Zalal had some moments pressing forward and even took Sterling's back at one point in the third, locking up a body triangle. Sterling did a good job defending — Zalal was clearly not committing to the rear naked choke and was practically just stalling back there. By round four, the tide turned decisively. Sterling secured the body triangle on the fence and just went to work — smashing Zalal with ground and pound, working a modified rear naked choke with the arm in, and transitioning to full mount when Zalal wriggled free. Zalal technically made it back to his feet at the end of round four, though it felt more like Sterling letting him up than Zalal escaping.


Round five was pure attrition. Zalal was completely gassed, his punches carrying nothing behind them. Sterling went back to the takedown, got to the back, secured the body triangle again, and worked ground and pound until the final horn. Dominant. Sterling called for a title shot after the decision was announced — and he's earned the right to ask. Movsar Evloev has to be next in line — but don't be surprised if the UFC routes him through Jean Silva first. As for Zalal: this was a reality check. Wins over a faded Emmett and an aging Kattar look a lot less impressive when an elite grappler gets a hold of you for 25 minutes.

Joselyne Edwards vs. Norma Dumont

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Joselyne Edwards was the aggressor from the opening bell, charging at Norma Dumont looking for the finish. In the first round, she overcommitted on a rush forward and Dumont capitalized with a takedown. What followed was a mess — Edwards working elbows from the bottom, Dumont doing absolutely nothing from on top, and eventually Edwards scrambling into mount before the round ended. A chaotic, sloppy first frame.


Rounds two and three settled into something less exciting and more predictable. Dumont walked backwards for the better part of six minutes, and Edwards chased her around the octagon without ever quite landing the shot that would make the scorecards moot. There were clinch sequences, some weird knee strikes from Edwards, a thai clinch from Dumont that she immediately let go, a spinning backfist to end the third. The output died down as both women tired. It was the kind of fight you find yourself checking the clock on.


The right fighter won. Edwards was the one moving forward, at least trying to engage, and Dumont spent too much of the fight running backward to have any claim on the scorecards. The unanimous decision for Edwards was correct, and Dumont's stunned reaction at the announcement was genuinely baffling — she had to have known she wasn't winning that fight. It was a boring fight, but at least it had the right outcome. A Dumont title reign would have been painful to watch.

Rafa García vs. Alexander Hernandez

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

This was the best fight on the main card and it wasn't particularly close. Rafa García came in with a clear game plan — hold the center, let Alexander Hernandez work on the outside, and walk him down — and he executed it beautifully for three rounds.


Hernandez spent most of the fight moving laterally around the outside of the octagon while García planted himself in the center and shifted to cut off the cage. In the first round García went in for takedowns repeatedly, slamming Hernandez down twice before Hernandez finally bounced back up and denied a third attempt. By round two, García had clearly figured Hernandez out. He stopped moving entirely at one point, walked to the center, and literally gestured at Hernandez to come meet him. Hernandez had no answer. García walked him down, peppered him with jabs and right hands, landed a hook that visibly stung, and by the middle of the round Hernandez's face was busted up against the cage.


The third was more of the same — Hernandez tried to be more aggressive knowing he was behind on the cards, but García clinched him up, landed some clean shots in the exchanges, and controlled the pace to the final horn. García looked legitimately good in this one — sharp striking, reasonable wrestling, and the kind of ring generalship that comes from knowing exactly what style beats your opponent. Three-fight win streak. Give this man a ranked matchup. And look — if Chandler loses in June and doesn't retire, just give García the fight. These guys outside the rankings are beating the people that Paddy Pimblett got a title fight conversation off of. It's kind of messed up how important it is to build a gimmick around yourself in this sport.

Davey Grant vs. Adrian Luna Martinetti

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Davey Grant is 40 years old and just outwitted a hyped Contender Series prospect over three rounds. Another unc doing what he does.


Adrian Luna Martinetti came in aggressive and willing to trade, but Grant used his veteran instincts to keep the fight from ever turning into the brawl that would have suited the debutant. Grant was stomping the leg early, catching kicks and converting them to trips, and landing clean shots every time Martinetti got too close. In round two, Grant landed a head kick and a follow-up knee to the head on the feet — clean, precise, the kind of shot that reminds you the old man can still put things together. Martinetti had his moments pushing forward and landing some combinations, but he never threatened a finish. The third round was another strike-fest where neither fighter did anything too damaging — and that was the problem for Martinetti.


In a fight this close, where nothing is blowing anyone away, you can't take a round off and just coast. Martinetti faded in the third and seemingly wasn't trying to go for a finish, which is the worst possible approach when you're on the wrong side of a close scoreline. Grant took the unanimous decision, and the DWCS hype machine officially gets pumped with the brakes. The Vologdin fight that made Martinetti famous is looking worse by the week — Vologdin got styled on for three rounds in his UFC debut last weekend. Good for Grant. Crazy that 40 years old and still competing at this level.

Raoni Barcelos vs. Montel Jackson

Result: Decision, Split at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Raoni Barcelos is five straight and showing no signs of slowing down. This one wasn't clean though — it was a split decision that felt appropriately contested given how the rounds actually unfolded.


Round one went to Montel Jackson pretty clearly. He landed a combination early that put Barcelos on the canvas, followed him down, and started landing ground and pound before Barcelos somehow survived the onslaught. Jackson is genuinely terrifying in that window when he's fresh and his hands have pop. The problem is that window closes fast. By round two, Jackson was suddenly timid, not letting his hands go, and Barcelos smelled it. He started pushing for takedowns, got in on the single leg, and while Jackson fought it off, the dynamic of the fight had completely shifted. Barcelos wore on him in the clinch, kneed him to the body, and ended the round with Jackson on his back with a body triangle and a near rear naked choke going into the bell.


Round three was more of the same Barcelos pressure — clinch work, single leg attempts, eventually getting Jackson to the ground repeatedly. Jackson kept tagging him with high kicks and left hands on the feet, which is why it was a split, but the fight ended with Barcelos on Jackson's back. That image — dominating the final sequence of every round — is what wins fights on the judges' cards. Five straight for Barcelos. He needs a top-10 opponent next. Jackson's issue is exactly what it's always been: one-punch power that evaporates after round one, followed by a passive, low-output performance that hands the decision away.

Ryan Spann vs. Marcus Buchecha

Result: KO/TKO, Right Cross at 2:10 Round 2 of 3, 7:10 Total

Ryan Spann put Marcus Buchecha stiff with a right cross in the second round, and the only surprise was that it took that long.


The first round was Buchecha doing what Buchecha does — shooting for takedowns repeatedly, briefly getting on top, briefly getting to mount. But he was already gassed by the end of round one. Spann's guillotine threat was real enough to complicate the takedown entries, and every time Buchecha got on top he didn't have the MMA-specific grappling to actually do anything with the position. Spann ended the round on top landing ground and pound while Buchecha sucked wind underneath him.


Round two didn't last long. Herb Dean stopped things twice for finger extensions — never a good sign for the pace of a fight — and then Buchecha took it down to the mat again, to no avail. Spann got back to his feet, put together a 2-3 combination, and Buchecha went out stiff. Nasty finish. Spann needed that win badly after a rough stretch, and it delivered in the most definitive way possible. As for Buchecha: cut him. He is one of those guys who was supposed to be the next coming of something great and has done essentially nothing in the UFC because he is terrible at fighting, jiu-jitsu background notwithstanding. MMA is not a jiu-jitsu tournament and Buchecha has never figured that out.

Prelims

Eric McConico vs. Rodolfo Vieira

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Live by the sword, die by the sword. Rodolfo Vieira came out in round one looking exactly like Rodolfo Vieira — a spinning back fist landed clean early and Eric McConico went down, Vieira jumped on him, got the back, went for the rear naked choke, transitioned to an armbar when McConico rolled, and somehow McConico survived all of it and got back to his feet. Vieira got another takedown, got on the back again against the cage, and again McConico scraped his way out. It was the kind of round where you think it's only a matter of time.


Then round two happened. The grappling slowed down, the fight went to the feet, and McConico started teeing off. Vieira, who had spent all that energy in round one chasing submissions that never came, had nothing left to work with. McConico landed some nice shots over the course of rounds two and three and took the unanimous decision going away.


The lesson here is the same one it's always been with Vieira: if you survive the early grappling storm, you're fine. If you don't, you're cooked. McConico survived, and Vieira — who is aging and clearly being figured out — paid for burning out in the first round. Good win for McConico. Vieira's run at relevance in the UFC middleweight division appears to be winding down.

Jackson McVey vs. Sedriques Dumas

Result: Submission, D'arce Choke at 2:14 Round 1 of 3

Jackson McVey went out there and got the finish in round one, and this one wasn't particularly close at any point. McVey got Sedriques Dumas against the cage in the clinch early, landed some knees and shoulder strikes, and then caught Dumas with a right hand that sent him to the canvas. McVey pounced immediately and started landing serious ground and pound — the fight could have been stopped at any point during that sequence, and honestly the referee let it go longer than he probably should have. McVey kept working, found the d'arce choke, and got the tap.


Good win from McVey. He kept his composure, applied pressure throughout, and finished when the opportunity presented itself. What is Dumas doing in the UFC at this point? He doesn't look like he enjoys it, keeps getting his ass whooped, and seems to exist on the roster purely to collect a show check and tap out eventually. Cut him and let someone who actually wants to be there take the spot.

Michelle Montague vs. Mayra Bueno Silva

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

Pretty shit fight. Both fighters were garbage out there, and neither one is going to be confused for a polished all-around MMA fighter anytime soon.


Michelle Montague took the fight to the ground in round one and just ate up Mayra Bueno Silva — side control, ground and pound, Bueno Silva trying to survive and throwing up a heel hook that Montague escaped without breaking a sweat. Round two was messier. Bueno Silva had some moments — she hurt Montague with a right against the cage and even went for a ninja choke that Montague rolled out of — but every time she created something good, she gave it back. The most egregious moment came in round three: Bueno Silva reversed a takedown, got to the back, got a hook in, and then just stopped fighting for the submission. She removed the hooks herself and lost the position. Lazy. Montague got back to side control and landed some nasty elbows to close the fight.


The decision was correct. Montague's wrestling and top control were the difference. The problems are real though — Montague's striking is genuinely bad. The footwork is poor, her defense is nonexistent on the feet, and she can't put combinations together in any meaningful way. That is going to be a serious liability against better competition. As for Bueno Silva: she's 34, on a five-fight losing streak, and getting out-grappled at her own game. It's time to retire.

Cody Durden vs. Jafel Filho

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

This was embarrassing from Jafel Filho, who somehow lost a decision to a guy who took the fight on a couple days' notice and couldn't even make the original weight class. Cody Durden came in more aggressive, moved forward with pressure throughout, and dragged Filho into the kind of chaotic war that the submission specialist was completely unprepared for.


Durden was the busier fighter from the opening minute — shooting for takedowns, landing ground and pound when he got on top, maintaining pressure on the feet. Filho had his moments: he reversed position in round one, threatened kneebars, nearly locked up an arm triangle in round two and gave up his back before being saved by the bell. But Filho was visibly gassing by the third round, surviving more than competing, just trying to hang on while Durden wore on him from behind. The fight ended with Durden on Filho's back and Filho completely spent.


Granted, the fight was contested at a higher weight class because Durden couldn't make flyweight on short notice — that's real context. But you can't gas out against a replacement opponent who took the fight on two days' notice. Filho had no offensive grappling on the ground, nothing on the feet, and was effectively just waiting for a submission opportunity that never came. God knows what's next for him.

Francis Marshall vs. Lucas Brennan

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

The word "fight" is doing a lot of heavy lifting here. Francis Marshall dominated every minute of all three rounds against a man who had no business being in the octagon.


Marshall came out with a frantic pace, landing right hands from the opening bell and getting Lucas Brennan to the ground in round one. From there it was one long controlled beatdown — side control, ground and pound, Brennan scrambling and going for a heel hook that Marshall calmly escaped before getting back to mount. Round two was Marshall picking his shots selectively on the feet, landing violently every time he connected, before taking it down again and working elbows from on top. Round three, Brennan threw a body kick, Marshall caught it, tripped him, and let him back up — almost out of boredom. Just continued landing right hands while Brennan stood there visibly hurt and going nowhere.


Marshall dominated. Give him a legitimate opponent next time. Brennan accepted this fight on four days' notice to make his UFC debut and showed he is not UFC caliber. Go back to the regional scene, put in the work, and come back when you're ready. And whoever thought this was an appropriate booking — just make the card twelve fights instead of thirteen. We don't need filler like this.

Victor Valenzuela vs. Max Griffin

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

A pretty dull three rounds that ended with the right result. Victor Valenzuela won a unanimous decision in his UFC debut over Max Griffin, but neither man is going to be rewatching this one for highlights.


Valenzuela tried to make things happen — he was the more aggressive fighter throughout, looking for spinning attacks and big shots, clipping Griffin bad against the fence in round one to the point where Griffin immediately shot for a takedown out of self-preservation. In round two, Valenzuela landed a spinning attack that hurt Griffin again, and Griffin again went immediately for the takedown. It became a pattern: Valenzuela lands something clean, Griffin clinches or grabs a leg to buy time and reset. Valenzuela's problem was the bullshit he was throwing up between the good shots — Griffin is crafty enough to read telegraphed attacks and capitalize on them for takedowns. By the third round, both men looked tired despite not having done all that much, and Valenzuela was more willing to commit while Griffin was mostly just standing there, feinting and backing up.


The right fighter won. Griffin is 40, three losses in a row. It's time. Do one more fight as a retirement bout if the UFC wants to give him a send-off, then ride into the sunset — because 0-3 in your last three at that age is not a starting point for a title run comeback.

Talita Alencar vs. Julia Polastri

Result: Decision, Unanimous at 3 Rounds, 15:00 Total

A fight that told you exactly where both fighters need to improve their game, and delivered a unanimous decision result that was slightly more clear-cut than the action suggested.


Julia Polastri was the better striker by a significant margin in rounds one and two — lighter on her feet, better at keeping range, peppering the jab while Talita Alencar lumbered forward flat-footed and increasingly slow. Polastri was finding her range and making Alencar pay for walking in without any real setup. The problem is that striking is only half the sport. Every time Alencar got close enough, she converted to grappling — a takedown in round one that led to side control for the final thirty seconds, and another takedown in round two that produced half guard, ground and pound, and a Polastri survival mode that lasted the rest of the frame. Polastri's grappling was non-existent; whenever the fight went to the ground, it was pure survival.


Round three slowed down — Polastri was no longer moving as much, Alencar's nose appeared to be bothering her — and both women looked a step slower. The unanimous decision for Alencar was correct. It's either time to give her a ranked opponent or someone just outside the rankings that leads somewhere meaningful — the UFC basically threatened to cut her before her last fight, and three straight wins at 35 years old deserves a clearer next step than another fight in the dark.
